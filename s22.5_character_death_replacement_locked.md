## 22.5 Character Death & Replacement — LOCKED

When a named character dies, the world does not simply replace them at the same level. Death has permanent consequences.

- The position they held becomes vacant immediately.

- The next most senior character below them is promoted — at a lower Insight Rank than the deceased.

- If no suitable character exists to fill the role, a newly generated Rank 1 character is assigned — representing a raw recruit thrust into responsibility they are not ready for.

- The capability gap is real and lasting — it takes years of in-game time for a replacement to reach the level of the character who died.

- Disposition values held by the deceased character are lost — relationships they built die with them. Their family may inherit some historical modifiers but the personal relationships are gone.

- Commander death consequences in battle are already documented in Section 11.7 — Morale penalties, bonus removal, next commander takes over.

**SUCCESSION SYSTEM — LOCKED**

When a named character holding a lordship position (Local Daimyo or above) dies, retires, is exiled, or is removed from their position, a succession process fires. Succession is not automatic inheritance — it is a political act requiring confirmation from the authority above. The transition period between vacancy and confirmation is one of the most politically dangerous moments in Rokugan, and the game is designed to make players feel that danger. Captivity does NOT trigger succession — per Section 22.9, a hostage’s position is held open and waits for their return. Only death, retirement, exile, or formal removal triggers the succession process. Jeweled Champion and Emperor’s Chosen positions are NOT lordship positions — they do not have vassals, do not control provinces, and do not participate in the tax cascade. Their vacancies are handled by the Championship and Imperial Appointment systems (Section 11.5), not by the succession process.

**Succession Order — LOCKED:**

The confirming authority (see below) selects from eligible candidates in the following priority order. Priority is a starting point, not a binding constraint — the confirming lord may pass over higher-priority candidates for political, personal, or strategic reasons.

Priority 1 — Designated Heir: The deceased lord formally named a successor before death. Designation is a deliberate action (1 AP) recorded on the character sheet. If a designated heir exists and the confirming authority accepts them, succession is immediate. This is the fastest and cleanest path.

Priority 2 — Eldest Legitimate Child: The deceased’s eldest living biological child of the same clan. “Legitimate” means acknowledged by the deceased lord during their lifetime. Unacknowledged children (born of affairs, secret relationships) are not eligible unless formally adopted before death.

Priority 3 — Other Biological Children: Younger legitimate children, in birth order. Each is a valid candidate the confirming lord may select.

Priority 4 — Adopted Heir: A character formally adopted into the family for the purpose of succession. Adoption is a deliberate action (1 AP) that generates an Adoption topic (Section 16.5) with the adoption_variant flag (clear_succession or questionable_legitimacy per Section 15.7). Adopted heirs are politically weaker than biological children — their claim is more easily contested.

Priority 5 — Siblings of the Deceased: Brothers and sisters of the same family. Viable but politically fraught — sibling succession often signals that the deceased’s line has failed.

Priority 6 — Confirming Lord Selects: If no candidates in Priorities 1–5 exist or are acceptable, the confirming authority selects any named character of the same clan and appropriate school type. This is a direct political appointment — effective but carries the stigma of the family line being broken. The selected character is adopted into the family as part of the appointment.

Priority 7 — Generated Replacement: If no suitable named character exists at all, a newly generated Rank 1 character is created per Section 22.5. This is the worst-case outcome — a raw recruit thrust into authority they are not ready for.

**Confirmation Authority — LOCKED:**

Each tier of the feudal hierarchy has a specific confirming authority one level above. The confirming lord must spend 1 AP to formally confirm the successor. Confirmation can be done in person, by letter (subject to delivery delay), or at court. Local Daimyo successor: confirmed by the Provincial Daimyo. Provincial Daimyo successor: confirmed by the Family Daimyo. Family Daimyo successor: confirmed by the Clan Champion. Clan Champion successor: confirmed by the Emperor. If the Emperor is unavailable or the position is contested, this becomes an automatic Tier 1 crisis — the stability of an entire Great Clan is at stake. If the confirming authority is themselves dead or vacant (a cascading death — both a Provincial Daimyo and their Family Daimyo killed in the same battle), confirmation authority passes to the next level up. If no confirming authority exists in the chain, the vacancy escalates to the Emperor automatically.

**Emperor Succession — Special Case — LOCKED:** The Emperor’s succession does not follow the standard lordship succession order. It follows the Hantei Succession Conventions (Section 2.1.19). The designated heir of the Imperial dynasty takes the throne — typically the eldest legitimate child. Confirmation is by consensus of the three non-dynastic Imperial family daimyo (Seppun, Otomo, Miya) rather than by a single superior. If no designated heir exists and no direct children survive, the Otomo family — which serves as the repository for the Imperial bloodline through the Ritual of Abdication — traces the family’s blood lineage to the nearest living candidate. Non-inheriting siblings of the new Emperor undergo the Ritual of Abdication, renouncing their claim and joining the Otomo (or occasionally the Seppun). An Imperial spouse whose child ascends also takes the Otomo name. If the Imperial families cannot reach consensus on a successor, or if no viable candidate exists in the bloodline, an Imperial Succession Crisis fires (Section 16.3). The Emperor’s personality archetype (Section 55.10.1) is set on the new Emperor’s character sheet at the moment of succession — a new Emperor may have a completely different archetype than his predecessor, producing an immediate and dramatic shift in the political landscape.

**Transition Period — LOCKED:**

The period between vacancy and confirmed successor is the transition window. Duration: minimum 7 daily ticks (1.75 real days) for a clean succession with a designated heir the confirming lord accepts without dispute. Maximum 60 daily ticks (15 real days) for a contested succession that must be resolved at court. The confirming lord’s disposition toward the candidate, personality, and current priorities all affect how quickly they act.

During the transition window the following effects apply. Tax Cascade (Section 4.3.7): the vacant tier’s cut is suspended. Rice that would have been deposited into the deceased lord’s stockpile instead remains at the tier below — the vassal lords retain it temporarily. When the successor is confirmed, they inherit the settlement stockpile as it stood at death but do not retroactively collect the suspended cut. Rice retained by vassals during the transition is theirs to keep. This creates a small economic incentive for vassals to delay confirmation. Koku Flow (Section 4.3.9): the vacant tier stops passing Koku upward and stops distributing stipends downward. Characters below the vacant tier receive no stipend during the transition. The reduced/no stipend consequences from Section 4.3.9 apply. When the successor is confirmed, Koku flow resumes from the next monthly tick. Vassal Objectives (Section 18.6): all primary objectives derived from the deceased lord become obsolete immediately. Vassals default to their standing objectives. The new successor must reassign primary objectives after confirmation — by AP action or letter. The Negligent Lord / Vassal Self-Selection system (Section 55.26) governs what vassals do if reassignment is slow. Army Command (Section 11.7): if the deceased commanded an army in the field, the commander death consequences from Section 11.7 fire immediately — Morale penalties, bonus removal, next officer in the chain assumes tactical command. The tactical successor is not the political successor — a Chui who takes over a legion in battle does not become the new lord. Political succession and military succession are separate processes. Settlement Stockpile: the deceased lord’s settlement stockpile (Rice, Iron, Arms, Koku) is frozen during the transition. No withdrawals except ongoing garrison and PU consumption, which continue automatically. The confirmed successor inherits whatever remains.

**Clean vs. Disputed Succession — LOCKED:**

Clean Succession occurs when ALL of the following are true: a designated heir exists (Priority 1), OR there is exactly one eligible candidate at the highest available priority level; the confirming authority’s disposition toward the candidate is Acquaintance (+11) or above; no other named character with a claim (Priorities 1–5) formally contests the succession; the deceased died of natural causes, in honorable battle, or by seppuku — not by assassination, disgrace, or suspicious circumstances. Clean succession resolves within 7–14 daily ticks. A Tier 4 topic (Promotion or Appointment) is generated. No crisis.

Succession Dispute occurs when ANY of the following are true: multiple candidates at the same priority level exist and the confirming authority has not designated a preference; the confirming authority’s disposition toward the highest-priority candidate is Rival (−11) or below — they do not want this person in power; a named character with a claim (Priorities 1–5) formally contests the succession (contesting costs 1 AP and generates a Succession Dispute topic immediately); the deceased died by assassination, and concealment failed — suspicious circumstances make everything about the succession politically charged; the confirming authority is themselves dead, vacant, or incapacitated — no one has clear authority to confirm. A Succession Dispute escalates to a Tier 2 crisis. The transition period extends to a maximum of 60 daily ticks. If unresolved within 60 ticks, the next authority up (or the Emperor) intervenes and appoints a successor directly — ending the dispute but generating significant political fallout.

Succession Dispute Court Resolution: a Succession Dispute can be resolved at any court session where the confirming authority (or their superior) is present. The court actions available mirror the Minor Clan Dispute crisis type (Section 16.3): mediate, advocate for one candidate, propose a duel between claimants, apply pressure or leverage. The confirming authority makes the final decision — court actions influence their disposition and position but do not override their authority. If two candidates agree to resolve the dispute by iaijutsu duel (Section 4.8.1), the winner is confirmed immediately. The loser may be offered a lesser position, exile, or — in extreme cases — expected to commit seppuku.

**Assassination and Succession — Cross-Reference:** When a successful assassination kills a lord, the following sequence fires. (1) Death topic generated per Phase 3 concealment outcome (Section 12.8) — natural causes, suspicious, or violent murder variant. (2) Position becomes vacant immediately. (3) Succession process begins. If concealment failed (suspicious or violent murder variant), the suspicious circumstances condition automatically triggers a Succession Dispute even if a clean designated heir exists — because the political environment is poisoned. Someone killed the lord. Who benefits from the successor? Was the heir involved? (4) All cascade effects (tax, Koku, vassals, army) fire as defined above. (5) Investigation system engages per the Magistrate System Interface Contract (Section 12.8). A perfectly executed assassination with full concealment can produce a clean succession — the lord died of illness, the heir steps in smoothly, and nobody asks questions. A botched assassination guarantees a Succession Dispute on top of the murder investigation — compounding chaos that may be more damaging to the target clan than the death itself. Confirmed successor inherits Major Favors from the deceased per Section 12.10.

**HEIR EVALUATION AND DESIGNATION — LOCKED**

A lord does not simply designate their eldest child. Heir designation is an active evaluation of all eligible candidates, scored through weighted factors that reflect what the lord personally values. Two lords looking at the same set of candidates may choose differently based on their personality, their knowledge, and their relationship with each candidate. The evaluation fires during the seasonal Strategic Review (Section 55.2) for any lord-tier NPC holding a lordship position who has no designated heir or whose re-evaluation triggers have fired.

**The Heir Evaluation Score — LOCKED:** Each eligible candidate receives a composite score from nine weighted factors. The lord selects the highest-scoring candidate.

Factor 1 — Disposition (base weight: 15). The lord’s personal disposition toward the candidate. Disposition +61 or above (Trusted Ally/Devoted): full 15 points. +31 to +60 (Friend): 10 points. −10 to +30 (Acquaintance/Stranger): 5 points. −11 to −30 (Rival): 1 point. −31 or below (Enemy/Blood Enemy): 0 points — a lord will not designate someone they actively hate unless no other candidate exists.

Factor 2 — Birth Order (base weight: 12). Tradition matters in Rokugan. Eldest legitimate child: 12 points. Second child: 8 points. Third child or later: 5 points. Adopted heir: 4 points. Sibling of the lord: 3 points. Unrelated appointee: 0 points.

Factor 3 — Honor Rank (base weight: 10). Honor Rank 7+: 10 points. Honor Rank 5–6: 8 points. Honor Rank 3–4: 5 points. Honor Rank 2: 2 points. Honor Rank 1 or below: 0 points.

Factor 4 — Glory Rank (base weight: 8). Glory Rank 6+: 8 points. Glory Rank 4–5: 6 points. Glory Rank 2–3: 3 points. Glory Rank 1 or below: 1 point.

Factor 5 — Insight Rank (base weight: 10). Insight Rank 5+: 10 points. Insight Rank 4: 8 points. Insight Rank 3: 6 points. Insight Rank 2: 3 points. Insight Rank 1: 1 point.

Factor 6 — School Type Relevance (base weight: 8). Primary school type matches the position’s dominant demand (bushi for military province, courtier for political province, shugenja for spiritually significant province): 8 points. Secondary match (capable but not ideal): 4 points. Mismatch: 1 point.

Factor 7 — Relevant Skill Ranks (base weight: 7). Evaluated against the top 3 most relevant skills for the position. Average of top 3 relevant skills Rank 5+: 7 points. Average Rank 3–4: 5 points. Average Rank 2: 3 points. Average Rank 1 or below: 1 point.

Factor 8 — Known Achievements (base weight: 10). This is where the topic system connects directly to heir evaluation. A lord can only credit achievements they know about. The lord’s topic pool is checked for topics involving the candidate. Each relevant topic adds points, but only if the lord holds that topic in their active or historical topic memory. Achievement categories and points per qualifying topic: Battle Outcome (candidate participated, winning side): +2 per topic. Battle Outcome (candidate was commander, winning side): +3 per topic. Duel (candidate won honorably): +2 per topic. Promotion or Appointment (candidate elevated): +1 per topic. Completed primary objective (candidate achieved an assigned task, per Section 18.5): +2 per topic. Disgrace or Defeat (candidate involved negatively): −2 per topic. Betrayal (candidate was the betrayer): −5 per topic. Cap: maximum 10 points from achievements. Minimum 0 — negative achievement scores floor at 0, they do not subtract from other factors.

Information asymmetry: a lord who is well-connected, attends courts regularly, and maintains active correspondence knows more about their candidates’ achievements than one who is isolated. A candidate who performed brilliantly at a distant court gains nothing from it if their lord never heard about it. This creates a genuine incentive for candidates to ensure their accomplishments reach their lord’s ears — through letters, through allies at court, through the daily conversation system. A Scorpion who controls information flow can suppress knowledge of a rival candidate’s achievements.

Factor 9 — Titles and Positions Held (base weight: 5). Currently holds a position at Status 5+: 5 points. Status 3–4: 3 points. Status 1–2: 1 point. No current position: 0 points.

**Personality-Driven Weight Modifiers — LOCKED:** The base weights represent a neutral lord. Each Bushido and Shourido virtue modifies specific factor weights. Both apply simultaneously — they stack.

Bushido Virtue Weight Modifiers: Jin (Compassion): Disposition ×1.5, Honor ×1.3, Glory ×0.7, Achievements ×0.7. A compassionate lord chooses someone they trust and who shares their values — military glory matters less. Jin lords are least likely to prioritize combat achievements. Yu (Courage): Achievements ×1.8, Glory ×1.3, Disposition ×0.7, Birth Order ×0.7. A courageous lord wants a proven warrior — what you have done matters more than who your mother was. Yu lords actively weight battle and duel topics in the achievement score. Rei (Courtesy): Birth Order ×1.5, School Type ×1.3, Disposition ×1.2, Achievements ×0.8. A courteous lord respects tradition and propriety — the eldest child with proper training is the natural choice. Chugi (Duty): Disposition ×1.5, Insight Rank ×1.3, Birth Order ×1.2. A duty-bound lord wants someone loyal and capable. Gi (Honesty): Honor ×2.0, Achievements ×0.8, Disposition ×0.8. An honest lord values integrity above all else — the candidate with the highest Honor is almost always their choice. A candidate with Honor below 3.0 is effectively disqualified. Meiyo (Honor): Honor ×1.8, Glory ×1.2, Skills ×1.2, Disposition ×0.8. Values the complete honorable samurai. Makoto (Sincerity): Disposition ×1.3, Honor ×1.3, Achievements ×1.2, Glory ×0.7. A sincere lord wants an heir who is genuine and proven — Glory matters less than actual demonstrated capability.

Shourido Virtue Weight Modifiers: Seigyo (Control): Disposition ×1.8, Insight Rank ×0.8, Achievements ×0.7. A Control lord wants someone they can manage — they actively avoid candidates with high Insight Rank or Glory who might resist direction. This is the only virtue that penalizes candidate capability. Re-evaluates every season regardless of trigger conditions. Ketsui (Determination): Achievements ×1.5, Insight Rank ×1.3, Birth Order ×0.7. Respects results — tradition means nothing if the candidate has not proven themselves. Dosatsu (Insight): Skills ×1.5, Insight Rank ×1.3, School Type ×1.3, Achievements ×1.2. Evaluates the candidate’s full capability profile — selects the most objectively qualified candidate regardless of birth order or personal affection. Chishiki (Knowledge): Skills ×1.8, Insight Rank ×1.3, School Type ×1.2. Values education and expertise above all. Kanpeki (Perfection): Honor ×1.3, Glory ×1.3, Skills ×1.3, Insight Rank ×1.3, Achievements ×1.2. Wants the candidate who excels across the board — no single factor dominates. Kyoryoku (Strength): Achievements ×1.8, Glory ×1.5, Skills ×1.2, Birth Order ×0.5. Wants a powerful successor proven in battle. Birth order is almost irrelevant. Overwhelmingly weights combat-related achievement topics. Ishi (Will): Birth Order ×2.0, Disposition ×1.3, all other factors ×0.8. Makes their choice once and never revisits it — defaults heavily to tradition reinforced by personal loyalty. Once designated, the heir is permanent regardless of what happens afterward. Does not re-evaluate. Only the designated heir’s death or the lord’s own standing objective change triggers re-evaluation.

**Re-evaluation Triggers — LOCKED:** Most lords re-evaluate their heir designation at the seasonal Strategic Review if any of the following have changed: a new eligible candidate has come of age (gempuku); a candidate’s Honor, Glory, or Insight Rank has changed significantly (±1.0 or more); the lord’s disposition toward a candidate has crossed a threshold boundary; a significant achievement topic involving a candidate has entered the lord’s topic pool; the current designated heir has been disgraced, captured, or killed; a marriage has produced a new child. Exception — Ishi (Will): does not re-evaluate; designation is permanent once made. Exception — Seigyo (Control): re-evaluates every season regardless of changes.

**Topic Memory and Achievement Decay — LOCKED:** Achievement topics in the lord’s topic pool follow the standard Tier 4 topic decay rules (Section 16.5). A battle victory from ten years ago that nobody has discussed in years has decayed out of the lord’s active topic memory and no longer contributes to the achievement score. Recent achievements weight more heavily than old ones because the lord remembers them. A candidate whose achievements are regularly discussed maintains their achievement score longer. A candidate whose achievements fade from conversation loses their score over time. A candidate with powerful allies who keep their name in circulation has a structural advantage over one who is equally capable but socially isolated. A lord with Chishiki (Knowledge) or Dosatsu (Insight) virtue retains topics longer in their pool due to their natural attentiveness to information — this is an emergent benefit already handled by the topic system, not a special case here.

**NPC Decision Logic — Designated Heir — LOCKED:** An NPC lord evaluates heir designation as part of the seasonal Strategic Review (Section 55.2). If they hold a lordship position and have no designated heir, the engine flags this as a low-priority task. If they are above age 40 or have active enemies at Blood Enemy disposition, the priority increases.

Togashi serves as both removing authority and confirming authority for the replacement. Full mechanics in Section 55.10.2. **Phoenix Clan:** Shiba Clan Champion succession bypasses the standard process entirely.
