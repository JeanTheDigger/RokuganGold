## 57.36 Lesser Zone Subtype Definitions and Flag Matrix

### 57.36.1 Overview

SUPREMACY CLAUSE: this section defines the authoritative flag values for all Lesser Zone subtypes. Where any other section references a zone flag (performance_permitted, wall_art_slot, displayed_art_slot, fusuma_slot, bonsai_display_slot, tokonoma, garden_eligible, shrine_eligible), the values defined in this section’s flag matrix are binding.

Section 4.3.4 originally defined two categories of Lesser Zone: Urban zones and Wilderness zones. The v564 amendment adds the Navigation Zone middle tier, making the Castle/Estate Compound a Navigation Zone that contains child Lesser Zones. This section defines the third category of Lesser Zone: Castle Interior zones — rooms and spaces within a Castle/Estate Compound Navigation Zone that require distinct flag values for the artisan, performance, garden, and worship systems to function. Castle Interior zones are Lesser Zones. They use the existing Lesser Zone infrastructure: zone_id, zone_name, zone_type, parent zone (the Castle/Estate Compound Navigation Zone), exits, NPC pools, zone_event_log. They count toward the settlement’s total Lesser Zone allocation (Section 4.3.4). A city with 7–10 Lesser Zones may allocate 3–4 of those to castle interior zones depending on the lord’s rank.

### 57.36.1a Japanese Castle and Manor Architecture

This subsection provides architectural reference for implementers and designers. Rokugani castles and manors follow Japanese architectural principles — the zone subtypes defined below are rooted in real building layouts, not invented abstractions. Understanding the physical architecture is essential to making the zone system feel authentic.

The compound, not the building. A Japanese castle is not a single structure. It is a walled compound containing multiple interconnected buildings arranged around courtyards and gardens. The compound is enclosed by walls — stone, timber, or earth depending on wealth and era — with formal gates controlling access. Within the walls, separate buildings serve distinct purposes: the main hall for audiences, residential wings for the lord and guests, service buildings for kitchen and storage, a shrine for worship, training grounds for martial practice. Buildings are connected by covered corridors (watariro) and open-sided verandas (engawa) that serve as circulation, transitional spaces between interior and exterior, and informal gathering areas. Walking through a castle compound means moving between buildings across courtyards, not walking down corridors inside a single structure.

Enclosures. Larger castles organise their compounds into concentric or nested enclosures. The honmaru (main enclosure) contains the lord’s residence and the primary audience hall — the most secure and prestigious area. The ninomaru (second enclosure) houses retainers, senior staff, guest quarters, and administrative buildings. The sannomaru (third enclosure) contains barracks, stables, armouries, and service buildings. Each enclosure has its own gate and walls, creating layers of security. A visitor approaching the lord passes through the outer enclosure (functional, military), through the second enclosure (administrative, residential), and finally enters the main enclosure (political, personal). This layered approach maps directly to the zone system: outer zones are less restricted, inner zones are more private. A Provincial Daimyo’s modest castle may have only a honmaru and a loose outer area. A Clan Champion’s capital has all three enclosures fully developed.

The tenshu (tower keep). Some castles feature a multi-story tower — the iconic image of a Japanese castle. The tenshu is a symbol of authority and a last-resort defensive position, not a residence. Lords live in the palace buildings at the base of the tenshu, not in the tower itself. Not all castles have a tenshu; many Rokugani holdings are manor-style compounds (yashiki) without a tower, especially at Provincial and City Daimyo rank. The tenshu is primarily a Clan Champion or major military fortification feature.

Shoin-zukuri (study-hall style). The dominant architectural style for Rokugani elite residences. Rooms are defined by tatami mat flooring, fusuma (opaque sliding panels painted with landscapes, birds, or abstract patterns) for walls between rooms, shōji (translucent paper screens) for exterior walls admitting soft light, and a tokonoma (recessed decorative alcove) in the most important room. The tokonoma displays seasonal art — a hanging scroll (kakemono), an ikebana arrangement, and sometimes a prized object. The tokonoma wall is the focal point of the room; the most honoured guest sits with their back to the tokonoma. Ceiling height, the quality of timber, the refinement of fusuma paintings, and the size of the tokonoma all communicate the room’s status and the lord’s wealth.

Fusuma and flexible space. Fusuma panels slide in grooved tracks and can be removed entirely, transforming several small rooms into one large hall or subdividing a hall into private chambers. A castle’s ōhiroma (great hall) may be a single enormous room for formal audiences or may be divided by fusuma into smaller meeting spaces for daily use. This flexibility is a core feature of Japanese architecture — rooms are not fixed in purpose. A guest room becomes a strategy meeting room becomes a performance space depending on which fusuma are open or closed. In the game, this flexibility is abstracted: each zone has a fixed set of flags, but the narrative description should convey that rooms shift and reconfigure.

The engawa (veranda). The raised wooden platform that wraps around buildings, sheltered by the eaves of the roof. The engawa is the transition between inside and outside — shoes are removed here, visitors are greeted here, tea is served here overlooking the garden. It is simultaneously a corridor (connecting rooms along the building’s exterior), a social space (a place to sit and converse while watching the garden), and a defensive perimeter (attackers must cross the engawa before reaching shōji screens). In the zone system, the engawa is part of whichever interior zone it borders — it is not a separate zone.

Gardens as architecture. The garden is not separate from the building — it is designed to be viewed from specific rooms through specific frames. A tsuboniwa (inner courtyard garden) is a tiny enclosed garden between building wings, visible from the engawa and through shōji screens. It exists to be seen from inside, not to be walked through. A karesansui (dry landscape garden) of raked gravel and placed stones creates a meditative landscape viewed from a seated position. Larger strolling gardens (kaiyu-shiki) are designed with paths that reveal composed views at each turn. The relationship between building and garden is deliberate: a room’s tokonoma may frame a view of the garden through an adjacent window, so the seasonal art inside and the seasonal garden outside complement each other.

The chashitsu (tea room). A purpose-built structure for the tea ceremony, often a small detached building in or near the garden. Deliberately austere — low ceiling, tiny entrance (nijiriguchi, a small crawl-through door forcing all entrants to bow, equalising status), a single tokonoma with a seasonal scroll and flower arrangement, a hearth for boiling water. The tea room seats 4–6 people. Its design enforces intimacy, humility, and attention. A chashitsu appears only at Family Daimyo rank and above — it requires deliberate investment in cultural refinement. A Provincial Daimyo conducts tea in the audience chamber; a Clan Champion has a dedicated tea room because their court demands that level of cultural infrastructure.

The shrine compound. Most castles include a small Shinto shrine within or adjacent to the walls — a torii gate, a stone or timber altar, shimenawa rope with paper shide, and often a small wooden building housing a kami representation. Buddhist chapels are less common in castle compounds but appear at temples and larger holdings. The shrine is the spiritual anchor of the compound — the lord worships here, seasonal rites are performed here, and the spiritual health of the holding radiates from this point. In the zone system, the shrine may be a separate Lesser Zone (at Clan Champion rank) or tiles on the courtyard’s ASCII map (at Provincial rank).

Manor (yashiki) vs castle (shiro). A yashiki is an unfortified or lightly fortified residential compound. It follows the same architectural principles — multiple buildings, engawa, fusuma, tokonoma, garden — but lacks the defensive walls, gates, and military infrastructure of a shiro. A village headman’s yashiki might be a single building with a thatched roof, one audience room, and a small garden. A wealthy retainer’s yashiki in a city might rival a modest shiro in refinement. The distinction in the zone system is scale: a yashiki generates fewer interior zones than a shiro of the same lord rank, because it has fewer physically distinct spaces. A city estate owned by a courtier is a yashiki — 1–2 interior zones at most, regardless of the owner’s personal rank, because the building is modest even if the owner is powerful.

Seasonal awareness. Japanese architecture is designed to change with the seasons. Fusuma panels may be swapped between summer (light, airy paintings) and winter (warm, enclosed scenes). Shōji screens are opened wide in summer for air circulation and closed in winter for insulation. Tokonoma displays rotate — cherry blossom in spring, hydrangea in summer, chrysanthemum in autumn, pine in winter. Gardens are composed to be beautiful in every season: cherry trees for spring, moss and ferns for summer, maple for autumn, snow-covered stones for winter. This seasonal rhythm is already implemented in the painting system’s seasonal rotation (Section 57.27), the ikebana system’s seasonal arrangement (Section 57.29), and the garden system’s seasonal maintenance (Section 57.23). The architecture IS the container for the seasonal art cycle.

### 57.36.2 Castle Interior Zone Scaling

The Castle/Estate Compound defined in Section 4.3.4 expands into multiple interior Lesser Zones based on the lord’s rank. These interior zones replace the single “Castle/Estate Compound” zone — they are the castle, not additions to it. The compound’s gate, walls, and overall access restrictions are shared properties of the parent settlement Greater Zone; the interior zones are the navigable spaces within.

Village Headman or Minor Lord (1–2 interior zones): OHIROMA (combined great hall and audience room — the headman’s one formal space), COURTYARD (outdoor space, doubles as outer courtyard and garden). A village “castle” is a fortified manor, not a sprawling compound. The headman has one public room and one yard.

City Daimyo or Provincial Daimyo (3–4 interior zones): OHIROMA (great hall — court sessions, major performances, formal receptions), AUDIENCE_CHAMBER (private meetings, tokonoma, fusuma), GUEST_WING (guest quarters, tokonoma, wall art), COURTYARD_AND_GARDEN (outer courtyard + tsuboniwa combined, bonsai display, garden). Optionally a CASTLE_SHRINE if the holding includes a shrine compound. Privacy compression: at 3 zones (low end), the audience chamber and guest wing share a single Lesser Zone. Zone-level actions (EAVESDROP, SEARCH_QUARTERS, zone_event_log reads) operate across both spaces — a spy lodged in the guest wing can eavesdrop on private audiences because they are in the same zone. The lord’s private quarters exist as tiles on the ōhiroma’s ASCII map, meaning SEARCH_QUARTERS at Z4 covers both the great hall and the lord’s bedroom. This is intentional: smaller castles offer less operational security. A Provincial Daimyo’s modest compound does not have the physical scale for true privacy compartmentalization. At 4 zones (high end), the audience chamber and guest wing separate, partially resolving this. Full privacy resolution occurs at Family Daimyo rank (4–6 zones) where LORD_QUARTERS, AUDIENCE_CHAMBER, and GUEST_WING are all independent zones.

Family Daimyo (4–6 interior zones): OHIROMA, AUDIENCE_CHAMBER, GUEST_WING, ENKAI_HALL (banquet hall — entertainment, less formal), TSUBONIWA (inner garden courtyard, separate from outer courtyard), CASTLE_SHRINE. Larger compounds add LORD_QUARTERS (private, fusuma, wall art) and CHASHITSU (tea room, tokonoma, intimate performances).

Clan Champion Capital (5–8 interior zones): All Family Daimyo zones plus DOJO (training hall), WAR_COUNCIL_ROOM (military planning, no art, no performances), OUTER_COURTYARD (large, formal outdoor space, festival performances and bonsai displays). The great clans maintain the most refined castles in the Empire; their zone count reflects this.

Imperial Palace — Otosan Uchi (10+ interior zones): Handcrafted (Section 4.3.4). Every zone type defined below is present. Multiple instances of some types (several guest wings, multiple garden courtyards, dedicated music pavilion). The Imperial Palace is the most complex interior space in the game.

Functional zones (BARRACKS, ARMORY, KITCHEN, STOREROOM, PRISON, STABLES) exist on the ASCII map as rooms within the compound but are NOT separate Lesser Zones. They are tiles and tile clusters on the castle’s ASCII map accessible within whichever Lesser Zone they physically occupy. They carry no art or performance flags because they are functional spaces, not social or aesthetic ones. The NPC engine never needs to track a character’s location at the barracks level — a character “at the castle” who is in the barracks is simply in the OUTER_COURTYARD or equivalent parent zone on the world simulation layer.

### 57.36.3 Castle Interior Zone Subtypes

OHIROMA (Great Hall): the primary formal space. Court sessions are held here (CALL_COURT). Major performances. Formal receptions. The lord sits on the dais. Largest interior room. Flags: performance_permitted true, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot true, fusuma_slot true, bonsai_display_slot false, tokonoma false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

ENKAI_HALL (Banquet Hall): entertainment and feasting. Less formal than the ōhiroma but still public. Performances welcome. Flags: performance_permitted true, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot true, fusuma_slot true, bonsai_display_slot false, tokonoma false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

AUDIENCE_CHAMBER (Private Audience Room): one-on-one political conversations. The lord’s private meeting space. Smaller, intimate. Flags: performance_permitted false, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot true, fusuma_slot true, tokonoma true, bonsai_display_slot false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

CHASHITSU (Tea Room): tiny, intimate, designed for tea ceremony. 4–6 person capacity. A performance venue — tea ceremony itself is a performance, and intimate musical performances (a single biwa player) are historically appropriate. Audience broadcast reach is capped at the room’s capacity. Flags: performance_permitted true, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot false, fusuma_slot false, tokonoma true, bonsai_display_slot false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

GUEST_WING (Guest Quarters): rooms for visiting dignitaries. Refined but semi-private. Visitors are lodged here. Flags: performance_permitted false, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot false, fusuma_slot true, tokonoma true, bonsai_display_slot false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

LORD_QUARTERS (Lord’s Private Chambers): the lord’s personal space. Private — only those invited may enter. PERFORM_FOR works here (no venue restriction) but PUBLIC_PERFORMANCE does not. Flags: performance_permitted false, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot true, fusuma_slot true, tokonoma true, bonsai_display_slot false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible false.

WAR_COUNCIL_ROOM (Military Planning): maps, strategy, command meetings. No art. No performances. Purely functional. Flags: all false.

DOJO (Training Hall): martial training and practice. Functional. Flags: all false.

OUTER_COURTYARD (Castle Outer Courtyard): the main outdoor space within the compound. Mustering point, outdoor ceremonies, festival performances. Bonsai displays. Garden cultivation possible in designated areas. Flags: performance_permitted true, wall_art_slot false, displayed_art_slot false, fusuma_slot false, tokonoma false, bonsai_display_slot true, garden_eligible true, shrine_eligible false.

TSUBONIWA (Inner Courtyard Garden): small enclosed garden between buildings. Contemplation space. Not a performance venue — too intimate and focused on natural beauty. Bonsai display. Garden cultivation. The adjacent engawa often serves as a tea-preparation area overlooking the garden — tokonoma is true, making this a valid Tea Ceremony venue (Section 57.37). Flags: performance_permitted false, wall_art_slot false, displayed_art_slot false, fusuma_slot false, tokonoma true, bonsai_display_slot true, garden_eligible true, shrine_eligible false.

CASTLE_SHRINE (Compound Shrine): Shinto shrine within the castle compound. Worship, offerings, shide placement, religious statue placement. Wall art for religious paintings. Flags: performance_permitted false, wall_art_slot true, displayed_art_slot false, fusuma_slot false, tokonoma false, bonsai_display_slot false, garden_eligible false, shrine_eligible true.

### 57.36.4 Urban District Zone Flags

The urban district zone types defined in Section 4.3.4 receive the following flags. These are the district-level Lesser Zones that exist for all settlements.

MARKET_STREET: performance_permitted true (street performers, festival performances in market squares). All art flags false — no permanent interior spaces for art display. garden_eligible false. shrine_eligible false.

RESIDENTIAL_QUARTER: all flags false. Private homes are not performance venues, art display spaces, or worship sites at the district level. Individual NPC homes on the ASCII map may contain tokonoma, but the zone-level flag is false — no system-level art or performance activity fires at the district level.

TEMPLE_GROUNDS: performance_permitted true (festival performances at temples are a core part of Rokugani religious and cultural life). wall_art_slot true (religious paintings in the main hall). displayed_art_slot true (byōbu in the main hall). fusuma_slot true (temple fusuma paintings). tokonoma false (temples use altar arrangements, not tokonoma). bonsai_display_slot true (temple gardens). garden_eligible true (temple gardens). shrine_eligible true.

PLEASURE_QUARTER: performance_permitted true (geisha houses are THE performance venue in Rokugan outside of courts). wall_art_slot true (geisha houses display art). displayed_art_slot true (byōbu in fine establishments). fusuma_slot true. tokonoma true (refined geisha houses have tokonoma). bonsai_display_slot false. garden_eligible false. shrine_eligible false.

DOCKS_WATERFRONT: all flags false. Functional commercial space.

POOR_QUARTER: all flags false.

GOVERNMENT_QUARTER: performance_permitted false (magistrate offices are not performance venues). wall_art_slot true (official art in government buildings — Imperial decrees, clan banners). All other art flags false. shrine_eligible false.

### 57.36.5 Wilderness Zone Flags

All wilderness zone types (Road, Forest Path, Mountain Pass, River Crossing, Farmland) have all flags set to false. No permanent structures, no art display, no performances, no gardens, no worship infrastructure. Exception: SHRINE_CLEARING has shrine_eligible true — wilderness shrines are valid worship sites. All other flags false.

### 57.36.6 Flag Definitions

performance_permitted (boolean): when true, PUBLIC_PERFORMANCE is valid at this zone. When false, PUBLIC_PERFORMANCE is rejected by the validator. PERFORM_FOR (targeted, private) has NO venue restriction and works in any zone regardless of this flag. Source: Section 15.4 Category 2. wall_art_slot (boolean): when true, the zone supports one kakemono hanging scroll. Placement via DISPLAY_PAINTING. One slot per zone — new placement displaces existing. Source: Section 57.27. displayed_art_slot (boolean): when true, the zone supports one byōbu folding screen. Large rooms only. Visitor disposition modifier at quality +1. Source: Section 57.27. fusuma_slot (boolean): when true, the zone has fusuma sliding doors that can be painted in place via COMPOSE_PAINTING (AT_ZONE). Source: Section 57.27. tokonoma (boolean): when true, the zone has a tokonoma alcove for ikebana display and seasonal art arrangement. Also gates CONDUCT_TEA_CEREMONY (tokonoma OR shrine_eligible required). Source: Section 57.29 (ikebana system), Section 57.37 (Tea Ceremony system). bonsai_display_slot (boolean): when true, the zone supports bonsai display via DISPLAY_BONSAI. Outdoor zones only (courtyards, gardens). Source: Section 57.24. garden_eligible (boolean): when true, CULTIVATE_GARDEN and MAINTAIN_GARDEN are valid at this zone. Requires outdoor space. Source: Section 57.23. shrine_eligible (boolean): when true, PERFORM_WORSHIP fires at this zone. Religious paintings and statues at this zone generate passive Worship Points. Guardian statues eligible. Also qualifies as a CONDUCT_TEA_CEREMONY venue (tokonoma OR shrine_eligible required). Source: Sections 4.3.21, 57.27.11, 57.28.6, 57.28.7, 57.37.
