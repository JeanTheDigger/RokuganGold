## 11.11 Insurgency System --- LOCKED

Insurgencies are internal threats that operate within provinces rather than against them from outside. Unlike a Shadowlands Incursion or a formal Clan War, an insurgency does not arrive with an army. It grows from within — from desperation, corruption, or supernatural contamination — and is detected, contained, and suppressed through investigation and targeted military action rather than pitched battle.

**Insurgencies as Player Content --- LOCKED**

Insurgencies are not background simulation events. They are the primary source of ground-level character content — the things player characters actually do with their action points beyond court. When a Maho Cult is detected in a province, someone has to go there. When a Peasant Revolt erupts, the garrison commander needs support. When a Ronin band is burrowed into the mountains, a bushi lord sends warriors to root them out. These are not abstract dice rolls resolved on the World Map. They are quests.

The workflow is: the World Map detects or surfaces the insurgency through the Stability and detection systems below. A lord assigns characters to investigate or suppress — costing Action Points (Section 14) and requiring travel to the affected province. Once on the ground, the character enters the ASCII map layer (Section 4.4.2) for the actual investigation and combat. The insurgency’s lair, hideout, or corrupted area is a generated environment on the ASCII map — rooms to explore, enemies to fight, evidence to find, and decisions to make. Suppression rolls are the mechanical outcome of what happens in those rooms. A successful dungeon run reduces Strength. A failed or incomplete run does not. The ASCII map is where the drama lives; the World Map tracks the consequences.

This means insurgencies serve three simultaneous design purposes: they give player characters meaningful things to do with their AP and travel budget; they create emergent quests driven by the simulation rather than scripted content; and they make province governance feel consequential — a well-run province generates no insurgency content because there is nothing for insurgencies to feed on. Neglect and misfortune produce the content. Good governance prevents it.

**⚙️ CROSS-REF (Quest System):** The full ASCII map mission generation system --- map templates, terrain probability pools, procedural layout rules, enemy roster composition, population placement, objective types, resolution mechanics, morale rules, named NPC integration, and relocation mechanics --- is defined in Section 56 (Quest System). This section (11.11) defines the World Map insurgency lifecycle. Section 56 defines what happens when a player character enters the ASCII map to engage with that insurgency.

Seven insurgency types exist: Maho Cult, Peasant Revolt, Ronin Bandit Uprising, Province Taint Manifestation, Nezumi Infestation, Urban Criminal Network, and Pirate (Wako) Fleet. All seven share the same underlying mechanics — a hidden Strength value, a Concealment value, detection conditions, spread behavior, and suppression methods — with type-specific modifiers that reflect their different natures. Province Taint Manifestation is additionally governed by a separate Province Taint Level tracker described below. The Urban Criminal Network is the only settlement-level insurgency type — it attaches to a specific settlement rather than operating at the province level. This section defines the shared system first, then the type-specific entries.

**⚙️ CROSS-REF (Law ****&**** Order):** The Ronin Bandit Uprising type should align with the bandit lore in Sections 2.8.10 and 11.4.1. The Maho Cult type connects to the three specialized anti-maho organizations (Asako Inquisitors, Kuni Witch-Hunters, Scorpion Kuroiban) documented in Section 11.3.5 — these are natural counter-agents for Maho Cult detection and suppression. Urban gang and criminal activity (Section 11.4.2–4.5) is now modelled as the Urban Criminal Network insurgency type below. Pirate activity (Section 11.4.3) is modelled as the Pirate (Wako) Fleet insurgency type below. Naval system fully designed in Section 11.9.

**PROVINCE STABILITY ****&**** UNREST SCALE --- LOCKED**

Every province has a Stability score ranging from 0 to 100. Stability represents the baseline loyalty, contentment, and orderliness of the civilian population. High Stability means a province is well-governed, fed, and garrisoned — its people are not looking for alternatives. Low Stability means the province is vulnerable to exactly the kind of internal threats this system covers.

Stability is the inverse of Unrest. The unrest increments already locked in Section 4.3.6 map onto this scale: a province starts at 100 and loses Stability as unrest accumulates. The two terms are interchangeable in the system — Stability = 100 minus total accumulated Unrest. Both values are tracked but Stability is the number displayed to the player.

**Stability Thresholds --- LOCKED:**

- **76–100 (Stable):** No insurgency can spawn. Garrison operates at full effectiveness. Koku generation unmodified. Province is a reliable political and economic asset.

- **51–75 (Restless):** Ronin Bandit Uprisings and Nezumi Infestations can spawn. Garrison effectiveness −10%. Koku generation ×0.9. The province is showing strain — people are discontent but not yet desperate.

- **26–50 (Volatile):** Ronin Bandit Uprisings, Peasant Revolts, and Nezumi Infestations can spawn. Garrison effectiveness −25%. Koku generation ×0.75. An active insurgency in this range spreads more easily. The province is visibly unstable — neighboring lords notice.

- **0–25 (Broken):** All five insurgency types can spawn. Garrison effectiveness −50%. Koku generation ×0.5. Maho Cults find fertile ground here — desperate people make poor choices. Province Taint Manifestation spawns independently of Stability via the PTL system (see below). An active insurgency in a Broken province escalates its crisis tier by one automatically.

**Stability Gains and Losses --- LOCKED:**

The following events modify Stability. These incorporate the unrest increments already locked in Section 4.3.6 and add new modifiers from governance and military events.

- Shortage (first season of deficit): −1 Stability per season. Already locked in Section 4.3.6.

- Hunger (2nd consecutive season of deficit): −3 Stability per season. Already locked in Section 4.3.6.

- Famine (3rd+ consecutive season of deficit): −10 Stability per season. Already locked in Section 4.3.6.

- Under-garrisoned province (garrison below 5% of settlement PU): −2 Stability per season. Already referenced in Section 4.3.11.

- Active War Status in or adjacent to the province: −2 Stability per season while War Status persists.

- Successful raid on the province: −5 Stability immediately.

- Active insurgency present in the province: −1 Stability per season while insurgency persists. Insurgency feeds instability, which in turn feeds further insurgency.

- Province Clear (no starvation, garrison at or above minimum, no active insurgency, no War Status): +2 Stability per season. Recovery is slow. A province devastated by famine and war takes years to stabilize.

- Peace bonus active (4+ consecutive seasons without combat event, per Section 4.3.6): +1 additional Stability per season. Stacks with the Clear recovery bonus for a maximum natural recovery of +3 Stability per season.

**THE SHARED INSURGENCY MECHANICS**

Every insurgency, regardless of type, operates through the same five-phase lifecycle: Spawning, Hidden Growth, Detection, Active Crisis, and Suppression. The system tracks each insurgency as a province-level object with a Strength value (1–10) and a Concealment value (1–10).

**Phase 1 — Spawning:**

Each season, the system checks every province whose Stability falls within a tier that permits that insurgency type. A spawn roll is made. The base spawn chance is determined by Stability tier and modified by type-specific conditions (see type entries below).

- Restless (51–75): 10% base spawn chance per season per eligible insurgency type.

- Volatile (26–50): 25% base spawn chance per season per eligible insurgency type.

- Broken (0–25): 50% base spawn chance per season per eligible insurgency type.

A province can host multiple insurgencies simultaneously. Each insurgency type spawns and operates independently. A province in deep crisis might have a Ronin Bandit Uprising and a Peasant Revolt at the same time, or a Maho Cult and a Province Taint Manifestation coexisting. Each insurgency tracks its own Strength, Concealment, and lifecycle independently. Multiple active insurgencies each contribute their -1 Stability per season penalty, compounding the province’s decline. When a new insurgency spawns it starts at Strength 1 and Concealment equal to its type’s base Concealment value.

**Phase 2 — Hidden Growth:**

Each season while an insurgency remains undetected, it grows. Strength increases by +1 per season automatically. Concealment decreases by −1 per season as the insurgency becomes harder to hide — larger operations leave more traces. When Concealment reaches 0, the insurgency is automatically detected regardless of whether any investigation action was taken.

An insurgency that reaches Strength 5 while still undetected generates a detection hint — a sign that something is wrong without yet confirming what. The hint type depends on the insurgency: a Jigoku bleed event for Maho Cult or Province Taint Manifestation; a Tier 4 Banditry topic for Ronin Bandit Uprising or Peasant Revolt; and missing or ransacked food stores for Nezumi Infestation. The lord receives this as an observable indicator but not a confirmed crisis.

At Strength 7, even an undetected insurgency begins spreading. The operation has grown large enough that its influence bleeds outward regardless of how carefully it hides. A spread check fires each season against each adjacent province at half the base chance (7.5% base, modified by the adjacent province’s Stability tier: Restless ×1, Volatile ×2, Broken ×3, Stable ×0). A spread event spawns a new Strength 1 insurgency of the same type in the adjacent province. That new insurgency starts hidden — its Concealment is set to its type’s base value, as if freshly spawned. This means a powerful undetected insurgency can seed neighbours before anyone knows it exists, creating the nightmare scenario of multiple simultaneous insurgencies whose common origin is unclear.

**Phase 3 — Detection:**

An insurgency can be detected in two ways: automatically when Concealment reaches 0, or early through an active investigation action. A lord who suspects a problem may commit a magistrate or shugenja character to investigate the province — this costs 1 AP and makes an Investigation roll against the insurgency’s current Concealment value as TN (Concealment × 5).

- Success: Insurgency detected. Its type, approximate Strength (within ±1), and location within the province are revealed. Crisis generated at the appropriate tier.

- Partial success (beat TN by less than 5): Type detected but Strength unknown. Crisis generated. Investigation continues next season with a −5 bonus TN on the follow-up roll.

- Failure: Nothing confirmed. The insurgency is aware it was probed — its Concealment increases by +1 as it goes to ground. A Maho Cult may attempt to eliminate the investigator (see type entry).

Detection generates a crisis topic at the tier appropriate to the insurgency type. From this point the crisis is public knowledge within the topic system and other lords can respond through court commitments.

**Phase 4 — Active Crisis:**

Once detected, the insurgency generates a crisis at the tier defined by its type. Each season it remains unsuppressed, Strength continues to increase by +1 (up to a maximum of 10) and the crisis generates momentum per the standard momentum system. At Strength 5 the insurgency begins spreading: each season a spread check fires against each adjacent province. Base spread chance is 15% per adjacent province, modified by that province’s Stability tier (Restless: ×1, Volatile: ×2, Broken: ×3, Stable: ×0 — Stable provinces cannot be spread into). A spread event spawns a new Strength 1 insurgency of the same type in the adjacent province.

**Phase 5 — Suppression:**

Suppression requires military force, investigation, or both depending on the insurgency type. A suppression action costs 1 AP and commits either a military unit or named characters to the province for one season. The suppression roll is made against a TN of (insurgency Strength x 5). Each participant who physically performs the suppression makes their own roll --- a magistrate who sends yoriki uses the yoriki's skills, not the magistrate's. COMBAT SUPPRESSION FORMULA (Ronin Bandit Uprising, Peasant Revolt, Nezumi Infestation): Each participant rolls (Highest Bugei Skill + Agility) k Earth + (Insight Rank x 3). This represents personal combat effectiveness --- weapon proficiency, striking power, survivability, and overall experience. A skilled swordsman with high Kenjutsu is devastating in a bandit camp even if they have no Battle skill. INVESTIGATION SUPPRESSION FORMULA (Urban Criminal Network, Maho Cult): Each participant rolls (Investigation + Perception) k Awareness + (Insight Rank x 3). This represents detective work --- finding hidden networks, gathering evidence, identifying members, dismantling operations. For Maho Cult specifically, Lore: Shadowlands may substitute for Investigation, and shugenja using Sense Evil gain +5 to the roll. SPIRITUAL SUPPRESSION FORMULA (Province Taint Manifestation): Each participant rolls (Lore: Shadowlands + Intelligence) k Void + (Insight Rank x 3), or (Theology + Intelligence) k Void + (Insight Rank x 3). This is purification, not combat. NAVAL SUPPRESSION FORMULA (Pirate (Wako) Fleet): Each participant rolls (Sailing + Intelligence) k Water + (Insight Rank x 3). This represents naval combat effectiveness --- ship-handling under fire, reading sea conditions, coordinating boarding actions. Ships provide the platform but the roll is the commander’s personal skill. The Mantis Clan suppression bonus (+3 to all pirate suppression rolls) and the Crab Koutetsukan bonus (automatic +2 Strength reduction per season when a Koutetsukan is committed) apply on top of this formula per the Pirate (Wako) Fleet type specification later in this section. MIXED TYPES (Nezumi Infestation): Participants may choose either the combat or investigation formula depending on their approach. A bushi hunts and fights; an investigator tracks and seals tunnels. LEADER BONUS: When multiple characters coordinate suppression, one is designated as the leader. The leader rolls Battle/Perception once. The result is added as a flat bonus to every participant's individual suppression roll that season, including the leader's own roll. This applies identically to all four formula branches --- combat, investigation, spiritual, and naval. This represents tactical coordination --- the leader reads the situation and directs the team's efforts. A leader with Battle 5, Perception 4 rolling 9k4 can add +25 or more to every team member's roll. A team without a designated leader receives no coordination bonus --- each member rolls independently. MILITARY PU SUPPRESSION: When a military unit (garrison or field army) is committed instead of named characters, the unit commander rolls Battle/Perception as the suppression roll. If no named commander exists, use base Battle 3, Perception 2 (5k2). Military PU do not use the Bugei formula --- they are abstracted forces, not individual fighters.

- Success: Insurgency Strength reduced by −3. If Strength reaches 0 the insurgency is suppressed. Crisis ends. Momentum resets to 0. Stability gains +5 immediately.

- Partial success (beat TN by less than 5): Insurgency Strength reduced by −1. Progress but not resolution.

- Failure: No reduction. Committing troops or magistrates who achieve nothing is itself a Tier 4 topic — the lord is seen as ineffective.

- Critical failure (10+ below TN): The suppression force suffers casualties or is compromised. Insurgency Strength increases by +1. For Maho Cults specifically, a critically failed suppression attempt risks Taint exposure for the committed characters. Named character consequences on critical failure: the character takes wounds. Roll on the Wound Severity table: 1-3 on 1k1 = Nicked (minor injury, -3 penalty for remainder of season), 4-6 = Hurt (serious injury, -10 penalty, one season recovery), 7-9 = Crippled (severe injury, -20 penalty, two season recovery), 10 = Down (incapacitated, removed from duty for the season, may die without medical attention --- Earth roll TN 20 to survive). A named character who dies in a suppression action generates a Tier 3 topic and the magistrate/yoriki position is vacant until replaced. For military PU, critical failure inflicts 10% PU casualties on the committed unit.

Multiple characters can commit to suppression of the same insurgency in the same season. When they do, this is flagged as coordinated suppression. Each character rolls separately against the same TN (insurgency Strength x 5). Their Strength reductions are cumulative --- a magistrate who rolls success (-3) and two yoriki who roll partial success (-1 each) produce a total reduction of -5. This makes staffing directly relevant: a magistrate with four yoriki all committed to suppression can potentially reduce an insurgency by -15 in a single season. Military units and named characters can coordinate freely. Each participant also faces individual risk on their own roll --- a critical failure on one character's roll inflicts consequences on that character even if others succeeded. Risk assessment: before committing to suppression, a character can evaluate the insurgency's approximate Strength through PROBE (1 AP), ORDER_PATROL (1 Civilian or Military Order per Section 57.34), or other intelligence actions. Success reveals the insurgency Strength value, allowing the magistrate to decide whether to commit personally, send yoriki, request military support, or wait for reinforcements. Without assessment, the character commits blind --- they do not know the TN they are rolling against.

**TYPE-SPECIFIC ENTRIES**

**Maho Cult**

A cell of blood sorcerers operating in secret within a province. The most dangerous insurgency type. Maho Cults do not need desperation to spawn — they are planted deliberately by the Bloodspeaker Cult or grow from isolated maho practitioners. They are the only insurgency type that can spawn in Stable provinces, though at very low probability.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 1 once detected. Momentum gain +3/tick × scope multiplier. Consistent with the existing Maho Cult Spreading crisis design in Section 16.3.

- **Spawn conditions:** Base spawn chance by Stability tier as above, plus 5% additional chance if a maho use event has been recorded in the province in the past 4 seasons (the Jigoku bleed event from Section 2.6.3 indicates prior maho activity). Uniquely, Maho Cults also have a 2% base spawn chance in Stable provinces — the Bloodspeaker Cult does not restrict itself to vulnerable regions.

- **Base Concealment:** 8. Maho practitioners are extremely skilled at hiding their activities. They are the hardest insurgency type to detect early.

- **Detection modifier:** Shugenja investigators receive a +1k0 bonus to Investigation rolls against Maho Cults due to supernatural sensitivity. Kuni Witch-Hunters and Asako Inquisitors receive +2k0. Standard magistrates without shugenja training receive no bonus and may not recognize what they have found even on a success — they report “strange activity” rather than confirming maho, requiring a follow-up shugenja visit to confirm the type.

- **Suppression method:** Requires both a shugenja character (for ritual purification — end condition per Section 16.3) and a military or magistrate force (to physically capture or kill the practitioners). A suppression action using only military force reduces Strength by −1 maximum regardless of roll result — soldiers alone cannot fully root out a Maho cult without ritual support. A shugenja-led suppression uses the standard suppression table.

- **Special — Investigator threat:** On a failed investigation roll, the cult becomes aware of the investigator. If the cult is Strength 4 or above, it may attempt to eliminate the investigator — a Taint-based attack that the investigator must resist (Stamina + Willpower vs TN 20). Failure inflicts Taint Rank 1 on the investigator. This is the only insurgency type that actively fights back against detection.

- **Special — Strength 10 consequence:** A Maho Cult that reaches Strength 10 triggers an automatic Oni Manifestation crisis (Section 16.3) in the province. The cult has completed a major summoning ritual. The insurgency crisis continues alongside the new Oni Manifestation crisis — both must be resolved.

**Peasant Revolt**

Desperate civilians taking up whatever tools they have against their lord. Peasant Revolts are the direct mechanical consequence of governance failure — they do not spawn from external manipulation but from accumulated misery. They are the loudest signal the game can send that a province has been neglected.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 3 at Strength 1–4. Escalates automatically to Tier 2 if Strength reaches 5 or if the province is in the Broken stability range — a large-scale revolt in a devastated province is a major political crisis, not a local problem.

- **Spawn conditions:** Only spawns in Volatile or Broken provinces (Stability 50 or below). Spawn chance increased by +10% if the province is currently in Hunger or Famine. Increased by a further +10% if the garrison is under-garrisoned. Decreased by −10% if the lord has the Jin (Compassion) Bushido virtue — a compassionate lord is less likely to push his people to revolt.

- **Base Concealment:** 3. Peasants are not subtle. A revolt is visible almost immediately — the question is how large it has grown before the lord responds.

- **Detection modifier:** No specialist required. Any lord with eyes on the province — a garrison commander, a tax collector, a passing merchant — can report a revolt. Automatic detection roll fires each season regardless of whether the lord spends an AP on investigation, using the garrison commander’s Perception + Investigation (base stats, Rank 1 character if no named commander present).

- **Suppression method:** Military suppression only. Magistrates can investigate and reduce Concealment but cannot suppress a Peasant Revolt — the crisis is physical, not investigative. Military suppression uses the standard suppression table. Note: a lord who suppresses a Peasant Revolt militarily without addressing the underlying cause (starvation, under-garrisoning) will find Stability continuing to decline and a new revolt spawning within seasons.

- **Special — No Honor cost for suppression (CORRECTED):** Suppressing a Peasant Revolt with military force carries no Honor cost. Killing heimin does not affect Honor under Rokugani caste law — a samurai who disciplines rebellious peasants is within their rights. The consequences of suppression are practical, not moral: PU loss from killing the province’s own workforce, continued Stability decline if the underlying cause is not addressed, and political perception from other lords who may view the revolt as evidence of incompetent governance. No Glory is earned for suppressing peasants. Cross-reference: Section 56.10.4 (Peasant Revolt roster) for ASCII map mission mechanics.

- **Special — PU loss on suppression:** Each successful or partially successful suppression action kills civilians. PU in the affected province decreases by (Strength × 0.1) per successful suppression action. This is unavoidable — putting down a revolt means killing people. Combined with the ongoing PU loss from starvation, a poorly governed province can spiral into depopulation rapidly.

- **Special — Strength 10 consequence:** A Peasant Revolt at Strength 10 seizes control of the province. The lord’s garrison is expelled or destroyed. The province generates no tax and no Koku for the lord until retaken by military force. Retaking a revolted province functions as a standard military assault on a lightly defended settlement.

**Ronin Bandit Uprising**

Masterless samurai who have turned to banditry, preying on trade routes and settlements. Unlike Peasant Revolts, which erupt from below, Ronin Bandit Uprisings are organized by displaced or disgraced warriors who bring real military capability to the problem. They are more dangerous per unit of Strength than a Peasant Revolt but less existentially threatening than a Maho Cult.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 3 throughout. Does not escalate to Tier 2 regardless of Strength — a large bandit problem is still a local problem, not a political crisis in the Clan War sense. However, a Ronin Bandit Uprising that disrupts a major trade route generates a separate Tier 3 Trade Route Disruption topic that can escalate independently.

- **Spawn conditions:** Spawns in Restless, Volatile, or Broken provinces. Spawn chance increased by +15% if the province is adjacent to a province where War Status recently ended (within 2 seasons) — wars create ronin. Increased by +10% if the lord recently disbanded military units without paying full stipends (Section 4.3.9 reduced payment consequences). These represent the real historical sources of banditry in Rokugan: war’s aftermath and economic desperation among the warrior class.

- **Base Concealment:** 5. Ronin bandits hide in wilderness and mountains but their raids on trade routes and settlements leave a trail. Moderately difficult to locate precisely, but their existence becomes obvious quickly.

- **Detection modifier:** Bushi-role investigators receive +1k0 to suppression rolls — a warrior recognizes warrior tactics. Ronin Bandit Uprisings also generate automatic Koku loss on the trade routes passing through the province: −(Strength × 0.05) Koku per season from affected routes. This economic drain makes the problem visible even without an investigation action.

- **Suppression method:** Military suppression only, but at a higher effective difficulty than Peasant Revolts — ronin are trained fighters. The TN for suppression rolls is (Strength × 7) rather than the standard (Strength × 5), reflecting their military competence. Magistrates can investigate and reduce Concealment but cannot suppress directly.

- **Special — Hiring option:** A lord may attempt to hire the ronin band rather than suppress them. This costs 2.0 Koku per Strength point (matching the ronin hire cost from Section 4.3.11) and requires a Courtier + Awareness roll vs TN (Strength × 5). Success converts the band into a hireable Ronin unit available to the lord immediately. The insurgency ends without military action and without Stability penalty. Failure means the ronin reject the offer and Strength increases by +1 — they took the lord’s approach as a sign of weakness. Personality gate: Meiyo (Honor) lords will not hire bandits under any circumstances. Gi (Honesty) lords will not negotiate without first formally demanding surrender.

- **Special — Strength 10 consequence:** A Ronin Bandit Uprising at Strength 10 has effectively become a small army. All trade routes through the province are severed entirely. The province’s Koku generation drops to zero. The uprising now functions as a hostile military force and must be engaged through the standard Army Combat System (Section 11.7) rather than the insurgency suppression roll — it has grown beyond policing and requires a military campaign.

**PROVINCE TAINT LEVEL --- LOCKED**

Every province has a Province Taint Level (PTL) ranging from 0 to 10. PTL is separate from character-level Taint Rank — it measures how thoroughly Jigoku’s corruption has saturated the land itself, not any individual. A province with PTL 0 is spiritually clean. A province with PTL 10 has become a local extension of the Shadowlands, functionally unusable until purified.

PTL is the most insidious threat in the system because it accumulates silently. It does not announce itself with a rebel flag or a burning village. It seeps in through maho use, Shadowlands proximity, Jigoku bleed events, and unresolved Taint Manifestation insurgencies. By the time most lords notice it, the province is already compromised.

**PTL Gains --- LOCKED:**

- Each confirmed maho use event in the province: +1 PTL immediately (per Section 2.6.3).

- Active Maho Cult insurgency present: +1 PTL per season while the cult exists unsuppressed.

- Active Province Taint Manifestation insurgency present: +1 PTL per season while unsuppressed.

- Province is adjacent to the Shadowlands and the Kaiu Wall condition is degraded: +0.5 PTL per season.

- A Lost character (Taint Rank 5+) is present in the province for a full season: +0.5 PTL.

**PTL Losses --- LOCKED:**

- Shugenja performing a purification ritual: −1 PTL per ritual (1 AP, character physically present, Earth or Void affinity preferred).

- Province Taint Manifestation insurgency fully suppressed: −1 PTL immediately on suppression.

- Natural decay: −0.5 PTL per season if no PTL gain events fire and no active Taint insurgency is present. The land heals slowly if the source is removed.

**PTL Adjacent Bleed --- LOCKED:**

A province with elevated PTL does not contain its corruption neatly within its borders. Jigoku’s influence seeps outward through the land itself — through water, soil, and the spiritual fabric of Ningen-do. Each season, a province with PTL 6 or above generates a passive bleed into every adjacent province:

- **PTL 6–7 (Corrupted threshold, lower range):** +0.25 PTL per season into each adjacent province. Noticeable over time but slow — a neighbouring province that does nothing will see its PTL rise by 1 point per year from this source alone.

- **PTL 8–10 (Corrupted to Blighted):** +0.5 PTL per season into each adjacent province. A Blighted province is an active spiritual cancer. Without intervention, adjacent provinces will reach PTL 3 (Touched) within two years and begin spawning their own Taint Manifestation insurgencies. The problem multiplies.

Adjacent bleed is reduced by the presence of active jade stockpiles at the clan level — a clan that maintains sufficient jade supply (per Section 2.6.3) reduces adjacent bleed into their own provinces by half. This is one of the primary strategic reasons jade acquisition matters beyond individual character protection. A lord who secures jade for their clan reduces the Taint pressure on their entire territorial border, not just the characters fighting in the field.

Adjacent bleed cannot be blocked entirely by good governance or high Stability. A Stable, well-fed province with a strong garrison still absorbs PTL from a Blighted neighbour — Jigoku’s corruption does not respect political order. The only way to stop the bleed is to reduce the source province’s PTL below 6 through purification campaigns.

**PTL Thresholds and Effects --- LOCKED:**

- **PTL 0–2 (Clean):** No mechanical effect. Province Taint Manifestation cannot spawn.

- **PTL 3–5 (Touched):** Characters spending a full season here must resist Taint (Stamina + Willpower vs TN 15) or gain 0.5 Taint Rank. Province Taint Manifestation can spawn. Jigoku bleed events generate automatically each season even without a Maho Cult.

- **PTL 6–8 (Corrupted):** Taint resistance TN rises to 25. Jade items degrade one quality tier per season of exposure. Stability loses −3 additional per season. Maho Cult spawn chance doubles. A Tier 2 crisis generates automatically the first time the province reaches this threshold.

- **PTL 9–10 (Blighted):** Taint resistance TN rises to 35. PU loses −5% per season regardless of food supply. No Koku or Rice generated. Tier 1 crisis generated immediately. Only Crab Clan characters with appropriate training and jade supplies can operate freely. A full purification campaign costing multiple seasons of shugenja AP is required before normal governance resumes.

**PTL Detection --- LOCKED:**

PTL is hidden behind observable symptoms. A standard magistrate or courtier can report that something is wrong but cannot measure PTL without specialist assistance. Three detection layers exist: observable symptoms (Jigoku bleed events at PTL 3+, visible to any lord monitoring province reports); shugenja investigation (1 AP, physically present, Perception + Lore: Shadowlands vs TN of PTL × 5, Kuni and Asako receive +2k0); and passive jade resonance (any character carrying jade in a PTL 3+ province notices the jade warming or discoloring — confirms spiritual contamination without revealing the PTL value).

**Province Taint Manifestation**

When PTL reaches 3 or above, Jigoku’s corruption begins generating physical manifestations — Tainted animals, minor oni, undead remnants, and corrupted spirits. These are not a coordinated invasion. They are symptoms of a spiritually sick land that prey on the population, accelerate PTL, and provide ASCII map dungeon content until suppressed.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 3 at PTL 3–5. Tier 2 at PTL 6+. Tier 1 at PTL 9+.

- **Spawn conditions:** Spawns automatically when PTL first reaches 3. No spawn roll required and no Stability threshold check — PTL is a separate axis from Stability. A perfectly well-governed, high-Stability province with elevated PTL from prior maho activity will still spawn a Province Taint Manifestation when PTL hits 3. Stability does not protect against PTL-driven spawning. A new Manifestation spawns each time PTL increases by a full point above 3 while no active Manifestation insurgency exists in the province.

- **Base Concealment:** 6. Visible through effects but locating the concentration requires investigation. Standard magistrates detect symptoms but not the source without shugenja support.

- **Suppression method:** Requires shugenja for purification AND military force to engage corrupted entities. Military-only suppression reduces Strength by −1 maximum — entities keep reforming without spiritual cleansing. Each successful shugenja-led suppression action reduces PTL by −0.5 in addition to standard Strength reduction.

- **Special — ASCII map enemies:** Entities drawn from Section 54 Bestiary (Gaki, bog hags, zombies, minor oni, Tainted animals) scaled to current Strength. At Strength 8+ a named Oni presence may require full Oni Manifestation crisis mechanics (Section 16.3).

- **Special — Maho Cult acceleration:** If both a Maho Cult and a Taint Manifestation are active in the same province simultaneously, PTL gains are doubled. This is the most dangerous insurgency combination in the system and the one most likely to generate a Tier 1 crisis rapidly.

**Nezumi Infestation**

A tribe of Nezumi — the intelligent bipedal ratlings of Section 54.2 — has established a warren within the province. In this version of Rokugan, Nezumi found within the Empire’s borders are feral and hostile. They are not the cautious trading partners the Crab occasionally deal with at the Wall’s edge. These are aggressive territorial creatures that attack samurai on sight, raid food stores systematically, and defend their warrens ferociously. There is no diplomacy path. They must be suppressed by force. Notably, Nezumi are immune to the Shadowlands Taint (Section 54.2) — a Nezumi Infestation does not accelerate PTL and cannot be detected through spiritual means. It is a purely physical threat requiring purely physical solutions.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 3 at Strength 1–7. Tier 2 at Strength 8–10. Follows the same Strength-threshold escalation model as Urban Criminal Network (Tier 2 at Strength 8–10) and Peasant Revolt (T2-34 PEASANT_UPRISING at Strength 8+). At Tier 2, propagation expands to CLAN_LORDS — the Clan Champion becomes a relevant decision-maker. Tier 2 crisis entry: Nezumi Colony Crisis in Section 16.3. Topic chain: T4-85 NEZUMI_INFESTATION + T3-36 NEZUMI_INFESTATION_CRISIS at Phase 3 detection → Tier 2 T2-35 NEZUMI_COLONY_CRISIS at Strength 8+. AMENDED v618 — previously read "Tier 3 throughout. Never escalates regardless of Strength."

- **Spawn conditions:** Can spawn in any province in the Empire. Base spawn chance 5% per season regardless of Stability tier — Nezumi respond to food and shelter, not Rokugani politics. Spawn chance increased by +10% in provinces adjacent to the Shinomen Forest or the Shadowlands border, and by +10% in provinces where a prior Nezumi Infestation was suppressed within 8 seasons — the tribe remembers and may try again nearby. Large grain stockpiles are more attractive targets but do not modify the spawn roll directly.

- **Base Concealment:** 7. Extraordinary burrowers, underground warrens, scatter at samurai approach. Spiritual detection is completely ineffective. Only physical tracking and patrol can locate the warren.

- **Detection modifier:** Shugenja receive no bonus — Nezumi are immune to spiritual detection. Bushi and scouts with Hunting or Stealth receive +1k0. Lore: Nezumi grants an additional +1k0. Kuni Witch-Hunters and Asako Inquisitors have no advantage here. Raided Rice stores generate an automatic partial detection hint regardless of investigation rolls.

- **Economic effect:** Steals (Strength × 0.1) Rice from province stockpile per season. Does not disrupt trade routes or Koku generation directly.

- **Suppression method:** Military suppression at standard TN (Strength × 5). The warren is an underground dungeon on the ASCII map — tunnels, ambush points, warriors protecting females and pups. Nezumi fight fiercely to protect their home. No PU loss on successful suppression — casualties are Nezumi, not provincial civilians.

- **Special — Strength 10 consequence:** A major tribal migration. Rice theft rises to 1.0 per season. The colony is now permanent and must be dislodged through full army combat mechanics (Section 11.7). An ignored Strength 10 colony will independently cause Shortage conditions through sustained Rice drain. Nezumi Colony Force Roster (army-scale Company stat blocks) defined in Section 54.2 alongside the individual Nezumi archetypes. Added v618.

- **Special — Spread behavior:** Nezumi Infestations follow the standard spread mechanics at Strength 5 with one exception: unlike other insurgency types, a Nezumi Infestation can spread into Stable provinces. The standard ×0 Stable multiplier does not apply to Nezumi — they are not deterred by good governance, only by physical suppression. Spread chance into a Stable province is 5% per adjacent province per season (equivalent to Restless ×1, without the Stability modifier).

**Urban Criminal Network --- LOCKED**

A criminal organization operating within a settlement — smuggling cartels, protection rackets, fireman gangs, opium distribution rings, or combinations thereof. Unlike every other insurgency type, Urban Criminal Networks do not spawn from desperation or supernatural corruption. They spawn from opportunity. A large, wealthy settlement with active commerce is a natural habitat for organized crime. The criminals do not want to destroy the economy — they want to parasitize it. A functioning, prosperous city is their ideal environment.

Urban Criminal Networks are settlement-level insurgencies, not province-level. They attach to a specific settlement (town or city) rather than operating across the province generally. A province with three towns could have a criminal network in one and not the others. This is the only insurgency type with this granularity — all others operate at the province level.

- **Crisis tier:** Tier 4 at Strength 1–4 (local nuisance — rumors of smuggling, occasional protection racket complaints). Tier 3 at Strength 5–7 (noticeable economic drain, trade route disruption, magistrate reports of organized activity). Tier 2 at Strength 8–10 (entrenched criminal economy, officials corrupted, outside intervention may be required). Never reaches Tier 1. Criminals are not an existential threat to the Empire.

- **Spawn conditions:** Does NOT use the province Stability-based spawn table. Uses settlement-specific conditions. Base spawn chance per season: Village (PU below 500) 0%, Town (PU 500–2000) 3%, City (PU 2000–5000) 8%, Major City (PU 5000+) 15%. Modifiers: trade route through settlement +5% per route; garrison below 5% of settlement PU +10%; garrison at or above 10% of settlement PU −5%; province Stability below 50 +5%; corrupt magistrate assigned +10%; prior network suppressed within 4 seasons +5%; opium trade active in province +5%. Floor: minimum 2% spawn chance per season in any qualifying settlement regardless of modifiers. Crime is inevitable in cities.

- **Base Concealment:** 7. Criminal networks operate behind legitimate fronts — merchant houses, fireman gangs, labor organizations, teahouses. They hide in plain sight. High Concealment reflects the difficulty of distinguishing criminal activity from normal commerce without dedicated investigation.

- **Detection modifier:** Magistrates with Commerce 3+ or Underworld Knowledge: +1k0. Emerald Magistrates: +2k0. Scorpion Clan magistrates in own territory: +1k0. Bushi investigators: −1k0 penalty. Shugenja investigators: no bonus. Automatic detection hint at Strength 3: the settlement’s Koku generation visibly drops below expected values — a report of lower-than-expected revenue, not a confirmed criminal network.

- **Economic effect — Progressive Koku drain:** Settlement Koku generation reduced by Strength-scaled percentage. Strength 1–3: (Strength × 2)%. Strength 4–6: (Strength × 3)%. Strength 7–10: (Strength × 3)%. A Strength 10 network siphons 30% of settlement Koku. The drain affects the settlement specifically, not the entire province.

- **Stability effect — Tiered:** Strength 1–4: no Stability penalty. The network operates quietly; the population does not notice. Strength 5–7: −1 Stability per season. Protection rackets squeeze merchants, gang violence becomes visible. Strength 8–10: −2 Stability per season. Open criminal activity, enforcers operating in daylight, officials visibly compromised.

- **Suppression method:** Magistrate-led investigation and law enforcement. Magistrate suppression: 1 AP, Investigation + Awareness vs TN (Strength × 5), standard suppression outcome table. Military suppression: can be attempted but TN is (Strength × 7); on any result the settlement loses (Strength × 0.5)% Koku generation for 2 seasons from disrupted commerce, PU loss of (Strength × 0.05) from collateral damage; military suppression at Strength 8+ risks a Tier 3 diplomatic topic. Corrupt magistrate penalty: if the assigned magistrate has the hidden corruption flag, suppression TN increases by +10. Emerald Magistrate intervention: at Strength 8+, if the local lord has failed to suppress for 4+ consecutive seasons, an Emerald Magistrate may be assigned automatically — operates with +2k0, ignores corrupt magistrate penalty, but generates a Tier 3 political topic.

- **Special — Official Corruption:** At Strength 4, the network begins actively corrupting local officials. Each season, a corruption check fires against every named NPC in the settlement holding a magistrate, tax collector, or administrative role. Uses the existing Susceptibility Framework with two additions: Commerce skill 3+ adds +1 Susceptibility; Sincerity below 3 adds +1 Susceptibility (blackmail vulnerability). A corrupted official remains in position, outwardly unchanged. Corruption manifests as: −1 to all detection and suppression rolls in their jurisdiction; Koku drain increases by +2% while the official is corrupted; any investigation action that would reduce Concealment is reported to the network, increasing Concealment by +1. A corrupted official inside a castle or clan compound extends the network’s information visibility into that space (see Network Awareness below). Detection follows Section 12.8 (Secret System).

- **Special — Network Awareness:** A criminal network embedded in a settlement hears things. When any system generates a topic within the settlement, there is a chance the network learns of it. This chance scales with Strength: Strength 1–3: 20%; Strength 4–6: 40%; Strength 7–9: 60%; Strength 10: 80%. Never 100%. Location visibility modifies this chance. High visibility (full chance): commercial transactions, trade route activity, warehouse movements, dockside activity, merchant quarter, pleasure district, gambling dens, docks — spaces the network controls or frequents. Medium visibility (half chance): public streets, temples, open markets, inns. Low visibility (quarter chance): samurai estates, clan compounds, castle interiors — exception: if the network has corrupted an official inside the castle, that space upgrades to medium visibility. Zero visibility: anything outside the settlement — wilderness, roads, military camps. The same mechanic applies to secrets (Section 12.8) generated or maintained by characters operating within the settlement. Connected settlements: topics learned by criminal contacts in other settlements along shared trade routes have a 10–15% chance per season of reaching the local network. Only topics generated in high-visibility spaces in those connected settlements travel through this channel.

- **Special — Tolerance Option (Personality-Gated):** A lord may choose to tolerate a criminal network rather than suppress it. Not Scorpion-specific — any lord whose NPC Decision Engine evaluates the cost-benefit favorably may make this choice. Tolerance requires: lord’s dominant Bushido virtue is NOT Gi (Honesty) or Meiyo (Honor). On tolerance: the lord accepts the Koku drain; Strength is frozen at its current value. In exchange, topics and secrets the network learns through the Network Awareness mechanic are passed to the lord’s awareness pool through a back channel — the network boss communicates through a corrupted official or intermediary. Connected settlement intelligence also flows to the lord. Personality gate for NPCs: Seigyo (Control) dominant most likely to tolerate; Dosatsu (Insight) dominant may tolerate if intelligence benefit outweighs cost; Chugi (Loyalty) dominant tolerates only for clan strategic interest; all others default to suppression. Tolerance is a Tier 3 severity secret (Section 12.8 — criminal conduct) attached to the lord. If exposed, generates an immediate Tier 3 Honor scandal topic.

- **Special — Non-Tolerated Network: Intelligence Liability:** A non-tolerated network still has Network Awareness. It still learns topics and secrets. The lord does not receive this information and does not control where it goes. A non-tolerated network may: sell information to rival lords through trade route contacts (functions as a covert acquisition on behalf of the buyer per Section 12.8); use information to protect itself (if it learns about a suppression operation, Concealment increases by +1); tip off connected criminal networks in other settlements about magistrate crackdowns or garrison redeployments. An entrenched non-tolerated network is an active intelligence liability — every topic generated in the settlement has a chance of reaching the lord’s enemies. This creates a genuine strategic dilemma: tolerate the network and control where its intelligence goes, or suppress it and accept the information risk while it exists.

- **Special — Strength 10 consequence:** The network has captured the settlement’s economy. 30% Koku siphoned. Multiple officials corrupted. Magistrate hierarchy compromised. Local suppression becomes impossible — requires outside intervention: Emerald Magistrate with full investigation charter (automatic event) or clan-level purge ordered by the lord’s superior. A clan-level purge generates a Tier 2 political crisis. The network at Strength 10 also begins exporting: +10% spawn chance modifier in every settlement connected by trade route.

- **Special — Spread behavior:** Criminal networks do NOT spread by province adjacency. They spread along trade routes. At Strength 5, each season: for each trade route connecting the settlement to another settlement of Town size or larger, a spread check fires. Base chance: 5% per connected settlement. Modified by: destination garrison above 10% PU −5%; destination already has a criminal network 0%; high-value trade route +5%. Geographic centrality is a vulnerability — a city at a trade crossroads is both the most likely to develop a network and the most likely to spread one.

- **Special — ASCII map content:** Uses the Urban Hideout template (Section 56.15). Physical space: warehouse, opium den, gambling house basement, or connected merchant cellars. Enemy roster: Gang Thugs (bonge enforcers with Stealth and Commerce), Ronin Enforcers (Section 54.8 Simple/Experienced Bandit stats), Corrupt Doshin (unique archetype — Investigation and Kenjutsu trained), Network Boss (named NPC if simulation generated one, otherwise generic leader with Commerce 4+ and Courtier 3+). Non-combat resolution path: a magistrate character with Investigation 4+ may gather evidence (series of Investigation + Perception rolls against room-specific TNs; 3 successful rolls allows withdrawal and formal arrests next season, reducing Strength by −2 rather than the −3 from successful combat suppression). The non-combat path exists because this is a magistrate problem — magistrates solve problems with warrants, not swords.

**SETTLEMENT BUILDING FRAMEWORK --- LOCKED**

The Criminal Network Presence Module below operates on the settlement ASCII map — the Navigation Zone maps defined in Section 4.4.1. Those maps are populated with buildings. This framework defines what a "building" is as a game data object, what building types exist per zone type, and how building density scales with settlement size. It does not define tile-by-tile layout algorithms or rendering — that is implementation work for the coder. It defines the design-level data the procedural generator works with. Although defined here because the Criminal Network module was the first system to require it, this framework is referenced by any system that needs to interact with settlement buildings — including the Urban Hideout surface search phase (Section 56.15.2).

**Building Access Categories --- LOCKED**

All buildings on the settlement map fall into three categories based on their function and module state.

Enterable buildings are infrastructure items from Section 2.3.2 — forges, tea houses, dojo, okiya, shrines, markets, and all other functional infrastructure. These are already Lesser Zones with their own interior ASCII maps generated deterministically from the zone seed (Section 4.4.1, Deterministic Map Generation). They exist in the game data as real functional locations with permanent NPCs. The street map shows their facades with functional doors. These buildings exist regardless of any module.

Facade buildings provide visual density. A residential quarter is not three enterable houses and empty space — it is three enterable houses and twenty facade buildings that create the visual density of a real neighbourhood. Facade buildings have no interior. Their doors do not open. They are atmosphere — the row of heimin houses along a street, the closed workshop, the garden wall. Facade buildings are only used for building types that are not public-facing businesses — residences, garden walls, and other private structures that a player would have no reason to walk into uninvited.

Promoted buildings are facade buildings that a module has flagged as gameplay-relevant. When a module (Criminal Network, Maho Cult surface search, or any future module) needs a building to become interactive, it promotes a facade building. The promotion generates an interior Lesser Zone from building_type + building_id seed. The interior is normal for its type — a warehouse looks like a warehouse. The promotion is invisible to the player — they do not know which buildings were promoted by a module and which were always enterable. The player discovers gameplay-relevant buildings through investigation, not through trying doors.

Public-facing businesses are always enterable. Every shop, sake house, inn, gambling den, vendor, fishmonger, bathhouse, noodle shop, and any other business that serves walk-in customers has an accessible interior during business hours, regardless of module status. This is how commercial districts work — doors are open because the business wants customers. A network-connected sake house and a clean sake house both have open doors. The investigation challenge is figuring out which of the many accessible businesses is dirty, not finding which door opens.

Private buildings are locked by default. Residences, warehouses, cargo offices, and any building that does not serve walk-in customers require a reason to enter. Access requires one of: legal authority (magistrate status — the player presents credentials and the owner must comply), a warrant (evidence presented to a superior, formal authorisation granted through court action), owner invitation (social interaction — the player is invited inside), or break-in (criminal act — Stealth/Lockpick to enter covertly, or force the door openly, logged to the zone_event_log and subject to legal consequences per Section 2.8). This applies whether or not the building is network-connected. Warehouses are locked because warehouses are locked, not because they are hiding something.

**Building Data Object --- LOCKED**

Every building on the street map — enterable, facade, or promoted — carries a data record with the following fields:

- **building_id:** Deterministic hash from settlement_name + zone_name + building_index. Same seed always produces the same building in the same location. Consistent with the Deterministic Map Generation rule (Section 4.4.1).

- **building_type:** From the Building Type Catalog below.

- **building_name:** Generated from building_type + building_id seed. Public businesses get named establishments ("Hanako's Tea House," "The Sunrise Warehouse"). Residences get family names. Generic buildings get descriptive names. Permanent NPCs (Section 4.4.1, NPC Population Rules) are the named proprietors of their businesses — same seed produces the same name every time.

- **zone_parent:** The Navigation Zone this building's facade sits in.

- **lesser_zone_ref:** If enterable or promoted, the Lesser Zone representing the interior. Null for facades.

- **owner_id:** Character reference. For infrastructure, this is a permanent NPC generated from the zone seed. For other buildings, a lightweight NPC record (name, caste, disposition — enough for module mechanics such as Criminal Network corruption checks to operate).

- **owner_caste:** Samurai, merchant, heimin, monk, or eta. Determines the building's social character and who frequents it.

- **access_type:** PUBLIC (open during business hours, anyone can enter) or PRIVATE (locked by default, requires authority, invitation, or break-in). Determined by building_type — all customer-facing businesses are PUBLIC, all residences and storage buildings are PRIVATE.

- **building_size:** SMALL (1–2 rooms if promoted or enterable), MEDIUM (3–4 rooms), LARGE (5+ rooms). Affects facade width on the street map and interior generation if promoted.

- **frontage_tiles:** How many tiles wide the facade occupies on the street-level ASCII map. SMALL: 2–3 tiles. MEDIUM: 4–6 tiles. LARGE: 7–10 tiles.

- **flags:** A set of module-applied tags. Empty by default. Modules write here. Examples: network_front, cult_site, smuggling_point, corrupted_owner. Flags are not visible to the player — they are internal data read by module systems.

**Building Density by Settlement Size --- LOCKED**

**CRIMINAL NETWORK PRESENCE MODULE --- LOCKED**

The hideout raid described in the ASCII map content entry above is a deliberate mission — the player goes there with a suppression objective. This module describes a second content layer: any character physically present in a settlement on the ASCII map can notice and investigate the criminal network's surface-level operations while walking the streets and entering buildings. This is not a dedicated investigation mission — it is a consequence of being physically present in the settlement and having the skills to notice what is happening.

⚙️ CROSS-REF: The settlement ASCII map is the Navigation Zone map defined in Section 4.4.1. The Building Framework above provides the building data the module operates on. The Urban Criminal Network insurgency mechanics (spawn, Concealment, suppression, corruption, tolerance, Network Awareness) are defined earlier in this section. The Urban Hideout Template surface search phase (Section 56.15.2) uses the same settlement map and investigation mechanics.

**Network Building Selection --- LOCKED**

When the module activates (Urban Criminal Network at Strength 1 or above in the settlement, whether detected or undetected), it selects buildings from the settlement's existing building pool and flags them as network-connected. The network does not build new buildings — it infiltrates existing ones. Selection is deterministic from insurgency_id + settlement_seed, so the same network always occupies the same buildings unless disrupted.

Connected building count by Strength: Strength 1–3: 1 connected building per zone where the network operates. Network operates in 1 zone only — the zone with highest criminal recruitment potential. Selection priority: Docks, Pleasure Quarter, Poor Quarter, in that order. If none of these zone types exist, Market Street. Strength 4–6: 2–3 connected buildings per zone. Network expands to 2 zones. Strength 7–9: 3–5 connected buildings per zone. Network operates in 3 or more zones. Strength 10: 4–6 connected buildings per zone. Network operates in every non-restricted zone in the settlement. Castle/Estate Compound is never directly penetrated — the network reaches it only through corrupted officials per the corruption mechanic defined earlier in this section.

Building type preferences for network connection: The network selects from available buildings with weighted preference. High preference: warehouse (storage, smuggling), gambling den (money laundering, recruitment), sake house (meetings, information gathering), inn/ryokan (transient population provides cover), pawn shop (fencing stolen goods), flophouse (housing operatives). Medium preference: merchant shop (legitimate front), fishmonger (docks access), cargo office (shipping fraud), sailor's inn (smuggling contacts), labour broker (muscle recruitment). Low preference: bathhouse, noodle shop, cloth merchant, tea stand. These can be used but only when higher-preference buildings are exhausted. Never selected: temples, shrines, magistrate offices, doshin barracks, samurai residences, monk quarters, record halls, courthouses. The network avoids spaces with inherent surveillance or spiritual authority.

Connected building promotion: When the module selects a PRIVATE facade building (e.g. a warehouse), it promotes it to enterable — an interior Lesser Zone is generated from building_type + building_id seed. The interior is normal for its type, with network-specific elements layered in (see Network Signs below). PUBLIC buildings selected by the module are already enterable — the module adds network-specific interior elements without changing access.

**Network Signs in Connected Buildings --- LOCKED**

A network-connected building looks normal on casual inspection. The differences are subtle and require investigation to notice. The following elements may be present inside connected buildings, visible only through the Search Building action or passive observation:

Concealed storage: a hidden compartment under a floor, behind a wall panel, or in a false-bottomed container. Contains contraband, smuggled goods, or evidence. Not visible without a successful Search roll.

Back room access: a locked or concealed door to a back room that clean versions of the building type do not have. The back room may contain meeting space, accounting ledgers, or a passage to an adjacent building.

Coded marks: small marks near the entrance visible at close range — scratched symbols, a specific arrangement of objects in the window, a particular lantern colour. Recognisable with Lore: Underworld at TN 15 or a successful passive observation that specifically notices the mark.

Nervous proprietor: the permanent NPC who owns the building behaves differently when a samurai enters — interrupts conversations, signals to specific patrons, becomes evasive under questioning. Observable through the Question Local mechanic below.

Unusual traffic: patrons who do not fit the building type — rough-looking men in a high-class sake house, well-dressed merchants in a flophouse. Observable through passive detection.

**Network Activity Generation --- LOCKED**

Connected buildings generate observable activity on the street-level Navigation Zone ASCII map. Activity generates on a per-IC-day schedule and manifests as visible events when a player character is present on the map during the right conditions.

Activity types: Smuggling run — NPCs carry crates or bundles between a connected warehouse and a connected merchant building or docks building. Typically at night (Hour of the Rat through Hour of the Tiger — 11pm to 5am). The NPCs move along the street, enter buildings, emerge without the goods. Visible to any player on the same Navigation Zone map. Protection collection — a network enforcer visits a non-connected merchant building. Brief interaction at the door — the merchant hands over something, the enforcer moves on. Daytime (Hour of the Dragon through Hour of the Monkey — 7am to 5pm). Visible pattern if the player watches long enough to see the enforcer visit multiple merchants. Meeting — two or more network NPCs enter a connected sake house, gambling den, or inn simultaneously. Unusual clustering of rough-looking individuals who are not regular patrons. Daytime or night. Handoff — a network NPC meets a non-network NPC (a corrupted official, an informant, a buyer) at a connected building entrance or nearby street tile. Brief exchange, both leave in different directions. Hard to distinguish from normal social interaction without context. Muscle display — network enforcers loitering near a connected building or walking a repeated route through the zone. Intimidation presence. Distinguishable from normal foot traffic because they do not enter any building and they repeat the same path.

Activity frequency by Strength: Strength 1–3: 1 activity event per zone per IC day. Sparse. Easy to miss on a short visit. Strength 4–6: 2–3 events per zone per IC day. A pattern becomes visible if the player watches over multiple days. Strength 7–10: 4–6 events per zone per IC day. Blatant to anyone paying attention. At Strength 10, activity is essentially open — enforcers operate without pretence.

Activity timing: Most smuggling and meetings occur at night. Protection collection occurs during business hours. Muscle display occurs throughout the day. A player who visits only during the day sees protection and muscle. A player who stakes out at night sees smuggling and meetings. Both are clues; neither alone reveals the full picture.

**Passive Investigation --- LOCKED**

Every time a network activity event fires while a player character is present on the same Navigation Zone ASCII map, a passive detection check occurs automatically. The player does not choose to roll. The check represents peripheral awareness — the character notices something unusual in their surroundings.

Roll: Investigation (Notice) / Perception vs TN set by activity type and current Concealment.

Base TN: Concealment × 5. At Concealment 7 (the Urban Criminal Network base value), TN 35 — extremely difficult to notice passively. At Concealment 3 after investigation has worn it down, TN 15 — hard to miss.

Activity type modifier to TN: Smuggling run −5 (physical goods moving, multiple NPCs, crates are hard to hide). Protection collection +0 (looks like a normal merchant interaction). Meeting −5 (unusual clustering). Handoff +5 (brief, could be anything). Muscle display −10 (conspicuous loitering is conspicuous).

Time-of-day modifier to TN: Night-type event during nighttime when streets are quiet −5 (fewer people means unusual activity stands out). Day-type event during busy daytime +5 (lost in the crowd).

Skill bonuses from the Urban Criminal Network detection modifier defined earlier in this section apply: magistrates with Commerce 3+ or Underworld knowledge +1k0, Emerald Magistrates +2k0, Scorpion Clan magistrates in own territory +1k0, bushi investigators −1k0 penalty.

Success: The player receives a descriptive text notification of what their character observed. Example: "You notice two rough-looking men carrying crates from the warehouse at the east end of the docks toward the sake house. They move quickly and avoid the main path." The notification is descriptive, not diagnostic — it does not say "criminal activity detected." The player interprets what they saw.

Failure: Nothing. The player sees a normal street.

Accumulation: Each successful passive observation is logged to the player's investigation_notes for this settlement. Observations accumulate across visits. One sighting is a curiosity. Three sightings involving the same building establish a pattern. Five sightings across multiple buildings suggest a network. The system does not draw conclusions for the player — it provides raw observations and lets the player connect the evidence. A player who passes through the settlement once might catch one event. A player stationed in the settlement for a full season — a magistrate assigned to the town — is present for dozens of activity cycles. Passive detection accumulates naturally over time.

**Active Investigation --- LOCKED**

A player who suspects criminal activity can shift from passive observation to deliberate investigation. These actions take place on the ASCII map. They are not the formal 1 AP suppression action defined earlier in this section — that is the World Map system. These are ground-level detective actions.

Observe Building: The player positions their character within visual range of a building on the Navigation Zone map and selects "Observe." The character watches the building for a period. Action cost: Free Action, character must remain stationary. Each IC hour of observation generates one check if any network activity involves that building during that hour. Roll: Investigation (Notice) / Perception vs TN (Concealment × 3) — lower than the passive TN because the player is deliberately watching a specific location. Success reveals who entered, who left, what they carried, and how long they stayed. Logged to investigation_notes. Repeat observation of the same building across multiple IC days reveals patterns — regular visitors, delivery schedules, the identity of the enforcer. Each additional day of observation of the same building grants a cumulative −2 to the TN (minimum TN 10) as the pattern becomes unmistakable.

Follow NPC: The player spots a suspicious NPC (from a successful passive or Observe result) and follows them on the ASCII map. Action cost: sustained — the player moves when the target moves, maintaining visual range without closing distance. Roll: Stealth (Shadowing) / Agility vs the target's Investigation (Notice) / Perception. Contested, rolled once at the start of the follow and again each time the target changes direction or enters a building. Success: the player follows undetected and sees where the NPC goes — which buildings they enter, who they meet. The trail may lead to other connected buildings the player did not know about. All observations logged to investigation_notes. Failure: the target notices the tail, enters a building and does not emerge (vanishes into the neighbourhood). Network awareness check triggers (see Network Response below). Critical failure (miss TN by 10+): the target identifies the player. The player's identity is known to the network. Network awareness check triggers automatically.

Search Building: The player enters a building and searches its interior for evidence of criminal activity. Action cost: Complex Action per room searched. A SMALL building (1–2 rooms) takes 1–2 Complex Actions. A LARGE building (5+ rooms) takes 5 or more. Precondition: the player must have legal access to the building — it is a PUBLIC building during business hours, the player has magistrate authority, the player has a warrant, or the player broke in. Roll: Investigation (Search) / Perception vs TN (10 + Concealment × 2) per room. Success reveals one piece of evidence per successful room search. Evidence types: ledgers with coded entries (decoded with Lore: Underworld or Cipher skill TN 15), hidden compartments with contraband, smuggled goods with traceable markings, a list of payments to local officials, correspondence with network contacts in other settlements. Each evidence item is a discrete game object that feeds back to the World Map. Each evidence item found reduces the insurgency's Concealment by 1 — the network is being mapped. Diminishing returns: first search finds the obvious evidence, second search of the same building at +5 TN finds the hidden compartment, third search at +10 TN finds the concealed floor safe, fourth search finds nothing — the building has been thoroughly searched. Resets only if the network restocks the building (requires at least 1 IC season and the building remaining network-connected). Searching a non-connected building finds nothing suspicious — the player has wasted time but learned through elimination.

Question Local: The player speaks to a permanent NPC — a merchant, a teahouse owner, a labourer — about what they have seen in the settlement. Action cost: social interaction, no AP cost. Requires the player to be in the same building or adjacent tile as the NPC. Roll varies by NPC relationship to the network. Unconnected civilian: Courtier (Manipulation) / Awareness or Intimidation (Control) / Willpower vs TN 15. Cooperative if approached respectfully. They may have noticed odd comings and goings but do not understand what it means. Success provides one observation equivalent to a passive detection success — the NPC describes what they have seen. The NPC's observations are limited to their own zone and building vicinity. Intimidated civilian (network Strength 5+): TN 20. The NPC knows something but is afraid. Courtier works if the player can promise protection. Intimidation works but generates a Tier 4 topic if the player is seen threatening civilians. Network sympathiser: TN 25. Actively misdirects. A failed roll wastes the player's time — the NPC sends them to the wrong building or claims ignorance convincingly. A success reveals the misdirection itself — the player realises the NPC is lying, which is itself useful information. A sympathiser who has been identified as lying can be confronted again at TN 20 with Intimidation, or reported to a magistrate for formal questioning. Corrupted official: TN 30. Denies everything. May report the investigation to the network (automatic network awareness trigger). Success reveals the official's discomfort — evasive language, refusal to make eye contact, changing the subject — but not definitive proof of corruption. Definitive proof requires evidence from a building search or a formal Probe action per Section 15.4.

**Network Response --- LOCKED**

The network is not passive. When it detects investigation, it reacts. Each active investigation action (Observe, Follow, Search, Question) triggers a contested awareness check separate from whether the action succeeded — a player can successfully observe a building and still be noticed doing it.

Contested awareness roll: The network rolls Investigation (Notice) / Perception using an effective stat line scaled by Strength, against the investigator's opposing roll. Network effective stat line by Strength: Strength 1–3: Investigation 2, Perception 2 (3k2) — a loose collection of criminals with poor counter-surveillance. Strength 4–6: Investigation 3, Perception 3 (4k3) — organised enough to post lookouts and debrief members. Strength 7–9: Investigation 4, Perception 4 (5k4) — professional operation with dedicated counter-surveillance. Strength 10: Investigation 5, Perception 4 (6k4) — eyes everywhere, near-impossible to investigate undetected.

Investigator's opposing roll by action: Observe Building: Stealth (Concealment) / Agility — the investigator is trying to watch without being noticed watching. Follow NPC: Stealth (Shadowing) / Agility. Search Building (covert entry): Stealth (Sneaking) / Agility. Search Building (legal authority): no opposing roll — the network is automatically aware that a magistrate searched the building. Open searches always trigger awareness. Question Local: Courtier (Manipulation) / Awareness — the investigator is trying to extract information without making it obvious they are investigating.

Result: If the network's roll meets or exceeds the investigator's roll, awareness is triggered. If the investigator's roll exceeds the network's, the action went unnoticed.

Modifier — corrupted official in zone: If a corrupted official (per the Official Corruption mechanic defined earlier in this section) is assigned to the same zone where the investigation takes place, the network rolls with +1k0. The official reports investigative activity through the back channel.

**Awareness Escalation --- LOCKED**

Awareness accumulates per IC season, not per individual trigger. The network processes intelligence the way any organisation does — reports filter up, patterns are recognised over time, decisions are made. A single detected investigation action puts the network on alert. It does not immediately produce a coordinated response.

Awareness tracking: The network maintains an awareness_level for the settlement, starting at 0 each IC season. Each time the contested awareness roll succeeds (the network notices an investigation action), awareness_level increments by +1. The counter resets to 0 at the start of each new IC season. The network's institutional memory is short — turnover, paranoia, and compartmentalisation mean last season's suspicions do not carry forward cleanly.

Response thresholds within a single season:

Awareness 1–2 (Cautious): The network tightens up. Concealment increases by +1. Activity frequency in the affected zone drops by half for the remainder of the season. Smuggling routes shift. Meeting locations rotate to different connected buildings. No direct action against the investigator. This is the network being careful, not aggressive.

Awareness 3–4 (Concerned): The network attempts to identify the investigator. If the investigator was seen during any of the triggering actions (failed their opposing roll by 10 or more on any single check), the network knows who they are. If not, the network knows someone is investigating but not who. Either way, the network assigns a watcher — a network NPC begins following the investigator using the Follow NPC mechanic in reverse. The watcher rolls Stealth (Shadowing) / Agility vs the investigator's Investigation (Notice) / Perception. If the investigator detects the watcher, that discovery is itself a clue — someone is counter-surveilling them, confirming organised criminal activity. The watcher is a network NPC with stats appropriate to the network's Strength tier.

Awareness 5+ (Hostile — investigator identified): The network responds directly, scaled by Strength. Strength 1–4: intimidation — a warning delivered through an intermediary (a threatening note, a dead animal at the player's door, a whispered message). Tier 4 topic generated. Strength 5–7: confrontation — enforcers block the investigator on the street. Social or physical encounter at the player's choice. The enforcers demand the investigation stop. If the player refuses, the enforcers may attack (generating an ASCII map combat encounter on the street, with all attendant noise, witness, and legal consequences). Strength 8–10: assassination attempt. The network hires a professional or sends its best enforcers to eliminate the investigator. This generates a Defend Against Assassination quest seed per Section 56.1.6.

Awareness 5+ (Hostile — investigator unidentified): The network cannot retaliate against a specific person, so it purges. All connected buildings are cleaned — Search Building TNs increase by +10 permanently in those buildings (the evidence has been destroyed or moved). Activity drops to zero for the remainder of the season and the following season. Concealment resets to base value. The network went dark. The investigation hit a wall. The player must wait for the network to resume operations before gathering new evidence.

Season reset rationale: Criminal networks in this setting are not intelligence agencies. They are gangs, smuggling rings, and protection rackets. Their organisational memory is limited by the fact that their members are heimin and ronin operating under constant threat of exposure. The paranoia that follows a detected investigation fades when nothing happens for a season. A magistrate who investigates hard one season, backs off, then returns the next season starts against a reset counter. This rewards patient, multi-season investigation over brute-force single-season raids.

**Information Yield and World Map Feedback --- LOCKED**

Everything discovered on the ASCII map feeds back to the World Map insurgency tracker.

Passive observations: Each successful passive detection is logged as a clue. When a player has accumulated 3 or more observations involving the same building, the building is flagged as "suspected" in the player's investigation_notes. When 3 or more buildings are flagged as suspected, the insurgency can be formally detected on the World Map — if not already detected — without waiting for Concealment to reach 0. This is the passive detection pathway. It allows a perceptive, patient character to detect a criminal network through accumulated street-level observation rather than a dedicated investigation action.

Evidence from searches: Each evidence item found through Search Building reduces Concealment by 1 on the World Map. Evidence also generates secrets per Section 12.8 — financial records expose the money trail, name lists expose members, coded ledgers can be decoded with Lore: Underworld or Cipher skill (TN 15) to reveal connected networks in other settlements.

Identified NPCs: Network members identified through Follow NPC or Observe Building become known to the magistrate system on the World Map. They can be arrested (magistrate authority), interrogated (generates further intelligence), or surveilled (assigned a doshin tail through Section 57.34 Civilian Order Budget).

Corrupted official identification: If evidence or investigation reveals a corrupted official, this feeds into the Official Corruption mechanic defined earlier in this section. The official can be confronted through Section 18.9 betrayal consequences. Identifying a corrupted official also removes their +1k0 bonus to the network's awareness rolls in that zone.

**Interaction with Formal Suppression --- LOCKED**

The ASCII map investigation and the World Map suppression action (1 AP, defined earlier in this section) are complementary, not competing systems. They operate on different layers of the same problem. Ground-level investigation reduces Concealment and produces intelligence — it is preparatory work, the detective phase. The formal suppression action uses that reduced Concealment to calculate its TN (Strength × 5). A magistrate who spent two seasons doing ground-level investigation before committing to formal suppression faces a much lower TN because Concealment has already been eroded by evidence recovery. Evidence found on the ASCII map can reveal the network boss's identity and location, enabling a targeted arrest rather than a broad sweep. A broad sweep (formal suppression without prior ground-level work) works — it is what the suppression roll represents — but it is harder, less efficient, and produces less intelligence. The module rewards preparation without punishing directness.

**Interaction with Urban Hideout Template (Section 56.15) --- LOCKED**

When a Maho Cult operates in the same settlement as a Criminal Network, both modules attach to the same settlement street map. The Criminal Network's connected buildings and the Maho Cult's surface lookouts and sympathisers (Section 56.15.2) coexist. A player walking the streets might notice both criminal smuggling and a cultist slipping into a basement — each through their respective detection mechanics (Investigation for crime, Sense/spiritual detection for Taint). The Urban Hideout Template's surface search phase (Section 56.15.2) takes place on the same settlement ASCII map. The search behaviours described in Section 56.15.2 — talking to locals, observing who enters which buildings at odd hours, detecting spiritual contamination with shugenja abilities, following suspicious figures — are actions the player performs using the Observe Building, Follow NPC, and Question Local mechanics defined in this module, supplemented by shugenja-specific detection (Sense spell, Kansen detection per Section 56.6.4) that the Criminal Network module does not provide. The underground hideout beneath a specific building remains entirely Section 56.15's domain — this module governs the street surface, Section 56.15 governs what lies beneath.

- **Emergent interactions:** Maho Cult: the network’s awareness check applies to cult activity in commercial and public spaces — a tolerated network may inadvertently alert the lord to maho activity. Competing criminal networks: a settlement can have only one Urban Criminal Network insurgency at a time. Kolat operations (Section 54.7): the Kolat uses commercial cover, and the network notices commercial anomalies — Kolat operatives face higher detection risk in settlements with entrenched criminal networks.

**Pirate (Wako) Fleet --- LOCKED**

Pirate fleets are the seventh insurgency type and the only naval insurgency. They attach to coastal or river provinces, not to settlements or inland territory. A province can have both a land insurgency (Ronin Bandit Uprising) and a pirate fleet simultaneously — the bandits raid caravans on roads while the pirates raid shipping on the water. Pirate lore is in Section 11.4.3. Ship types and naval combat stats are in Section 11.9.

Spawn Conditions: Pirate fleets spawn on coastal or river provinces when conditions attract displaced fighters to the water. Base spawn chance: 5% per season on any coastal province during wartime (War Status active anywhere in the Empire). +10% if the province’s clan is currently at war. +10% if Province Stability is below 50. +5% per adjacent coastal province that already has a pirate fleet (piracy spreads along coastlines). Mantis provinces: −10% spawn chance (Mantis naval dominance deters wako). The Mantis expulsion of piratical elements (lore, Section 11.4.3) means the first pirate fleet to spawn in an era is 50% likely to appear near former Mantis territory. Pirate fleets cannot spawn on landlocked provinces.

Strength: Starts at 1–2 (a handful of boats). Grows by +1 per season while conditions persist. Maximum Strength 8. Each Strength point represents approximately 2–3 Kobune and 20–30 crew. A Strength 8 fleet is 16–24 ships — a serious regional naval threat.

Effects: Naval trade route Koku drain — total drain is 0.05 Koku × Strength per season (cap −0.4 at Strength 8), distributed proportionally across all naval routes passing through or adjacent to the province. A Strength 4 fleet hitting 1 route drains −0.2 from that route. The same fleet hitting 3 routes drains −0.067 from each (total still −0.2). The fleet’s raiding capacity is fixed — more routes means attacks spread thinner, not multiplied. This is the primary economic damage. Merchant shipping avoidance — at Strength 4+, merchant traffic begins rerouting. Naval trade route income drops an additional 10% as merchants avoid the area. At Strength 6+, the route is effectively closed — no merchant will sail through pirate-infested waters without escort. Coastal raiding — at Strength 3+, pirates raid coastal villages. Province Stability drops −1 per season (less than bandit raids because pirates target shipping, not peasants directly). Fisher and coastal PU suffer −5% productivity. Reputation — a province known for piracy suffers −3 disposition from trade-dependent clans (Yasuki, Ide, Mantis) per season the fleet persists.

Concealment: Base Concealment 6 (high — pirates hide in coves, river bends, and island chains). Detection requires naval patrols, not magistrate investigation. A lord with no ships cannot detect pirates. Detection methods: PATROL_PROVINCE with ships assigned (Investigation + Perception vs Concealment TN), informant reports from coastal merchants (PROBE or BRIBE_FOR_INFO at port settlements), Mantis intelligence network (Mantis clan magistrates gain +2k0 on pirate detection rolls — they know how pirates think). Once detected, the pirate fleet’s approximate location (which coastal sub-tile they operate from) is known.

Suppression: Naval combat. The lord must commit warships (Kobune, Sengokobune, or Koutetsukan) to hunt the fleet. Suppression resolves as a naval engagement using the naval combat stats (Section 11.9). The pirate fleet fights using Kobune stats at a ratio of 2–3 Kobune per Strength point. NPC suppression resolves through engine dice rolls. Player character involvement generates a Ship Boarding ASCII template (PENDING). Suppression formula: (Sailing + Intelligence) k Water + Insight Rank × 3 for the fleet commander. This follows the same (Skill + Trait) k Ring + Insight Rank × 3 structure as land suppression formulas. The ships provide the platform but the roll is the commander’s personal skill. Leader bonus: Battle / Perception roll added flat to the suppression total, same as land. Each successful suppression season reduces pirate Strength by 2–4 (PROVISIONAL). Failure means the fleet survives and may grow. Mantis clan suppression bonus: +3 to all suppression rolls against pirates (naval expertise). Crab Koutetsukan bonus: if a Koutetsukan is committed, automatic +2 Strength reduction per season (the iron turtle is devastating against wooden pirate boats).

Crisis tier: Tier 3 throughout. Pirates are an economic nuisance, not an existential threat. They generate court topics (“Lord X cannot protect his coastline”) and trade disruption but do not threaten the survival of a province. Exception: a Strength 8 fleet effectively blockading a port province may escalate to Tier 2 if the province depends heavily on naval trade income.

Pirate NPC Archetypes: Use the Ronin NPC Archetypes: Pirates stat blocks from Section 54.8. Pirate crews are ronin and displaced heimin — same stat range as bandit gangs but with Sailing skill and naval equipment. Pirate captains are named NPCs generated by the world population system, same as bandit lords.

**CHARACTER CORRUPTION ****&**** DEFECTION --- LOCKED**

Insurgencies do not only threaten provinces from the outside. They reach into the named character layer and corrupt, recruit, or radicalize the people already inside the political structure. Three distinct mechanics govern this: Peasant Revolt Defection, Maho Cult Recruitment, and Passive Taint Accumulation. Each operates independently but all three share a common susceptibility framework based on character personality, Honor, and loyalty disposition.

**THE SUSCEPTIBILITY FRAMEWORK --- LOCKED**

Before any corruption or defection roll is made, the system calculates a Susceptibility Score for the target character. This score is not displayed to the player — it operates as a hidden modifier. Higher Susceptibility means the character is more vulnerable. A Susceptibility of 0 or below means the character cannot be affected by that mechanic regardless of roll. Susceptibility is calculated fresh each season — a character whose circumstances improve becomes less vulnerable over time.

- **Disposition toward their lord:** Enemy (−60 to −31): +3. Rival (−30 to −11): +2. Stranger/Acquaintance (−10 to +30): +1. Friend or above (+31 or higher): 0.

- **Honor Rank:** Honor Rank 6+: −2. Honor Rank 4–5: 0. Honor Rank 2–3: +1. Honor Rank 1 or below: +2.

- **Glory Rank:** Glory Rank 5+: −1. Glory Rank 3–4: 0. Glory Rank 1–2: +1. Glory Rank 0: +2. A character with no public reputation has nothing to lose.

- **Shourido dominance:** +1 if the character’s Shourido virtue drives more decisions than their Bushido virtue in their behavioral profile (Section 19). The dark virtues create ambition and self-interest that insurgencies can exploit.

- **Stipend status:** Stipend reduced or unpaid for 1+ seasons: +1. Stipend unpaid for 3+ consecutive seasons: +2. Economic grievance is the most reliable radicalizer.

- **Existing Taint Rank (Maho Cult recruitment only):** Taint Rank 1: +1. Taint Rank 2+: +2. A character already corrupted is significantly easier to recruit — Jigoku’s influence is already working on them.

These modifiers do not create a separate score to subtract from a TN. Each modifier that applies adds directly to the insurgency’s effective pull value for that character. A character with no modifiers faces the insurgency’s raw Strength. Each modifier stacks, making the insurgency’s effective pull harder to overcome with the resistance roll.

**PEASANT REVOLT DEFECTION --- LOCKED**

Once a Peasant Revolt reaches Strength 3 and enters the Active Crisis phase, it begins exerting a pull on named characters present in the province with Susceptibility above 0. A defection check fires each season for every eligible character. This is a contested Temptation roll per Section 32: the revolt’s pull is represented by its Strength as a flat value. The character resists with Etiquette + Willpower (rolled), then adds their Honor Rank as a flat bonus to the total (per The Strength of Honor, Section 32). If the insurgency Strength value plus any situational modifiers below exceeds the character’s total, the character defects. A character whose total exceeds the insurgency’s pull holds firm for this season.

- **Monk characters (Brotherhood of Shinsei):** +2 Susceptibility if the province is in Famine and the revolt was triggered by starvation. Monks with Jin (Compassion) add a further +1. Their vow to protect the weak creates genuine moral pull toward the revolting peasants.

- **Samurai characters:** No additional modifier. Meiyo (Honor) grants −2 Susceptibility for this check specifically — a samurai who grounds their identity in personal honor finds the revolt beneath them even if they hate their lord.

- **Named Ronin characters:** +1 Susceptibility flat. Ronin have no lord to betray and no clan to shame. The revolt offers them purpose and food. Their +1 Susceptibility stacks with any other modifiers and applies directly to the insurgency’s effective pull against them, making them easier to draw in than a samurai in the same circumstances. If both a Peasant Revolt and a Ronin Bandit Uprising are active in the same province, the Ronin chooses based on standing objective — military or survival objectives point toward the Uprising; personal or social objectives toward the Revolt.

- **On defection:** Insurgency Strength +1. Character removed from their role. Lord notified via Betrayal topic (Tier 3 for samurai, Tier 4 for monk or ronin). Character appears as a named enemy on the ASCII map during suppression runs — can be killed, captured, or interrogated. If captured after suppression, the lord chooses: execute (−0.3 Honor if samurai, no cost if ronin/monk), formally disown, or pardon (significant political cost).

**MAHO CULT RECRUITMENT --- LOCKED**

The Maho Cult actively recruits named characters once it reaches Strength 2. Unlike the Peasant Revolt, which pulls through desperation, the cult pushes through temptation, blackmail, and Jigoku’s direct spiritual pressure. It also targets characters passing through — any character spending even part of a season in the province is eligible if their Susceptibility is 3 or above. Recruitment check fires once per season. This is a contested Temptation roll per Section 32: the cult’s pull is its Strength as a flat value plus 5 (reflecting Jigoku’s supernatural pressure, which makes the cult harder to resist than a mundane revolt). The character resists with Etiquette + Willpower (rolled), then adds their Honor Rank as a flat bonus to the total (per The Strength of Honor, Section 32). If the cult’s value exceeds the character’s total, the character is recruited. A character whose total exceeds the cult’s pull resists for this season.

- **Sleeper (Seigyo, Dosatsu, or Chugi dominant):** Continues normal life, outwardly unchanged. Covertly reports to the cult, passes information, subtly protects it from detection. No observable change in disposition values, court behavior, or stated objectives. Can only be discovered through a successful Probe action at 2 Raises (Section 15.4) or by being physically caught in a covert act. When the insurgency is suppressed, a Sleeper not identified during investigation walks away clean — carrying a hidden cult_affiliation flag on their character sheet and entering the Sleeper Aftermath below.

- **Active Saboteur (Kyoryoku or Ketsui dominant, OR Taint Rank 1+):** Begins acting against their lord immediately. Generates Jigoku bleed events in their current location, covertly drains province Rice (−0.1 per season), and obstructs investigation and suppression. After 2 seasons of active sabotage a Tier 4 Betrayal topic enters circulation. If named, confronted through Section 18.9. Active Saboteurs typically fight alongside the cult on the ASCII map and rarely survive suppression.

**Note on Ishi (Will) — Complete Immunity:**

A Will-dominant character cannot be turned by either mechanism. Their course is set and immovable — the cult’s temptation finds no purchase and the revolt’s pull finds no crack to exploit. If a Will-dominant character has modifiers that would otherwise increase the insurgency’s effective pull, the defection and recruitment checks do not fire for them at all. Ishi is a complete immunity to both mechanics. This is intentional — a Will character’s rigidity that makes them so difficult to redirect in other systems (Section 19.4) is the same quality that makes them unturnable here.

**Sleeper Aftermath — The Undetected Agent:**

Each season after surviving cult suppression undetected, the Sleeper generates these effects in their current province:

- **Passive Taint spreading:** +0.25 PTL in their current province per season. They are a walking Taint source — not deliberately, but spiritually. Provinces they inhabit see PTL tick upward without obvious cause.

- **Cult seeding:** After 4 seasons in a province: +15% to Maho Cult spawn chance there. After 8 seasons: +30%. The Sleeper is not consciously seeding a cult — they are a spiritual focal point. If a new cult does spawn, the Sleeper’s Susceptibility is automatically set to maximum for the recruitment check.

- **Personal Taint accumulation:** Taint resistance check each season (Stamina + Willpower vs TN 15). Failure: +0.5 Taint Rank. At Taint Rank 3 the character begins exhibiting observable symptoms that may alert a shugenja.

- **Detection:** Probe action at 2 Raises (Section 15.4); shugenja Detect Taint once Rank 1 is reached; or PTL anomaly investigation tracing back to the Sleeper. Detection triggers Section 18.9 betrayal consequences plus Taint Rank becoming public knowledge.

**PASSIVE TAINT ACCUMULATION FROM PTL EXPOSURE --- LOCKED**

A character who accumulates Taint through PTL exposure is not a cult member. They have not chosen Jigoku — they are being poisoned by the land. This distinction matters for how other characters respond to them but not to Jigoku itself. The PTL thresholds already define resistance roll TNs (Section 11.11 PTL Thresholds). Failed rolls add 0.5 Taint Rank. Susceptibility modifies that roll — each point of Susceptibility above 0 reduces the effective TN by 2 for that character.

- **PTL Susceptibility modifiers:** Earth Ring 4+: −2. Carrying jade: −1 per jade item (maximum −3). Existing Taint Rank: +1 per Rank. Crab Clan: −2 from training and cultural practice.

- **Becoming Lost:** A character reaching Taint Rank 5 through PTL exposure becomes Lost per Section 42. The cause does not matter to Jigoku. Intermediate ranks carry all standard Section 42 consequences. A character at Rank 3+ in a high-PTL province without jade is on a visible downward spiral — shugenja present will notice. Lords can intervene: send the character away from the province, provide jade, assign a shugenja to monitor them. At Rank 4 without resolution, the lord must make a difficult decision before Rank 5 is reached.
