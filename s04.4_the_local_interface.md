## 4.4 The Local Interface

When players are active in a specific location, they operate through one of two sub-modes, both of which represent the same physical space at different levels of interaction.

### 4.4.1 MUD Interface — Zone Navigation & Out-of-Locality Actions --- LOCKED

The MUD interface is one of two player-facing modes (the other being the ASCII map). There is no World Map mode — the World Map is the simulation layer running behind the scenes (provinces producing rice, armies moving, lords making decisions, insurgencies growing). Players never interact with it directly. They experience the world through MUD text navigation and ASCII tile maps.

The MUD interface is the navigation and management layer. The player sees a text description of their current location, a list of exits to other locations, who is present, and available actions. Movement between zones, travel between settlements, sending letters, issuing orders, managing retainers, reading reports, shopping — all handled here. This is the fast, lightweight mode for everything that does not require spatial positioning on a tile grid.

**Zone Hierarchy --- LOCKED:**

The game world is organized into two tiers of zones. Greater Zones are large areas that cannot be entered on the ASCII map — they are navigation containers. Lesser Zones are the actual play spaces, each with a MUD text description and an ASCII map the player can enter.

**Greater Zones:** Three types exist. Province Sub-tile Greater Zones: each province is divided into 4–5 sub-tiles (per Section 11.7 army movement). Each sub-tile is a Greater Zone. From a sub-tile Greater Zone the player accesses the settlements and wilderness areas within that portion of the province. This maps the zone navigation system directly onto the existing military movement grid — armies move sub-tile to sub-tile on the World Map, characters move sub-tile to sub-tile through Greater Zones. Settlement Greater Zones represent an entire settlement (village, town, city, major city) within a sub-tile — from here the player accesses the settlement’s Navigation Zones or Lesser Zones (see Navigation Zone amendment below). Travel Route Greater Zones represent roads or paths between sub-tiles or settlements, containing Navigation Zones or Lesser Zones the player passes through during travel. The player selects a Greater Zone to travel to, then navigates through Navigation Zones and Lesser Zones within it.

**Map Reference View:** Players can open a visual map showing provinces, sub-tiles, army positions, supply tethers, trade routes, and strategic information. This is a reference tool — information, not interaction. All player actions (movement, orders, combat) happen through MUD commands and ASCII map gameplay, never by clicking on the map. The map is for situational awareness, like opening a map from your inventory.

**Lesser Zones:** The leaf-node play spaces. Each Lesser Zone has a zone_id, zone_name, zone_type (determines ASCII map generation), parent zone (either a Navigation Zone or a Greater Zone for simple settlements), exits to adjacent Lesser Zones and to the parent Navigation Zone, a list of permanent NPCs generated from the zone seed, a transient NPC pool populated at runtime from World Map positions, time-of-day state affecting NPC presence and lighting, and a zone_event_log recording all named character actions at this zone (see Section 29.15.24 for full specification — entries purge after one IC season, read by Kitsuki’s Eye, magistrate investigations, Secret System detection, and insurgency detection). Each Lesser Zone has both a MUD text window (description, exits, who is present, available actions) and an ASCII map. The MUD window is the default view; the player enters the ASCII map explicitly for tile-by-tile movement and interaction. From any Lesser Zone, the player can navigate to sibling Lesser Zones, return to the parent Navigation Zone, or enter the ASCII map.

**Lesser Zone Count by Settlement Size --- LOCKED:**

Village (PU below 500): 2–3 Lesser Zones (village square, farmland/outskirts, temple if applicable). Town (PU 500–2000): 4–6 Lesser Zones (market street, residential quarter, temple district, lord’s estate/garrison, outskirts/road gate, docks if coastal/river). City (PU 2000–5000): 7–10 Lesser Zones (all town zones plus pleasure quarter, merchant quarter, craftsman district, castle/fortification, poor quarter/eta district). Major City (PU 5000+): 10–15 Lesser Zones (all city zones plus multiple market/merchant zones, government quarter, additional residential quarters by social class, foreign quarter if applicable, arena/tournament grounds).

**Urban Lesser Zone Types --- LOCKED:**

Each zone type determines what the ASCII map generates. Market Street: wide central road, vendor stalls, shop buildings with interiors, high NPC density by day, empty at night. Residential Quarter: narrower streets, houses, neighborhood shrine, quiet. Temple Grounds: open courtyard, temple building(s), garden, monk NPCs at all hours, combat here has severe consequences. Pleasure Quarter: lantern-lit streets, geisha houses, sake houses, gambling dens, high NPC density at night (reversed from other zones). Docks/Waterfront: piers, warehouses, cargo areas, water tiles, workers by day, quiet and dangerous at night. Castle/Estate Compound: walls, gates, courtyards, audience hall, private quarters, garrison barracks, restricted access. Poor Quarter: cramped streets, run-down buildings, high criminal recruitment ground. Government Quarter: magistrate offices, record halls, doshin patrols.

**Wilderness Lesser Zone Types --- LOCKED:**

Road: path through terrain, trees and rocks on either side, occasional roadside shrine or tea stand, width and quality depend on road importance. Forest Path: dense trees reducing visibility, narrow path, wildlife, potential ambush site. Mountain Pass: rocky terrain, steep elevation, narrow path, weather exposure, slow movement. River Crossing: bridge or ford, water tiles, chokepoint. Farmland: open fields, rice paddies (seasonal), scattered farm buildings, wide visibility. Shrine Clearing: small sacred space in the wilderness, torii gate, spiritual significance.

**Deterministic Map Generation --- LOCKED:**

ASCII maps for Lesser Zones are generated deterministically from a fixed seed: settlement name + zone name + zone type. The same inputs always produce the same map. No storage required — the map regenerates identically each time a player enters. Only deltas (destroyed buildings, new construction, physical changes to the space) are stored. This means a player returns to the same streets, the same buildings, the same layout every visit. The city is a persistent place, not a disposable dungeon.

**Handcrafted Maps --- LOCKED:**

Some locations use handcrafted maps instead of procedural generation. The system checks: does a handcrafted map exist for this zone? If yes, use it. If no, generate procedurally from the seed. Three tiers: Fully handcrafted (Otosan Uchi, Ryoko Owari, and any fortress or castle with sourcebook floor plans — hand-placed tile maps with specific building interiors, NPC placement, and unique features). Partially handcrafted (major clan capitals — castle/estate Lesser Zone is hand-placed from lore, surrounding city zones are procedural). Fully procedural (everything else — villages, towns, minor cities, wilderness zones). Exact list of handcrafted locations is a content creation task.

**NPC Population Rules --- LOCKED:**

Two categories of NPCs populate the ASCII map. Permanent NPCs are generated from the zone seed — same seed produces the same vendor names, the same teahouse owner, the same shrine keeper every time. They give the zone identity and persistence. They appear during appropriate hours (market vendors by day, pleasure quarter staff by night) and are absent otherwise. They are local color, not named characters in the Section 22 sense — they do not participate in the World Map simulation and exist only on the ASCII map when a player is present. Transient NPCs are named characters from Section 22 currently registered as being in this settlement on the World Map. When a player enters the ASCII map, the system checks which named NPCs are present and places them at logical positions based on character type: a samurai lord at the castle, a courtier at the estate or pleasure quarter, a merchant at the market, a monk at the temple, a magistrate on patrol. If no player is in the ASCII map, transient NPCs do not exist on any tile grid — they exist only at the World Map level. Other player characters in the same Lesser Zone appear on the same ASCII map — visible, approachable, shareable space for social roleplay, combat, trade, and observation.

**Time of Day --- LOCKED:**

The ASCII map reflects current time of day. NPC presence shifts (market vendors at dawn, pleasure quarter at dusk, temple monks always, guards on rotating shifts). Lighting changes (full visibility by day, reduced at night except near light sources — integrates with the existing Environmental Modifiers in Section 4.4.2). Activity level varies (market bustle by day, quiet residential streets at night). Criminal network operations are more or less visible depending on time — smuggling runs at night, protection racket collections during business hours.

**System Integration:** Urban Criminal Network awareness location visibility tiers (Section 11.11) map directly to zone types regardless of tier: Market Street and Docks are high visibility, Residential is medium, Castle/Estate is low, Wilderness is zero. Insurgency access points connect through zones — a Maho Cult’s hideout is entered from a specific zone, a bandit camp from a wilderness zone. Court sessions (Section 15) happen in the Castle/Estate’s audience hall Lesser Zone. Travel between Greater Zones costs time per existing World Map travel rules. Movement between zones within the same parent (Lesser Zones within a Navigation Zone, Navigation Zones within a Greater Zone) is instantaneous in MUD view. Entering the ASCII map costs no time — it is a rendering mode, not a travel action.

**AMENDMENT v564 — Navigation Zone (Middle Tier):**

The zone hierarchy is extended from two tiers to three. Greater Zone → Navigation Zone → Lesser Zone. The Navigation Zone is a new intermediate tier that serves as a structural container within settlements. It exists because many locations in the game world are too large for a single ASCII map but too granular to be Greater Zones. A district, a street, a castle courtyard, a harbour waterfront — these are areas a player moves through to reach specific buildings or spaces, not areas that fit on one screen.

Greater Zone: unchanged. Province sub-tile, settlement container, travel route container. Pure navigation. Never has an ASCII map. The player selects a destination from a list. Examples: “You are in Otosan Uchi. Districts: Brutal Flame, Emperor’s Road, Juramashi…” or “You are in Garanto Province, sub-tile 3. Settlements: Shiro Hida. Routes: Road to Ishigaki.”

Navigation Zone (new): a mid-scale area within a settlement or wilderness that contains child zones — either Lesser Zones or other Navigation Zones. Each Navigation Zone has a zone_id, zone_name, zone_type, parent Greater Zone, exits to sibling Navigation Zones, a list of child zones (Lesser Zones or nested Navigation Zones) the player can enter, and a MUD text description. A Navigation Zone may or may not have an ASCII map. Navigation Zones with ASCII maps represent walkable outdoor spaces — streets, plazas, harbour fronts, castle courtyards — where the player moves through a tile grid with building facades, vendor stalls, NPCs, and entrances to child zones rendered as doors or gates on the map. The player walks up to a door and enters the child zone, loading its interior ASCII map (Lesser Zone) or its own street-level map (nested Navigation Zone). Navigation Zones without ASCII maps are pure MUD navigation — the player sees a text description and a list of exits to child Lesser Zones and sibling Navigation Zones. Examples: a city district that lists its streets, a castle compound that lists its buildings. Whether a Navigation Zone has an ASCII map is determined by the handcrafted map tier (Section 4.3.4) and settlement importance. Handcrafted settlements (Otosan Uchi, Ryoko Owari, major clan capitals) will have ASCII-mapped Navigation Zones for key streets and public spaces. Procedural settlements may have MUD-only Navigation Zones — the structural benefit is organizational even without a rendered map.

Lesser Zone: the leaf-node play space. Always has both a MUD text window (description, exits, who is present, available actions) and an ASCII map (tile grid the player can enter for spatial interaction). The MUD window is the default view — the player sees the text description when they arrive. They enter the ASCII map explicitly when they want tile-by-tile movement, combat, investigation, or face-to-face interaction. Infrastructure items from Section 2.3.2 (forges, tea houses, dojo, okiya, shrines, markets, etc.) are Lesser Zones. Each has its own interior ASCII map. A Lesser Zone’s parent is either a Navigation Zone or a Greater Zone (for simple settlements where the middle tier adds no value — a village with 2–3 Lesser Zones may skip the Navigation Zone tier entirely).

NPC resolution: NPCs appear on the ASCII map when a player character is present in the zone — transient NPCs are placed at logical positions based on character type (Section 4.3.4, NPC Population Rules). When no player is present, no ASCII map is generated and NPC actions resolve through engine dice rolls on the World Map layer (Section 9, Design Intent). Navigation Zones and Lesser Zones exist for NPC purposes as location tags for co-location checks, Daily Conversation triggers, and zone_event_log entries regardless of whether an ASCII map is currently rendered.

zone_event_log: attaches to both Navigation Zones (with ASCII maps) and Lesser Zones. Actions that occur on a street are logged to the Navigation Zone’s event log. Actions inside a building are logged to the Lesser Zone’s event log. A Kitsuki investigator reading a zone_event_log reads the specific zone where the action occurred — an eavesdrop inside the Forge is logged to the Forge’s Lesser Zone, not to the parent Market Street Navigation Zone.

met_characters: triggers when two named characters share the same zone that has an ASCII map — whether Navigation Zone or Lesser Zone. Being in the same Navigation Zone (same street) or the same Lesser Zone (same building) both satisfy co-location for met_characters, Daily Conversation, SEARCH_PERSON, and all other co-location requirements. Being in sibling Lesser Zones under the same Navigation Zone does NOT satisfy co-location — a character in the Forge and a character in the Tea House across the street have not met, even though both buildings exit to the same Navigation Zone.

Criminal Network visibility: the visibility tier is determined by zone type, not zone tier. A Market Street Navigation Zone has the same “high visibility” as the old Market Street Lesser Zone. A Castle/Estate Navigation Zone has “low visibility.” Child Lesser Zones within a Navigation Zone inherit their parent’s visibility tier unless they have a specific override (a hidden basement Lesser Zone inside a Market Street Navigation Zone could be “zero visibility”).

Movement: movement between sibling Navigation Zones within the same Greater Zone is instantaneous in MUD view (unchanged from the existing Lesser Zone rule). Movement between Lesser Zones within the same Navigation Zone is instantaneous. Entering an ASCII map from MUD view costs no time (unchanged). Travel between Greater Zones costs time per World Map travel rules (unchanged).

**Navigation Zone Types — PROVISIONAL:**

Urban Navigation Zone types (used within settlements): CITY_DISTRICT (neighbourhood-scale, contains streets and buildings — used for Otosan Uchi Toshisoto districts and large city quarters), EKOHIKEI_DISTRICT (restricted-access inner city district — used for Otosan Uchi Ekohikei districts, access gated by ekohikei_access flag), FORBIDDEN_CITY (imperial restricted zone — used for the Forbidden City, access gated by forbidden_city_access flag), ACCESS_LAYER (structural container grouping districts by access tier — used for Toshisoto, Ekohikei, and Forbidden City layer Navigation Zones within Otosan Uchi), STREET (a specific named road or lane with building entrances — typically has an ASCII map showing facades), PLAZA (open public space — market square, shrine courtyard, festival ground — typically has an ASCII map), CASTLE_COMPOUND (the castle/estate as a whole — contains interior Lesser Zones per Section 57.36), WATERFRONT (docks and harbour area with pier entrances and warehouse doors), RESIDENTIAL_BLOCK (a quiet neighbourhood section — houses and small shops). Wilderness Navigation Zone types: ROAD_SEGMENT (a stretch of road between settlements containing roadside encounters), FOREST_TRAIL (a path through wilderness with branching clearings), MOUNTAIN_APPROACH (a route through mountain terrain with pass and cave entrances).

**Reconciliation with LOCKED definitions above:**

The Urban Lesser Zone Types list (Market Street, Residential Quarter, Temple Grounds, Pleasure Quarter, Docks/Waterfront, Castle/Estate Compound, Poor Quarter, Government Quarter) and Wilderness Lesser Zone Types list (Road, Forest Path, Mountain Pass, River Crossing, Farmland, Shrine Clearing) remain valid. Under the three-tier model, these zone type names can function at either tier depending on settlement scale. A “Temple Grounds” in a village is a single Lesser Zone (small shrine, one ASCII map). A “Temple Grounds” in a major city is a Navigation Zone containing multiple child Lesser Zones (individual temples, meditation gardens, monk quarters). The zone type determines what the ASCII map generates; the zone tier determines whether it contains children. Castle/Estate Compound is always a Navigation Zone — Section 57.36 already defines 3–4 interior zones within it. Market Street in a town may be a single Lesser Zone; Market Street in a city is a Navigation Zone with individual shop Lesser Zones inside. The amendment formalizes what 57.36 was already doing for castles and extends the pattern to all settlement zones where the middle tier adds value. For simple settlements (villages with 2–3 play spaces), the Navigation Zone tier can be skipped — Lesser Zones parent directly to the Greater Zone, preserving the existing two-tier model. The three-tier model activates only where the middle tier adds value: cities, large towns, complex castles, and handcrafted settlements. Lesser Zone Count by Settlement Size (Village 2–3, Town 4–6, City 7–10, Major City 10–15) counts leaf-node Lesser Zones (ASCII play spaces). Navigation Zones are structural containers on top of that count and do not inflate it. A Town with 5 Lesser Zones may organize them under 2 Navigation Zones, but the count is still 5. CITY_DISTRICT, EKOHIKEI_DISTRICT, and FORBIDDEN_CITY in the Otosan Uchi zone entries (Section 2.3.23) are Navigation Zone types. Each district is a Navigation Zone containing child Lesser Zones (individual buildings, infrastructure). The settlement hierarchy for Otosan Uchi is: Settlement Greater Zone → Access Layer Navigation Zones (Toshisoto, Ekohikei, Forbidden City) → District Navigation Zones → Building Lesser Zones. Navigation Zones can nest: an ACCESS_LAYER Navigation Zone contains CITY_DISTRICT Navigation Zones, which contain Lesser Zones.

### 4.4.2 ASCII Map Interface — Movement, Combat & Direct Interaction

When precision matters — entering a building, fighting, sneaking, or having a face-to-face conversation — the game shifts to an ASCII tile map. This is where spatial positioning, L5R4e movement rules, and tactical play live.

- The map is a grid of ASCII tiles representing the immediate environment: a city district, a battlefield, a castle interior, a forest clearing.

- Each tile has terrain properties — open ground, difficult terrain, walls, water — affecting movement costs and combat.

- Characters move according to L5R4e movement rules: Water Ring determines base movement, terrain modifies it, Full Move vs. Attack Move trade-offs apply.

- Line of sight and range matter — ranged attacks, spells, and social interactions (shouting vs. whispering) are spatially resolved.

- All player-to-player and player-to-NPC direct interactions (conversation, dueling, trade, combat) take place here.

**IMPLEMENTATION NOTE:** *Tile size and scale — how large is a typical map? What is one tile in shaku/feet? Resolved during engine development.*

**ASCII Tile Rendering --- LOCKED:**

Tiles are rendered in a square grid in Godot. Each tile cell is a uniform square regardless of the underlying character’s natural aspect ratio. Characters are standard Unicode — no custom font required. The square cell ensures horizontal and vertical lines (walls, roads) are the same visual length. Colour is used throughout: the symbol conveys what the tile IS, the colour conveys CONTEXT (grass vs dirt vs stone, all using the same dot symbol). Foreground colour is the symbol itself. Background colour is used sparingly — water, taint zones, and spirit realm bleed only.

**Walls --- LOCKED:**

Three wall materials with distinct visual weight. Stone walls: █ (full block) in grey — solid, heavy, impenetrable. The only material that uses the full block. Castles, fortifications, heavy construction. Wood walls: ┃━┏┓┗┛┣┫┳┻╋ (heavy/thick box-drawing lines) in brown — timber framing, structural but not as massive as stone. Shoji screens (rice paper walls): │─┌┐└┘├┤┬┴┼ (light/thin box-drawing lines) in white — delicate, block LOS but can be cut through or slid open, destroyed in one action. Visual weight descends clearly: solid block → thick line → thin line. Stone → wood → paper.

**Floors --- LOCKED:**

Natural ground uses dots. Constructed floors use distinct symbols — visually different from natural ground at a glance. Natural ground: . (dot) in green for grass, brown for dirt/earth, white for snow, yellow for sand. , (comma) in brown for mud/swamp. Constructed floors: = (equals) in brown for wooden floor (reads as parallel planks). ≡ (triple line) in yellow for tatami (reads as woven mat, formal rooms). ∷ (four dots) in grey for stone floor (reads as fitted flagstone, castle and temple interiors).

**Water --- LOCKED:**

~ (tilde) is always water. Colour conveys depth and type. ~ in blue: shallow water (streams, puddles, ponds, wadeable, movement cost +1). ~ in dark blue: deep water (rivers, lakes, sea, impassable on foot). ≈ (double tilde) in cyan: rapids/current (moving water, pushes characters, dangerous to cross). ~ in green on dark blue background: flooded rice paddy (shallow water plus vegetation, seasonal spring/summer).

**Vegetation --- LOCKED:**

Tree species determined by province biome (Regional Biome System, Section 56.6.1), expressed through colour variation on the same symbol. ♣ (club) in dark green: evergreen tree (pine, cedar, cypress). Blocks movement and LOS. ♣ in green: deciduous tree (oak, maple). Changes colour in autumn. ♣ in pink: cherry blossom (spring only, green otherwise). ♠ (spade) in green: bamboo. Blocks movement, partial LOS (gaps between stalks). Visually distinct narrow profile. “ (double quote) in green: bush/shrub. Does not block movement, partial concealment. * (asterisk) in various colours: flowers (pink, yellow, purple depending on type). Decorative. , (comma) in green: groundcover, moss. No mechanical effect. % (percent) in yellow: standing crops (rice, wheat). Partial concealment, movement cost +1. ¥ (yen) in brown: dead/bare tree. Blocks movement but no LOS block (no canopy). Winter or burned areas.

**Doors --- LOCKED:**

+ (plus) in white: shoji sliding door, closed. Blocks LOS, fragile. ’ (apostrophe) in white: shoji sliding door, open. Barely visible — a slid-aside screen. + in brown: wooden door, closed. Heavier barrier. / (slash) in brown: wooden door, open. ╬ (double cross box-drawing) in brown or grey: gate, closed. Heavy, reinforced. ∏ (pi capital) in brown or grey: gate, open. Wide opening.

**IMPLEMENTATION NOTE — Remaining Tile Categories:** *Characters (player, NPCs, enemies), furnishings and objects, roads and paths, terrain obstacles (boulders, hills, mountains), special tiles (taint zones, spirit realm bleed, jade wards, points of interest, fog of war), roofing/elevation indicators. The design principles and rendering approach are locked. Individual tile categories will be defined during engine development following the same pattern: symbol conveys type, colour conveys context, visual weight conveys material.*

**Field of Vision ****&**** Fog of War --- LOCKED**

Characters see a circular radius of tiles centered on their position. Everything within that radius is visible. Everything outside it is hidden — rendered as blank space or a darkened tile. There is no memory of previously seen tiles — if a tile leaves the radius it goes dark again immediately. The world exists only as far as the character can currently perceive it.

**Base Vision Radius --- LOCKED:**

Vision radius = Perception Trait in tiles. A character with Perception 2 (the starting minimum) sees 2 tiles in every direction. A character with Perception 4 sees 4 tiles. A character with Perception 6 sees 6 tiles. This gives Perception genuine mechanical weight throughout the game — not only in Investigation rolls but in every ASCII map context. A high-Perception shugenja sent into a Maho Cult warren sees more of the dungeon than a low-Perception bushi, and that information matters.

Perception 2: 2-tile radius (most starting characters). Perception 3: 3-tile radius (trained or naturally alert). Perception 4: 4-tile radius (sharp, experienced). Perception 5+: 5+ tile radius (exceptional — rare at character creation, achievable through advancement).

**Environmental Modifiers --- LOCKED:**

Environmental conditions reduce the effective radius. The modifier is subtracted from the character’s Perception value before calculating the radius. Minimum effective radius is always 1 — a character is always aware of their immediately adjacent tiles regardless of conditions.

- **Clear conditions (day, open area):** −0. No modifier. Full Perception radius applies.

- **Overcast / light rain / dusk:** −1. Slightly reduced visibility. Most characters notice little difference at this level.

- **Heavy rain / light fog / dense forest / indoor torchlight:** −2. Meaningful reduction. A Perception 2 character is effectively limited to adjacent tiles only.

- **Night / heavy fog / underground without light source:** −3. Severe reduction. Even high-Perception characters are largely blind. Movement becomes dangerous — unseen enemies, traps, and terrain hazards are credible threats. Torches and lanterns carried by the character restore 2 to the effective radius.

- **Supernatural obscurement (Maho ritual concealment, certain spirit effects):** −4. Near-total blindness. Reserved for specific in-game abilities and Maho effects. Cannot be mitigated by light sources — only spiritual countermeasures (shugenja abilities, jade) restore vision.

**Walls and Solid Obstructions --- LOCKED:**

Walls, closed doors, and solid terrain block vision regardless of radius. A character with Perception 6 cannot see through a stone wall. The radius defines the maximum range of vision — solid obstructions clip it within that range. Open doors and archways allow vision through them up to the radius limit.

**Stealth Interaction --- LOCKED:**

A character using Stealth (Section 4.5.4) is not automatically visible to all characters within whose radius they fall. Detection of a stealthed character requires the observing character’s tile radius to include the stealthed tile AND a successful contested roll (Investigation/Perception vs Stealth). A character outside the radius cannot attempt the roll. A character within the radius must still beat the Stealth roll to notice the hidden figure. This means high-Perception characters have more opportunities to notice hidden threats but are not guaranteed to find them.

**Multiplayer Consideration --- LOCKED:**

In multiplayer sessions, each player sees only their own character’s vision radius. Allied characters in the same map do not share vision automatically — a player character standing in an adjacent corridor cannot see what their ally sees around the corner. This creates genuine tactical coordination pressure and makes scouting characters with high Perception genuinely valuable to a group.

**THE ASCII MAP AS PRIMARY PLAYER GAMEPLAY — DESIGN INTENT NOTE**

The ASCII Map is the layer that grounds players in the world at human scale. Every objective requiring physical action, personal presence, or danger has an ASCII Map equivalent for player characters. Where an NPC resolves an action through the engine — a roll, a probability, a stat check — a player character physically moves through a procedurally generated visualization of the location and accomplishes that objective through direct play.

This is the primary distinction between player and NPC experience. NPCs never experience the ASCII Map layer. It is exclusively the player’s domain — the space where the grand systems of the World Map (politics, economy, warfare) are made personally real.

**ASCII Map by Player Type — DESIGN INTENT**

- Bushi, Shinobi, Shugenja, Monks — the ASCII Map is their primary gameplay space. Missions, combat, infiltration, scouting, dungeon-crawl objectives. These players spend most of their active time here. Every dangerous personal action — an assassination operation, a scouting run into enemy territory, clearing a bandit stronghold, protecting a caravan — is an ASCII Map mission.

- Courtiers — the court system and direct NPC interaction is their primary arena. The ASCII Map appears for traversal (physically moving between locations on the World Map), personal danger situations (being attacked, defending against assassination), and direct physical NPC encounters that are not court-mediated. Present but secondary.

- Lords — lords are NPCs governed by the NPC Decision Engine (Section 55). Their strategic decisions (governance, military deployment, diplomacy) are autonomous. A player character who rises to a lord position manages their domain through MUD commands (assigning vassals, issuing orders, reading reports) but day-to-day governance runs through the NPC systems. The ASCII Map appears when the player character is personally present at a location and chooses to engage directly.

**The Grounding Effect — DESIGN INTENT**

The ASCII Map ties grand-scale systems down to human experience. A player character is not just a token on a map issuing orders — they are physically present somewhere, walking through it, perceiving it at ground level. The political intrigue happening at the World Map level has a personal physical reality for those who participate in it. A bushi following their lord’s orders to escort a diplomat walks that road on the ASCII Map. A shinobi infiltrating a rival’s stronghold moves through its corridors tile by tile. A shugenja investigating a tainted shrine enters it and sees what is there.

This creates a meaningful hierarchy of gameplay depth: the World Map governs the shape of the world, the court system governs its politics, and the ASCII Map is where players personally inhabit both. The game is not just strategy — it is also the experience of being a person within the strategy.

**Objective-Driven ASCII Map Missions — DESIGN INTENT (Full Design in Section 56)**

Every primary objective assigned to a player character that requires physical action generates an ASCII Map mission. The mission is a procedurally generated visualization of the relevant location — the target’s stronghold, the bandit camp, the shrine, the forest path — with objectives to complete without being detected, without causing unacceptable collateral damage, or within a time constraint. Success and failure have World Map consequences. This is the core non-courtier gameplay loop.

Examples: An assassination operation is an ASCII Map infiltration mission through the target’s settlement. A scouting assignment is an ASCII Map reconnaissance mission through an enemy sub-tile. Defending against an assassination attempt is an ASCII Map security mission in the player’s own stronghold. Full mission generation system designed in Section 56 (Quest System — ASCII Map Mission Generation — LOCKED).

### 4.4.3 Transitioning Between Modes --- LOCKED

The player operates in two modes: MUD view (zone navigation) and ASCII map (tile-by-tile tactical view). Transitions between them are driven by player action.

- MUD → ASCII: The player is in a Lesser Zone in MUD view and chooses to enter the ASCII map. This renders the zone as a tile grid with terrain, buildings, NPCs, and other players. Triggered by: selecting “Enter” from the Lesser Zone menu, initiating combat, or any action requiring spatial positioning.

- ASCII → MUD: The player leaves the ASCII map and returns to zone navigation. Triggered by: walking to the map edge (exits to an adjacent zone, back to the parent Navigation Zone, or back to the Greater Zone), selecting a fast-travel option, or completing a mission objective.

- Greater Zone navigation (MUD only): Moving between Greater Zones (e.g. traveling from one settlement to another, or from a settlement to the province level) is handled entirely in MUD view. Travel time is computed from World Map distance and applied as in-game time cost. The player does not render any ASCII map during Greater Zone travel unless they choose to enter a wilderness Lesser Zone along the route.
