## 16.4 Personal Relevance Score — LOCKED

Every character has their own weighted view of each active crisis. Global momentum tells you how big a crisis is empire-wide. Personal relevance tells you how much a specific character cares about it. A distant major war at momentum 80 may matter less to a provincial lord than a local famine at momentum 40 consuming their own people.

**Personal Relevance Formula — LOCKED:**

Start with the crisis's global momentum value, then apply the following modifiers:

**Clan Proximity Multiplier:**

- Crisis in your own clan's territory: ×2.

- Crisis in a neighboring clan's territory: ×1.5.

- Crisis in an allied clan's territory: ×1.5.

- Crisis in a distant clan's territory: ×1.

- Crisis in an enemy clan's territory: ×0.75 — you care less, possibly even quietly pleased.

**Family Proximity Flat Bonus:**

- Crisis in your own family's specific provinces: +20 flat.

- Crisis in another family within your clan: +10 flat.

**Crisis Tier Override:**

- Tier 1: Everyone receives a minimum personal relevance of 50 regardless of geography — supernatural threats concern all of Rokugan.

- Tier 2: No minimum. Pure proximity calculation.

- Tier 3: No minimum. Proximity calculation with a maximum personal relevance cap of 60 — local problems stay local.

**How Personal Relevance Affects Court — LOCKED:**

- Personal relevance determines how much weight a character's opinion on a topic carries at court — a lord whose own lands are burning speaks with more authority than a distant observer.

- Characters with high personal relevance on a topic are more likely to spend action points on it and more likely to be moved by successful court actions targeting that topic.

- Characters with low personal relevance may be present at court but largely indifferent — their positions are harder to move but also matter less to the final outcome.

**Personal Relevance Amplification Formula — LOCKED:** Personal relevance modifies court resolution in two ways: it makes high-relevance characters harder to move (resistance), and it makes their positions count for more in aggregate calculations (weight).

Position Resistance: position movement from court actions (Section 15.5) is divided by (1 + relevance / 100). At relevance 0: full movement — an indifferent lord is easy to sway. At relevance 50: movement halved — a lord whose neighbouring province is threatened digs in. At relevance 100: movement divided by 2 — a lord whose own lands are burning is extremely difficult to move from their position. The base position movement values in Section 15.5 (Negotiate +8, Persuade +12, etc.) are the pre-resistance values. After resistance: effective_movement = base_movement / (1 + target_relevance / 100). Example: Persuade succeeds against a target with relevance 60. Base movement +12. Effective movement = 12 / 1.6 = 7.5. The target’s position shifts by 7 (rounded down). The same Persuade against a target with relevance 0 shifts the full 12. This makes high-relevance characters expensive to move — a focused courtier can still shift them, but it takes more actions and better rolls.

Position Weight: when computing aggregate weighted opinion (used for Emperor Edict selection at Winter Court and NPC room-reading), each lord’s contribution is weighted by Status × (relevance / 50). A Clan Champion (Status 7) with relevance 100 contributes weight 14. A Provincial Daimyo (Status 3) with relevance 25 contributes weight 1.5. A lord with relevance 0 contributes weight 0 — they are present but their opinion carries no weight in the aggregate. This means the aggregate is dominated by high-Status lords who care deeply about the topic, which matches Rokugani political reality: a Clan Champion whose territory is under attack speaks with far more authority than a distant Provincial Daimyo attending out of obligation.

Aggregate Weighted Opinion formula: for a given action topic at court close, sum each attending lord’s (position × weight) and divide by the sum of all weights. Result is a weighted average position on the −100 to +100 scale. This aggregate does NOT produce a binding outcome (Section 15.2 — court produces individual commitments, not collective mandates). The aggregate serves two specific purposes only: (1) the Emperor uses it to decide Imperial Edicts at Winter Court close, and (2) NPCs reading the room use it to decide whether to commit. A lord whose individual position is +30 but sees the aggregate at +60 may be pushed toward declaration by social pressure. A lord at +60 who sees the aggregate at −20 may hesitate. All values PROVISIONAL pending playtesting.

**Commitment Execution Bridge — LOCKED:** When a lord crosses +50 on an action topic and makes a Public Declaration (Section 15.5), or when an Imperial Edict compels all lords (Section 16.4 Edict Selection), a commitment_record is written to the lord’s character sheet. The commitment_record is a constraint on the NPC Decision Engine — it does not bypass the engine or fire ActionIDs directly. The lord must still travel, issue orders, and move resources through normal game systems over real time. The declaration is a promise. Fulfillment is a process.

**commitment_record structure:** {lord_id: int, topic_id: int, commitment_type: string (from crisis type’s possible commitments in Section 16.3 — e.g. “send_military_aid,” “send_supplies,” “send_shugenja,” “send_magistrates”), resource_amount: int or null (null for character-dispatch commitments), source: “voluntary” | “edict,” declared_at: IC day, deadline: IC day (default: last day of the next IC season), fulfilled: boolean (default false), good_faith: boolean (default true)}.

**HONOR_COMMITMENT NeedType (81st NeedType):** When a commitment_record exists on a lord’s sheet with fulfilled = false, the NPC Decision Engine generates an HONOR_COMMITMENT need in Phase 1 (Need Assessment). This need scores extremely high — base priority 95 — because failing it causes Honor loss, disposition damage, and a public disgrace topic. The engine treats it as one of the lord’s most urgent objectives and allocates AP accordingly through the normal five-phase pipeline (Section 55). The lord’s personality modifies the urgency: high Chugi (Duty) lords treat HONOR_COMMITMENT at priority 100. High Seigyo (Control) lords evaluate whether fulfillment serves their strategic interest and may deprioritize if it conflicts with a higher-value objective — accepting the renege penalty as a calculated cost. Low Makoto (Honesty) lords are more willing to renege without personality resistance.

**Fulfillment process:** The engine decomposes HONOR_COMMITMENT into the appropriate ActionIDs based on commitment_type. “send_military_aid” decomposes to ORDER_DEPLOY (routing troops to the target province). “send_supplies” decomposes to SHARE_SUPPLIES (transferring Rice, Arms, or Koku). “send_shugenja” or “send_magistrates” decomposes to ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE (dispatching the character to the target province). These ActionIDs route through the normal Decision Engine scoring in Phase 5. The lord spends AP on them over multiple IC days. Resources and troops move through the existing movement and transfer systems at their normal speeds — letter delivery time for orders (Section 12.7), army movement rate (Section 57.34), character travel time (Section 4.4). A lord at Winter Court in Otosan Uchi who commits to sending troops must first return home or send a letter to their commander, who then issues the deployment order, and the army marches at standard speed. This unfolds naturally over IC days and seasons.

**Fulfillment detection:** Checked at each seasonal tick. Resource commitments: fulfilled when cumulative SHARE_SUPPLIES transfers from this lord to the target reach the committed resource_amount. Military commitments: fulfilled when committed units physically arrive in the target province. Character commitments: fulfilled when the dispatched character arrives in the target province. When fulfilled = true fires, the commitment_record is archived and HONOR_COMMITMENT is removed from the lord’s active NeedType pool.

**Renege detection (binary, not proportional):** At each seasonal tick, the engine evaluates whether the lord has made good-faith efforts toward fulfillment. Good faith means the lord has spent at least some AP on HONOR_COMMITMENT decomposition actions since the commitment was made — they are actively working toward it, even if progress is slow due to circumstances (famine reducing available Rice, army delayed by terrain, province invaded mid-fulfillment). If the lord has spent zero AP toward the commitment and the deadline passes, good_faith flips to false and renege fires. The evaluation is binary: did the lord try, or did they do nothing? A lord whose province was invaded while fulfilling a commitment is not reneging — the Decision Engine naturally deprioritized HONOR_COMMITMENT for DEFEND_PROVINCE, and that is a legitimate reason observers would recognize.

**Renege consequences:** Voluntary commitment renege: at-act Honor loss via Table 2.3 “Disloyalty to lord, spouse, or superior” (Section 4.6), scaled by Honor Rank. Disposition: −15 permanent historical modifier with all characters who witnessed the original declaration. Topic: Tier 3 — “[Lord] failed to honor their commitment on [topic].” Edict-compelled renege: all voluntary renege consequences plus additional Honor −3.0 (flat — defying the Emperor’s explicit command), Status consequences per existing Edict refusal text (Section 15.2), and every other lord is justified in acting against the refuser. Player characters: the commitment_record appears in the player’s obligation tracker. The player decides how to prioritize fulfillment against their other objectives. If the deadline passes without action, the renege consequences fire the same way they do for NPCs. All renege values PROVISIONAL pending playtesting.

**Winter Court Imperial Edict Selection — LOCKED:** At Winter Court close (after 120 IC days), the Emperor evaluates the top 3 highest-momentum crises on the agenda and decides whether to issue an Imperial Edict on each. Maximum 3 Edicts per Winter Court. One Edict per crisis — if multiple action topics exist for the same crisis, the Edict targets the one with the highest positive aggregate.

Emperor’s weight in the aggregate: the Emperor’s position is included in the aggregate weighted opinion formula (Section 16.4) but with a ×3 multiplier on weight. Emperor weight = Status (10) × (relevance / 50) × 3. At relevance 100, the Emperor’s weight is 60 — dominant but not absolute. A unanimous court of seven Clan Champions (each weight 14 at full relevance = total 98) can outweigh a dissenting Emperor, but it takes near-total consensus. The Emperor’s personality expresses through their position on each topic via the same personality modifier system as every other character (Section 15.6). A Jin Emperor starts positive on famine relief. A Seigyo Emperor starts positive on power consolidation. No separate personality layer is needed — the Emperor’s character drives their position, and the ×3 multiplier amplifies it into Edict decisions.

Edict thresholds: for each crisis’s highest-aggregate action topic, the Emperor decides based on the aggregate value. Aggregate above +25: the Emperor issues an Edict compelling the majority action. All lords in the Empire are bound — present at court or not, supportive or opposed. Aggregate between −25 and +25: no Edict. The lords are too divided and the Emperor does not force the issue. The crisis continues accumulating momentum. Aggregate below −25: the Emperor issues an Edict blocking or opposing the action (rare — only fires when overwhelming opposition exists, such as the court rejecting a proposed alliance with a disgraced clan). If no action topic for a crisis exceeds +25 in aggregate, no Edict is issued for that crisis regardless of its momentum.

What an Edict does mechanically: an Edict writes a commitment_record on every lord in the Empire, not just those present at court. The commitment is compelled — lords who were absent, lords who opposed, lords who said nothing are all bound. The commitment_record specifies: lord_id, topic_id, commitment_type (from the crisis type’s possible commitments list in Section 16.3), resource_amount (if applicable, set by the Edict’s terms), deadline (default: end of next IC season). Refusal consequences are already defined: instant catastrophic Honor loss, loss of all court standing, every other lord justified in acting against the refuser (Section 15.2). No rational NPC refuses an Imperial Edict. A player who refuses has chosen to operate outside the political order entirely. All Edict threshold values PROVISIONAL pending playtesting.
