## 56.4 Occupied Village Template --- LOCKED

The Occupied Village template generates above-ground settlement environments where occupants have imposed themselves on an existing civilian space. High reuse --- applicable to Ronin Bandit Uprising, Peasant Revolt, assassination missions, and Taint Manifestation in settlements. Fundamentally different from Cave: open sightlines, civilian presence, multiple approach vectors, and the settlement exists as a functional place that was not built for combat.

### 56.4.1 Size Categories --- LOCKED

Village size is driven by the settlement’s existing Population Units from the simulation, not a random roll. The bandits did not build this place --- they occupied it. Two size categories:

- **Hamlet:** 3-6 buildings, one road or path, minimal infrastructure. A few farmhouses, maybe a shrine or well. Tight, simple. Can house up to Strength 3.

- **Village:** 7-15 buildings, a main road with branching paths, a headman’s house, possibly a market area. Can house up to Strength 7. Above that the insurgency has outgrown what bandits occupying a village looks like --- it is becoming an army.

Same Strength floor rule as Cave. If the insurgency is too big for a Hamlet, it bumps to Village. But a small gang in a Village means they are only occupying part of it --- some buildings are just villagers trying to stay out of the way.

### 56.4.2 Entry and Flow --- LOCKED

**Entry Points:** Villages are not funnels. The player can approach from any direction. The generator creates the village and defines approach vectors based on surroundings: a road from the direction the player traveled, fields or tree lines on the sides, possibly a river on one edge limiting approach. The player chooses how to come in. Walk up the main road openly --- visible but fast. Circle around through the fields --- slower, more cover, avoids sentries on the road. This is a meaningful tactical choice the cave does not offer.

**Flow Pattern:** Open with clusters. Buildings create natural cover pockets and sightline breaks, but the space between them is exposed. The village layout is the flow --- main road acts as the spine, buildings cluster along it, gaps between structures create movement corridors. No branching paths to choose like cave tunnels --- instead the player navigates between cover points across open ground.

**Depth Rule Adaptation:** The leader does not sit in the deepest room --- they sit in the most defensible building. Headman’s house, largest structure, center of the village. The objective is protected by spatial layers --- outer perimeter, inner village, the strongpoint --- not sequential rooms.

**No Perimeter Defenses:** Villages are farming settlements, not forts. The bandits moved in because it was easy, not because they could fortify it. The complexity comes from buildings, paths, and how the settlement is structured. No walls, no barricades, no stockades.

**River and Bridge:** Occasional feature. River along one edge or cutting through the settlement. Bridge creates a single crossing point --- natural chokepoint for approach and escape. Approximately 20-30% chance of generation.

### 56.4.3 Population Placement --- LOCKED

Same roles as Cave adapted for open settlement space:

- **Sentry:** Posted on the road approaching the village, or sitting on a roof or porch with a view of approaches. One or two. If the player comes through the fields instead of the road, they may bypass sentries entirely.

- **Patrol:** Walking through the village between buildings, along paths. More visible than cave patrols because the space is open, but they also have better sightlines. One patrol in a Hamlet, one or two in a Village.

- **Camp Group:** Inside buildings. Eating, drinking, sleeping. Not visible from outside unless the player looks through a door or window. The player can move past an occupied building without knowing anyone is inside until they hear noise or see light.

- **Leader Group:** In the headman’s house or largest building. Best fighters with the leader. Same 20-25% of total roster.

**No Guard Posts:** There are no chokepoints to guard. Instead, the bandits cluster where the resources are --- the food stores, the headman’s house, the common area.

**Civilians:** Villagers are present --- in their homes, hiding, going about their day under threat. They are not enemies. They are obstacles, witnesses, potential hostages, and potential information sources. A civilian can tell the player where the leader is. A civilian caught in a fight can die. Killing heimin does not affect Honor (per Rokugani caste law), but dead villagers reduce the province’s PU. The lord who sent the player now has fewer people producing Rice. Unnecessary civilian deaths mean the objective was partially failed if the lord specifically ordered protecting the village.

**Density Pattern:** Opposite of Cave. Sentries are on the outside, most population is in the center around the leader. The player pushes inward, not downward.

### 56.4.4 Environmental Features --- LOCKED

- **Time of Day:** Matters here unlike underground. A night raid means fewer civilians moving around, bandits sleeping in buildings, reduced vision per the Perception system. A daytime approach means full visibility both ways. The quest seed can set this or the player can choose when to attack.

- **Buildings:** The primary terrain feature. Block sightlines, provide hard cover, create interiors the player can enter or avoid. Doors and windows matter --- entering through a door is a chokepoint, a window gives a sightline into the room without entering. Each building is a mini encounter space.

- **Crops and Fields:** Surrounding the village. Tall crops provide concealment for approach but not hard cover. Harvested or fallow fields offer nothing. Season from the existing calendar system (Section 4.3.2) determines crop state --- a summer raid has standing rice to hide in, a winter approach is bare ground.

- **Livestock and Carts:** Scattered around. Minor cover, minor movement obstacles. A panicked ox could create noise. Carts block paths between buildings.

- **Fire:** A weapon and a hazard. The player or the bandits could set a building on fire. Fire spreads to adjacent structures. Drives people out. Creates chaos, smoke reduces visibility, destroys goods inside. Dangerous and costs province infrastructure if buildings burn. Civilian casualties from fire reduce PU.

- **River:** When present. Difficult terrain to cross except at the bridge. Limits movement on that edge. Could be used for stealthy approach if the player accepts slow movement through water.

- **Mud and Paths:** Dirt paths are normal movement. Open ground between buildings may be muddy depending on season. Mud is slower and louder, same noise principle as water in caves.

### 56.4.5 Objective Placement --- Occupied Village Template --- LOCKED

Same primary objective types as Cave, adapted for open settlement:

- **Kill the Leader:** Leader is in the headman’s house or largest building. The player knows roughly where it is because villages have an obvious center. Getting there means crossing open ground past occupied buildings. The leader can flee out a back door and into the fields if alerted --- unlike the Deep Chamber, no single chokepoint traps them.

- **Recover Stolen Goods:** Stored in a specific building --- a barn, a storehouse. Placed away from the leader’s building but in the village interior. The player could identify it by asking a civilian or by spotting bandits moving goods in and out.

- **Burn the Camp:** Does not generate for Occupied Village seeds. The village is not the bandits’ construction --- burning it destroys the province’s own infrastructure. A lord would never order this. If it somehow happens, massive PU and economic consequence.

- **Rescue Hostages:** Civilians held in a specific building under guard. Could be anywhere in the village. Other civilians may know which building. Noise from a fight nearby alerts the guards.

- **Drive Them Out:** New objective unique to this template. The goal is to break the bandits’ hold on the village, not to kill everyone. Kill enough, kill the leader, or create enough chaos that the rest flee. A threshold --- once a percentage of the roster is down or the leader is dead, surviving bandits scatter into the fields. The village is freed without clearing every building.

**Secondary Objectives:** Same principle as Cave. Discovered on site, not assigned. Stolen koku in a building, a letter, a hidden weapons cache.

### 56.4.6 Resolution and Consequence --- Occupied Village Template --- LOCKED

Same spectrum as Cave with village-specific factors:

- **Full Success:** Primary objective completed. Strength reduction same as Cave by objective type. Village is liberated. Province PU remains intact. Trade route reopens if applicable. Lord gains disposition with the local population.

- **Partial Success:** Bandits disrupted but not broken. Leader escaped, some enemies scattered. Strength reduced by kills. The village may still be held by remnants --- a smaller force digs in and the insurgency continues at reduced Strength from the same location.

- **Retreat:** Player pulled out. Strength reduced only by kills. Unlike Cave, the bandits do not fortify. But they become more aggressive with civilians. Hostage risk increases for next mission. The bandits might relocate to a different settlement or switch templates --- they know someone is coming.

- **Failure:** Same as Cave. No reduction, Strength increases by 1.

**Village-Specific Consequences:**

- **Civilian Casualties:** Every civilian killed during the mission subtracts from province PU. Tracked and reported to the lord. A clean operation with zero civilian deaths is the ideal outcome.

- **Structural Damage:** Buildings destroyed by fire reduce the settlement’s infrastructure. Takes seasons to rebuild. A village burned during liberation produces nothing until restored.

- **Civilian Information:** If the player talked to villagers and learned useful intelligence, that feeds back into the wider insurgency. The bandits may have a second camp, or they are being supplied by someone. World Map receives new information.

- **Displacement:** If the mission failed or caused heavy damage, surviving civilians may flee. PU drops from migration, not death. Stability takes an additional hit.
