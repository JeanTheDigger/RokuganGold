## 56.3 Cave Template --- LOCKED

The Cave template generates underground environments: tunnels, chambers, chokepoints, and darkness. Highest reuse across quest seeds --- applicable to Ronin Bandit Uprising, Nezumi Infestation, Maho Cult, Province Taint Manifestation, and Shadowlands content. The cave exists independently of its occupants. Bandits found it and moved in; they did not build it to spec.

### 56.3.1 Size Categories --- LOCKED

The generator rolls a size category from its own probability curve. Map size is determined by terrain and randomness, not by insurgency Strength. Strength sets a minimum floor only. If the generated size falls below what the insurgency needs to physically house its population, the generator rerolls or bumps up to the minimum. Otherwise, whatever size came up is used. This means the same Strength 4 uprising could produce a tight, dangerous cave or a sprawling one with room to maneuver depending on the roll.

- **Small:** 3-5 rooms, 1-2 corridors. A crack in a hillside leading to a few chambers. Can house up to Strength 3.

- **Medium:** 6-10 rooms, 2-4 corridors, at least one branching path. A proper cave system. Can house up to Strength 6.

- **Large:** 11-16 rooms, multiple branches, dead ends, possibly a water feature or vertical drop. Can house up to Strength 9.

- **Extensive:** 17+ rooms, complex branching, multiple entry points possible, internal hazards. Can house Strength 10 and functions as a full dungeon crawl.

### 56.3.2 Entry and Flow --- LOCKED

**Entry Points:** Small and Medium caves have one entrance. Large caves have a 30% chance of a second entrance --- a back exit the occupants use for escape or resupply. Extensive caves always have at least two entrances, possibly three. The player only knows about the main entrance unless scouting or intelligence revealed the others beforehand.

The generator selects a flow pattern for the cave layout:

- **Linear:** Rooms connect roughly in sequence. One main path, maybe a short side branch. Tight, predictable, every fight is head-on. Most common in Small caves.

- **Branching:** A central junction splits into two or more paths that do not reconnect. Player must choose which way to go. Clearing one branch means backtracking to clear the other. Common in Medium caves.

- **Loop:** Paths reconnect, creating a circuit. Enemies can flank. Player can also flank. Tactical movement matters more. Common in Large caves.

- **Web:** Multiple interconnected paths, rooms with two or three exits each. Chaotic, hard to control. Enemies can reposition, retreat, reinforce. Player can get lost or surrounded. Extensive caves only.

**Depth Rule:** The objective --- the leader’s chamber, the stolen goods, the cult altar, whatever the quest seed specifies --- is always placed in the deepest room from the entrance. The player has to go through the cave, not stumble into the goal immediately.

### 56.3.3 Room Types --- LOCKED

Each room the generator places has a type that determines its shape, size, and what can exist inside it.

- **Entry Tunnel:** Always the first space. Narrow, one tile wide or two at most. Short, connects to the first real room. Only one per entrance.

- **Corridor:** Connects rooms. Narrow, long. Limited combat options --- no flanking, ranged advantage for whoever holds the far end. Can contain patrol routes.

- **Small Chamber:** 4-6 tiles across. Fits a few enemies, a campfire, stored supplies. Single combat encounters. One or two exits.

- **Large Chamber:** 8-12 tiles across. Main living spaces. Multiple enemies, furniture, obstacles that break sightlines. Two or three exits. Where most fights happen.

- **Junction:** Where paths split. Open enough to see the options but not deep. Decision point, not primarily a combat space. Enemies can guard it.

- **Dead End:** A branch that goes nowhere. Could hold loot, a trap, a prisoner, or nothing. Costs the player time to check. Rewards exploration or punishes carelessness.

- **Deep Chamber:** Always the final room. Largest space in the cave. Where the leader and the objective sit. Wide enough for a proper fight with room to maneuver. Generated once per map. Connects to exactly one Corridor or Large Chamber --- one way in and out.

**Connection Rules:** Entry Tunnel always connects to a Corridor or Small Chamber. Corridors always connect two rooms. Junctions require at least three connections. Dead Ends terminate. Deep Chamber connects to exactly one Corridor or Large Chamber.

### 56.3.4 Population Placement --- LOCKED

The enemy roster comes from the quest seed --- Strength value determines headcount and quality. The generator distributes enemies using the following roles:

- **Sentry:** Posted at or near the entry tunnel. One or two enemies. The early warning system. If they spot the player and are not killed quickly, they alert deeper rooms. Placed in Entry Tunnel or first Corridor.

- **Patrol:** Moves between rooms on a route. Not stationary. The player can time movement around them or get caught between a patrol and a static group. One patrol per 4-5 rooms. Only in Medium or larger caves.

- **Camp Group:** Clustered in a Large Chamber or Small Chamber. Eating, sleeping, talking. Stationary but alert. Two to four enemies together. Most of the population is distributed in these.

- **Guard Post:** One or two enemies holding a Junction or Corridor chokepoint. Specifically placed to control movement through the cave. Will not leave their position voluntarily.

- **Leader Group:** In the Deep Chamber. The leader plus their best fighters. Always the toughest encounter. Approximately 20-25% of total enemy count concentrated here.

**Density Rule:** Population gets denser toward the Deep Chamber. Entry and early rooms are sparse --- sentries and maybe a patrol. Middle rooms have camp groups. Final approach is guard posts and then the leader group. The player faces escalating pressure.

**Vacancy Rule:** If the cave is larger than the Strength needs, some rooms are simply empty. No enemies, maybe old campfire remains or discarded supplies. This tells the player the space is bigger than the group occupying it.

**Alert and Investigation:** Enemies are not passive. Combat generates noise. Noise propagates through corridors. Nearby enemies investigate based on their role and disposition. A loud fight in a Small Chamber could pull the patrol from the next corridor or cause a Guard Post to shift toward the noise. Stealth kills and quick clean fights matter tactically. Full alert and investigation AI behavior to be defined in the enemy AI subsystem.

### 56.3.5 Environmental Features --- LOCKED

Features the generator can place in rooms that affect gameplay beyond walls and floors.

**Darkness:** Default state deeper in the cave. Reduces player vision radius per the Perception system in Section 4.4.2. However, human occupants (ronin, cultists) light their living spaces. Occupied rooms have torches, lanterns, or a campfire. Unoccupied rooms are dark with full darkness penalties. Corridors between occupied rooms have intermittent light. Patrol routes are partially lit because the patrol carries a light source that moves with them. Entry tunnels are dark --- occupants do not light the front door. Lit rooms ahead tell the player someone is in there. Non-human occupants (Nezumi, Taint creatures) may not use light at all --- their spaces are pitch black.

- **Water:** Shallow streams or pools. Difficult terrain, movement cost increased. Can appear in corridors or chambers. Splashing through water generates extra noise, tying into the alert system.

- **Chokepoint:** A corridor narrows to one tile. Only one character can pass at a time. Massively favors the defender. Generator places these naturally between rooms.

- **Elevation:** A chamber with a raised ledge or a dropped floor. Ranged advantage for whoever holds the high ground. Common in Large Chambers and the Deep Chamber.

- **Rubble/Debris:** Partial cover, blocks movement on specific tiles. Breaks up open rooms into tactical spaces with sightline interruptions.

- **Unstable Ground:** Loose rocks, crumbling floor. Moving through generates extra noise. Possible collapse if a fight happens on it --- area denial.

- **Fire Hazard:** Fire in a stone cave does not spread to walls but produces smoke that fills corridors and chambers. Smoke reduces visibility to zero and is lethal to anyone remaining in the affected area --- player and enemies alike. The chimney effect funnels smoke through connected tunnels, spreading from the fire's origin outward along corridor paths. Setting fire inside a cave is a weapon but also a self-imposed time limit. The player must plan an escape route before lighting anything. The Burn the Camp objective in a Cave is a fundamentally different tactical choice than in open-air templates like Forest or Stockade --- it is an evacuation tool that clears rooms at the cost of making them impassable. Cross-reference: Ravine Camp (Section 56.11.5) for similar chimney effect in enclosed terrain.

### 56.3.6 Objective Placement --- Cave Template --- LOCKED

The quest seed passes in what the player needs to accomplish. The generator places objectives accordingly. Primary objectives for the Ronin Bandit Uprising seed:

- **Kill the Leader:** The default objective. Leader is in the Deep Chamber. The objective is a person, not a location. If the leader escapes through a back entrance, the objective moves with them. Completing this breaks the uprising’s organization --- biggest Strength reduction.

- **Recover Stolen Goods:** Trade goods raided from caravans. Placed in a Large Chamber near the Deep Chamber but not in it. A storeroom the occupants are guarding. Player could potentially grab and extract without fighting everyone.

- **Burn the Camp:** Deny the hideout itself. Requires hitting multiple rooms, destroying supplies and infrastructure. Not a single target --- the objective is spread across all occupied rooms. Forces the player to move through the whole cave.

- **Rescue Hostages:** Merchants or villagers being held. Placed in a Small Chamber or Dead End, guarded but separate from the main population. Creates tension --- a loud fight nearby could cause the guards to harm the hostages. Stealth and speed matter.

**Placement Rule:** The primary objective is never in the first third of the cave. Always deep. The player must commit to the space before reaching it.

**Secondary Objectives:** Optional, discovered on-site. Not assigned by the lord. Stolen koku in a side room, a letter revealing who is supplying the bandits, a hostage nobody mentioned. These go in Dead Ends and side branches. Reward exploration.

**Combined Objectives:** The quest seed can stack objectives. Kill the leader AND recover stolen goods. Driven by what the lord ordered and what the insurgency’s history generated on the World Map.

### 56.3.7 Resolution and Consequence --- Cave Template --- LOCKED

How the ASCII map outcome feeds back into the World Map simulation. A mission does not simply pass or fail --- there is a spectrum.

- **Full Success:** Primary objective completed, player extracts alive. Strength reduction based on objective type: Kill the Leader reduces Strength by 3-4. Recover Stolen Goods reduces by 2 and restores partial Koku to the province. Burn the Camp reduces by 2 and prevents the same cave from being reused by the insurgency. Rescue Hostages reduces by 1 but generates significant Honor and Glory bonuses and disposition gains.

- **Partial Success:** Player achieved something but not the primary objective. Killed a significant portion of enemies but the leader escaped. Found the goods but could not extract them. Strength reduced by enemy casualties --- roughly 1 Strength per 25% of total enemy roster killed. The insurgency survives and adapts.

- **Retreat:** Player entered, fought, pulled out. Strength reduced only by actual kills. The insurgency is now alerted --- Concealment drops to 0, but the occupants fortify. Next mission against the same uprising generates a harder layout: more guards at entry, traps in corridors, patrols tightened.

- **Failure:** Player killed or captured. No Strength reduction. The insurgency gains confidence --- Strength increases by 1. Lord loses a character. Political consequences --- the lord who sent warriors and lost them looks incompetent.

**Enemy Casualties Persist:** Every enemy killed on the ASCII map is subtracted from the insurgency’s population. Even a retreat where the player killed six ronin before pulling out means six fewer next time. The simulation remembers.

**Kill-to-Strength Conversion:** The exact ratio between enemies killed on the ASCII map and Strength reduction on the World Map is flagged as values pending playtest. The structural rule is locked: enemy kills reduce insurgency Strength proportionally. Quality matters --- killing the leader counts for more than killing a sentry. The precise conversion ratio requires tuning once combat pacing is testable.

**World Map Ripple Effects:** Successful suppression improves Stability recovery. Trade routes reopen. The lord’s Glory and political standing benefit. Failed or ignored missions let Strength keep climbing toward the threshold where the insurgency becomes a full army requiring the Army Combat System (Section 11.7).
