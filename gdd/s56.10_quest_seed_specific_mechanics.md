## 56.10 Quest Seed Specific Mechanics

This section defines mechanics specific to each quest seed type: enemy roster composition, headcount scaling, role assignment rules, unit stat blocks, and morale behavior. Map templates (Sections 56.3 through 56.12) define the space. This section defines who is inside it.

### 56.10.0 Morale Override Rule --- LOCKED

Population placement roles (Sections 56.3 through 56.12) define intended behavior: move toward breach, hold chokepoint, investigate noise, patrol route. Unit morale thresholds (defined per unit type in Section 54.8) override role behavior when triggered. A Bandit Rabble assigned to any role will abandon that role and flee when their morale breaks. A Bandit Thug will hold longer but eventually break. Ronin continue executing their role until killed or the situation is truly lost.

This creates a natural split during combat. Early in an engagement, all enemies converge as their roles dictate. As casualties mount, the composition separates under pressure: Bandit Rabble scatter first, Bandit Thugs break next, ronin hold last. The player faces a wave, then the wave splits --- the weak peel off, the dangerous ones keep pushing. This applies uniformly across all map templates and all quest seeds. Each seed defines its own unit types and morale thresholds; the override rule is universal.

### 56.10.0a Individual Variance Rule --- LOCKED

When the generator populates a roster with generic units, each individual has a 30-40% chance of one stat variance. When it fires, the generator picks one Trait or one Skill and adds +1. This makes every generic unit slightly different --- instead of fighting twenty identical Rebel Peasants, some are a bit tougher, some a bit faster, one happens to have Hunting 2 instead of 1. Small variation, significant feel difference.

**Constraints:** Only one bump per individual --- never two. A bumped stat cannot exceed the next tier’s equivalent stat --- a Rebel Peasant with Earth 3 is a tougher peasant, not a Rebel Ashigaru. A Bandit Rabble with Earth 3 is not a Bandit Thug. The tiers stay distinct. Skills can only bump skills the unit already has or skills that make sense for the unit type --- a Rebel Peasant does not randomly get Kenjutsu 1 but might get Athletics 2 or Hunting 2. A Rebel Ashigaru might get Spears 3 or Defense 2. This rule applies universally to all generic unit types across all quest seeds.

### 56.10.1 Ronin Bandit Uprising --- Roster Composition --- LOCKED

The Ronin Bandit Uprising roster is a mix of trained ronin and untrained peasant bandits. The ratio between them shifts based on the province’s current Stability at the time the quest seed generates the ASCII map. As conditions worsen, more desperate heimin join the gang. The ronin always provide leadership and the combat core. The peasants provide numbers.

This composition shift is the recruitment mechanic made visible. The Hidden Growth phase (Section 11.11) adds +1 Strength per season. That growth represents peasants joining as conditions deteriorate. As Stability drops, more desperate heimin turn to banditry for survival, shifting the roster composition toward higher Bandit Rabble percentages. This happens naturally through the Stability-driven composition ratios below --- it does not require a separate merger mechanic.

**Cross-Reference:** Ronin stat blocks (Simple Bandit, Experienced Bandit, Bandit Lord) from Section 54.8. Peasant Bandit stat block defined below in Section 56.10.2.

### 56.10.1a Leader Tier by Strength --- LOCKED

- **Strength 1-3:** Simple Bandit as leader. Barely more capable than the rest. Just the one who had the nerve or the idea.

- **Strength 4-6:** Experienced Bandit as leader. Hardened, organized, knows how to run a gang.

- **Strength 7+:** Bandit Lord as leader. Charismatic, dangerous, commands real loyalty. The insurgency has military structure.

### 56.10.1b Headcount per Strength Point by Stability --- LOCKED

More peasants means more bodies per Strength point. Total enemy count on the ASCII map scales with both Strength and the peasant ratio.

- **Restless (51-75):** 3-4 enemies per Strength point. Mostly trained fighters. A Strength 4 uprising fields 12-16 enemies.

- **Volatile (26-50):** 4-5 enemies per Strength point. Peasants swell the ranks. A Strength 4 uprising fields 16-20 enemies.

- **Broken (0-25):** 5-6 enemies per Strength point. Overwhelmingly peasant. A Strength 4 uprising fields 20-24 enemies.

### 56.10.1c Roster Mix by Stability Tier --- LOCKED

Three-tier composition. Ronin are the trained samurai core. Bandit Thugs are committed heimin criminals. Bandit Rabble are desperate commoners. All stat blocks in Section 54.8.

**Restless (51-75):**

- **Ronin (Simple/Experienced Bandits):** 60-70% of roster.

- **Bandit Thugs:** 15-25% of roster.

- **Bandit Rabble:** 10-15% of roster.

**Volatile (26-50):**

- **Ronin (Simple/Experienced Bandits):** 25-35% of roster.

- **Bandit Thugs:** 20-30% of roster.

- **Bandit Rabble:** 40-50% of roster.

**Broken (0-25):**

- **Ronin (Simple/Experienced Bandits):** 15-20% of roster.

- **Bandit Thugs:** 15-25% of roster.

- **Bandit Rabble:** 55-70% of roster.

### 56.10.1d Role Assignment by Unit Type --- LOCKED

The population placement roles defined in each map template (Sections 56.3 through 56.12) are filled based on unit type. Ronin hold critical positions. Bandit Thugs fill secondary combat roles. Bandit Rabble fill expendable and outer roles.

- **Leader Group:** Always ronin. The leader plus the best ronin fighters. No Bandit Thugs or Bandit Rabble in the leader group under any composition.

- **Guard Posts / Chokepoint Holders:** Always ronin. Trained fighters hold the defensive positions.

- **Edge Defenders / Wall Watchers:** Ronin preferred. Bandit Thugs acceptable when insufficient ronin are available.

- **Rim Watchers (Ravine):** Ronin. A critical solo position requires reliability.

- **Camp Groups / Room Groups:** Mixed. Weighted by the Stability composition ratio. One ronin per group as backbone. Bandit Thugs and Bandit Rabble fill the rest.

- **Patrols:** Bandit Thug leading Bandit Rabble. The Thug provides discipline. The Rabble provide extra eyes.

- **Sentries:** Bandit Rabble. Expendable early warning. They do not need to fight --- they need to see and shout.

- **Rubble Lurkers (Ruined Structure):** Bandit Thugs. Requires nerve to sit alone in the dark.

### 56.10.1e Gameplay Experience by Stability Tier --- LOCKED

The three-tier composition creates distinct gameplay experiences at different Stability levels against the same insurgency type using the same map templates.

**Restless:** Hard, tight fights. Fewer enemies but each one is a real threat. The player faces trained ronin who hold positions, fight competently, and do not break easily. Bandit Thugs support competently. The few Bandit Rabble present cluster behind the real fighters. Victory requires tactical skill. Feels like a military operation against a small professional force.

**Volatile:** Mixed difficulty. Outer positions crumble quickly as Bandit Rabble scatter, but the ronin core and Bandit Thugs hold. The player cuts through the screen and then hits the wall. Morale cascades in the Rabble groups create opportunities --- break one group and nearby Rabble may flee. Bandit Thugs hold longer but eventually break too. The ronin are the last to fall.

**Broken:** Swarm combat. Waves of desperate Bandit Rabble, poorly armed, breaking and running. Bandit Thugs provide a tougher middle layer that holds longer. But sheer numbers of Rabble are dangerous through volume. The ronin at the center are still lethal --- reaching them through the layers is the challenge. Morale cascades can clear rooms rapidly but the player takes chip damage from the mob. Feels like wading through chaos to reach the real fighters.

### 56.10.2 Bandit Unit Types --- Reference --- LOCKED

Three tiers of non-ronin bandit exist within a Ronin Bandit Uprising. Their stat blocks are defined in Section 54.8 (Ronin NPC Archetypes: Non-Ronin Bandit Archetypes). The three tiers and their summary characteristics:

- **Bandit Rabble:** Earth 2, no weapon skill, Void 1. Morale breaks at 40-50% visible casualties. A desperate commoner. The bulk filler at low Stability. Mobs targets, flees when the fight turns.

- **Bandit Thug:** Earth 3, weapon skill 2, Defense 1, Agility 3, Void 1. Morale breaks at 60% visible casualties. A committed criminal. Fights with rough competence, uses cover, holds positions briefly.

- **Simple Bandit (ronin):** Earth 3, weapon skill 3, Defense 1, Void 2. Trained warrior. Does not break easily. Holds chokepoints, fights competently, dangerous one-on-one.

- **Experienced Bandit (ronin):** Per Section 54.8. Insight Rank 3. Hardened veteran. Present at Strength 4+.

- **Bandit Lord (ronin):** Per Section 54.8. Insight Rank 4. Charismatic commander. Present at Strength 7+.

**Cross-Reference:** Full stat blocks in Section 54.8. Role assignment rules in Section 56.10.1d. Morale and fleeing behavior in Section 54.8 (Non-Ronin Bandit Archetypes).

### 56.10.2a Named NPC Integration --- LOCKED

The roster composition system (Sections 56.10.1 through 56.10.2) builds the generic floor for any insurgency encounter. Named NPCs from the Named Character System (Section 22) populate it with specific people when the simulation produces them. Most insurgencies are generic. Some have a named NPC who makes them unique and memorable. The player does not know which until they are on the ASCII map.

**How Named NPCs Join Insurgencies:** The NPC Decision Engine (Section 55) evaluates options for every named character each tick. A ronin NPC who lost their position, got disgraced, or burned through their contacts may find that joining a bandit gang is their best available option. The engine routes them into the insurgency through the standard objective and decomposition system --- no special-case code required. The same applies to Brotherhood monks whose personality (high Jin/Compassion) drives them to support a Peasant Revolt, or any named character whose circumstances and personality align with an active insurgency. The simulation puts people where their decisions lead them.

**Role Assignment:** A named NPC who joins an insurgency takes the highest role their capabilities support. A named ronin with leadership qualities replaces the generic Bandit Lord or Experienced Bandit as the leader. A named shugenja sits in the Deep Chamber or leader’s building providing magical support. A named monk is with the main group, visible and identifiable. They do not fill expendable roles --- the simulation placed them here because they have value, and the roster reflects that.

**They Fight Differently:** Named NPCs use their actual character sheet from Section 22 --- school techniques, advantages, disadvantages, specific skills. They are not just tougher stat blocks. They are mechanically distinct. A named shugenja casts spells the player must deal with. A named monk uses Kiho. A named ronin has a school technique that changes how they fight. A bandit camp that was supposed to be a straightforward bushi clearance mission could have someone who throws Earth spells. The quest seed did not script that. The simulation put that person there.

**Discovery:** The player does not know a named NPC is present until they encounter evidence. They might see them mid-fight, hear about them from a captured bandit, recognize them by description, or find a personal item during exploration. The quest seed flags internally that a named NPC is present, but the player’s information depends on what intelligence they gathered beforehand. A lord who investigated thoroughly before sending warriors might know that a disgraced ronin shugenja joined the gang. A lord who sent warriors blind gets a surprise.

**World Map Consequences:** Killing or capturing a named NPC has consequences beyond the insurgency itself. They have relationships, secrets, a history. A named ronin might carry a letter from someone important --- revealing a connection to a lord, a clan, or a conspiracy. A named monk’s death might anger the Brotherhood of Shinsei, generating a diplomatic topic. A named shugenja might carry a scroll the Phoenix Clan wants recovered. Capturing a named NPC alive opens interrogation possibilities that yield intelligence about the wider world, not just the bandit camp. Named NPCs turn a tactical encounter into a narrative event.

**Design Principle:** It will always be more interesting for the player to fight a named NPC who had a place in the world than a generic stat block. The generic roster provides the baseline challenge. Named NPCs provide the stories. The simulation generates both. The quest system presents both. The player discovers which they are dealing with through play.

**Cross-Reference:** Section 22 (Named Character System) for character sheets and generation. Section 55 (NPC Decision Engine) for how named NPCs evaluate and choose to join insurgencies. Section 18 (NPC Objective System) for the objectives that drive named NPCs toward banditry, revolt, or other insurgency participation. Section 12.8 (Secret System) for secrets that named NPCs may carry into insurgency encounters.

### 56.10.4 Peasant Revolt --- Roster Composition --- LOCKED

The Peasant Revolt roster is fundamentally different from the Ronin Bandit Uprising. The insurgents are the local population fighting their lord --- not criminals hiding in the wilderness. Almost entirely untrained commoners with one or two capable leaders. The quality gap between the Rebel Leader and everyone else is enormous. Named NPCs (ronin, monks, shugenja) may be present if the simulation’s decision engine routed them into the revolt through Section 56.10.2a --- there is no percentage roll for their presence.

**Cross-Reference:** Rebel stat blocks (Rebel Peasant, Rebel Ashigaru, Rebel Leader) in Section 54.8 (Peasant Revolt Archetypes). Honor cost correction in Section 11.11 (Peasant Revolt entry). Suppression carries no Honor cost per Rokugani caste law.

### 56.10.4a Leader by Strength --- LOCKED

- **Strength 1-4:** Rebel Leader as leader. A village headman or charismatic farmer. The only person with any combat capability.

- **Strength 5-7:** Rebel Leader as leader, supported by a cluster of Rebel Ashigaru as personal guard. The revolt has organization.

- **Strength 8+:** Rebel Leader as leader. Multiple Rebel Ashigaru forming a core. At this scale the revolt has become a genuine military problem and approaches the threshold where it must be engaged through the Army Combat System (Section 11.7) rather than insurgency suppression.

### 56.10.4b Headcount per Strength Point --- LOCKED

Peasant Revolts are numerous. These are whole villages, not small gangs. Headcount is fixed regardless of Stability tier --- the revolt draws from the local population, which is already desperate by definition (Stability must be Volatile or Broken for a Peasant Revolt to spawn per Section 11.11).

**Headcount:** 5-6 enemies per Strength point. A Strength 4 revolt fields 20-24 people. A Strength 8 revolt fields 40-48.

### 56.10.4c Roster Mix --- LOCKED

- **Rebel Peasants:** 70-80% of roster. The bulk. Untrained, improvised weapons, fighting because they have nothing left to lose.

- **Rebel Ashigaru:** 15-25% of roster. Former conscripts who retained training. The backbone that makes the revolt hold together.

- **Rebel Leader:** One per revolt. Always present.

### 56.10.4d Role Assignment --- LOCKED

- **Leader:** The Rebel Leader. Center of the village or deepest position.

- **Chokepoints / Defensive Positions:** Rebel Ashigaru. The only ones who can hold a line.

- **Sentries, Patrols, Camp Groups:** Rebel Peasants. Mass bodies in every role.

### 56.10.4e The Occupied Village Problem --- LOCKED

When a Peasant Revolt uses the Occupied Village template, the standard civilian mechanics do not apply. In a bandit occupation, civilians and enemies are distinct. In a revolt, the villagers ARE the insurgents. Everyone in the village is hostile --- or at least potentially hostile.

**No Civilian Information Sources:** The player cannot ask a friendly villager where the leader is. Everyone is either a rebel or hiding in fear of both sides.

**Non-Combatants:** Not every villager is fighting. Women, children, the elderly, the sick --- they are present in their homes. They are not enemies but they are not allies. The player must distinguish between armed rebels and unarmed non-combatants. Killing non-combatants does not affect Honor but reduces province PU.

**No Drive Them Out:** Unlike bandits in a village, rebels have nowhere to go. This is their home. They do not scatter into the fields. They fight until broken or suppressed.

**PU Loss is Inherent:** Every rebel killed is a provincial citizen lost. Suppression directly reduces the province’s workforce. The PU cost per Section 11.11 (Strength x 0.1 per suppression action) applies on the World Map. On the ASCII map, every Rebel Peasant and Rebel Ashigaru killed contributes to this loss. A lord who sends bushi to crush a Strength 6 revolt might lose 10-15% of the village’s population in the process.

**Gameplay Feel:** Grim. The player is killing farmers. The enemies are weak and numerous. The fights are easy and unpleasant. This is the game showing the player what bad governance produces --- not a challenge to overcome but a consequence to witness. The difficulty is not tactical. The difficulty is watching the province’s population die because someone let it get this bad.

### 56.10.6 Nezumi Infestation --- Roster Composition --- LOCKED

The Nezumi Infestation roster is entirely non-human. No named NPC integration applies unless the simulation has produced an extraordinarily unusual circumstance. The roster is a tribal structure: scouts on the perimeter, warriors in the tunnels, archers in elevated positions, broodmothers in the deepest nesting chamber, and a chieftain commanding from the center.

**Cross-Reference:** Nezumi stat blocks in Section 54.2 (Nezumi Warren Archetypes). Base Ratling entry in Section 54.2 for Taint Immunity and Name trait. Insurgency mechanics in Section 11.11 (Nezumi Infestation).

### 56.10.6a Headcount per Strength Point --- LOCKED

**Headcount:** 4-5 enemies per Strength point. Smaller than human revolts but each one is faster and stealthier. A Strength 4 warren fields 16-20 Nezumi. A Strength 8 warren fields 32-40.

### 56.10.6b Roster Mix --- LOCKED

- **Nezumi Warriors:** 50-60% of roster. The standard fighters. Packs of three or four swarming from multiple directions.

- **Nezumi Archers:** 15-20% of roster. Ranged threat from darkness. Posted in elevated positions and side tunnels.

- **Nezumi Scouts:** 10-15% of roster. Ambush layer in entry tunnels and outer passages.

- **Nezumi Broodmothers:** 5-10% of roster. Deepest chamber only. Scale with warren size --- one in Small, two to three in Medium, four or more in Large and Extensive.

- **Nezumi Chieftain:** One per warren. Always present.

### 56.10.6c Equipment Tiers by Strength --- LOCKED

The Nezumi do not improve their skills as Strength grows. They accumulate stolen equipment from raids. The warren’s Strength reflects how long the tribe has been raiding and what they have acquired. Same Nezumi, same skills, sharper objects.

- **Strength 1-3 (Fresh Warren):** Crude weapons only. Bone spears, sharpened wood, stone knives. Base damage as per the 54.2 stat block (4k2). Bone-tipped arrows. No armor. Everything is self-made from scraps.

- **Strength 4-6 (Established Warren):** Mixed equipment. Warriors carry stolen steel --- iron-tipped spears, real knives, farming tools repurposed as weapons. Damage improves by approximately +1k0 from weapon quality alone. Archers have stolen steel arrowheads. Still no armor. Raids have been going on long enough to accumulate real equipment.

- **Strength 7-9 (Entrenched Warren):** Stolen military equipment. Steel spears, real swords used clumsily, possibly crude armor pieces --- a stolen do-maru chest plate strapped on wrong, a helmet worn backwards. Damage improves further. Some warriors gain 1-2 points of Reduction from scavenged armor. The player recognizes equipment that came from provincial stores or dead soldiers.

**The Tell:** Equipment quality communicates information to the player. Stolen Crab weapons mean the tribe has been raiding military supply lines. Crane-made farming tools mean village raids. The equipment is intelligence about how long the problem has been ignored and what has been lost.

**Stat Impact:** Skills and Rings do not change with equipment tier. The Nezumi are not learning to fight better --- they just carry sharper objects. Damage dice improve from weapon quality. Attack rolls do not change because the Nezumi does not know how to use the weapon properly.

### 56.10.6d Role Assignment --- LOCKED

- **Sentries / Ambush Layer:** Nezumi Scouts. In entry tunnels and side passages. The first contact --- they hit from hiding and retreat.

- **Ranged Positions:** Nezumi Archers. Elevated spots, dark corridors with sightlines, side tunnels overlooking main passages. They fire from positions the player cannot see.

- **Main Fighting Force:** Nezumi Warriors. In chambers and wider tunnels. Fight in packs, swarm from multiple directions.

- **Nesting Chamber Defense:** Nezumi Broodmothers. Deepest chamber only. Do not leave. Do not flee.

- **Command:** Nezumi Chieftain. In or adjacent to the nesting chamber.

### 56.10.6e The Dark Warren --- Gameplay Rules --- LOCKED

The Nezumi warren is the only insurgency map where the occupants use no light whatsoever. Nezumi see in darkness. The entire warren is pitch black. The existing Section 4.4.2 environmental modifier for underground without light source applies: -3 to effective Perception radius. A player with Perception 2 is limited to adjacent tiles only. Torches and lanterns restore 2 to the effective radius but make the player a visible target to every Nezumi in the area.

**The Torch Dilemma:** The player needs light to navigate and fight. But carrying a torch makes them the only lit object in a dark warren. Every Nezumi can see the player. The player can only see what the torch illuminates. Archers fire from darkness into the light. Scouts track the torch glow through tunnels. The player is always visible; the enemies almost never are.

**No Alert System:** Nezumi do not investigate noise the way humans do. They already know the warren better than the player ever will. When combat starts, nearby Nezumi reposition instantly through tunnels too small for humans. They appear behind the player. They emerge from holes in the wall. The warren has passages the player cannot use that the Nezumi move through freely. The player is always being flanked. There is no safe direction.

**Pup Chambers:** The deepest room contains Nezumi pups --- non-combatants. They are not enemies. They flee, hide, and scream. The screaming draws every nearby Broodmother into a frenzy. Reaching the pup chamber triggers the hardest fight in the warren because the Broodmothers will never stop coming. The pups themselves are not a combat threat but their presence is the trigger for the most dangerous enemies in the warren.

### 56.10.6f Objective Placement --- Nezumi Infestation --- LOCKED

Nezumi Infestation objectives differ from human insurgencies. There is no leader to assassinate for political gain, no stolen trade goods to recover, no hostages to rescue. The objective is suppression --- reduce the warren’s ability to function.

- **Clear the Warren:** The default objective. Kill enough Nezumi to reduce Strength. Every kill counts. The warren does not need to be fully cleared --- reducing the population below the point where it can sustain itself is sufficient. Functionally equivalent to Kill the Leader in outcome but achieved through attrition across the whole warren rather than a single target kill.

- **Kill the Chieftain:** Optional high-value objective. If the Chieftain is killed, the tribe loses cohesion. Strength reduction bonus --- equivalent to 2-3 additional kills in terms of Strength impact. But the Chieftain is in the deepest section, guarded by Broodmothers.

- **Destroy the Nesting Chamber:** The most decisive objective. If the nesting chamber is destroyed (fire, collapse, direct assault), the tribe cannot reproduce. Even if survivors escape, the infestation cannot regrow. Equivalent to Burn the Camp in terms of denying the location permanently. But reaching and destroying the nesting chamber means fighting through Broodmothers --- the hardest enemies in the warren.

- **Recover Stolen Goods:** The warren contains accumulated loot from raids --- Rice, equipment, provincial supplies. Stored in chambers near the center. Recovering them restores partial Rice to the province stockpile. Secondary objective, not primary.

### 56.10.6g Resolution and Consequence --- Nezumi Infestation --- LOCKED

Same spectrum as other seeds, adapted for the non-human context.

- **Full Success:** Primary objective completed. Strength reduction by objective type. Clear the Warren reduces by kills. Kill the Chieftain adds a bonus reduction. Destroy the Nesting Chamber prevents regrowth --- the infestation cannot recover even if the Chieftain escaped. No PU loss. No political consequence. Province Rice drain stops.

- **Partial Success:** Player entered the warren, killed Nezumi, but did not reach the primary objective. Strength reduced by kills. The warren is alerted and Nezumi reposition --- next mission encounters a reorganized defense with scouts pushed further out and archers repositioned.

- **Retreat:** Player pulled out. Strength reduced only by kills. Clean retreat is harder than human caves because Nezumi pursue through their own tunnels --- the player may take hits on the way out.

- **Failure:** No Strength reduction. Insurgency remains at current Strength. See Section 56.9 --- no automatic Strength increase for Nezumi on failure.

**No Relocation:** Unlike human insurgencies, Nezumi do not relocate after a failed or partial suppression. The warren is their home. They rebuild, reposition, and dig new tunnels. The next mission against the same warren faces a modified layout --- new passages, blocked old ones, repositioned defenders. The warren evolves between visits.

### 56.10.8 Maho Cult --- Roster Composition --- LOCKED

Maho Cult rosters are smaller than bandit or revolt rosters but qualitatively more dangerous due to magic and undead. Composition varies by cell establishment path (Section 56.14.7). Living headcount is low --- 1-2 per Strength point. Zombie count is separate and scales with time the cell has been Active.

**Cross-Reference:** Cult combat archetypes (Cult Initiate, Maho Cultist, Bloodspeaker Adept) in Section 54.3 (Maho Cult Combat Archetypes). Zombie stat block in Section 54.2. Cell establishment paths in Section 56.14.7. Bloodspeaker Cult Network System in Section 56.14.

### 56.10.8a Whispering Cell (PTL Corruption Path) --- LOCKED

Amateurs corrupted by rising Taint. The weakest cell variation but the most spiritually hazardous environment.

- **Total Living:** 4-6. Headcount 1-2 per Strength point.

- **Cult Initiates:** 50-60% of roster.

- **Maho Cultists:** 30-40% of roster.

- **Bloodspeaker Adepts:** None. These are amateurs.

- **Zombies:** None. They do not know the porcelain mask technique.

- **Leader:** The strongest Maho Cultist. Generic. No special technique.

- **Primary Threat:** Kansen environmental hazards (deferred --- see Section 56.6.4). The enemies are weak but the spiritual environment is hostile. Taint exposure risk in the ritual space.

### 56.10.8b Bloodspeaker Proper (Agent Infiltration Path) --- LOCKED

A trained cell planted by the Bloodspeaker network. The most dangerous standard cell.

- **Total Living:** 5-8. Headcount 1-2 per Strength point.

- **Cult Initiates:** 30-40% of roster.

- **Maho Cultists:** 30-40% of roster.

- **Bloodspeaker Adepts:** 10-20% of roster (minimum 1).

- **Zombies:** Approximately 1 per season the cell has been Active. Maximum cap: 10 zombies regardless of time active. Corpse availability in the province is the natural limiter --- a province that recently had famine or battle provides more raw material. A prosperous province limits supply to the cult’s own sacrificial victims, grave robbery, and murdered travelers.

- **Leader:** A Bloodspeaker Adept with Iuchiban’s Method technique. Trained, organized, dangerous.

- **Primary Threat:** Layered defense. Zombies hold corridors (Reduction 5, no morale, Fear 3) while living cultists cast maho from behind them. The Adept can animate fresh corpses during combat, turning dead allies or killed cultists into new zombies mid-fight.

### 56.10.8c Fallen Pillar (Named NPC Path) --- LOCKED

Built around one powerful individual. The cell is that person plus followers they recruited.

- **Total Living:** 3-5 followers plus the named NPC leader.

- **Cult Initiates:** 40-50% of followers.

- **Maho Cultists:** 30-40% of followers.

- **Zombies:** Only if the named NPC learned porcelain mask creation. If present, same cap of 10 maximum.

- **Leader:** Named NPC with full character sheet (Section 22). School techniques, advantages, disadvantages, specific skills. Their capabilities define the cell. A fallen shugenja has elemental magic plus maho. A corrupted lord has political connections. A disgraced magistrate knows investigation counter-techniques.

- **Primary Threat:** The leader. Remove them and the cell collapses instantly. Followers surrender or scatter. But reaching the leader means getting through whatever defenses their specific capabilities create.

### 56.10.8d Zombie Scaling and Cap --- LOCKED

Zombie count scales with time Active, not Strength. Strength represents living members. Zombies are accumulated separately.

- **Rate:** Approximately 1 zombie per season the cell has been Active.

- **Maximum Cap:** 10 zombies regardless of time active or corpse availability. A cell active for 15 seasons still has at most 10 zombies --- older ones degrade and are replaced.

- **Corpse Availability Modifier:** A province that recently experienced famine, battle, or plague has more available corpses. In such provinces, the zombie accumulation rate may be faster (up to 2 per season) but the cap remains 10. A prosperous province limits supply --- the cult must create its own corpses through sacrifice, grave robbery, or murder.

- **Combat Animation:** A Bloodspeaker Adept can animate a fresh corpse during combat using a maho spell (not the porcelain mask method --- that requires preparation). Combat animation takes a full round of casting and requires the Adept to be adjacent to the corpse. The player can disrupt the casting by attacking the Adept during that round. A combat-animated zombie is temporary --- it collapses after the scene ends. Only porcelain mask zombies are permanent. Detailed casting mechanics deferred to Section 56.6.5.

### 56.10.8e Role Assignment --- Maho Cult --- LOCKED

Maho cell roles use the template population placement system but with a distinct layered structure: undead front, casters behind.

- **Chokepoint Holders / Guard Posts:** Zombies. They do not need morale to stand in a doorway. They absorb hits, block movement, and generate Fear 3 while the living cultists operate behind them.

- **Sentries:** Cult Initiates. Watching, alerting. They run to warn the leader, not to fight.

- **Camp Groups / Room Groups:** Maho Cultists. The main living threat. They cast from behind zombie screens.

- **Leader Position:** In the ritual space --- the deepest or most central room. Behind maximum zombie and cultist layers. The Bloodspeaker Adept or named NPC operates from here.

- **Ritual Space:** Every Maho cell has one. A room dedicated to blood magic. This is where the PTL damage is concentrated. Entering it risks Taint exposure (Stamina + Willpower vs TN 20). It is where the leader operates and where the primary objective sits. The ritual space replaces the Deep Chamber as the final destination in Cave templates, or the leader’s building in Village templates.

### 56.10.8f Resolution --- Maho Cult Specific --- LOCKED

Standard resolution spectrum applies with Maho-specific additions:

- **Taint Exposure:** Characters who fought in the ritual space or were hit by maho spells during the encounter must make a Taint resistance check after the mission. TN depends on exposure intensity --- fighting zombies in corridors is lower risk than entering the ritual space. Shugenja with jade are partially protected.

- **Artifact Recovery:** If the cell was Artifact Discovery path (Section 56.14.7 Path 4), the artifact must be recovered or destroyed. Leaving it in the lair means a new cell can form around it. Recovering it generates a secondary objective --- transport the dangerous object safely to someone who can contain it (Kuni Witch-Hunters, Asako Inquisitors).

- **Hydra Check:** On suppression, the Hydra Rule (Section 56.14.4) fires. If the cell was Active long enough, a new Dormant cell already exists somewhere in the empire. The player has no way to know.

### 56.10.10b Tainted Animal Overlay --- LOCKED

When the generator creates a Tainted animal for the encounter, it takes the base animal stat block from Section 54.1 and applies the following Taint modifications. The animal is not a new species --- it is a recognizable creature that has been corrupted.

- **Taint Rank:** Assigned based on how long the animal has been in the corrupted area. Typically Rank 1-3 for most Tainted animals. Rank 4+ is rare and produces barely recognizable monstrosities.

- **Physical Changes:** +1 Strength per Taint Rank. Earth unchanged. The corruption makes them stronger but not tougher. They hit harder but die at the same rate.

- **Behavioral Change:** No morale threshold. Tainted animals do not flee. The corruption drives them to attack anything living relentlessly. A normal wolf retreats when injured. A Tainted wolf fights until it physically cannot move.

- **Fear:** Tainted animals at Rank 2+ generate Fear 1. At Rank 3+, Fear 2. They are visibly wrong --- discolored eyes, patchy fur, exposed bone, unnatural movement. Seeing them is disturbing.

- **Taint Exposure:** Melee combat with a Tainted animal is an exposure event. Each hit received requires an Earth roll vs TN 10 + (animal’s Taint Rank x 5) or gain 1 Point of Taint. Ranged combat avoids this --- another reason distance matters tactically.

### 56.10.10c Focal Point Types --- LOCKED

Every Province Taint Manifestation has a focal point --- a specific location where the corruption is worst. The focal point is generated from the province’s history, same principle as Ruined Structure origins. The generator selects based on what exists in the province.

- **Corrupted Burial Site:** Improperly cremated dead, old graves, a family crypt. The ground is wrong. Things are climbing out. Could use Cave template (underground crypt) or open ground (graveyard). The deeper layers hold older, stronger revenants.

- **Former Ritual Site:** Where a Maho Cult operated before being suppressed. The cultists are gone but the spiritual scar remains. Uses the Ruined Structure template or Urban Hideout naturally. Kansen are thick. The ritual space is the focal point.

- **Poisoned Natural Feature:** A spring, a grove, a cave mouth by a river. Open landscape. The corruption flows outward from the water source. Tainted animals congregate because they drink from it. No walls, no structure --- sick land.

- **Old Battlefield:** Hastily buried dead stirring. Flat terrain, exposed. Zombies and revenants emerging from the ground. The focal point is the mass grave or the site of the most violent death.

### 56.10.10d Encounter Structure --- Three Zones --- LOCKED

The encounter is structured as a gradient from the outer edge to the focal point. Not rooms and corridors --- concentric zones of increasing corruption.

**Approach Zone:** Outer area. Light corruption. Tainted animals wandering in unpredictable patterns --- not patrolling a route, drifting. Some corrupted terrain to navigate around. Manageable Taint exposure. The player gets a feel for how bad it is. Exposure checks are infrequent --- only when contacting specific corrupted features (a Tainted stream, a patch of dead earth).

**Corruption Zone:** Middle area. Heavy corruption. Corrupted water and soil are common. Spontaneous undead emerge from the ground --- proximity triggered, not alert triggered. The player might be standing on a burial site when a zombie claws up. Tainted humans if present --- at Rank 3-4, paranoid, attacking on sight. Exposure checks become frequent. Jade is degrading. The player commits --- pushing forward means more exposure, retreating means the mission fails.

**Focal Point:** The center. Maximum corruption. Ambient Taint every round --- Earth roll vs TN (PTL x 3) each round present. The Focal Point Guardian --- the most corrupted thing in the area. Could be a massive Tainted animal barely recognizable as what it was, a powerful Undead Revenant, or a Tainted Human who reached Rank 5 and became Lost. This creature must be cleared before the shugenja can begin purification. The shugenja performs the purification ritual while the bushi holds the perimeter against whatever the corruption throws at them.

### 56.10.10e Environmental Taint Hazards --- LOCKED

Specific terrain features on the ASCII map that are Taint exposure sources. These are not creatures --- they are the corrupted land itself.

- **Tainted Water:** Streams, pools, rice paddies corrupted by PTL. Wading through triggers an Earth roll vs TN 15. Drinking is TN 25. A corrupted river means the player routes around it or accepts exposure. Visible --- the water is discolored, smells wrong, dead vegetation along the banks.

- **Corrupted Soil:** Visibly wrong ground. Discolored, dead vegetation, ground that feels warm or pulses. Walking on marked tiles triggers an Earth roll vs TN 10. Not every tile --- specific patches. The player reads the terrain and routes around the worst spots.

- **Corrupted Vegetation:** Twisted trees, plants that should not exist, wrong growth patterns. Touching or pushing through is an Earth roll vs TN 10. In forested areas, corrupted undergrowth becomes a Taint hazard instead of just a noise hazard.

- **Emergent Undead Tiles:** Specific ground tiles where corpses are buried. When a living creature steps on them or remains adjacent for more than one round, a zombie begins emerging --- takes 2 rounds to fully rise. The player can move away before it emerges or fight it as it climbs out (easier to hit while emerging, cannot attack until fully risen).

- **Ambient Corruption (Focal Point only):** The air itself is thick with Kansen. Earth roll vs TN (PTL x 3) each round present at the focal point. No specific tile --- the entire focal point area radiates Taint constantly. Jade items degrade visibly. Prolonged presence is genuinely dangerous even for high-Earth characters.

### 56.10.10f Roster Composition --- By PTL Tier --- LOCKED

Creature mix and density scale with PTL, not insurgency Strength. Strength determines the total Taint energy available. PTL determines what forms it takes.

**PTL 3-5 (Touched):** Kansen environmental hazards. Tainted animals in small numbers --- a lone wolf, a pair of boars. Maybe a single zombie at an old burial site. Tainted humans at Rank 1-2 if anyone has been living near the focal point long enough. Sparse, unsettling, manageable. Headcount: 3-5 creatures per Strength point.

**PTL 6-8 (Corrupted):** Tainted animals in packs. Multiple zombies emerging from burial sites. Undead revenants at older graves. **PTL 9-10 (Blighted):** Everything above, more numerous and more powerful. Tainted animals at Rank 3-4 --- barely recognizable monstrosities. Undead revenants in armor rising from warrior graves. Headcount: 5-8 creatures per Strength point.

### 56.10.10g Creature Behavior --- Not Organized --- LOCKED

Province Taint Manifestation creatures are not organized. They have no leader, no structure, no roles. They do not defend positions. They are drawn to the focal point by spiritual gravity and attack anything living that enters their territory. The role assignment system from other seeds does not apply. Instead:

- **Wanderers:** Tainted animals moving in unpredictable patterns. Not patrolling --- drifting. The player cannot time their movement or predict their paths. They may wander away from the player or directly toward them.

- **Emergent Undead:** Zombies and revenants that rise from the ground in specific locations. Proximity triggered. The player might be standing on one. They emerge when something living gets close.

- **Clustered Pack:** Tainted animals grouped near the focal point. Not defending it --- feeding on the corruption. Dense, hostile, territorial.

- **Tainted Humans:** Variable. Those at Rank 1-2 might be encountered wandering, confused, possibly still capable of speech. Those at Rank 3-4 attack on sight. Those at Rank 5 (Lost) are near the focal point, fully consumed.

- **Focal Point Guardian:** The most corrupted thing present. Not a leader commanding others --- the thing closest to the source. A massive Tainted animal, a powerful Revenant, or a Lost human. Must be cleared before purification can begin.

### 56.10.10h The Shugenja Requirement --- LOCKED

Province Taint Manifestation is the only quest seed with a hard party composition requirement. Section 11.11 already states military-only suppression reduces Strength by -1 maximum --- entities keep reforming without spiritual cleansing. A bushi can fight the symptoms but cannot cure the disease.

**The Purification Ritual:** A shugenja must physically reach the focal point and perform purification. This takes multiple rounds --- the shugenja is occupied and cannot fight. The bushi holds the perimeter, fighting whatever the corruption sends while the shugenja works. Each successful purification round reduces PTL by a fraction. Full purification of the focal point takes extended effort.

**Without a Shugenja:** A bushi-only mission can kill creatures and temporarily reduce insurgency Strength through attrition. But the focal point remains. The creatures reform next season. The PTL does not decrease. The mission produces tactical success but no strategic result. The lord must send a shugenja eventually or the problem persists indefinitely.

**Shugenja Vulnerability:** The shugenja performing purification is exposed to maximum ambient Taint for the duration of the ritual. They need jade protection. They need the bushi to keep creatures off them. If the shugenja is interrupted, the ritual must restart. If the shugenja accumulates too much Taint during the ritual, they must stop or risk their own corruption.

### 56.10.10i Resolution and Consequence --- Province Taint Manifestation --- LOCKED

- **Full Success (Purification Complete):** Focal point purified. PTL reduced by -1 to -2 immediately. Insurgency Strength drops significantly. Creatures in the area lose their spiritual anchor --- Tainted animals scatter and eventually die as the corruption leaves them, undead collapse. Tainted humans at Rank 1-2 stop accumulating new Taint and may recover over time. Tainted humans at Rank 3+ are too far gone --- they must be dealt with separately.

- **Partial Success (Creatures Cleared, No Purification):** Bushi killed creatures but no shugenja purified the focal point. Strength reduced by kills. PTL unchanged. Creatures reform next season. The mission bought time but did not solve the problem.

- **Retreat:** Player withdrew. Strength reduced only by kills. Exposure accumulated during the mission persists --- characters must check for Taint gained. Jade items degraded during the mission remain degraded.

- **Failure:** No Strength reduction. Characters may have gained Taint from the attempt. Jade consumed for nothing.

**Unique Consequence --- Taint Exposure:** Every character who participated in the mission must make a final Taint assessment after returning. Taint Points accumulated during the encounter are permanent. A successful mission that purified the focal point might still leave the participating bushi with Taint Rank 1. The victory has a personal cost. Lords who repeatedly send the same characters into Tainted provinces risk those characters progressing through the Taint Ranks over multiple missions. Jade Petal Tea and shugenja monitoring become necessary between missions.

**Unique Consequence --- Salvageable Tainted Humans:** If the focal point is purified, Tainted humans at Rank 1-2 in the area stop accumulating new Taint. They can potentially recover through shugenja treatment, jade exposure, and time. Identifying and saving these people is a post-mission humanitarian task --- not combat content but World Map governance content. A lord who purifies the land and then treats the afflicted gains significant disposition from the surviving population.

**Regeneration:** If PTL remains above 3 after the focal point is purified, a new Manifestation can spawn when PTL increases again. The land is still sick --- just less sick. Full recovery requires driving PTL below 3 through sustained purification campaigns (multiple shugenja AP over multiple seasons). A single successful mission is one step in a longer war against Jigoku’s influence.

### 56.10.15 Urban Criminal Network — Hideout Raid — LOCKED

The Urban Criminal Network insurgency type (Section 11.11) generates ASCII map hideout raids when a magistrate-led suppression action reaches the raid phase. The raid uses the Urban Hideout template (Section 56.15) with criminal-specific modifications. The mission operates across three layers: Investigation (MUD/existing systems), Raid (ASCII), and Post-Raid Consequences (World Map).

**Investigation Phase (MUD / World Map)**

Before the raid generates, the player must locate the hideout. Two investigation paths are available, and the player may use either or both. MUD Investigation: abstract and AP-driven. The Investigation Loop (Section 11.3.13) drives detection — zone_event_log entries, informants, PROBE actions, BRIBE_FOR_INFO. Efficient but produces only categorical intelligence results. ASCII Investigation: the player enters the settlement district on the ASCII map and investigates spatially. Walk the streets, use the Social Menu to PROBE merchants about unusual deliveries, READ_CHARACTER on nervous-looking patrons, CHARM a teahouse owner for gossip, use Investigation (Notice) / Perception to spot suspicious activity — a building with heavy foot traffic at night, a warehouse where the workers look more like fighters, a lookout loitering on a corner. Follow leads physically — someone mentions strange noises under the gambling den on the east side, so walk there and observe. ASCII investigation is slower but richer, and the player might stumble onto the entrance directly. Risk: if the network’s lookouts spot a magistrate asking questions on the ASCII map, the Alert system (Section 56.6.3) fires. The boss gets warned. Evidence may be destroyed. The hideout might relocate before the raid can launch. The quality of pre-raid intelligence from either path determines how much the player knows going in. No intelligence: the player knows only that a criminal network exists in this settlement. Partial intelligence: the player knows the business front (which building) and approximate network Strength. Full intelligence: the player knows the layout tier (Single Basement / Connected Basements / Catacombs), the number of enforcers, whether the boss is present, and the location of escape tunnels. Pre-raid intelligence is not required to launch a raid — a magistrate can kick in every suspicious door in the district. But going in blind means civilians everywhere, no idea which building hides the entrance, and the boss has maximum time to flee.

**The Raid (ASCII Map)**

Map size scales with network Strength using the Urban Hideout template’s three size tiers (Section 56.15.1). Strength 1–3: Single Basement — back room of a teahouse, cellar under a shop. Compact map. Strength 4–6: Connected Basements — tunnels linking cellars of multiple buildings. Warehouse with hidden storage. Mid-size map. Strength 7+: Catacombs — multi-building compound, extensive underground network, multiple escape routes. Full-size map. The map generates with two zones: the surface level (the legitimate business and surrounding district) and the hidden level (the criminal operation beneath or behind). The surface zone contains civilians. The hidden zone contains criminals, contraband, evidence, and the boss.

Business Front Variants: The surface-level business is generated based on the network’s primary criminal activity. Gambling den — open room with patrons, dice tables, sake. Legitimate-looking from the front. Back room leads down. Opium house — dimly lit rooms, sleeping patrons, heavy incense masking smells from below. Multiple small rooms make searching slow. Warehouse — large open storage space, loading dock entrance, crates and barrels providing cover. Concealed passage behind stacked goods. Teahouse — public dining area, private rooms in the back, cellar access through the kitchen. High civilian density. Merchant shop — store front with goods on display, living quarters above, hidden basement access. The business front is functioning — real customers, real staff, real commerce happening alongside the criminal operation.

Hidden Level Room Types: Counting room — Koku stores, ledgers, the financial heart of the operation. Always present. Evidence spawns here. Storage — contraband goods (opium, stolen merchandise, gaijin pepper, smuggled weapons). Scales with Strength. Barracks — sleeping quarters for enforcers who live on-site. Present at Strength 4+. Boss quarters — the network leader’s private room. Better furnished than everything else. Always present. Evidence spawns here. Meeting room — where the boss meets contacts. Present at Strength 4+. Escape tunnel — concealed exit leading to another part of the settlement. Always present. The boss uses this to flee. At Strength 7+, two escape tunnels exist.

**Raid Party Composition**

NPC Resolution: When an NPC magistrate leads a raid, the entire operation resolves through the suppression formula in Section 11.11 Phase 5. The magistrate’s yoriki and doshin contribute to the roll. No ASCII map generates. This is the standard Section 9 resolution path for NPCs.

Player Resolution: When a player character leads or participates in a raid, the ASCII map generates and the player selects a raid party before entering. Yoriki — named characters assigned to the player via operational_superior_id (Section 11.3.18). They appear on the ASCII map using their full character sheet stats. They are AI-controlled allies who fight, investigate, and make decisions based on their personality (Section 19). A competent yoriki is a genuine asset — another sword, another pair of eyes, someone who can hold a corridor while the player searches rooms or block an escape tunnel while the player pursues the boss. Doshin — recruited from the local settlement garrison (Section 11.3.19). They appear using the doshin stat block defined above. Doshin follow simple orders: hold this position, guard this exit, follow me. They do not make independent tactical decisions. They are peasant law enforcement, not samurai — useful for blocking escape routes and watching exits, not for leading the assault.

The player decides who comes. Before launching the raid, the player selects from their assigned yoriki and the settlement’s available doshin pool. Trade-offs exist: more bodies means better coverage of escape routes and faster room clearing, but also more noise (Section 56.6.3), more chaos in tight spaces, and higher risk of civilian casualties from AI allies who cannot distinguish lookouts from customers as reliably as the player. A solo raid is quiet and precise but leaves escape tunnels unguarded. A full raid team locks down the building but announces itself the moment it enters. Doshin ASCII map behavior (following orders, simple command interface) is designed in Section 57.46 (Allied NPC Companion System).

**Civilian Presence**

The surface level contains civilian NPCs — customers, staff, passersby. They are not enemies. They are not combatants. They are heimin going about their lives in a business they may or may not know is a criminal front. Civilians and criminals are not immediately distinguishable on the surface level. The Look command provides the first filter — selecting a figure reveals visible tells: armed vs unarmed, watching the street vs shopping vs drinking, calloused hands vs soft ones, nervous glances toward the back room. Suspicious-looking NPCs can then be confirmed with an Investigation (Notice) / Perception roll (TN 15 for a thug pretending to be a customer, TN 20 for a professional lookout) or READ_CHARACTER through the Social Menu. Without this identification step, the player cannot reliably distinguish a criminal from a bystander. Attacking a civilian triggers an immediate Crime Record (Section 11.3.14e) for Violence Against Persons (Section 11.3.12). The crime is “unsanctioned killing of a heimin” — minor by samurai legal standards but politically damaging. Multiple civilian casualties generate a court topic and disposition damage with the settlement’s lord. Civilians flee when combat begins — they run for the nearest exit. They do not fight back. They generate Loud noise (screaming) that alerts the hidden level.

**Roster Composition by Strength**

Criminal NPCs use the bandit archetype stat blocks from Section 54.8 (Bandit Rabble, Bandit Thug) supplemented by ronin archetypes. The roster scales with network Strength. Strength 1–2: 3–6 enemies. Mostly Bandit Thugs with knives and clubs. One ronin enforcer at Strength 2. No dedicated lookouts. The boss is a Bandit Thug with better stats and a weapon. Strength 3–4: 8–12 enemies. Mix of Bandit Thugs and 2–3 ronin enforcers. One or two lookouts on the surface level (Unaware state, expanded vision radius of Perception + 2 tiles, positioned at entrances and street corners). The boss is a ronin or experienced criminal with Insight Rank 2. Strength 5–6: 14–20 enemies. Bandit Thugs, 3–5 ronin enforcers, 2–3 lookouts, possibly one corrupted doshin (Section 11.3.19) acting as inside man. Doshin individual stat block: Air 2 | Earth 2 | Fire 2 | Water 2 | Void 1 | Agility 3, Perception 3. Insight Rank: 1. Skills: Investigation 2, Knives 2, Defense 1, Athletics 2, Intimidation 1. Equipment: jitte (uses Knives skill — a small iron truncheon designed to catch sword blades and subdue suspects), light armor. A corrupted doshin fights like an Ashigaru with better awareness — dangerous as an informant, not as a combatant. The boss is a named NPC with Insight Rank 2–3 and personal bodyguards. Strength 7+: 20–30 enemies. Full criminal organisation. Thugs, ronin, lookouts, corrupted officials, specialist enforcers. Multiple named NPCs in the command structure. The boss has an entourage and the escape tunnel is guarded.

**Enemy Behavior**

Criminals are not fanatics. Their behavior is fundamentally different from maho cultists, Shadowlands creatures, or samurai soldiers. Bandit Rabble and Thugs: flee when they see a losing fight. If the player cuts down two enforcers in the first room, the thugs in the next room hear the combat (Loud noise per Section 56.6.3) and many of them run. Fleeing criminals drop weapons and head for exits. They do not fight to protect the boss — they fight to protect themselves. Ronin Enforcers: professional mercenaries. They evaluate the situation. If the player is clearly more dangerous than what they are being paid for, they may surrender or flee. If the odds are reasonable, they fight competently. They use cover, fall back through doorways, and coordinate. A ronin who sees the boss flee may decide employment has ended. Lookouts: their job is to alert the hidden level and then flee. They do not fight. Lookouts have expanded vision radius (Perception + 2 tiles compared to normal enemies) and make active detection rolls every round rather than passive. If a lookout spots the player approaching and is not immediately killed, the entire hidden level transitions to Alert (Section 56.6.3). The Network Boss: the boss’s primary instinct is escape, not combat. When the boss becomes aware of the raid (Alert status reaches them), they grab essential items from their quarters and move toward the escape tunnel. The boss fights only if cornered — if the escape tunnel is blocked or they are intercepted. A boss who reaches the escape tunnel exits the map. The network loses Strength from the raid but the boss survives and can rebuild. Killing or capturing the boss is the difference between crippling the network and merely damaging it.

**Evidence as Secondary Objective**

Physical evidence spawns in the counting room and boss quarters. Ledgers, correspondence, cargo manifests, names of corrupted officials, trade route contacts, and records of operations in other settlements. Evidence is collected by using EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (Simple Action) on designated evidence tiles. Note: EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE Annex D skill map entry is in Section 57.15.1. Each evidence item collected produces one secret (Section 12.8) or one piece of physical proof linking the network to a named NPC. A character who fights through the hideout killing everyone but never searches rooms gets the Strength reduction from clearing the hideout but misses the intelligence. A character who methodically searches each room before advancing finds documents that feed back into the court and political layer — potentially more valuable than the raid itself. The tension is time: the longer the player spends searching, the more time the boss has to escape through the tunnel. This creates a genuine tactical choice — pursue the boss or secure the evidence.

**Post-Raid Consequences (World Map)**

The raid outcome feeds back into the Urban Criminal Network insurgency (Section 11.11). Hideout cleared, boss killed or captured: maximum Strength reduction. Network drops by 60–80% of current Strength (PROVISIONAL — values pending playtest). If reduced to 0, the insurgency is eliminated. Boss captured alive produces additional intelligence (interrogation per Investigation Loop). Hideout cleared, boss escaped: moderate Strength reduction. Network drops by 30–50% (PROVISIONAL). The boss survives and begins rebuilding from a new location in the settlement. The network may spawn a new hideout within 1–2 seasons. Hideout cleared with evidence: each evidence item collected provides one actionable secret or physical proof. Corrupted official evidence triggers the corruption investigation system (Section 4.3.7 Audit Roll). Trade route contact evidence reveals network connections to other settlements. Named NPC links feed into the court and political layer as topics (Section 16). Civilian casualties during raid: each civilian killed generates a Crime Record (Section 11.3.14e). Three or more civilian deaths generate a court topic with negative framing for the raiding character. The lord of the settlement suffers −5 disposition toward the magistrate who ordered the raid. Collateral damage is politically expensive even when legally justified.

### 56.10.13 Maho Cult to Taint Manifestation Aftermath Chain --- LOCKED

A suppressed Maho Cult leaves PTL damage. Every season the cult was active added +1 PTL (Section 11.11). Even after suppression, the PTL remains elevated. If PTL is at 3 or above after the cult is destroyed, a Province Taint Manifestation can spawn from the spiritual scar the cult left behind. The cultists are gone. The corruption they created is not. This is an emergent narrative sequence: the player suppresses the Maho Cult (quest seed 1), the lord celebrates, and seasons later the land starts producing Tainted animals and spontaneous undead (quest seed 2) because nobody sent a shugenja to purify what the cult did to the province. The two quest seeds are mechanically independent --- the Taint Manifestation does not know or care that a Maho Cult caused the PTL rise. It responds to PTL alone. But narratively they form a connected story arc that rewards players and lords who think beyond the immediate threat.

### 56.10.14 Gaki-do Encounters --- LOCKED

Gaki-do Encounters — Quest Seed — LOCKED

Gaki (hungry dead from Gaki-do, Section 2.6.2) are not Shadowlands creatures and are not part of the Province Taint Manifestation seed. They originate from an entirely separate spirit realm governed by craving, not corruption. This quest seed connects the world simulation to the fully designed Gaki-do encounter mechanics in Section 56.16.6.

Trigger Thresholds: The simulation generates a Gaki-do encounter when any of the following conditions are met. Famine: a province has been in Starvation (Section 4.3.6 Starvation Cascade) for 2 or more consecutive seasons. Starvation draws hungry dead — mass human suffering resonates across the boundary between Ningen-do and Gaki-do. Mass Battle Aftermath: a province hosted a battle with 500+ total casualties (combined both sides) within the last season. The violent deaths of so many souls, many with unfinished business, weakens the barrier. Worship Failure: 2 or more Great Fortunes at Displeased or worse in the province (Section 4.3.21 Failure Cascade). Spiritual neglect creates gaps in the Celestial Order. Obsessive Death Site: a named NPC with the Greedy, Compulsion, or Addiction disadvantage dies in the province. Their obsessive craving anchors a fragment of Gaki-do to the location. This is the rarest trigger but the most narratively specific. Multiple triggers stack — a famine province that also hosted a battle and has worship failures produces a stronger manifestation (higher creature density, faster Willpower erosion per Section 56.16.6a).

Map Template Selection: Gaki-do encounters do not use a single fixed template. The map generates from the location where the overlap manifests, using the existing template that matches the site. Village template (Section 56.4) if the overlap centres on a settlement. Open terrain template if at a battlefield site. Urban zone template if at a pleasure district or market. The Gaki-do visual overlay (Section 56.6.1 spirit realm interaction — colour drains, vegetation wilts, grey washed-out palette) applies on top of whatever base template generates. The encounter zone structure (outer, middle, heart) from Section 56.16.6c layers onto the map regardless of template.

Roster: Uses the Gaki-do Roster (Section 56.16.6b) in full. Creature density scales with the number of active triggers — one trigger produces a sparse encounter (mostly Muzai-gaki clusters with 1–2 mid-tier combatants), two triggers produce a standard encounter (full range of creatures), three or more triggers produce a dense encounter (heavy Willpower pressure, multiple Shozai-gaki, possible Gashadokuro as a boss-tier threat).

Resolution and Consequences: The encounter is resolved when the player reaches the heart of the overlap and performs the banishment ritual (Theology / Void or Spellcraft / Void per Section 56.16.6c). Success: the overlap dissipates. Gaki-do influence in the province drops. +0.2 Honor, +0.1 Glory for the party leader. If the underlying trigger conditions are still active (famine continues, worship still failing), the encounter may regenerate next season — you banished the symptoms, not the cause. Addressing the root cause (ending the famine, restoring worship, honouring the dead from the battle) prevents recurrence. Failure (party retreats or is driven out): the overlap persists and may intensify next season. Province suffers −5 Stability. Peasant unrest increases. The province gains a reputation as haunted — immigration drops, trade caravans avoid the area (Koku drain similar to the Ronin Bandit Uprising trade disruption).

Key Design Distinction: Gaki-do encounters are not combat problems with spiritual dressing. They are spiritual problems where combat is a secondary concern. The real threat is Willpower erosion (Section 56.16.6a) and the post-mission Buruburu haunting (Section 56.16.6e). A pure bushi team can fight through the creatures but will be spiritually battered. A team with a shugenja or monk resolves the encounter more cleanly through ritual and prayer. The ideal approach is addressing the worldly conditions that attracted the Gaki in the first place — ending the famine, performing funeral rites for battle dead, restoring worship. Jade is useless here. Gaki-do is not Jigoku. The Celestial Order sanctions this realm.
