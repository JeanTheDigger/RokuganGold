# 49. Artisan & Crafting System

This section defines how characters create objects of art and practical craft in Rokugan, how those objects are tracked, and how their histories accumulate meaning over time. The system is divided into two parallel tracks: the Artisan track covering fine arts and prestige items, and the Craft track covering practical production and functional objects. Both tracks share the same underlying mechanics — quality tiers, provenance records, and history accumulation — but feed into different game systems. Artisan output feeds into the social and court layer. Craft output feeds into the military and economic layer.

**THE TWO TRACKS**

- Artisan Track: Fine arts of Rokugani society. Bonsai, Ikebana, Origami, Painting, Poetry, Sculpture, Tattooing, and other noble art forms. Uses the Artisan (Awareness) skill. Output is prestige items — objects of beauty, cultural significance, and social weight. Directly relevant to gift-giving, court performance, and disposition systems.

- Craft Track: Practical production. Armorsmithing, Weaponsmithing, Blacksmithing, Bowyer, Carpentry, Brewing, Cooking, Masonry, Shipbuilding, Tailoring, and others. Uses the Craft (Varies) skill — the relevant Trait varies by sub-skill. Output is functional items — weapons, armor, tools, structures, consumables. Directly relevant to Arms production, equipment, and the World Map resource system.

Any character with the relevant Artisan or Craft skill may attempt to create an item. There is no restriction on who may craft — a bushi who has trained in Painting may produce a work of art, a courtier with Weaponsmithing may forge a blade. Skill rank determines quality. The crafter's reputation, school background, and social standing affect how the world perceives the item but do not prevent the attempt.

**THE CRAFTING ROLL**

To create an item, the character makes a skill roll using the appropriate Artisan or Craft sub-skill paired with its governing Trait. The roll result is compared against the quality tier thresholds to determine the item's initial quality. The roll represents the character's full effort — the time, focus, and skill invested in the work.

Crafting consumes Action Points. Creating an item of any significance takes at least one AP slot. Complex or ambitious works may require multiple AP across multiple IC days — this is a design decision left to the GM and game systems based on the nature of the item. A poem written in a single sitting costs 1 AP. A suit of armor takes many days of work.

**CRAFTING TIME**

The time required to craft an item is determined by two factors: the material being worked and the item's market cost. Time is calculated in units — Hours, Days, or Weeks — based on material type, then multiplied by a divisor based on the item's cost denomination.

Time unit by material:

- Steel: Bu-denominated items = Days per unit. Koku-denominated items = Weeks per unit. Zeni-denominated steel items are too cheap to exist meaningfully.

- Metal or Glass: All denominations = Days per unit.

- Other materials (wood, cloth, paper, bone, clay, etc.): Zeni and Bu = Hours per unit. Koku = Days per unit.

Number of time units based on item cost:

- Zeni-priced items: cost divided by 10, minimum 1 unit.

- Bu-priced items: cost divided by 5, minimum 1 unit.

- Koku-priced items: cost divided by 3, minimum 1 unit.

Converting time units to Action Points (2 AP per IC day):

- Hours: 1 AP (represents a half-day of focused work).

- Days: 2 AP (one full IC day of crafting).

- Weeks: 14 AP (seven IC days of sustained work).

Example — Katana (25 koku, steel): Time unit = Weeks. Units = 25 ÷ 3 = 8 weeks (rounded up). Total AP = 8 × 14 = 112 AP, spread across 56 IC days (~14 OOC days) of dedicated work. A master smith forging a katana is a genuine commitment.

Example — Kemari ball (a few zeni, other material): Time unit = Hours. Units = 1. Total AP = 1. A simple cloth and stone ball takes half a day.

Crafting AP does not need to be spent consecutively. A smith may work on a weapon across many IC days, spending AP on crafting whenever their schedule allows. The work accumulates. The roll is made only when the full AP cost has been invested.

**CRAFTING DIFFICULTY — COST-BASED TN**

The base TN for any crafting roll is determined by the item's market cost. This replaces the need for a GM to arbitrarily assign difficulty — the item's price in the economy reflects its complexity and the skill required to produce it. Every +5 above the final listed cost bracket increases TN by an additional 5.

Base TN by cost (Zeni / Bu / Koku columns):

- Cost 1–10 zeni / 1–10 bu / 1–10 koku: TN 10 / TN 15 / TN 20.

- Cost 11–25 zeni / 11–20 bu / 11–20 koku: TN 15 / TN 20 / TN 25.

- Cost 26–50 zeni / 21–50 bu / 21–25 koku: TN 20 / TN 25 / TN 30.

- Cost 50+ zeni / 50+ bu / 25+ koku: TN 25 / TN 30+ / TN 35.

- Every additional 5 above the final bracket adds +5 TN.

This cost-based TN is the base crafting difficulty. It determines whether the item is produced at all (meeting the TN = Normal quality) and serves as the foundation for higher quality tiers. To achieve Fine quality, the crafter must exceed the base TN by 10 (effectively TN+10). Exceptional requires TN+20, Masterwork TN+30, Legendary TN+40. Alternatively — and more elegantly — the crafter rolls against the base TN and uses Raises to push quality higher, one quality tier per 2 Raises above the base.

**EXCEPTIONAL WEAPONS — FORGING RULES**

Standard crafting produces weapons of Normal (Average) quality. To forge an Exceptional weapon — one that possesses Special Qualities — the crafter follows modified rules drawn from the L5R 4th Edition alternate weapon-forging system.

Requirements for Exceptional weapon crafting:

- The crafter must have Craft: Weaponsmithing at rank 7 or higher. Exception: Characters from the Kaiu or Tsi school families may attempt Exceptional weapons at rank 5.

- The cost of materials and the koku value of the weapon are both tripled for the purpose of calculating base TN. A katana normally worth 25 koku is treated as a 75 koku item for TN purposes when forging Exceptional.

- A failed roll when attempting an Exceptional weapon ruins the item entirely. The materials are wasted. The smith must begin again from scratch. There is no partial success — the weapon is wholly useless.

Special Qualities are conferred through Raises on the crafting roll. Each Special Quality has a Raise cost. A smith who meets the base TN and has Raises remaining may spend those Raises to confer one or more Special Qualities on the weapon. Characters trained in Kaiu or Tsi school traditions receive one Free Raise for the purpose of conferring Special Qualities.

Weapon Special Qualities and their Raise costs:

- Balanced (4 Raises): The wielder gains +1k0 on all attack rolls made with this weapon.

- Signature (2 Raises): The weapon bears a unique stamp identifying its creator. Automatically adds the creator to the weapon's provenance record with full detail. Relevant to all future provenance investigation rolls.

- Swift (4 Raises): The wielder gains +5 to their Initiative score while wielding this weapon.

- True (6 Raises): This weapon decreases the target's Reduction by the wielder's Strength on every hit.

- Radiant (6 Raises): This weapon counts as Jade for the purpose of attacking Invulnerable targets.

- Unbreakable (5 Raises): This weapon cannot be broken by any means.

A single weapon may carry multiple Special Qualities if the smith has sufficient Raises. A smith rolling exceptionally well on a Masterwork-level attempt may confer two or three qualities on a single blade — a feat that will be remembered and will add immediately to the weapon's provenance record as a notable creation event.

**SACRED WEAPONS — LEGENDARY CRAFTING**

Sacred Weapons are the pinnacle of Rokugani smithing — weapons imbued with spiritual power tied to a specific clan's traditions and identity. They represent our Legendary quality tier and are among the most politically and spiritually significant objects that can exist in the game world.

Requirements for Sacred Weapon crafting:

- The smith must first be crafting an Exceptional (Excellent) weapon — Sacred Weapon status is an elevation of Exceptional crafting, not a separate process.

- The smith must achieve 7 Raises on the crafting roll. Kaiu smiths require only 6 Raises due to their unmatched tradition.

- Sacred Weapons are clan-locked. A smith may only attempt to create the Sacred Weapon type of their own clan. A Crane smith may only forge Kakita Blades. A Crab smith may only forge Kaiu Blades. Attempting to create another clan's Sacred Weapon type is both mechanically impossible and a profound cultural violation.

Sacred Weapons automatically achieve Legendary quality tier in our system — they carry the maximum base Free Raises (+4) and begin accumulating History Points immediately upon creation, as their very existence is a notable event. A newly forged Sacred Weapon is already a Tier 4 topic.

**CRAFTING CATEGORIES**

All crafting falls into one of five categories, each using a specific skill:

- Armor: Uses Craft: Armorsmithing to produce ashigaru armor, light armor, heavy armor, riding armor, or any military armor type. Steel material rules apply.

- Artwork: Uses Artisan skills (Painting, Sculpture, Origami, Ikebana, etc.) to produce prestige items. Other material rules typically apply. Output feeds into the social and court layer.

- Engineering: Uses the Engineering skill to design structures or large-scale devices such as siege engines. Special rules apply — see Section 11.7.

- Equipment: Uses the appropriate Craft sub-skill to produce any item not covered by the other categories. Material rules vary by item.

- Weapons: Uses Craft: Weaponsmithing (blades, pole arms) or Craft: Bowyer (bows, arrows). The most prestigious crafting category. Steel material rules apply. Subject to Exceptional and Sacred Weapon rules above.

**QUALITY TIERS**

The roll result determines the item's quality tier at creation. This tier is permanent — an item's craftsmanship quality does not change after creation. Quality determines the base Free Raises the item provides when used in gift-giving or court performance rolls.

- Mundane (below TN 15): Failed craftsmanship. The item is functional but unremarkable. It has no provenance value and grants no Free Raises. It will not be remembered.

- Normal (TN 15): Standard quality. Competent, serviceable work. Grants 0 Free Raises. The baseline of professional output.

- Fine (TN 25): Quality work with noticeable craftsmanship. Grants +1 Free Raise. Worth presenting as a gift. Will be recognized by those with relevant expertise.

- Exceptional (TN 35): Outstanding work. Clearly the product of a skilled hand. Grants +2 Free Raises. Commands respect and attention. A character who produces Exceptional work regularly earns a reputation.

- Masterwork (TN 45): Near-perfect execution. Grants +3 Free Raises. Will be remembered as a significant work. Masterwork items are rare — most skilled craftsmen produce one or two in a lifetime.

- Legendary (TN 55): Once-in-a-generation craftsmanship. Grants +4 Free Raises. The item is immediately significant upon creation. Legendary works accumulate history faster than lesser items — their stories attract stories.

**MATERIALS**

Every crafted item requires raw materials. Materials are purchased from merchants and markets — characters do not gather materials themselves. The quality of materials used directly affects the crafting roll through Free Raises, and the finest materials are noted permanently in the item's provenance record.

**Material Tiers**

Materials are divided into four tiers. Each tier above Common provides Free Raises on the crafting roll. Free Raises from materials apply to the roll itself — they count toward both meeting the base TN and toward purchasing Special Qualities on Exceptional weapons.

- Common (0 Free Raises): The baseline of crafting. Wood, cloth, iron, basic leather, clay, paper, bone. Available at any settlement with a market. Standard market price.

- Uncommon (1 Free Raise): Noticeably superior materials. Fine steel, quality leather, rare dyes, polished glass, exotic wood. Available at towns and larger. Costs approximately twice the standard price.

- Rare (2 Free Raises): Exceptional materials with recognized prestige. Kaiu steel, sacred wood, gem-quality stone, Phoenix-blessed paper, Dragon jade dust. Available at cities and clan capitals, limited stock. Costs approximately five times standard price. Using Rare materials is noted in the item's provenance record.

- Legendary (3 Free Raises): Materials of spiritual or historical significance. Jade, obsidian, spirit-touched materials, meteoric iron, First Family relics. Available only at specific locations. Costs ten times or more standard price and may require political access. Always noted in provenance. The use of Legendary materials makes an item socially significant upon creation.

**Material Availability by Settlement**

- Village: Common materials only.

- Town: Common and Uncommon materials.

- City: Common, Uncommon, and limited Rare materials.

- Clan Capital: Common, Uncommon, Rare, and clan-specific Legendary materials.

- Specific sacred or geographic sites: Location-specific Legendary materials only.

**Clan-Specific Materials**

Certain materials are produced only within specific clan territories and are difficult or impossible to acquire elsewhere.

- Crab — Kaiu Steel (Rare): Produced exclusively in Kaiu forges. The finest functional steel in Rokugan. Required for Kaiu Blade Sacred Weapons.

- Crane — Kakita Paper (Uncommon): Fine paper from Crane provinces, prized for calligraphy and artwork.

- Dragon — Jade Dust (Rare): Processed from Dragon mountain jade deposits. Used in spiritual crafting and ritual items.

- Lion — Matsu Leather (Uncommon): High-quality leather from Matsu war-beast husbandry. Superior armor material.

- Phoenix — Blessed Paper and Ink (Rare): Paper and ink consecrated through Phoenix temple ritual. Used in scroll crafting and high-level calligraphy.

- Scorpion — Shadow-silk (Rare): A uniquely processed silk. Lightweight, strong, and difficult to trace.

- Unicorn — Gaijin Dyes (Uncommon): Imported dyes from beyond Rokugan's borders via Unicorn trade routes.

- Mantis — Deep-sea Materials (Rare): Coral, deep-water shells, sea-beast bone. Available only at Mantis ports.

- Any Clan — Jade (Legendary): Available through Imperial stockpiles and specific mine locations.

- Any Clan — Obsidian (Legendary): Available at specific volcanic locations. Spiritually potent and associated with the Shadowlands.

**MATERIAL SOURCING ****&**** MARKET SUPPLY**

Materials available for purchase at any given market are not abstract inventory — they come from specific places in the world and travel to the market through the same trade route system that governs Rice and Koku movement. A city's material availability is a live reflection of its economic connections, political relationships, and geographic position. This means that disrupting trade routes, starting wars, or letting banditry flourish has direct consequences for what crafters can obtain and where.

**Material Origins — Local Production**

Every material type has one or more source provinces where it is produced. Local production means the material is generated within the province itself — by forges, farms, forests, mines, or craftsmen — and is available at that province's markets without needing to travel anywhere. The material's provenance record begins here, noting the province of origin.

Examples of locally produced materials:

- Kaiu Steel is produced only in Crab provinces containing Kaiu forges. It is available at Crab markets without travel. Outside Crab lands, it must arrive via trade.

- Kakita Paper is produced in Crane provinces. Available locally in Crane markets.

- Common iron and wood are produced in any province with appropriate terrain — forests, mines, farmland. Available almost everywhere.

- Jade is produced only at specific Imperial-controlled mine locations. Even clan capitals must receive it via supply chains.

**Material Movement Along Trade Routes**

Materials move between settlements automatically along established trade routes — the same routes that carry Rice and Koku through the World Map. However, material movement is probabilistic rather than guaranteed. Each IC day, every material type in transit along a route has a chance of advancing one step toward its destination. The chance varies by material tier — common materials flow freely, rare materials move slowly and unpredictably.

Daily movement chance by material tier:

- Common (wood, cloth, iron, basic leather, clay, paper, bone): 50% chance per IC day of advancing along the route.

- Uncommon (fine steel, quality leather, rare dyes, polished glass, exotic wood): 25% chance per IC day.

- Rare (Kaiu steel, sacred wood, gem-quality stone, Phoenix-blessed paper): 10% chance per IC day.

- Legendary (jade, obsidian, spirit-touched materials, meteoric iron): 2% chance per IC day.

This means Common materials flow steadily and reliably — a settlement connected to a source province by trade will usually have Common materials in stock. Uncommon materials arrive less predictably. Rare materials may take many IC days to reach a distant market even along an established route. Legendary materials are genuinely difficult to acquire — even with an active trade connection to their source, weeks may pass between availabilities.

Delivery time from source to market also follows the World Map distance system — the same delay that governs letter delivery and army movement. A material in transit from a distant province takes longer to arrive than one from a neighboring province, with probabilities compounding across each leg of the journey.

**Market Stock Visibility**

Players and characters do not see raw stock numbers at a market. The market interface displays one of three availability states for each material type:

- Available: The market currently has stock. The material can be purchased at standard price.

- Limited: Stock is low. The material is available but may not be for long. Price may be elevated.

- Unavailable: No current stock. The material has not arrived or has been depleted. Cannot be purchased until supply is restored.

These states are calculated from hidden stock levels that the game tracks internally. The player knows what they can buy today — they do not know exactly how much is in the warehouse or when the next shipment will arrive. A crafter planning a major project must account for supply uncertainty, particularly for Rare and Legendary materials. Arriving at a city expecting to purchase Kaiu Steel and finding it unavailable is a real planning risk.

**Trade Route Disruption and Material Supply**

An active trade route is the prerequisite for material flow. If a route is disrupted — by war, banditry, siege, political embargo, or Shadowlands activity — materials stop moving along that route immediately. Settlements downstream of the disruption stop receiving supply of any materials that traveled that route.

Consequences of route disruption:

- Common materials deplete within a few IC days at busy markets. Smaller settlements may run out within a day.

- Uncommon materials deplete more slowly but eventually exhaust.

- Rare and Legendary materials, already infrequent, may simply never arrive for the duration of the disruption.

- When the route is restored, supply resumes at the normal probabilistic rate. There is no catch-up mechanism — materials do not flood in to compensate for the gap. Recovery is gradual.

This makes trade route control a meaningful strategic consideration beyond its Rice and Koku implications. A lord who controls the only route carrying Kaiu Steel into a region can deny that material to rival crafters. Cutting the Crane trade routes does not just reduce Koku flow — it drains Kakita Paper from every downstream market, affecting calligraphy, court correspondence, and artisan output across the region.

**Material Provenance and the Supply Chain**

Because materials travel from specific source provinces along specific routes, the provenance system can record not just that a material is Rare or Legendary, but where it came from and how it got here. A character with sufficient investigation skill examining a finished item may be able to trace its materials back to their province of origin — identifying that the jade in a ring came from Imperial mine X, or that the steel in a sword was forged in a specific Kaiu facility.

This supply chain provenance has social and political implications. An item crafted from Kaiu Steel in Lion territory raises questions — where did the smith get it? Was it purchased legitimately, obtained through a Crab ally, smuggled, or taken in battle? The material's geographic origin, combined with its presence in an unexpected location, is itself a Tier 4 Political topic worth investigating.

**Materials and Provenance**

Common and Uncommon materials are noted in the provenance record but carry no special social weight. Rare materials are flagged — a character investigating the item will learn what Rare materials were used and where they likely came from. Legendary materials are always prominently noted and immediately socially significant. An item crafted from jade or meteoric iron is recognized as exceptional before anyone examines its craftsmanship quality.

**THE COMPLETE CRAFTING PROCESS**

Bringing together all elements of Section 49, the complete crafting process proceeds as follows:

- Step 1 — Declare Intent: The crafter declares what they are making, what category it falls under (Armor, Artwork, Engineering, Equipment, or Weapons), and what quality they are attempting.

- Step 2 — Acquire Materials: Purchase materials from an appropriate market. Material tier determines Free Raises on the crafting roll.

- Step 3 — Determine TN and Time: Look up the item's market cost on the cost-based TN table. Calculate crafting time based on material type and cost denomination. For Exceptional weapons, triple the cost for TN purposes.

- Step 4 — Invest AP: Spend the required AP across IC days until the full crafting time is met. AP does not need to be consecutive — the work accumulates.

- Step 5 — Make the Roll: Roll the appropriate skill + trait against the base TN, applying Free Raises from materials. Raises above the base TN may push quality higher or purchase Special Qualities (weapons only, Exceptional tier or above).

- Step 6 — Determine Quality: Compare the roll result to quality tier thresholds. For failed Exceptional weapon attempts the item is ruined entirely and materials are lost.

- Step 7 — Record Provenance: Establish the item's provenance record: creator, creation date, quality tier, materials used, subject for Artisan items. Rare and Legendary materials are flagged. Signature Special Quality embeds the creator's full details permanently.

**WORKED EXAMPLE — KAIU SMITH FORGING AN EXCEPTIONAL KATANA**

A Kaiu Weaponsmith (Craft: Weaponsmithing 7, Agility 4) forges an Exceptional katana using Kaiu Steel.

- Step 1: Category = Weapons. Target = Exceptional. Attempting Exceptional weapon rules.

- Step 2: Kaiu Steel (Rare) purchased at a Crab city. Grants +2 Free Raises.

- Step 3: Katana base = 25 koku. Tripled for Exceptional = 75 koku. TN = 35 + 25 (for every 5 above 50 koku) = TN 60. Material = steel, koku-priced = Weeks. 75 divided by 3 = 25 weeks = 350 AP.

- Step 4: Smith invests 350 AP across 175 IC days — roughly 44 OOC days of sustained work.

- Step 5: Rolls 11k4 against TN 60. Applies 2 Free Raises from Kaiu Steel. Kaiu school grants 1 additional Free Raise for Special Qualities. Succeeds with 3 Raises remaining — spends them on Balanced (4 Raises total with school bonus) and Signature (2 Raises).

- Step 6: Roll met TN 60 — Exceptional quality. Balanced and Signature applied.

- Step 7: Provenance: Created by [smith name], Kaiu family, Crab Clan, [date]. Quality: Exceptional. Material: Kaiu Steel (Rare). Special Qualities: Balanced, Signature. The Signature quality permanently embeds the smith's identity into the blade.

**PROVENANCE — THE ITEM RECORD**

Every item created by a named character carries a provenance record — a permanent, tracked history of the item's origins and life. This record is part of the game world's data. It is not automatically visible to all characters — it must be discovered through play.

At creation, the provenance record is established with the following information:

- Creator: The named character who made the item, their Clan, Family, School, and Insight Rank at the time of creation.

- Creation date: The in-game date of completion.

- Quality tier: The result of the crafting roll.

- Materials: For Craft items — what materials were used, where they came from, who provided them. A sword notes the ore source, the smith who worked the metal, the artisan who wrapped the hilt. A ring notes where the gem was found, who cut the stone, who shaped the metal. For Artisan items — the medium used, the occasion it was made for, what is depicted or expressed.

- Subject: For paintings, poems, sculptures — what scene, person, or event is depicted. This becomes searchable. A painting of the Battle of the Cresting Wave is linked to that historical event in the world's records.

**HISTORY ACCUMULATION**

After creation, an item's history grows as it moves through the world. Every significant event involving the item adds to its provenance record and increases its History Point total. History Points translate into additional Free Raises on top of the item's base quality — an ordinary Fine sword that has passed through legendary hands becomes extraordinary.

Events that add History Points:

- Owned by a named character of Insight Rank 3 or higher: +1 History Point per owner.

- Owned by a named character of Insight Rank 5 or higher: +2 History Points per owner.

- Owned by a Clan Champion or Imperial figure: +3 History Points.

- Used in a significant battle or duel: +1 History Point.

- Gifted at Winter Court or a major ceremony: +1 History Point.

- Made the subject of a poem, performance, or public story: +1 History Point.

- Present at a historically significant event: +2 History Points.

History Points are cumulative and permanent. An item can only gain each specific event once — the same duel cannot add History Points twice. But an item that changes hands many times, is gifted at multiple courts, and is sung about in poems will accumulate a deep history over decades of in-game time.

**HISTORY TIER BONUSES**

History Points translate into bonus Free Raises that stack with the item's base quality tier:

- 0–2 History Points: No bonus. The item is what it is.

- 3–5 History Points (Notable): +1 Free Raise. The item has a story worth telling.

- 6–9 History Points (Storied): +2 Free Raises. The item is known in certain circles. Characters with relevant Lore skills may recognize it.

- 10+ History Points (Legendary): +3 Free Raises. The item has transcended its origins. Its name — if it has one — is known. Its history is a topic in itself.

Combined maximum: A Masterwork item with Legendary history provides +6 Free Raises total — +3 from quality, +3 from history. This represents a once-in-a-generation piece that has accumulated decades of significant ownership and events. Such items are extraordinarily rare and politically powerful.

**DISCOVERING PROVENANCE**

An item's provenance is not automatically known to those who encounter it. Basic information — that an item was clearly made by a skilled craftsman, or that a painting depicts a famous scene — is visible on inspection. But the full provenance record must be actively discovered.

Discovery rolls:

- Lore: History or Lore: [Relevant Clan/Family] roll to recognize a maker's style or signature. TN varies by how well-known the craftsman is. A Kakita master's brushwork is TN 15 to identify. An obscure provincial smith is TN 35.

- Investigation (Perception) roll to examine the item's physical details — materials, construction techniques, wear patterns — and infer its age, origin, and journey. Each Raise on this roll reveals one additional provenance detail.

- Courtier (Awareness) roll to recall social knowledge about the item — who has owned it, where it was displayed, what occasions it was present at. This is hearsay and court memory rather than physical examination.

A character who fully researches an item's provenance gains access to its complete history record. This knowledge is itself valuable — knowing that a sword was once carried by a famous general, or that a painting was commissioned as a love gift between rival clan members, creates social leverage, conversation topics, and Tier 4 Political or Personal topics that can be discussed and spread.

**ARTISAN ITEMS IN COURT**

Artisan items interact directly with the court and gift-giving systems. When a character presents an Artisan item as a gift, performs with one, or displays one at court, the item's Free Raises apply to the relevant roll. A Fine painting presented as a gift contributes +1 Free Raise to the gift-giving Awareness + Etiquette roll. An Exceptional poem performed at court contributes +2 Free Raises to the performance roll.

The item's history also matters socially. An item with a Storied or Legendary history is recognized by characters with sufficient Lore — and that recognition generates conversation. Presenting a sword once carried by a legendary Crane duelist as a gift to a Lion general is a calculated social maneuver. The Lion may be impressed, offended, or intrigued — depending on their personality and their relationship to that history.

**CRAFT ITEMS IN PLAY**

Craft items interact with the equipment and military systems. Weaponsmithing and Armorsmithing output feeds directly into the quality of the items characters carry — a Fine sword has better mechanical stats than a Normal sword. Exceptional and Masterwork weapons and armor provide bonuses defined in the Equipment section (Section 39).

Craft items with significant histories are treated as named items — they carry mechanical weight beyond their base stats. A Masterwork katana that has been carried through three wars and gifted between clan champions is not merely a weapon. It is a political object, a Tier 4 topic, and a social instrument as much as it is a blade.

**DESIGN NOTE — ITEM NAMING**

DESIGN REFERENCE — Dwarf Fortress Item History Model. This system is directly inspired by the item history model in Dwarf Fortress, where crafted objects accumulate a procedurally generated chain of ownership, events, and significance over time. In DF, a goblin bone earring made by a legendary craftsdwarf, worn by a queen who died in battle, recovered from a siege, and later traded to a merchant becomes a deeply storied object with its own detailed history. The Rokugan system preserves this philosophy but redirects it: where DF item history is interesting trivia, Rokugani item history is political currency. A sword with deep provenance is a social instrument, a court topic, a gift of immense weight, and potentially a casus belli if the wrong person possesses it. The key difference from DF is that history must be actively discovered through rolls rather than read as a tooltip — knowing an item's full history is itself a gameplay achievement.

**INSPECTION MODE**

Any character may enter Inspection Mode on any item they are holding or that is physically nearby. Inspection Mode is a dedicated UI layer that renders the item visually and exposes its known provenance. It is the primary interface through which characters — and players — interact with the item history system.

**The ASCII Representation**

Every item in the game has a unique, persistent ASCII image generated procedurally at the moment of its creation. The image is based on three inputs: the item's type and category, its quality tier, and the materials used. These three factors combine to produce a distinct visual that is fixed for the lifetime of the item — it does not change as the item ages or changes hands, though wear and damage may be reflected in future design iterations.

Quality tier affects visual complexity and detail density. A Normal katana is rendered simply — clean lines, minimal decoration. A Masterwork katana has a more intricate ASCII — elaborate hilt wrapping, visible grain in the steel, a distinctive profile. A Legendary Sacred Weapon is visually unmistakable even to a player who knows nothing of its history. The ASCII communicates quality at a glance.

Materials affect the characters and symbols used in the ASCII. A weapon forged in Kaiu Steel uses different visual textures than one in standard iron. A ring with a jade gem uses a different symbol for the stone than one with obsidian. A painting on Phoenix-blessed paper has a different border treatment than one on common paper. The visual vocabulary of the ASCII reflects what the item is actually made of.

The ASCII image is the item's visual identity. Two katanas of the same type and quality will look similar but not identical — procedural variation ensures that each item is visually distinct. A character who has seen an item before will recognize it again in Inspection Mode.

**The Inspection Panel Layout**

The Inspection Mode panel is divided into two sections displayed side by side:

- Left panel — ASCII Image: The item's procedurally generated visual representation, rendered at a size appropriate to the item type. Larger items (armor, long weapons) receive more ASCII real estate. Smaller items (rings, scrolls, vials) are rendered smaller but with proportionally higher detail density. Interactive hotspots are marked within the ASCII — specific components that can be selected with the mouse.

- Right panel — Known Provenance: A text display showing all provenance information the inspecting character currently knows about the item. At minimum this shows the item's type, quality tier, and any visible surface information. Additional provenance details appear here as the character investigates further. This panel updates in real time as new information is discovered.

**Interactive Hotspots**

The ASCII image contains interactive hotspots — specific visual components of the item that can be selected with the mouse cursor. Hovering over a hotspot highlights it. Clicking opens a popup specific to that component.

Hotspots are generated based on the item's construction. Every item has at least one hotspot (the item itself as a whole). Complex items have multiple:

- A katana: blade, hilt, tsuba (guard), hilt wrap, scabbard — five potential hotspots.

- A ring: band, setting, gem — three hotspots.

- A painting: the painting surface, the frame or mounting, any depicted subject of significance — two to four hotspots.

- A suit of armor: chest plate, shoulder guards, helm, arm guards, leg guards — five or more hotspots.

- A tea set: teapot, cups, tray — three hotspots.

Each hotspot corresponds to a component of the item — a material, a sub-craft, a specific element of the item's construction. The hotspot popup shows provenance information specific to that component.

**Two-Layer Information Display**

Provenance information in Inspection Mode is displayed in two layers. The distinction between layers reflects what a character can know through casual observation versus what requires deliberate investigation.

Layer 1 — Surface Information (always visible): Basic facts observable by anyone who looks at the item carefully. This layer is always displayed without any roll required.

- Item type and general category.

- Approximate quality tier — a trained eye can recognize the difference between Normal and Exceptional craftsmanship. Characters with the relevant Craft or Artisan skill at rank 3+ can identify quality tier precisely. Others see only a rough impression.

- Material type at a general level — the item is clearly steel, clearly silk, clearly jade. Specific material tier (Common vs Rare vs Legendary) may be visible for distinctive materials like jade or obsidian but not for subtler distinctions like Kaiu steel versus standard fine steel.

- Subject of Artisan items — what a painting depicts, what a poem is about, what scene a sculpture shows.

- Whether the item bears a Signature Special Quality — the maker's mark is physically present and visible, though identifying whose mark it is requires a roll.

Layer 2 — Deep Provenance (gated by Investigation rolls): Specific historical information that requires active examination to uncover. This layer populates progressively as the character investigates. Each successful Investigation, Lore, or Courtier roll (as defined in the Discovering Provenance rules above) reveals one or more additional provenance details, which immediately appear in the right panel and within the relevant hotspot popups.

- Specific material identification — recognizing Kaiu steel versus standard fine steel, identifying the geographic origin of a gem.

- Creator identification — who made the item, their clan and family, their school.

- Ownership history — who has held this item, in what order.

- Event history — what significant events the item was present for, what courts it passed through, what duels were fought with it.

- Component-level provenance — who cut the gem, who wrapped the hilt, who wove the silk, who wrote the poem that was later mounted alongside the painting.

**Component Popups**

Clicking a hotspot opens a popup anchored to that component. The popup displays:

- Component name and type (e.g. 'Blade — Kaiu Steel').

- Layer 1 information always shown: general material type, visible condition.

- Layer 2 information shown only for details the character has already discovered through investigation rolls: specific material origin, who worked this component, when, under what circumstances.

- A prompt indicating that further investigation may reveal more, if the character has not yet exhausted the component's provenance depth.

A ring with a Dragon jade gem might show, for a character who has made successful investigation rolls: 'Gem — Dragon Jade. Sourced from the Togashi mountain deposits, year [X]. Cut by [gem-cutter name], Dragon Clan artisan, Insight Rank 2 at time of cutting. Set by [smith name], Kaiu family, Crab Clan.' A character who has made no investigation rolls sees only: 'Gem — jade (approximate).'

**Inspection Mode and the Provenance System**

Inspection Mode is the player-facing interface for the provenance system described earlier in this section. The provenance record exists in the game's data regardless of whether any character has investigated the item — it is a complete history waiting to be discovered. Inspection Mode is how that history is surfaced to the player, piece by piece, as their character learns more about the object in their hands.

An item that has been fully investigated — every hotspot explored, every Layer 2 detail unlocked — displays its complete provenance in the right panel and all component popups. This is the equivalent of having read the item's full Dwarf Fortress-style history entry. The player now knows everything the game world knows about this object. That knowledge is theirs to use.
