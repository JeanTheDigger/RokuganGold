## 2.2 Great Clans

**LOCKED:** All seven Great Clans are documented below with role, culture, families, military structure, strategic mechanics, and cross-references.

### Crane Clan

The Crane are the political clan. Every clan has courtiers; the Crane have built their entire civilisation around the principle that the court is the real battlefield and steel is merely a tool for settling arguments that words cannot. Founded by Lady Doji, who served as Emperor Hantei’s closest advisor and personally shaped the Empire’s cultural and political institutions, the Crane have leveraged that founding advantage for over a thousand years. They are the wealthiest Great Clan, controlling fertile plains and long coastlines that produce more Rice and Koku than any comparable territory. They are the most politically connected, with generations of Crane spouses in the Imperial line creating institutional ties that survived even the fall of the Hantei Dynasty. And they are the most culturally dominant, having established themselves as the arbiters of taste, art, and etiquette across the entire Empire.

Their weakness is military. Crane armies are smaller than the Lion’s, less durable than the Crab’s, and slower than the Unicorn’s. The Crane know this. Their entire military doctrine exists to buy time — the Daidoji hold a defensive line long enough for the Doji to end the war at court on terms that give the Crane back everything they lost on the field, and usually something extra besides.

#### Special Mechanic: The Favour Engine

Every clan can accumulate favours through the Favour System (Section 12.10). What makes the Crane mechanically distinct is a three-part interlocking system that lets them generate, deploy, and weaponise favours at a scale no other clan can match.

**1. Favour Accumulation Advantage:** When a Doji Courtier successfully completes a favour-generating action (gift-giving, matchmaking, providing artisan services, cultural introductions), the favour follows the standard tier rules in Section 12.10 — no mechanical override. The Crane advantage is volume and consistency, not inflated value. Doji Courtier NPCs carry elevated scoring weights on all favour-generating actions and proactively scan for characters without existing Crane favours as targets. The result is that the Crane accumulate more favours, across more characters, faster than any other clan. Combined with the range of services only the Crane can package together — Kakita artisan commissions, Asahina tsangusuri, Otomo matchmaking introductions, cultural legitimacy — the Crane favour network grows wider and denser than any rival’s through sheer activity rather than mechanical bonus.

**2. The Duelling Deterrent:** When a Crane courtier is present at court with a Kakita Bushi assigned as their yojimbo (operational_superior_id pointing to the courtier per Section 11.3.18), all hostile-tagged ActionIDs targeting that courtier from non-allied characters receive a scoring penalty in the NPC Decision Engine. NPCs calculate the risk of provoking a duel challenge before acting against a Crane. The deterrent does not prevent hostile actions — it makes NPCs weigh the consequences. Characters with high Yu (Courage) or low concern for personal safety are less affected. Characters who have witnessed a Kakita kill someone in a duel are more affected. The penalty scales with the Kakita yojimbo’s School Rank: a Rank 1 Kakita is a mild concern; a Rank 5 Kenshinzen makes most NPCs find someone else to antagonise. The Crane do not assassinate — they engineer formal duel challenges that are both honourable and lethal, leave no criminal evidence, and cannot be prosecuted. PROVISIONAL: exact scoring penalty values per Kakita School Rank, disposition threshold defining “allied,” and the witnessed-kill memory flag implementation are pending playtesting. The design principle is locked.

**3. Cultural Prestige:** Crane provinces with active Kakita Artisan NPCs producing art (Section 49) generate a Cultural Prestige value that provides a slow, steady disposition bonus with all clans. Art produced by Kakita artisans has higher base quality in the Artisan system. A lord who hosts a Crane artisan at their court gains Glory. A lord who publicly rejects or insults Crane art loses Glory — the Crane define what the Empire considers beautiful, and defying that consensus carries social cost. This turns the Artisan and Crafting System into a genuine strategic lever: the Crane export culture, and culture generates goodwill, and goodwill creates openings for favours. PROVISIONAL: exact Cultural Prestige formula (how artisan output converts to disposition bonus), Glory gain/loss values for hosting or rejecting Crane art, and the threshold for what constitutes a “public rejection” as a game event are pending playtesting. The design principle is locked.

The three pieces feed each other: Cultural Prestige builds goodwill, which makes it easier to offer favours, which accumulates obligations, which gives the Crane leverage at court, which is backed by the duelling deterrent if anyone tries to resist. No other clan has this self-reinforcing loop.

#### The Four Families

**Doji — Courtiers:** The ruling family. Doji courtiers operate the favour network described above and dominate the Imperial Court through institutional connections dating back to Lady Doji’s role as the first Emperor’s advisor. Generations of Crane spouses in the Hantei line embedded Doji influence at every level of the Imperial bureaucracy. The Toturi and Iweko Dynasties weakened this stranglehold but the institutional ties remain. Doji courtiers are the primary drivers of the Crane’s political strategy: accumulating favours, managing clan-to-clan disposition, deploying cultural prestige as soft power, and using the court commitment system (Section 15) to convert political capital into territorial and economic gains without military action. The Doji Courtier school techniques are fully designed in Section 29.15.4.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Doji Courtier SchoolType): Elevated scoring weight on favour-generating actions (GIFT, NEGOTIATE, CHARM) and court attendance priority. Doji NPCs proactively scan for characters without existing Crane favours as targets for relationship-building. In free-choice situations they gravitate toward political solutions over field actions. A Doji assigned to military duty by their lord still follows orders — the lean adjusts priorities, it does not override directives.

**Kakita — Duelists and Artisans:** Kakita founded both the iaijutsu duelling form and the Empire’s first artisan academy as twin disciplines — he believed mastery of the sword and mastery of art demanded the same precision. The Kakita Duelling School produces the finest iaijutsu duelists in the Empire. The Kenshinzen — master duelists who have transcended normal training — are among the most feared individual combatants alive. They provide the duelling deterrent described above. The Kakita Artisan Academy is the other half: its masters define what constitutes excellence in art across Rokugan, and no individual artist’s work is accounted great by the wider Empire until a Kakita Master has endorsed it. This cultural arbitration power is what drives the Cultural Prestige mechanic. The iaijutsu duel system is defined in Section 4.8.1. The Artisan system is in Section 49.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Kakita Bushi SchoolType): When assigned as yojimbo to a courtier (operational_superior_id per Section 11.3.18), elevated weight on travel-with behavior and CHALLENGE_TO_DUEL when the paired courtier is targeted by a hostile-tagged action. The response weight scales with provocation severity and the Kakita’s own personality — a Kakita with high Yu responds more aggressively, one with high pragmatism may let minor provocations pass. When assigned to a field army rather than a courtier, the lean is inactive and the Kakita fights as a standard bushi.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Kakita Artisan SchoolType): Elevated weight on art production actions and court touring. Kakita Artisan NPCs naturally seek postings at high-prestige courts (Imperial Court, Clan Champion courts) where their output generates the most Cultural Prestige. They accept commissions from Doji courtiers, creating works intended as diplomatic gifts that feed the favour network. A Kakita Artisan reassigned during wartime follows orders but will seek to return to their artisan role when the conflict ends.

**Daidoji — Warriors and Merchants:** The military backbone. Daidoji bushi are defensive specialists who train extensively to compensate for the Crane’s smaller armies, mapping their own territory obsessively and favouring ambush, terrain advantage, and disciplined spear formations over open-field engagements. Their Heavy Spearmen (Section 11.6) anchor the Crane battle line with the highest base Defence of any Tier 2 unit. The Daidoji are also the Crane’s commercial operators — samurai who regard wealth as a strategic asset rather than a social embarrassment. Daidoji merchant patrons manage the trade networks that fund the clan’s political operations. They are pragmatists in a clan of idealists, willing to do necessary work that the Doji and Kakita would rather not acknowledge.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Daidoji Iron Warrior SchoolType): Elevated weight on defensive military actions (PATROL_PROVINCE, fortification, ambush positioning) over offensive operations. Daidoji bushi ordered to attack will attack — they are pragmatists, not pacifists — but in free-choice situations they favour holding ground over pressing forward. A subset of Daidoji NPCs with the commerce-oriented personality profile carry elevated CONDUCT_COMMERCE weights and trade route management priorities, representing the merchant patrons who fund the Doji’s political operations.

**Asahina — Pacifist Shugenja:** The Asahina are committed pacifists who refuse to kill and will not train anyone who does. Their sole exception is Shadowlands creatures, whose destruction they consider maintenance of the Celestial Order rather than violence. In inter-clan conflict they use their magic to protect Crane warriors rather than harm the enemy. Their primary contribution to the clan is the creation of tsangusuri — talismans and fetishes that are highly valued across the Empire. Doji courtiers use Asahina-crafted items as diplomatic gifts, feeding the favour network. The Asahina connect to the Artisan and Crafting System (Section 49) as magical artisans whose output has both mechanical utility and political value.

NPC Behavior — Hard Gate (Asahina Shugenja SchoolType): personality_filter.json removes all offensive combat ActionIDs against targets without the Shadowlands creature tag. This is not a lean — it is an absolute restriction. An Asahina Shugenja NPC will never cast offensive magic against a living person, never attack in melee, never participate in an assault. Defensive actions (Guard, protective spells, ward placement), ritual actions (PERFORM_RITUAL, PERFORM_WORSHIP), and crafting actions (tsangusuri production) remain in the pool. Offensive actions against targets with the Shadowlands tag remain in the pool. This creates real gameplay consequences: a province defended only by Asahina shugenja has no offensive magical capability against human attackers. The gate is keyed to SchoolType, not family name — an Asahina who trained at a different school does not carry this restriction, and a non-Asahina who trained at the Asahina Shugenja school does.

#### Gameplay Implications

A Crane-aligned player’s core loop is political. The primary activities are accumulating favours, managing the disposition network across multiple courts, deploying Kakita duelists as implicit threats behind negotiation, and leveraging Cultural Prestige from artisan output. Crane provinces are wealthy (fertile plains, coastal trade, crossroads commerce), providing a strong Koku base that funds political operations rather than military expansion. The Crane do not conquer territory — they make other clans concede it at court. Military engagement is a last resort, and when it happens, the Crane fight to stall: the Daidoji hold ground while the Doji work to end the war through negotiation before the front collapses. A player who tries to play the Crane as a military power will lose. A player who plays them as a political power with a defensive military backstop will dominate every court they attend.

### Lion Clan

The Lion are the military clan. They field the largest standing army in the Empire, hold more territory than any other Great Clan, and have served as the Emperor’s Right Hand — his primary military instrument — since the founding. Where the Crane dominate the court and the Crab hold the Wall, the Lion dominate the open battlefield. Their Akodo formations are the most efficient fighting force in Rokugan when properly assembled, their Matsu provide raw numbers no other family can match, and their Deathseekers are expendable shock troops that never break. The Lion do not win through cleverness or trickery — they win through discipline, superior numbers, and the absolute conviction that dying for one’s lord is not a sacrifice but a privilege.

Their weakness is political. The Lion are the most rigid adherents to Bushido in the Empire — Akodo One-Eye literally wrote the Code in his book Leadership, and every Lion is raised to follow it without compromise. This makes them honourable, courageous, and utterly predictable. A Scorpion or Crane who understands the Lion knows exactly what they will do in any given situation, because the Lion will almost always choose the honourable path even when it is strategically wrong. The Lion’s high baseline Bushido virtue weights in the NPC engine make them the hardest clan to corrupt and the easiest to manipulate.

#### Special Mechanic: Kitsu Ancestor Communion

The Kitsu family are descended from an ancient race of sentient lion-like spirit creatures that pre-dated humanity. Their bloodline gives them a unique connection to Yomi, the Realm of Blessed Ancestors (Section 2.6.9). The most powerful Kitsu — the Sodan Senzo — can commune directly with dead NPCs who reside in Yomi, retrieving information that would otherwise be permanently lost when a character dies.

This is a genuine strategic capability no other clan possesses. When an important NPC dies — a lord with secret alliances, a magistrate mid-investigation, a general who knew the enemy’s disposition — that information normally dies with them. A Kitsu Sodan Senzo can petition the dead character’s spirit in Yomi and ask questions. The spirit is not compelled to answer and may not know everything the living character knew (spirits in Yomi retain personality and wisdom but lose some connection to immediate mortal concerns), but the channel exists and no other clan has it.

Requirements: Kitsu Shugenja school (Sodan Senzo path), physically present at a location with strong Yomi connection (the Kitsu Tombs, a major ancestral shrine, or any province with an active Yomi overlap). The target spirit must reside in Yomi — characters who died dishonourably may have been sent to Gaki-do, Toshigoku, or Meido instead, and cannot be reached through this channel. Roll: appropriate Commune skill vs TN scaled by the spirit’s time since death and the specificity of the question asked. PROVISIONAL: exact TN formula, question limits per communion, and AP cost pending design of the full Kitsu school mechanic in Section 29.4.

Gameplay consequence: enemies of the Lion must account for the fact that killing someone does not bury their secrets. A murdered lord’s final knowledge can potentially be retrieved by the Kitsu. This creates a deterrent distinct from the Crane’s duelling threat — the Crane make you afraid to act against them in life; the Lion make you afraid that your actions will be discovered after death.

#### The Four Families

**Akodo — Strategists:** The ruling family, descended from the founding Kami. The Akodo are the clan’s mind — warrior-scholars who approach battle as a disciplined craft. They hone strategy, logistics, and leadership the way a sculptor refines stonework: testing principles, discarding what fails, perfecting what works. The Akodo are the most ascetic and self-denying of the Lion families, holding themselves to an exacting standard of Bushido compliance that even other Lion find demanding. Akodo One-Eye wrote the Code of Bushido itself, and his descendants consider any deviation from it a personal failure. After the Time of Shame (when the family was disbanded for failing to prevent the Scorpion Clan Coup), the restored Akodo adopted a more meritocratic approach to leadership — clan authority now goes to whoever is most qualified regardless of bloodline, a significant departure from their earlier rigidity.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Akodo Bushi SchoolType): Highest baseline Bushido virtue weights of any school in the game, particularly Chugi (Duty), Meiyo (Honour), and Yu (Courage). Elevated weights on formation-based military actions, TRAIN_TROOPS, and strategic planning. In free-choice situations, Akodo NPCs favour methodical preparation over impulsive action. They are the least likely bushi school to order a reckless charge and the most likely to spend AP on DRILL_TROOPS before committing to battle.

**Matsu — The Backbone:** The largest single samurai family in the Empire. The Matsu comprise the bulk of every Lion army and provide the raw military mass that makes the Lion the Emperor’s Right Hand. Without the Matsu the Lion would not have the manpower to fulfil their role. They are passionate, ferocious, and absolutely devoted to battle — where the Akodo approach war as a craft, the Matsu approach it as a calling. The Matsu focus on the martial tenets of Bushido (Yu, Meiyo, Chugi) and interpret the social tenets (Jin, Rei, Makoto) through a combative lens: compassion means protecting the weak by destroying their enemies, courtesy means giving your opponent a clean death. The Matsu have a matriarchal succession tradition — the eldest daughter inherits before the eldest son. This is a unique modifier within the succession system (Section 22.5) that applies only to Matsu family lordship positions.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Matsu Berserker SchoolType): Elevated weights on aggressive military actions (ORDER_BATTLE, CONDUCT_RAID) and direct confrontation over cautious approaches. High Yu, high Kyoryoku (Strength). In free-choice situations, Matsu NPCs favour attacking over waiting. This is a lean, not a hard gate — a Matsu ordered to hold position will hold position, but given discretion they will press forward. The Matsu Berserker school’s connection to Toshigoku (Section 2.6.8) means high-rank Matsu Berserkers may attract spirit attention from the Realm of Slaughter during prolonged combat.

**Ikoma — Historians and Bards:** The Ikoma are the Lion’s heart. They are the clan’s courtiers, historians, and emotional centre — the Lion who are permitted and expected to express what other Lion must suppress. They mourn the dead openly, record every deed of heroism and disgrace, and serve as the living memory of the clan. The Ikoma follow Bushido but interpret it with more pragmatic flexibility than the Akodo or Matsu, which makes them effective diplomats in courts where rigid honour is a liability. They maintain the official histories of both the Lion Clan and the Empire, working alongside the Otomo in the Imperial Archives. In military contexts, the Ikoma serve as scouts, wardens, and battlefield tacticians who view war from the broader strategic perspective rather than the immediate engagement.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Ikoma Bard SchoolType): The Lion’s primary courtier school. Elevated weights on court attendance, NEGOTIATE, and information-gathering actions. More balanced Bushido/pragmatism weighting than Akodo or Matsu — the Ikoma are willing to bend where other Lion would break. They serve as the connective tissue between the clan’s military identity and its political needs. The Ikoma Bard school techniques are fully designed in Section 29.15.8.

**Kitsu — Ancestral Priests:** The smallest and strangest of the Lion families. The Kitsu are descended from ancient spirit creatures — sentient lion-like beings that pre-dated humanity. The family was created by the union of the last five of these creatures with Akodo’s daughters. This non-human ancestry gives the Kitsu their unique connection to the Spirit Realms, particularly Yomi. The Sodan Senzo — the elite inner circle of the Kitsu — spend most of their lives cloistered in the Kitsu Tombs, communing with the honoured ancestors and rarely emerging into the mortal world. The broader Kitsu family serves as the Lion’s priests and spiritual advisors, guiding the clan in worship of the kami and the ancestors. Ancestor veneration is more central to Lion identity than to any other clan, and the Kitsu are its custodians.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Kitsu Shugenja SchoolType): Standing objective weighted heavily toward PERFORM_RITUAL and ancestor communion at shrines and tombs. Sodan Senzo NPCs have a strong location preference for the Kitsu Tombs and major ancestral sites — they rarely travel unless a specific spiritual need arises. When they do emerge, they carry elevated weights on PERFORM_WORSHIP and spiritual investigation actions. Non-Senzo Kitsu function as standard shugenja with an ancestor-worship emphasis.

#### Gameplay Implications

A Lion-aligned player’s core loop is military. The primary activities are managing the largest army in the Empire, maintaining formation discipline across multiple fronts, leveraging the Akodo coordination bonus (+1 Attack per adjacent Lion, max +3 from Section 11.6) through deliberate unit placement, and deploying Deathseekers and Lion’s Pride as precision instruments within the mass battle system. The Lion can fight on more fronts simultaneously than any other clan and can absorb losses that would cripple smaller forces. Their economic base is solid — extensive farmland in the central plains — but their massive permanent military consumes resources at a rate that leaves little surplus for political manoeuvring or cultural investment. The Lion are always fed but never rich.

The Lion’s court weakness is real. The Ikoma are competent courtiers but they are outnumbered and outmanoeuvred by the Crane and Scorpion at every major court. A Lion player who neglects politics entirely will find wars declared against them through court manipulation, alliances forming behind their back, and trade routes redirected away from their provinces. The Kitsu ancestor communion provides a unique intelligence advantage that partially compensates — the Lion may not read the living as well as the Crane, but they can consult the dead in ways no other clan can match.

The Lion are also more likely to raid their neighbours than any other clan. Lion daimyo and the Clan Champion carry elevated scoring weights on CONDUCT_RAID, SEEK_PRETEXT, and INITIATE_WAR_CHECK against adjacent non-allied clans. This is a clan-level behavioral lean reflecting the Lion’s conviction that military strength is political legitimacy — a Lion lord with idle troops and a weaker neighbour sees an opportunity, not a moral dilemma. A Lion lord with high Jin or strong diplomatic ties will still prefer peace, but the baseline aggression is higher than any other clan. The Matsu lean amplifies this further — a Matsu daimyo bordering Crane territory is practically guaranteed to start something. Other clans must factor this into their border planning: a province adjacent to Lion territory needs a credible garrison or the Lion will eventually test it.

#### Lion-Specific Threat: The Tsuno — LOCKED

The Tsuno are corrupted Kitsu — the same ancient spirit race whose last survivors became the Lion’s Kitsu family. Exiled into the Spirit Realms for their role in the destruction of the City of Night, the Tsuno spent centuries in Toshigoku (the Realm of Slaughter), where the realm’s nature resonated with their violent hearts. They built cities within Toshigoku, transformed their bodies into monstrous spiked and horned forms, lost their shapeshifting ability, and developed a brutal martial culture that prizes strength and treats all other races with contempt. Their warriors are called Ravagers; their spellcasters are Soultwisters, who steal power directly from Spirit Realms rather than communing with kami. The Tsuno carry a specific grudge against the Lion — the Lion nearly exterminated the original Kitsu before Akodo realised his error. During the Toturi Dynasty some Tsuno allied with the Dark Lord Daigotsu, accepting Shadowlands Taint by choice. Base Tsuno stat block is in Section 54. Ravager and Soultwister school stat blocks pending Bestiary expansion.

**Trigger — The Tsuno Probe Roll:** Fires once per season for each Lion province containing a major ancestral site (the Kitsu Tombs and any province with an active Sodan Senzo NPC). The Tsuno are not passive spirits waiting for a portal — they actively probe for weaknesses in the barrier, drawn by the spiritual energy of Kitsu ancestor communion. The trigger is paradoxical: strong ancestor worship attracts Tsuno attention. The Lion need communion to use their special mechanic, but using it paints a target. Base probability is low. Modified by: active Sodan Senzo communion performed this season (+X% per communion — more communion means more signal), province Yomi overlap strength (stronger overlap = more attractive target), previous successful probe not repelled (+X% cumulative — they found a weak point), previous failed probe repelled (−X% temporarily — they back off). PROVISIONAL: exact percentage values pending playtesting. The structural relationships are locked.

**Stage 1 — Probe:** A small Tsuno scouting party (3–5 warriors, possibly led by a single Ravager) crosses through a thin point in the barrier. ASCII map encounter using existing outdoor templates (Forest Approach, Hilltop Position, or Ravine Camp from Sections 56.5, 56.8, 56.11 depending on province terrain). A single PC or small group can handle a probe. If repelled (all Tsuno destroyed), the probe counter resets with a temporary probability reduction. If not repelled within the same season (no Lion response, or the response failed), the surviving Tsuno remain in the province and escalate to Stage 2.

**Stage 2 — Foothold:** The surviving Tsuno establish a semi-permanent anchor point — a localised Toshigoku bleed zone within the province. This is not a full Spirit Realm Overlap (Section 56.16) but a single corrupted site. The foothold generates Toshigoku-specific effects: a province-level aggression modifier (elevated frequency of duels, brawls, and unauthorised violence among the local population), the Battle Rage exposure mechanic from Section 56.16.8a at reduced intensity for characters spending extended time in the province, recurring Tsuno patrols that threaten travel safety (named character movement risk check per Section 4.3.11), and increased probe probability for this province while the foothold persists. The foothold does NOT generate PTL — Toshigoku corruption is spiritual agitation, not Jigoku’s Shadowlands Taint. Exception: if the Tsuno occupying the foothold are Tainted (Daigotsu-allied, carrying personal Taint Rank 4), their physical presence generates PTL from the Jigoku corruption in their bodies — not from the Toshigoku overlap itself. Kill the Tainted Tsuno and the PTL generation stops; the Toshigoku aggression effects continue until the foothold is sealed. ASCII map encounter: 6–10 Tsuno including at least one Ravager. The objective is reaching and destroying the anchor point (a focal object or ritual site within the map), not just killing the Tsuno — killing them without destroying the anchor means they regenerate next season.

**Stage 3 — Incursion:** If a foothold persists for 3+ seasons without being sealed or destroyed, the Tsuno commit a full warband. 12–15 Tsuno including multiple Ravagers and at least one Soultwister. Uses the largest ASCII map templates (Large Cave, Large Occupied Village from Sections 56.3, 56.4). The Soultwister draws power from up to three Spirit Realms simultaneously, making spell selection unpredictable — the player does not know which realms the Soultwister draws from until spells start firing. Generates a Tier 3 crisis topic (Tsuno Incursion) that enters the court system — other clans know the Lion have a Toshigoku problem, creating diplomatic vulnerability. The crisis drives the political and logistical response (who goes, what gets delayed, whether the Lion request outside help), while the actual combat remains an ASCII map mission. If the incursion reaches the Kitsu Tombs, it triggers the Tomb defence scenario described below.

**Stage 4 — Invasion:** Rare and catastrophic. Requires all three conditions simultaneously: a Stage 3 incursion has persisted for 4+ seasons unsealed, multiple footholds exist simultaneously in Lion territory (the barrier is weakening across a front), and a Tsuno Warlord has unified the factions within Toshigoku (a narrative trigger tied to a Daigotsu-linked plot or generated event). When all three conditions are met, the Tsuno commit an organised invasion force through a fully opened Toshigoku corridor. Multiple Companies of Tsuno — Ravager-led formations with Soultwister support — cross in numbers large enough to require the mass battle grid (Section 11.7). The Tsuno fight without supply lines or baggage trains (sustained by Toshigoku energy) but are numerous enough to require Company-scale resolution. Generates a Tier 2 crisis — comparable to a Shadowlands Incursion (Section 16.3). The Lion’s full military strength is required. If the invading Tsuno include Tainted elements, the incursion layers Jigoku corruption on top of Toshigoku agitation in the same provinces — a dual-realm contamination that requires both Kitsu (to seal the Toshigoku breach) and Kuni or equivalent (to purify the PTL) working the same territory, forcing the Lion to request Crab aid at significant diplomatic cost.

**Regression:** Destroying or sealing a foothold drops the province back to Stage 1. Repelling an incursion force drops it back to Stage 2 (the foothold still exists unless separately sealed). The Tsuno do not give up after a single defeat — they retreat, regroup in Toshigoku, and probe again. The only way to fully clear a province is to both repel the warriors AND seal the Toshigoku anchor point.

Gaki-do Consuming Hunger — target enemy Company takes 2 direct Morale damage, no Morale Defense applied. Chikushudo Bestial Nature — target allied Tsuno Company gains +2 Attack for 2 rounds. Sakkaku Mischief — target enemy Company’s Special ability is suppressed for 2 rounds (boolean flag, cleared after duration). Meido Lethargy — target enemy Company loses its next attack action (skips one round of attacking, boolean flag cleared after one round).

**Tsuno Modifiers:**

Valiant Modifier — applied to any Tsuno Company type: +1 Attack. +2 Morale. +1 Morale Defense. Cannot rout while above 25% Health (same pattern as the Crab Berserker rout gate in Section 11.6). These are ancient warriors who have been fighting in the Realm of Slaughter for centuries. Valiant can appear from Stage 2 onward.

Tainted Modifier — applied to any Tsuno Company type: +1 Attack. +1 Defense. Province-level effect: the province generates +0.1 PTL per season while Tainted Tsuno Companies are physically present. This PTL is sourced from the Jigoku corruption in their bodies, not from the Toshigoku foothold. Kill the Tainted Tsuno and the PTL generation stops; the Toshigoku foothold effects continue independently. Tainted can appear from Stage 3 onward.

Both modifiers may stack on a single Company, producing a Tainted Valiant unit. This combination appears only at Stage 4 under a Tsuno Warlord. A Tainted Valiant Ravager: Attack 10, Defense 7, Morale 20, Morale Defense 9, cannot rout above 25% Health, generates PTL, and gains +1 Defense for each 25% Health lost — reaching Defense 10 near death. This is a warlord-tier threat comparable to the most dangerous units in the game.

**SEAL_TOSHIGOKU_BREACH (1 AP):** Requirements: any shugenja school, physically present at the foothold site. Roll: Lore: Spirit Realms + Willpower, base TN 25. The TN increases the longer the foothold has persisted. Kitsu Shugenja bonus: add School Rank as Free Raises on this roll. A Rank 3 Kitsu effectively faces TN 10 — the system is designed to work smoothly when the Lion use their own people. Non-Kitsu shugenja receive no bonus and face the full TN, making the roll achievable for skilled shugenja but unreliable at higher foothold durations. Cooperative rolling follows the standard framework (Section 56.16.5b). Failure: the foothold is not sealed, the shugenja suffers Battle Rage exposure (Section 56.16.8a), no AP refund, may retry next season.

#### The Kitsu Tombs

The Kitsu Tombs are the single most sacred site in Lion territory — a vast temple complex housing the urns, ashes, and spirit tablets of centuries of Lion heroes, traditionally administered by the second child of the Matsu family daimyo. The Tombs sit at a permanent Yomi overlap zone, the strongest in the Empire. The Sodan Senzo conduct their ancestor communion here, and the Hall of Ancestors within the complex echoes constantly with the voices of the honoured dead.

The Tombs generate two types of content. First, the Yomi overlap means the Kitsu Ancestor Communion mechanic operates at its strongest here — lowest TN, most detailed answers from spirits. A Lion player who brings questions to the Tombs gets the best possible results from the communion system. Second, the Tombs are a target. The Tsuno are drawn to the Kitsu bloodline, and a Tsuno incursion reaching the Tombs creates a direct collision between Yomi and Toshigoku — the honoured dead versus the corrupted exiles. Defending the Tombs from a Tsuno assault is a high-stakes ASCII map mission where failure means damage to the Lion’s ancestor communion infrastructure, temporary loss of the Yomi overlap bonus, and potential desecration of irreplaceable spirit tablets. The original Kitsu spirit creatures are extinct in the mortal realm, but encounters with their echoes in Yomi are possible through the Sodan Senzo communion channel.

### Dragon Clan

The Dragon are the isolationist clan. They occupy the most mountainous, least accessible territory of any Great Clan, reachable overland only through the Dragonfly (Tonbo) valley — a single chokepoint controlled by a Dragon-allied Minor Clan. This geographic constraint is already established as a world map routing rule: any character who is not Dragon Clan must pass through Tonbo lands to reach Dragon territory. Any military invasion must force a single sub-tile pass before entering the mountains proper. The Dragon have the smallest effective military of any Great Clan but they rarely need it, because their mountains are a natural fortress that no conventional army can crack without catastrophic cost.

The Dragon do not participate in Empire politics the way other clans do. They have minimal court presence, few diplomatic entanglements, and little interest in the factional manoeuvring that consumes the Crane, Scorpion, and Lion. Their Clan Champion — historically the Kami Togashi himself, living in secret for over a thousand years — played a strategic game on a timescale no mortal could comprehend. The modern Dragon retain this detachment. They watch, they meditate, and when they intervene it is sudden, cryptic, and often decisive. From the outside, dealing with the Dragon is like negotiating with a mountain: it does not move for you, but occasionally it moves of its own accord and reshapes everything around it.

#### Special Mechanic: Kitsuki’s Method

The Dragon’s defining clan-level capability is the Kitsuki Method — an evidence-based investigation system that operates fundamentally differently from standard Rokugani law. In most of the Empire, criminal cases are resolved through eyewitness testimony weighted by the witness’s Honour. A high-Honour samurai’s word is treated as truth; a low-Honour witness is dismissed. The Kitsuki reject this. They examine physical evidence, draw logical inferences, and build cases from material facts rather than social standing. A Kitsuki investigator who sees a Crane-coloured obi bead lying next to a murder victim does not assume the killer is a Crane — they ask why the bead is there, whether it was planted, and what the physical evidence actually demonstrates.

Mechanically, this is already integrated into the investigation system (Section 2.8.5). Kitsuki school techniques grant bonuses to EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE rolls and allow Kitsuki investigators to detect deception in testimony where other magistrate schools take witnesses at face value. The tradeoff is social: most clans consider the Kitsuki Method borderline dishonourable, since questioning a samurai’s word is an insult. A Kitsuki magistrate assigned to a province improves investigation quality but may generate diplomatic friction with allies who find the method offensive. The Kitsuki are the Empire’s best investigators and its most socially awkward magistrates.

#### The Four Families

**Mirumoto — The Niten Swordsmen:** The largest Dragon family and the practical face of the clan, handling most day-to-day governance. The Mirumoto are descended from the warrior who created the Niten two-sword fighting style — a technique rivalled only by the Kakita iaijutsu tradition, and the source of a centuries-long rivalry between the two families. Mirumoto bushi are unusual among warriors in that they train alongside shugenja, giving them a deeper understanding of the Elements than most bushi possess. In Dragon armies, swordsmen and priests serve side by side rather than in separate formations — this is reflected in the Mirumoto Bushi’s mass battle stats (Section 11.6), where adjacent allied shugenja units gain +1 Attack. The Mirumoto are also poets, metaphor-users, and ascetics who view life through a lens of elemental philosophy — but they are still warriors first, and they seek glory in duels and battle whenever the Dragon’s isolation offers the opportunity.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Mirumoto Bushi SchoolType): Elevated weight on individual excellence actions (CHALLENGE_TO_DUEL, TRAIN_SKILL) over formation-based military actions. When deployed in armies, elevated weight on positioning adjacent to Tamori/shugenja units to activate the bushi-shugenja synergy bonus. In free-choice situations, Mirumoto NPCs favour meditative or philosophical pursuits when not engaged in combat duties — a Mirumoto assigned to garrison a remote mountain fortress will train, meditate, and compose poetry rather than grow restless.

**Kitsuki — Investigators and Courtiers:** Founded in 820 by Agasha Kitsuki, the family is the youngest of the Dragon families and the least traditional by outside standards — though within the Dragon they are considered the most methodical. The Kitsuki serve as the clan’s magistrates and its primary court representatives. Their investigative method is described above. The Kitsuki also include a faction called the Justicars — investigators who use iaijutsu duels to prove guilt when evidence alone cannot compel a conviction. The Kitsuki occasionally produce shugenja, but the family as a whole prefers the powers of the mind and senses over communion with kami.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Kitsuki Investigator SchoolType): Elevated weight on INVESTIGATE_THREAT, EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE, and investigation-related actions. Kitsuki NPCs proactively seek out unresolved criminal or political mysteries in their jurisdiction. When assigned as magistrates, they apply the Kitsuki Method school bonuses to crime scene examination (Section 2.8.5). In court, they function as courtiers with elevated weights on LISTEN_REFLECT and information-gathering actions — reading intentions and detecting deception rather than building favour networks.

**Tamori — Combat Shugenja and Alchemists:** Formerly the Agasha family, who served as Dragon shugenja for over a thousand years before the majority defected to the Phoenix during the tyrannical reign of Hitomi. The remnant who stayed, led by Agasha Tamori, renamed themselves and forged a more militant identity. The Tamori retained the Agasha’s alchemical traditions — kagaku (potions and devices) and hanabi (fireworks) — while intensifying their commitment to fighting alongside the Mirumoto on the front lines. Tamori shugenja are not rear-line support — they wade into battle carrying both blades and elemental magic. They are stubborn, intense, and practically oriented. The Tamori Furnace (formerly the Agasha Foundry) is a significant forge connected to the Artisan and Crafting System (Section 49).

NPC Behavior — Lean (Tamori Shugenja SchoolType): Elevated weight on combat-adjacent actions (PERFORM_RITUAL in field contexts, fighting alongside bushi rather than from the rear). Alchemy crafting actions carry elevated weight when not in combat. In armies, Tamori NPCs position themselves adjacent to Mirumoto Companies to activate the bushi-shugenja synergy (Section 11.6). Unlike most shugenja schools, Tamori NPCs do not avoid front-line positions — they seek them.

**Tamori Kagaku (Alchemy):** The Tamori (formerly Agasha) family’s alchemical tradition produces kagaku — practical items and potions created through elemental chemistry rather than spellcasting. Kagaku uses the Craft Track (Section 49) with Medicine (Alchemy) as the governing sub-skill, paired with Intelligence. The material type is alchemical reagents (herbs, minerals, chemical compounds), classified as “Other materials” in the crafting time table. Most kagaku items are Bu-priced consumables, giving them a time unit of Hours — meaning 1 AP per batch. One CRAFT action produces a batch of doses (number determined by the roll margin above the base TN, minimum 1 dose). Kagaku items are non-magical and therefore undetectable by Detect Magic or similar effects. They are not restricted by the social taboos surrounding gaijin pepper — kagaku is recognised as a legitimate scholarly pursuit. Production requires access to alchemical reagents, which are an Uncommon-tier material (Section 42) available at the Tamori Furnace and at settlements with active herbalist or apothecary NPCs. Representative kagaku items:

Flash Paper (Moeagaru): Thin paper treated with alchemical solutions that ignites from friction in a bright instantaneous flame. Combat use: ignite in front of an opponent’s eyes — target rolls Reflexes at TN 20 or is Blinded until next Round. Utility use: write messages on it for rapid destruction. Kitsuki Powder: Soft grey powder with an affinity for human footprints and blood. When scattered over an area, adheres to any footprints or bloodstains made in the last 24 hours, even if wiped up or obscured. Primary use: crime scene investigation — connects directly to the Kitsuki Method and EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE. Liquid Smoke (Ekitai Kemuri): Gummy liquid that emits thick black smoke when ignited, burning eyes and throat. Victims are Dazed (standard Earth roll to shake off). Burns for ten minutes per dose. Tactical use: area denial, escape cover, ambush preparation. Sleeping Fire (Hinemuri): Thin clear liquid that ignites when exposed to temperature changes (such as a warm foot stepping in it). Burns quickly — 1k1 damage — but can easily set buildings alight. Tactical use: trap-setting, arson, perimeter defence.

The full kagaku item list, individual Bu costs, batch sizes, and specific crafting TNs are pending design in Section 49. The items listed above are representative examples establishing the system’s scope. Kagaku items are a trade commodity unique to the Dragon — other clans can acquire them through commerce or diplomacy but cannot produce them without Tamori-trained alchemists. NOTE: The kagaku item list above is a starting set. Additional kagaku items to be designed in a future pass — the system is extensible and the Tamori tradition canonically includes dozens of formulas beyond these four.

**Togashi — The Tattooed Order:** The Togashi are not a family in the conventional sense. They are a spiritual brotherhood founded by the first followers of the Kami Togashi, sustained through recruitment and adoption rather than bloodline. Men and women from across the Empire who accept Togashi’s mystical tattoos take the Togashi name and join the order. The tattoos are inked in special rituals using ink laced with the blood of Togashi himself or of a fellow tattooed monk who has lived for centuries — this is the source of their mystical power, and the Togashi guard the secret absolutely. The tattoo system is a unique school technique framework defined in Section 29 with no equivalent in any other clan. Most Togashi are ascetics who remain in Dragon lands, pursuing enlightenment at the High House of Light (Kyuden Togashi) deep in the mountains. They train intensively in both martial arts and meditation. The order divides loosely into ise zumi (the general path), kikage zumi (violent and confrontational, martial emphasis), and tsurui zumi (mystical, spiritual emphasis).

NPC Behavior — Lean (Togashi Ise Zumi SchoolType): Strong location preference for Dragon mountain holdings, particularly the High House of Light. Elevated weight on MEDITATE, TRAIN_SKILL, and PERFORM_WORSHIP. The majority of Togashi NPCs are sedentary — they remain in the mountains their entire lives. However, a minority carry an elevated exploration weight that causes them to leave Dragon territory and wander the Empire. These wandering Togashi are a content generator: they appear in non-Dragon provinces as random or semi-random events, offering cryptic counsel, intervening in crises, rescuing endangered peasants, or leaving puzzle-box gifts on the doorstep of a confused samurai. Their actions are driven by the NPC engine’s normal motivation system but with atypically high randomness in target selection — the Togashi does not plan their interventions the way a Crane courtier plans a favour. They follow instinct, spiritual impulse, and the Tao. From a design perspective, wandering Togashi NPCs function as a low-frequency, high-impact quest seed source for any province they pass through. They do not stay long, they do not explain themselves, and their departure is as sudden as their arrival.

#### Wandering Togashi System

At world generation, each Togashi NPC is flagged as either sedentary (majority — stays in Dragon mountains) or wandering (minority — approximately 15–20% of the order). The flag is assigned based on personality: Togashi with high Dōsatsu (Insight) or high Jin (Compassion) are more likely to wander. High Seigyo (Self-Control) Togashi stay home and meditate.

**Departure Roll:** Each season, every wandering-flagged Togashi currently in Dragon territory makes a Departure Roll. Base probability approximately 30% per season. Modified upward by the number of active Tier 3+ crises anywhere in the Empire that the Togashi is aware of (information reaches the mountains through monks, pilgrims, and the Togashi’s own spiritual sensitivity). Modified downward if the Dragon are currently under military threat — even wanderers stay home when the clan needs them. PROVISIONAL: exact base probability and modifier values pending playtesting.

**Destination Selection:** When a Togashi departs, the engine selects a destination province. Not random — weighted by: active unresolved crisis topics in the province (strongest pull), spiritual disturbance (active Spirit Realm Overlap events, PTL above threshold, supernatural insurgency component), provinces the Togashi has never visited before (novelty-seeking), and provinces with active investigation topics that lack a magistrate. The Togashi does not report to the local lord upon arrival and does not announce themselves.

**Behaviour on Arrival:** The Togashi’s actions are driven by the normal NPC motivation system with two unique modifiers: atypically high randomness in target selection (they do not optimise the way a courtier or bushi would), and a custom standing objective — HELP_STRANGER — weighted toward nearby NPCs in distress, active crises, or spiritual corruption. The Togashi might solve a mystery the local magistrate missed, challenge a corrupt official to a philosophical exchange that shames them publicly, protect a village from bandits with bare hands, or sit in a tea house and say something that reframes a local lord’s perspective on an intractable problem. The exact interaction is determined by the engine. After 1–3 seasons, the Togashi departs for another destination or returns home.

The system is lightweight: an NPC generation flag, a seasonal departure roll, a weighted destination selector, and a custom standing objective. Everything else runs through the existing NPC engine. From a design perspective, wandering Togashi are the Dragon’s primary method of exporting gameplay to the rest of the Empire — through individual monks rather than through armies or courtiers.

#### Gameplay Implications

A Dragon-aligned player’s core loop is defensive and investigative. The Dragon territory is nearly impregnable — the Dragonfly pass routing constraint means the player can defend their homeland with minimal forces while focusing AP on other activities. The Dragon’s army is small but synergistic (bushi-shugenja coordination from Section 11.6 makes their formations punching above their weight), and their alchemical crafting gives them access to unique items through the Tamori Furnace. The player’s primary outward-facing tools are Kitsuki magistrates (investigation excellence) and Togashi wanderers (unpredictable intervention). The Dragon do not build favour networks, do not dominate courts, and do not conquer territory — they solve problems other clans cannot solve, and they do it through individual excellence rather than institutional force.

The Dragon’s weakness is projection. They cannot fight wars far from home — their army is too small and their supply lines too limited. They have minimal court influence because they rarely attend. Their economy is modest — mountain provinces produce less Rice and Koku than the fertile plains of the Crane or Lion. A Dragon player who tries to play an aggressive expansionist game will fail. A player who plays to the Dragon’s strengths — defence, investigation, individual intervention, and patience — will find the clan nearly impossible to dislodge and capable of decisive action at moments of their choosing.

### Scorpion Clan

“I will be your villain, Hantei.” The Scorpion are the covert operations clan. Where the Lion solve problems with armies and the Crane solve them at court, the Scorpion solve them in the dark — through espionage, assassination, blackmail, poison, and information warfare. They are the Empire’s institutional specialists in everything dishonourable. The first Emperor understood that Bushido alone could not hold the Empire together — someone had to do the work that honour forbids — and he tasked his brother Bayushi with that role. Every generation of Scorpion has carried that mandate forward, doing what is necessary and accepting the hatred that comes with it.

The Scorpion are not incompetent at conventional warfare or court politics. The Bayushi field a solid army and their courtiers are skilled manipulators. But the Scorpion’s defining advantage is their mastery of the space between the rules — the covert actions, Low Skills, and dishonourable methods that other clans use reluctantly if at all. The Scorpion use them systematically, institutionally, and with school techniques designed to make them more effective at it than anyone else in the Empire.

#### Special Mechanic: Covert Operations Supremacy

Every clan has access to the covert ActionIDs (BRIBE_FOR_INFO, EAVESDROP, FABRICATE_SECRET, INTERCEPT_LETTER, SEARCH_QUARTERS — Section 14 Category 6). Every clan can commission assassinations through Section 12.8. The Scorpion’s advantage is not exclusive access — it is institutional superiority across three dimensions:

**1. Shosuro Shinobi:** The Scorpion are the only clan with schools dedicated to producing professional espionage operatives. The Shosuro Infiltrator and Shosuro Actor schools (Section 29) train shinobi whose school techniques grant specific bonuses to covert ActionIDs — bonuses no other clan’s agents can access. A Shosuro Infiltrator executing EXECUTE_ASSASSINATION rolls with school technique bonuses that a Daidoji or Ikoma attempting the same action simply does not have. The shinobi are a small and specialised group — at most a few hundred operatives across the entire clan — but they are the best in the Empire at what they do. They are recruited primarily from the Shosuro family, with a minority from the Soshi. Shinobi do not dress as the popular “ninja” myth suggests — they disguise themselves as servants, peasants, minor samurai, or specific named individuals. The ninja myth exists precisely as a distraction from real shinobi operations.

**2. Reduced Honour Bleed:** The Scorpion’s doctrine of “necessary villainy” creates a cultural framework that partially insulates its members from the Honour costs of dishonorable actions performed on behalf of the clan. A Bayushi courtier who uses BRIBE_FOR_INFO, GOSSIP, or FABRICATE_SECRET loses less Honour than a Crane or Lion performing the same action, because the Scorpion have institutionalised dishonour as a tool rather than a personal failing. This is a passive school-level modifier applied to Honour loss calculations for Bayushi Courtier and Shosuro school types when the dishonorable action serves a clan objective. It does not eliminate Honour loss — it reduces it. And it does not apply to genuinely selfish betrayals or actions against the clan’s own interests. The Scorpion tolerate necessary villainy, not unnecessary villainy. PROVISIONAL: exact Honour reduction modifier values pending playtesting.

**3. Shosuro Poisons:** The Shosuro Gardens at Shiro no Shosuro are the Empire’s foremost source of toxic compounds. The Shosuro family’s apothecary tradition is dual-purpose — they produce both medicines and poisons, and their expertise in one informs the other. Poisons are craftable consumables using the CRAFT ActionID (Category 11) with Craft: Poison / Intelligence. The material type is exotic botanical reagents. Poisons connect to the assassination system (Section 12.8) as a method modifier — a poisoned assassination attempt uses the EXECUTE_ASSASSINATION pipeline with method set to “poison” — and to combat as consumable weapon coatings (Complex Action on the ASCII map). The full poison catalog — 4 poison types with effects, Craft TNs, Heal TNs, creation residue mechanics, weapon coating rules, and delivery channels — is in Section 57.48. NOTE: additional poison types to be designed in a future pass — the system is extensible.

#### The Four Families

**Bayushi — The Public Face:** The ruling family. The Bayushi are the Scorpion that the Empire sees — darkly charismatic courtiers, ruthless politicians, and competent warriors who fight with an edge of cruelty their opponents find unnerving. They field the clan’s conventional army and operate its military dojo. The Bayushi Courtier school (Section 29.15.13) produces the clan’s primary court operators — manipulators who accumulate leverage, exploit weakness, and use information as a weapon. The Bayushi Bushi school produces solid fighters who seek every dishonorable advantage available: poison on a blade, a feint that exploits an opponent’s injury, a pre-battle insult that rattles composure.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Bayushi Courtier SchoolType): Elevated weights on GOSSIP, ACQUIRE_LEVERAGE, BRIBE_FOR_INFO, and PROBE. Bayushi courtiers proactively seek secrets and leverage against every NPC they interact with — allies included. They maintain higher-than-average known_topics lists because they never stop collecting information. In free-choice situations they favour actions that generate leverage over actions that generate goodwill. NPC Behavior — Lean (Bayushi Bushi SchoolType): Elevated weights on exploit-weakness actions in combat. On the mass battle grid (Section 11.6), the Bayushi Bushi’s +2 Attack against enemies below 50% Morale reflects their doctrine of finishing what others have softened.

**Shosuro — Spies and Assassins:** The clan’s covert operations arm. The Shosuro hold second place in public prominence — a position they carefully cultivate to avoid attention. To most Rokugani, the Shosuro are entertainers and actors who provide distraction for the Bayushi. In reality, they provide the bulk of the clan’s spies, saboteurs, and assassins. The Shosuro Butei Academy (the acting school at Shiro no Shosuro) trains the clan’s most potent infiltrators — aspiring actors compete to attend, and those who graduate become the Empire’s most skilled operatives. The Shosuro also maintain the Gardens — the clan’s poison production facility. Shosuro NPCs are designed to be bland, unremarkable, and forgettable — they train to accentuate being nondescript.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Shosuro Infiltrator SchoolType): Elevated weights on all covert ActionIDs — EAVESDROP, SEARCH_QUARTERS, INTERCEPT_LETTER, EXECUTE_ASSASSINATION, FABRICATE_SECRET, SHADOW_TARGET, CONCEAL_ITEM. School technique bonuses apply to these rolls. In free-choice situations, Shosuro Infiltrators prioritise intelligence gathering and mission preparation over social engagement. They avoid drawing attention. NPC Behavior — Lean (Shosuro Actor SchoolType): Elevated weights on Acting, Sincerity (Deceit), and social infiltration actions. Shosuro Actors seek postings at courts and settlements where they can observe, gather information, and maintain cover identities. Their acting troupe role provides legitimate cover for extended stays in foreign territory.

**Soshi — Air Shugenja:** The clan’s spiritual core. The Soshi are Air-affinity shugenja who specialise in spells of illusion, concealment, observation, and communication. They do not fight on the front lines — they remain in the background, enhancing the clan’s espionage capabilities through magical support. A Soshi can render a shinobi invisible, create illusory distractions, eavesdrop through walls with Air magic, or spread disinformation that appears to come from a trusted source. The Soshi are also theologically educated — well-versed in the Tao and Fortunist doctrine — and some use that knowledge to manipulate believers, viewing religious faith as another lever rather than a genuine spiritual commitment.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Soshi Shugenja SchoolType): Elevated weights on illusion and concealment spells, information-gathering rituals, and support actions for Shosuro operatives. Soshi NPCs avoid direct confrontation and avoid drawing attention to themselves. In court they function similarly to the Bayushi but with a magical toolkit — using spells to enhance their intelligence gathering rather than social skill alone.

**Yogo — The Cursed Wardens:** The most infamous family in the Empire. Founded by the Phoenix shugenja Yogo, who was cursed during the First War against Fu Leng — a curse that passed to his bloodline and has haunted every generation since. The Yogo’s centuries of research into their curse made them experts in fighting the Shadowlands Taint and Jigoku corruption. In the seventh century they founded the Kuroihan (Black Watch), a secret sect dedicated to combating maho. The Yogo and the Kuni of the Crab Clan have found common cause in this work — both families are shunned by their own clans and by the Empire, and they cooperate against Shadowlands threats even when their clans are at odds. Yogo magic specialises in wards and protective charms — lasting magical phenomena inspired by their intimate understanding of curses. They have guarded the Black Scrolls that bound Fu Leng for nearly a thousand years.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Yogo Wardmaster SchoolType): Elevated weights on anti-maho investigation, ward creation, and Taint-related INVESTIGATE_THREAT actions. Yogo NPCs are dour, reclusive, and introspective. They avoid social engagement and prefer solitary work. The Kuroihan sub-faction carries an additional standing objective weighted toward detecting and suppressing maho activity — functionally similar to the Phoenix Inquisitors but operating in secret rather than with institutional authority.

#### Bushido Profile

Scorpion NPCs have the most skewed Bushido virtue weights in the game. Chugi (Duty/Loyalty) is extremely high — higher even than the Lion’s. A Scorpion will not betray a true friend or ally under any circumstances. Gaining a Scorpion’s genuine loyalty is one of the most valuable things in the game because it is absolute. Every other Bushido virtue — Gi (Honesty), Jin (Compassion), Rei (Courtesy), Meiyo (Honour), Makoto (Sincerity), Yu (Courage) — carries dramatically reduced weight. The Scorpion do not value these virtues as moral imperatives; they view them as tools to exploit in others and as expectations to subvert when necessary. This makes Scorpion NPCs the polar opposite of the Lion: completely predictable in their loyalty, completely unpredictable in their methods.

#### Gameplay Implications

A Scorpion-aligned player’s core loop is information warfare. The primary activities are accumulating leverage on other clans’ NPCs, deploying shinobi for intelligence gathering and targeted elimination, using Bayushi courtiers to manipulate court proceedings through blackmail and fabricated secrets, and employing Soshi shugenja to enhance all of the above. The Scorpion’s military is competent but not dominant — they cannot match the Lion in the field or the Crab on the Wall. Their economy is moderate. What they have is information superiority: the Scorpion know more secrets about more people than any other clan, and they use that knowledge as their primary strategic weapon.

The Scorpion’s weakness is trust. Everyone knows the Scorpion lie, cheat, and manipulate. Baseline disposition toward the Scorpion is lower than toward any other clan. Building genuine alliances is harder because every offer is suspected of concealing a trap. The Scorpion compensate by being indispensable — they solve problems no one else will touch, they do the work that honour forbids, and they deliver results. A player who manages the Scorpion’s reputation carefully — being reliably useful while maintaining just enough fear — will find the clan’s covert capabilities make them the most dangerous political force in the Empire. A player who overplays the villainy will find themselves isolated and destroyed by a coalition of everyone they’ve wronged.

### Crab Clan

The Crab are the Empire’s shield. For over a thousand years they have held the Kaiu Wall against the Shadowlands, absorbing an unending assault that would annihilate any other clan within a generation. This single obligation defines everything about them — their culture, their economy, their politics, their relationships with every other clan in Rokugan. Other clans see the Crab as crude, blunt, and culturally unsophisticated. The Crab see other clans as children who have never faced a real threat. Both are right.

The Crab are the most resource-intensive clan in the game. They naturally outperform other clans economically (the Yasuki Koku bonus) and militarily (the Hida are the toughest warriors in Rokugan), but every advantage they possess is perpetually consumed by the Wall. The jade they acquire is ground into powder and left on Shadowlands battlefields. The Koku their merchants generate is spent on maintenance, repairs, and garrison wages. The soldiers they train die on the Wall or return Tainted. The population growth malus (Section 2.4.12) bleeds their provinces of the men who would otherwise farm, trade, and grow. The Crab are strong enough to hold the Wall. They are never strong enough to do anything else as well as they could without it.

#### The Five Families

The Crab’s five-family structure is not decorative — it is the minimum viable organisation for holding the border. Each family serves a distinct, non-substitutable function. A Tower with strong walls but no Kuni coverage slowly poisons the province behind it. A Tower with clean ground but no Kaiu maintenance slowly crumbles. A garrison without Hiruma scouts operates blind. An army without Yasuki financing cannot buy the jade that keeps its soldiers alive. Remove any one family from the equation and the Wall fails within a generation.

**Hida — The Warriors:** The ruling family of the Crab Clan and the largest source of its military strength. Hida bushi garrison the Twelve Towers, man the Wall, and form the backbone of every Crab field army. They are widely regarded as the toughest warriors in the Empire — not the most skilled (that distinction belongs to the Crane and Lion), but the most physically resilient and the hardest to kill. A Hida Bushi at a fully maintained Tower is one of the most difficult units in the game to dislodge. The Hida Champion holds ultimate authority over the Wall and the clan, following standard succession rules (Section 22.5). The Champion’s defining tension is resource allocation: the Wall demands constant commitment, but the clan also needs field armies, political presence at court, and relationships with other clans to survive. The full Crab Champion AI decision logic is defined in Section 2.4.14.

**Hiruma — The Scouts:** The Hiruma lost their ancestral homeland to the Shadowlands centuries ago. Their castle, Shiro Hiruma, stands deep in Shadowlands territory — visible from the Wall on clear days, a constant reminder of what the clan has already lost. This history defines the Hiruma identity: they are survivors, scouts, and guerrilla fighters who operate beyond the Wall in the most hostile terrain in Rokugan. Every Hiruma Scout knows the way home — the direction of the Empire — even in the deepest Shadowlands (Hiruma Scout Rank 1 technique). Intelligence on incoming hordes is obtained exclusively through active Hiruma scouting (Section 2.4.4). A Crab player with no scouts deployed beyond the Wall receives no warning of approaching hordes — the first sign is the garrison already in combat. Hiruma Scouts are named characters operating in lethal conditions. They can die in the Shadowlands. They are not expendable, but they are irreplaceable for the intelligence they provide.

**Kaiu — The Engineers:** The Kaiu built the Wall and they are the only ones who can maintain it. Koku-based maintenance prevents seasonal SI decay (Section 2.4.3), but only a Kaiu Engineer can restore lost Structural Integrity through FORTIFY_WALL_SECTION or rebuild a breached section through SEAL_WALL_BREACH (Section 2.4.16). A Kaiu Engineer stationed at a Tower sets a Kaiu Reinforcement modifier that buffers against extraordinary decay — horde assault damage and budget shortfalls — scaled to the engineer’s School Rank. The modifier holds at full strength for a fixed duration then drops, requiring the Kaiu to return. Twelve Towers, limited Kaiu Engineers: the rotation is the game. A Rank 3 Kaiu can maintain four towers on a seasonal cycle. A Rank 5 can cover five. The Crab never have enough to cover all twelve simultaneously. Beyond the Wall, the Kaiu are also the Empire’s finest siege engineers and fortification designers — skills that make them valuable allies to any clan building or besieging castles.

**Kuni — The Purifiers:** The Kuni family is split into two distinct roles that complement each other but do not overlap. The Kuni Shugenja are priests who purify Tainted land through PURIFY_TAINTED_GROUND (Section 2.4.17), monitor garrison spiritual health (Section 2.4.11 Decision 7), and maintain the Kuni Wards that slow Jigoku’s creep into Crab provinces. All provinces directly adjacent to the Shadowlands accumulate +0.1 PTL per season as a baseline (Section 2.4.2) — without periodic Kuni Shugenja purification, even well-maintained Wall provinces slowly become spiritually inhospitable, threatening Tea cultivation and eventually spawning Province Taint Manifestation insurgencies (Section 11.11). The Kuni Witch Hunters are a bushi school, not priests — trained warriors who range across Crab territory and beyond, hunting Taint in people rather than land. They investigate maho use, monitor Tainted individuals, track Bloodspeaker cells, and eliminate the Lost. Their work drives the INVESTIGATE_THREAT motivation weight against Maho_Cult_Spreading and Low_level_Taint_Discovered topics. The Shugenja heals the ground; the Witch Hunter hunts what walks on it.

**Yasuki — The Financiers:** Originally a Crane family that defected to the Crab during the First Yasuki War, the Yasuki are the finest merchants in the Empire and the most commercially minded samurai family in Rokugan. The samurai caste publicly despises commerce — and the clan that depends most heavily on commerce is the one standing between the Empire and annihilation. The Yasuki Koku bonus (0.35 Koku per town PU per season, compared to the baseline 0.25) is the primary source of Wall financing (Section 2.4.15). Without it, the Crab could not sustain jade purchases, SI maintenance, and garrison upkeep simultaneously. The Yasuki also manage the jade trade: negotiating supply contracts, maintaining the trade networks that keep materials flowing to the Wall, and handling the diplomatic relationships that keep other clans selling their jade rather than hoarding it. The Hida hold the Wall. The Hiruma scout beyond it. The Kaiu build and repair it. The Kuni cleanse the Taint it faces. The Yasuki pay for all of it.

#### Governance and Command

The Crab follow standard clan governance: the Hida Champion at the apex, Family Daimyo beneath, Provincial Daimyo and Local Daimyo filling out the hierarchy. Succession follows the standard rules in Section 22.5. The unique Crab governance feature is the Wall command structure (Section 2.4.9) — a dedicated military hierarchy separate from the clan’s field armies. Two Shireikan (Southern Wall Commander covering Towers 1–6, Northern covering Towers 7–12) oversee the twelve Tower Commanders (Taisa). When unified command is required, the Champion elevates one Shireikan to Rikugunshokan with full Champion-level Wall authority. The Wall command chain is a standing instance of the Operational Hierarchy system (Section 11.3.18) — Taisa report to Shireikan, Shireikan report to Champion or Rikugunshokan, assignments are permanent for the duration of the posting.

#### Gameplay Implications

Playing in Crab territory means engaging with the most mechanically dense system in the game. The Wall is not a background threat — it is a living resource sink that demands constant personnel rotation, supply management, and strategic triage across twelve towers. The core gameplay loop for a Crab-aligned player involves: managing Kaiu Engineer and Kuni Shugenja rotation across towers and provinces; interpreting Hiruma Scout intelligence to decide where to concentrate garrison strength; balancing Wall spending against field army needs and political obligations at court; navigating the jade trade and Brotherhood relationships that keep Tea flowing to the garrison; and responding to discrete horde assaults that test everything the player has built.

The Crab are militarily powerful but diplomatically vulnerable. They need other clans — for jade (the Imperial monopoly means non-Crab mines must sell to them, but political leverage can delay delivery), for food (Crab border provinces are predominantly Mountains terrain, producing below subsistence), and for military aid when the Wall faces a crisis too large to contain alone. A Crab player who ignores court politics will find their supply lines threatened by clans who have no reason to prioritise Crab needs. A Crab player who neglects the Wall to play politics will find towers crumbling and provinces Tainted. The tension between the two is the Crab experience.

### Phoenix Clan

**Special Mechanic: The Elemental Council**

The Phoenix Clan is governed by a unique dual power structure that reflects its canon lore. The Shiba Clan Champion holds the title of Clan Champion officially, but wields little real authority. True power rests with the Isawa Elemental Council — five Masters, one for each Ring — who collectively control all major decisions. The Champion’s role is ceremonial leadership, diplomatic representation, and military command of an army the Council must first authorise.

#### The Reincarnation of Shiba

When the Clan Champion dies, the soul of the divine Shiba reincarnates into a randomly selected living Shiba character, who becomes the new Champion. This means succession is entirely unpredictable: the new Champion may be young or old, a soldier or a courtier, prominent or obscure. Their prior stats, personality, and relationships carry over from their previous life — only the title transfers. Players cannot engineer Phoenix succession the way they can manipulate inheritance in other clans. Attentive players may watch for omens — whispers from the Void Master, or unusual behaviour among Shiba NPCs — that hint at who the next vessel will be.

#### The Council Vote

Major decisions — going to war, deploying armies, enacting grand rituals — require a majority vote among the five Elemental Masters. There is no Isawa Family Daimyo; the Council jointly owns and administers all Isawa resources. Any expenditure or commitment above a threshold requires a majority vote. Each Master has a disposition shaped by their element:

**Fire — **Aggressive, decisive, volatile. Pushes for action.

**Water — **Adaptive, pragmatic, opportunistic. Follows advantage.

**Air — **Subtle, political, deceptive. Prefers court to battlefield.

**Earth — **Conservative, defensive, stubborn. Resists change and war.

**Void — **Inscrutable. Driven by omens and prophecy rather than politics. A genuine wildcard whose vote cannot be easily predicted or bought.

#### Gameplay Implications

The Council structure makes the Phoenix militarily slow but politically resilient. They almost never agree to offensive war, making Phoenix territory difficult to conquer through — but they rarely expand. Their deterrent is the grand ritual: a unanimous Council can unleash catastrophic elemental effects on a province. Players embedded in Phoenix court can influence individual Masters, shifting votes. Assassinating or discrediting a single Master shifts the vote balance without reducing the clan’s resource pool. A 2–2 deadlock with the Void Master abstaining pending an omen is a recognised crisis state that can be triggered, exploited, or resolved by player action.

#### The Asako Family — Inquisitors and Henshin

The Asako are the Phoenix’s scholars, historians, and diplomats — the family most likely to be found at Winter Court, in Imperial libraries, and embedded across other clans. Beneath that calm exterior they maintain two distinct hidden institutions: the Inquisitors and the Henshin.

#### The Asako Inquisitors

The Inquisitors are not a bespoke system. They are Asako NPCs with a strong INVESTIGATE_THREAT motivation weight against the existing topic types Maho_Cult_Spreading and Low_level_Taint_Discovered. When either topic becomes active in a province, nearby Inquisitor NPCs will autonomously route toward the source, interact with local characters, and either resolve or escalate the event — entirely within the existing NPC motivation and movement architecture.

The player intersects with this only situationally: if they are near an active maho event, if they become a person of interest in an investigation, or if they choose to assist or obstruct the Inquisitors. Their cross-clan jurisdiction is represented by high baseline disposition with Imperial magistrate NPCs and a mandate flag that suppresses normal clan-border friction when a maho topic is active. No new systems required.

#### The Henshin

The Henshin are a monastic order unique to the Asako family, housed in remote Phoenix monasteries. They are distinct from the Brotherhood of Shinsei and operate independently within Phoenix territory. They carry no special mechanics at this stage and may be developed further in a later pass.

### Unicorn Clan

The Unicorn are the mobility clan. They are the only Great Clan to fight primarily from horseback, and their cavalry forces are the fastest, most devastating open-field military in the Empire. Founded as the Ki-Rin Clan by the Kami Shinjo, they were sent beyond Rokugan’s borders at the Empire’s founding to explore the world and search for external threats. They did not return for over eight hundred years. When they came back, they brought foreign customs, gaijin blood, alien magic, and a worldview that no other clan could understand or fully trust.

Their strength is speed and versatility. Unicorn armies move faster than any other force on the World Map, their cavalry charges are the most devastating single-engagement action available, and their patrol coverage — mounted magistrates and Shinjo scouts ranging across the open plains — makes their territory the hardest in the Empire for criminals and insurgents to operate in. They are also the most compassionate clan toward the common people, a trait rooted in centuries of depending on non-samurai for survival in foreign lands.

Their weakness is court. The Unicorn are blunt, direct, and culturally foreign. Eight centuries of gaijin customs left them with habits that other clans find anywhere from eccentric to offensive — they eat red meat, they treat their horses as near-equals, they negotiate with an honesty that more sophisticated clans read as naivety or rudeness. Only the Ide family’s patient diplomacy keeps the clan from complete political isolation, and even the Ide cannot fully bridge the cultural gap. The Kolat conspiracy’s deep infiltration of the Shinjo family (Section 54.7) further damaged the clan’s reputation, and the stain has not fully faded.

#### Special Mechanic: Cavalry Supremacy

The Unicorn are the only clan with a true cavalry tradition. Other clans field mounted units — Light Cavalry is a generic unit available to all clans (Section 11.6) — but no other clan breeds warhorses at Unicorn scale, trains riders from childhood, or has developed the tactical doctrine to use massed cavalry as a primary battlefield arm. The Unicorn advantage is not a single unique rule but the cumulative weight of multiple existing systems all working in their favour simultaneously.

Mass Battle (Section 11.7): The mass battle system already differentiates cavalry through terrain modifiers, flanking mechanics, and reserve commitment. Light Cavalry gains +4 Attack when flanking, cannot be counter-attacked while flanking, and receives an additional +2 flanking bonus on Flat Plains. Heavy Cavalry held in the Reserve Row can be committed as charges into the Forward Row. Terrain restricts cavalry: forests, mountains, and urban terrain strip flanking bonuses and impose Attack penalties. The Unicorn exploit these mechanics disproportionately because they field more cavalry Companies than any other clan. A Unicorn army on flat terrain deploys multiple flanking cavalry units where other clans might have one, and commits heavy cavalry charges from reserve with units (White Guard, Utaku Battle Maidens) whose elite stat blocks far exceed generic Light Cavalry. On open plains, the Unicorn are the most dangerous army in the Empire. In forests, mountains, or urban terrain, their core advantage is neutralised.

Individual Combat (Section 40): The Mounted/Higher status grants +1k0 on attack rolls against unmounted or lower characters. Mounted characters cannot use the Full Attack Stance unless they have Horsemanship Rank 3 (which most Unicorn bushi do). Horses are semi-independent combatants — a controlled horse moves on the rider’s Turn but attacks on its own Turn in Initiative order, using its own stat block. Three horse tiers exist: Rokugani Ponies (combat-reluctant, require Horsemanship/Awareness TN 20 every round to attack), Unicorn Riding Horses (war-trained, single TN 10 roll at start of battle), and Utaku Battle Steeds (fight willingly with no roll, Fear 2 when charging, Reduction 5, stay at rider’s side even if she is dead). Horsemanship Mastery Rank 5 reduces mounting to a Simple Action; Rank 7 makes it a Free Action. The practical result is that a mounted Unicorn bushi in ASCII map combat is effectively two combatants — the rider attacking with +1k0 and the horse attacking independently — while most other samurai fight on foot.

The Utaku Battle Steed Bond: The Utaku Battle Steed’s source stat block already establishes the core of this bond — the steed fights intelligently without any skill roll, stays with its rider even in death, and inspires Fear 2 when charging. What the game adds is persistence: a Battle Maiden’s mount is a named entity tracked on the character sheet, not an interchangeable piece of equipment. If the mount is killed, the Battle Maiden suffers a temporary Willpower penalty representing grief and spiritual disruption. Replacement mounts can be bonded, but the process takes a full IC season — an Utaku without her steed is diminished for a meaningful period, not just until the next requisition. At high School Ranks, the bond deepens: the rider may use the higher of her own or the mount’s Initiative, and both may attack in the same action rather than on separate Turns. This is the most powerful individual mounted combat mechanic in the game, restricted to female Utaku characters trained at the Battle Maiden school.

World Map Army Movement: An army composed entirely of cavalry units moves 2 sub-tiles per real day on Plains terrain instead of the standard 1. On all other terrain types (forest, hills, mountains, river crossings), cavalry armies move at normal speed — the advantage is strictly an open-ground phenomenon. Any non-cavalry unit in the army reduces the entire force to normal speed — cavalry moves at the pace of its slowest element, same as any other army. This makes a pure Unicorn cavalry force the only army in the game that can cross two sub-tiles of open ground in a single day, allowing rapid redeployment, pursuit of retreating forces, and response to threats before slower armies arrive. The restriction is absolute: a single company of ashigaru infantry drops the army to standard movement. A Unicorn lord who wants speed must leave the foot soldiers behind.

#### The Six Families

The Unicorn’s six-family structure reflects the clan’s unusual history. Five families survived the long journey through the Burning Sands and returned to the Empire as established institutions. The sixth — the Horiuchi — was founded after the return and served a specialised role before being destroyed by supernatural catastrophe. Unlike the Crab’s five families, where each serves a non-substitutable military function, the Unicorn’s families overlap more in capability and diverge more in philosophy. The unifying thread is mobility: every Unicorn family, from the scholarly Iuchi to the diplomatic Ide, operates on the assumption that remaining in one place too long is a strategic error.

**Shinjo — Rulers and Scouts:** The founding family. Lady Shinjo led the Ki-Rin Clan beyond the Empire’s borders and her descendants ruled the clan for over eleven centuries. The Shinjo are scouts, rangers, and explorers — the clan’s eyes across its vast territorial holdings. They are also the most compassionate family in the Unicorn, a trait inherited from their founder’s embrace of the lower classes during the long wandering. Shinjo samurai are more likely than any other clan’s ruling family to listen to peasant complaints, act on heimin grievances, and treat the bonge as people rather than resources. This is not political calculation — it is genuine conviction, rooted in centuries of depending on commoners for survival in hostile foreign lands. The Shinjo are optimistic, resilient, and blunt. They embrace Honesty as their core Bushido tenet and their directness frequently creates friction with more politically sophisticated clans. The Kolat conspiracy’s deep infiltration of the Shinjo family (Section 54.7) devastated their reputation in the twelfth century, and while the purge removed the corruption, the clan’s magistrate corps — which was predominantly Shinjo — suffered lasting reputational damage across the Empire.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Shinjo Bushi/Scout SchoolType): Elevated scoring weight on patrol, scouting, and movement-related actions (ORDER_PATROL, reconnaissance, travel). Shinjo NPCs in free-choice situations gravitate toward information-gathering and territorial coverage over static garrison duty. When assigned as magistrates, Shinjo NPCs carry a disposition bonus toward bonge and heimin witnesses in the Investigation Loop (Section 11.3.13), reflecting the family’s tradition of actually listening to peasant testimony. This does not override the Testimony Weight formula (Section 11.3.10) — it adjusts the NPC’s willingness to seek out low-status witnesses that other magistrates might overlook.

**Moto — Cavalry Warriors:** The Moto are not originally Rokugani. Their ancestors were the Ujik-Hai, steppe nomads from the Burning Sands who were absorbed into the Ki-Rin Clan during the long wandering. Their gaijin heritage is still physically visible — taller stature, darker skin, sharper features, and more facial hair than native Rokugani. The Moto are the clan’s shock cavalry: aggressive, fearless, and perfectly willing to die on a charge rather than retreat. They are famous for their bloodlust in combat and their utter indifference to courtly manners. The fall of Moto Tsume to the Shadowlands and the resulting disgrace — when the family abandoned its red chrysanthemum mon for a white kabuki mask and swore to destroy their own fallen kinsmen — defined the Moto for generations. The surviving Moto in Rokugan became more ruthless, more determined, and more isolated, forming the elite White Guard to hunt the Dark Moto in the Shadowlands. The family’s shame lasted nearly three centuries before they were rehabilitated as clan leaders. The Moto are cunning and deadly warriors, but they have little interest in or aptitude for politics. They let the Ide handle diplomacy and the Shinjo handle governance, preferring direct action over discussion.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Moto Bushi SchoolType): Elevated scoring weight on aggressive military actions (ORDER_BATTLE, CONDUCT_RAID, cavalry charge positioning) over defensive or diplomatic options. Moto NPCs in free-choice situations prefer direct action over negotiation. High baseline Yu (Courage) and low baseline Rei (Courtesy) personality weights make Moto NPCs the most aggressive and least politically cautious Unicorn characters. When ordered to attend court by their lord, Moto NPCs comply but carry reduced effectiveness on all social actions except INTIMIDATE, which receives a bonus — the Moto’s reputation for violence is itself a form of social leverage.

**Utaku — Battle Maidens:** The Utaku (formerly Otaku) are unique in Rokugan: their combat school is restricted to women. The Battle Maidens are the most disciplined, most elite cavalry force in the Empire — where the Moto charge with fury, the Utaku charge in perfect silence, horse and rider moving as a single entity. The family’s founder, Otaku, was one of Shinjo’s first followers and one of the original Seven Thunders. She never swore a formal oath of fealty to Shinjo — or if she did, she never spoke of it — and this unspoken bond became a family tradition of action over words. Utaku Battle Maidens charge in silence, speak carefully, and dislike idle conversation. The mystical horse-bond described under Cavalry Supremacy above is the mechanical expression of this tradition. The men of the Utaku family are forbidden from fighting on horseback by long custom. They serve as horse breeders, stable masters, and — if they insist on combat roles — infantry. There are no finer horse breeders in the Empire, and Utaku steeds are coveted by every clan, though the family rarely sells its best stock outside the Unicorn. The Utaku possess a natural grace inherited from their ancestor and could make an impression at court if they chose, but they generally hold court life in low regard and consider political manoeuvring beneath them.

NPC Behavior — Hard Gate (Utaku Battle Maiden SchoolType): personality_filter.json removes all mounted combat ActionIDs for male characters with this SchoolType — the gate is keyed to gender, not family name. A male Utaku who somehow trained at the Battle Maiden school (edge case — should not occur in normal generation) would still be blocked. The Battle Maiden lean elevates cavalry charge and mounted patrol actions above all other combat options. Utaku Battle Maidens ordered to fight dismounted (in terrain that prevents mounted combat) fight as standard bushi with no special bonuses — their entire advantage is horse-bound. An Utaku Battle Maiden whose mount is killed mid-mission suffers the Willpower penalty described in the Cavalry Supremacy mechanic and will prioritise withdrawal from combat until a replacement mount is bonded.

**Ide — Diplomats:** The Ide are the Unicorn’s face to the Empire and the only reason the clan is not completely politically isolated. Founded by a man who was patient, intelligent, self-collected, and opposed to senseless violence, the Ide developed a diplomatic philosophy called wabukan — “the peaceful path” — during the clan’s long wandering. The Ide served as negotiators with every foreign civilisation the Ki-Rin encountered, and they brought that skill home to Rokugan. They are calm, friendly, well-organised, and capable of finding unorthodox solutions to seemingly intractable problems. The Ide were responsible for brokering the Unicorn’s first alliances with the Crane and Crab after the clan’s return, and they have since diversified into trade, Imperial bureaucracy, and magistrate service. The Ide are also the family most responsible for preserving Rokugani traditions within the clan during the long wandering, making them paradoxically both the most foreign-wise and the most traditionally Rokugani of the Unicorn families. The modern Ide believe that conflict usually stems from misunderstanding, and they are master negotiators and problem-solvers — though their solutions are usually advantageous to the Unicorn.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Ide Emissary SchoolType): Elevated scoring weight on diplomatic and disposition-raising actions (CHARM, NEGOTIATE, PERSUADE) with a cross-clan targeting preference — Ide NPCs proactively seek out characters from other clans rather than building internal relationships. In court situations, Ide NPCs carry elevated weight on LISTEN_REFLECT and READ_CHARACTER, reflecting their training in understanding what other people actually want before making an offer. The Ide lean also elevates WRITE_LETTER and SEND_INVITATION weights, representing the family’s extensive use of correspondence to maintain relationships across distances. When disposition between the Unicorn and another clan drops below Neutral, Ide NPCs assigned to diplomatic roles will autonomously prioritise actions to repair the relationship.

**Iuchi — Shugenja:** The Iuchi are the Unicorn’s priests, but they practise magic in ways that unsettle every other shugenja tradition in the Empire. During the long wandering, the Iuchi encountered gaijin magical traditions that operated without the kami — directly inscribing power into words and symbols rather than petitioning elemental spirits. The founder, Iuchi, studied these practices extensively, and his descendants developed meishodo, a form of name magic that uses Rokugani characters to produce spell-like effects without kami intermediation. What matters for the clan profile is that meishodo makes the Iuchi theologically suspect in the eyes of more orthodox shugenja families — particularly the Isawa, who regard any magic that bypasses the kami as borderline blasphemous. The Iuchi are open-minded, experimentally driven, and believe there is no single correct way to practise magic. They are also among the most battle-ready shugenja in the Empire, having learned during the wandering that pacifism in hostile territory is a death sentence. They develop techniques to support the Unicorn’s cavalry with speed enhancements and battlefield control rather than the raw elemental destruction favoured by the Isawa.

NPC Behavior — Lean (Iuchi Shugenja SchoolType): Elevated scoring weight on support and mobility magic over direct offensive casting. Iuchi NPCs in army compositions prioritise speed-enhancing and terrain-control spells. In free-choice situations, Iuchi NPCs carry elevated weight on magical research and experimentation actions, and they are more willing than other shugenja SchoolTypes to investigate unusual magical phenomena (spirit realm overlaps, foreign artefacts, anomalous kami behaviour). The Iuchi lean does not restrict offensive magic — unlike the Asahina hard gate, the Iuchi will fight — but it biases their spell selection toward support roles when both options are available.

**Horiuchi — Meishodo Specialists (Extinct):** The Horiuchi were the smallest and youngest Unicorn family, founded in 1119 when a young Iuchi shugenja-ko named Shoan saved Clan Champion Shinjo Yokatsu’s children from a bandit raid. Granted the privilege of founding her own family line, Shoan and her followers specialised in meishodo and were charged with patrolling the borders of the Shinomen Forest near the sleeping Naga. The Horiuchi never numbered more than a couple of hundred samurai. They were wiped out during the Kali-Ma Invasion of the late twelfth century, when a supernatural plague centred on their lands forced the Clan Champion to burn their settlements and decree the Horiuchi name extinct. The dozen survivors were ordered to swear fealty to other Unicorn families or retain their name until death without passing it on. At game start, there may be a handful of elderly Horiuchi NPCs still alive — remnants of a dead family, carrying a name that will vanish within a generation. They are a narrative curiosity, not a mechanical system.

NPC Behavior: No SchoolType lean. Any surviving Horiuchi NPCs use the Iuchi Shugenja lean as their school of origin.

#### Gameplay Implications

A Unicorn-aligned player’s core loop is mobility. The primary activities are exploiting the clan’s speed advantage on the World Map to patrol territory, respond to threats faster than rivals, and project military power across distances that would take other clans seasons to cross. In open-field combat, the Unicorn are devastating — a fully mounted Moto cavalry charge is one of the highest single-turn damage outputs in the game, and Utaku Battle Maidens are elite individual combatants who fight as extensions of their mounts. In siege warfare, the Unicorn are weak: cavalry cannot charge walls, and the clan lacks the engineering tradition of the Crab or the defensive infantry doctrine of the Crane’s Daidoji. A Unicorn player who gets drawn into a siege on either side is fighting at a disadvantage.

Court is the Unicorn’s most significant vulnerability. The clan’s foreign customs, blunt communication style, and lingering reputational damage from the Kolat scandal mean that Unicorn courtiers start with a social handicap that only the Ide can partially offset. A Unicorn player who neglects court entirely will find other clans making decisions about Unicorn territory without Unicorn input. A Unicorn player who invests heavily in court through Ide operatives can leverage the family’s unique cross-cultural perspective — the Ide understand how other clans think in ways that native Rokugani courtiers sometimes cannot, precisely because they learned diplomacy by dealing with people who shared no common culture at all.

The Unicorn’s compassionate magistrate tradition (Section 11.3) creates a distinctive law enforcement experience. Unicorn magistrates — predominantly Shinjo — are more willing to hear peasant testimony, more likely to investigate crimes against commoners, and more likely to pursue justice for the lower classes than magistrates from any other clan. This does not make them soft: Unicorn magistrates are mounted, fast, and patrol aggressively across their open plains, making Unicorn territory among the hardest in the Empire for bandits and insurgents to operate in. The combination of compassion and mobility means that peasant satisfaction in Unicorn provinces tends to be higher than average, suppressing the conditions that generate peasant revolt insurgencies (Section 11.11).
