## 2.6 The Spirit Realms

Rokugan exists within a cosmological web of Spirit Realms — parallel planes of existence that border, interpenetrate, and influence the mortal world in ways both subtle and catastrophic. Each realm embodies a specific principle or state of being, and each exerts a constant pressure on Ningen-do, the Mortal Realm. The Celestial Order dictates that these realms remain in balance, with Tengoku above providing divine mandate and Jigoku below providing the corruption that defines evil. Mortals die and pass through Meido before their souls are judged. Heroes ascend to Yomi. The wicked are claimed by Jigoku or lost in Gaki-do. Spirits populate Chikushudo and Sakkaku. Warriors condemned by fate linger in Toshigoku. Those whose destinies were stolen haunt Maigo no Musha. Dreamers brush against Yume-do. Every realm described below can manifest as a physical location, an event trigger, or a long-term political factor in the game.

Source: Secrets of the Empire (Chapter Five: The Spirit Realms). All game effects below describe mechanical interactions as implemented in this Godot adaptation.

### 2.6.0 The Celestial Order

The realms are arranged in a vertical hierarchy that mirrors Rokugan’s understanding of the universe. At the apex sits Tengoku, the Celestial Heavens, home of the Kami, Fortunes, and the Celestial Dragons. At the nadir lies Jigoku, the Realm of Evil. Between them, the mortal realm of Ningen-do occupies a precarious middle position, constantly pulled in both directions. The Heavens jealously guard their realms — each realm is controlled by a powerful being or force that views outside interference as a threat, and the barriers between realms are maintained by this mutual jealousy as much as by divine law.

When the barriers between realms thin — through maho, powerful emotional events, spiritual imbalance, the actions of Oracles, or the intervention of Celestial Dragons — both wonders and horrors can cross into Ningen-do. The game tracks realm stability as a background variable affecting regional events, haunting frequency, spiritual phenomena, and the power of shugenja magic in affected areas.

**The Realms at a Glance:**

- Tengoku — The Celestial Heavens: Divine law, Kami, Fortunes, Celestial Dragons

- Yomi — The Realm of Blessed Ancestors: Honored dead, ancestral spirits, heroes

- Yume-do — The Realm of Dreams: Dream-consciousness, prophetic visions, the unformed

- Chikushudo — The Realm of Animals: Animal spirits, nature spirits, shapeshifters

- Sakkaku — The Realm of Mischief: Trickster spirits, mischief, chaos without malice

- Ningen-do — The Mortal Realm: The world of the game

- Maigo no Musha — The Realm of Thwarted Destiny: Spirits whose fate was stolen from them

- Meido — The Realm of Waiting: All souls pass through here after death for judgment

- Toshigoku — The Realm of Slaughter: Warriors condemned to eternal battle

- Gaki-do — The Realm of the Hungry Dead: Hungry ghosts driven by insatiable craving

- Jigoku — The Realm of Evil: Oni, Lost, Shadowlands corruption, Fu Leng’s domain

### 2.6.1 Chikushudo — The Realm of Animals

Also known as: The Animal Realm, the Spirit Forest

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Chikushudo exists in parallel to the mortal world, a reflection of Ningen-do seen through the eyes of animals and nature spirits. Where Ningen-do has a road, Chikushudo has a game trail. Where Ningen-do has a fortress, Chikushudo has a rocky outcropping. The realm is not so much a separate place as a different way of perceiving the same space — experienced most strongly in forests, mountains, and wilderness far from human settlement. Animal spirits (kami of animals and nature) inhabit this realm, and some particularly sacred or ancient animals can cross between the realms at will. The Fox Clan’s Kitsune family has the strongest mortal connection to Chikushudo, through generations of intermarriage with kitsune fox spirits.

**Denizens:**

Animal spirits of all kinds, from minor nature kami to powerful fox spirits (kitsune) and other shapeshifting beings. The most powerful denizens are the spirit lords of major animal species, ancient beings of considerable power who have no interest in mortal politics but react violently to perceived threats to their domains. The koumori (bat spirits allied to the Bat Clan) also have a significant presence. Chikushudo is also home to nue — composite supernatural creatures — and various spirits that do not fit neatly into other classifications.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Chikushudo is not controlled by a single ruler but rather by a loose confederation of powerful spirit lords. The realm is relatively accessible compared to other Spirit Realms — places where Chikushudo and Ningen-do overlap are found throughout Rokugan’s wilderness, particularly in the Kitsune Mori (Fox Forest), the Shinomen Naga Forest, and remote mountain wilderness. Shugenja with Earth Affinity or the Kitsune school can interact with Chikushudo more easily than others. The Kitsune family of the (later) Mantis Clan acts as a bridge between the two realms.

**Game Effects:**

- Wilderness areas with strong Chikushudo overlap generate occasional animal spirit encounters — these can be benign, hostile, or informative depending on context and the character’s standing with nature spirits.

- Characters who gain the enmity of a Chikushudo spirit lord may find animals in entire regions behaving unnaturally — refusing to be ridden, fleeing hunting parties, or actively interfering with travel.

- Shugenja with Earth Affinity may attempt to Commune with Chikushudo kami in wilderness settings; success can yield weather information, knowledge of nearby threats, or identification of Tainted areas.

- The Kitsune Mori (home of the Fox Clan) is permanently marked as a Chikushudo overlap zone — characters entering it may experience spirit encounters regardless of magical ability.

- Strong overlap zones exist near the Shinomen Forest (Naga territory) and around several sacred mountains. These are flagged on the World Map as spiritually active provinces.

### 2.6.2 Gaki-do — The Realm of the Hungry Dead

Also known as: The Realm of Hungry Ghosts

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Gaki-do is the realm claimed by those who died in the grip of intense, unfulfilled craving — obsessive greed, addiction, insatiable hunger, compulsive lust for power or pleasure. The realm itself reflects this nature: it is a distorted mirror of Ningen-do filled with apparent abundance that always proves insubstantial. A table of food dissolves into ash when touched. A chest of gold turns to dirt. The gaki (hungry ghosts) are condemned to pursue their obsessions forever, never achieving satisfaction. Unlike the righteous dead of Yomi, gaki retain their personalities and memories but are wholly subsumed by their hungers. Some were samurai who broke their oaths for personal gain. Others were merchants who sacrificed family for wealth. A few were shugenja who pursued forbidden power.

**Denizens:**

Gaki come in many varieties, each defined by the specific craving that condemned them. The most common are food-gaki (who hunger for physical sustenance), wealth-gaki (who hunger for material possession), and power-gaki (who hunger for status and authority). Some gaki are relatively harmless — pitiable creatures seeking to fulfill their obsessions in harmless ways. Others are deeply dangerous, willing to destroy mortals to briefly satisfy their cravings. The realm’s most powerful denizen is Emma-O, the Fortune of Death, who holds authority over all the dead who pass through Meido but has particular responsibility for ensuring condemned souls reach Gaki-do rather than lingering in the mortal world.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Gaki-do bleeds into Ningen-do most strongly in places associated with great indulgence, tragic waste, or the deaths of those consumed by obsession — abandoned pleasure districts, sites of great famines, locations where merchants died clutching their wealth. The realm is not controlled by a single ruler; it is more accurately described as governed by the weight of accumulated sorrow and hunger. Emma-O’s jurisdiction ensures condemned souls reach this realm, but he does not rule within it. Breaking into Gaki-do is strongly discouraged — even the Kitsu family of the Lion, who have the strongest mortal connection to the dead, rarely venture there intentionally.

**Game Effects:**

- Haunting events can manifest as gaki encounters in locations with strong Gaki-do overlap — typically sites of famine, great indulgence, or obsessive deaths.

- Characters who display excessive greed, addiction behavior, or obsessive craving over multiple sessions may attract the attention of Gaki-do denizens — a subtle spiritual penalty tracked by the game.

- A province afflicted by repeated famine may develop persistent Gaki-do bleed — crops that fail for no explicable reason, livestock that starves despite adequate feed, or villagers experiencing collective hunger hallucinations.

- Gaki can sometimes be bargained with: offering them a brief taste of what they crave in exchange for information or safe passage. This is considered deeply dishonorable and risks attracting more of them.

- Jade and obsidian weapons are effective against gaki. Shugenja can banish them with Earth magic. A proper funeral rite performed by a shugenja or Brotherhood monk can prevent a recently-dead soul from becoming a gaki.

### 2.6.3 Jigoku — The Realm of Evil

Also known as: The Shadowlands, the Pit, Fu Leng’s Domain

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Jigoku is not merely a place — it is the embodiment of corruption itself, the antithesis of everything the Celestial Order stands for. Unlike the other Spirit Realms, which exist in a kind of equilibrium with the mortal world, Jigoku actively seeks to expand, to corrupt, to consume. It does not merely reflect a spiritual state; it actively creates one. The Shadowlands — the vast corrupted wasteland south of the Kaiu Wall — is the most visible manifestation of Jigoku’s influence on Ningen-do: a region where the barrier between realms has been so thoroughly breached that the two have partially merged. Fu Leng, the fallen Kami cast into Jigoku before the founding of the Empire, rules this realm — though ‘rules’ is perhaps too orderly a word. He is the realm’s most powerful entity, but Jigoku’s nature is chaos, and even Fu Leng must constantly assert his dominance over the realm’s other monstrous inhabitants.

**Denizens:**

Oni are the primary denizens of Jigoku — enormous demonic entities born of the realm’s corruption, each unique in form and ability, each defined by the particular vice or evil that spawned them. Oni require a mortal name to fully manifest in Ningen-do; a shugenja or maho-tsukai who grants an oni their name in exchange for power is making a literal pact with destruction. The Lost are humans so thoroughly consumed by the Shadowlands Taint that their souls have been claimed by Jigoku; they retain their intelligence and skills but serve the realm’s corruption entirely. Bog hags, ogres, goblins, zombies, and undead of all kinds fill out Jigoku’s armies. The Shadowlands Taint — the corruption that physically manifests in those touched by Jigoku — is the realm’s most insidious weapon, converting the living into servants.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Jigoku is the most aggressive of the Spirit Realms, constantly seeking to breach the barriers between worlds. The Kaiu Wall exists specifically to contain the most direct incursion of Jigoku’s influence into Ningen-do. The maho-tsukai (blood sorcerers) of the Bloodspeaker Cult and similar organizations deliberately create openings for Jigoku by using blood magic — every act of maho weakens the barriers slightly. Fu Leng’s influence can be channeled by those willing to accept the Taint, granting power at the cost of eventual corruption and damnation. Jade is the primary ward against Jigoku’s influence — it is spiritually antithetical to the realm and burns corrupted creatures. Obsidian, paradoxically, is also effective, being a material native to Jigoku that has been purified.

**Game Effects:**

- The Kaiu Wall is the primary game-mechanical barrier between Jigoku and the Empire. Its condition — tracked as a World Map variable — directly affects the frequency and severity of Shadowlands Incursion crises.

- Shadowlands Taint is tracked numerically on affected characters. Taint accumulates through contact with Jigoku-touched entities, use of maho, and exposure to heavily corrupted areas. Each Rank of Taint imposes mechanical penalties and eventually alters personality.

- Oni manifestations are triggered by named crises. An oni requires a mortal name to fully enter Ningen-do — this is a vulnerability exploitable by the player, who can potentially prevent an oni from fully manifesting if the name-granting is discovered and stopped.

- Maho use is tracked and creates a hidden spiritual debt — each use has a chance to generate a local Jigoku bleed event (unexplained deaths, Tainted animals, disturbing omens) in the province where it was cast.

- The Lost cannot be redeemed. A samurai who reaches Taint Rank 5 is considered lost to Jigoku and becomes an NPC under GM control, hostile to all former allies.

- Jade supplies are a strategic resource tracked at clan level. Clans that neglect jade acquisition face increased Taint vulnerability during Shadowlands Incursion events.

### 2.6.4 Maigo no Musha — The Realm of Thwarted Destiny

Also known as: The Realm of the Lost

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Maigo no Musha is the realm claimed by those whose destinies were stolen from them — not those who failed through their own weakness or corruption, but those who were prevented from fulfilling their rightful purpose by the actions of others or by cruel circumstance. A samurai assassinated before completing a great mission. A craftsman killed before finishing his masterwork. A parent who died before seeing their child grow to adulthood. These souls are not condemned — they are merely incomplete, and the realm reflects this: it is a grey, directionless place neither beautiful nor terrible, defined by a pervasive sense of unfinished business. The spirits here are often desperate, not malicious, though desperation can become dangerous. They seek completion: a final act, a message delivered, a duty fulfilled, before they can pass on to their proper rest.

**Denizens:**

The musha (warrior spirits) of this realm are its most distinctive denizens. These are samurai spirits still bound by their unfulfilled duties, often retaining full memory and personality, sometimes appearing almost entirely solid in the mortal world. They are not ghosts in the traditional sense — they do not haunt from malice but from need. More dangerous are the onryou — spirits whose thwarted destiny has curdled into rage and resentment, who have lost the ability to distinguish between those who wronged them and innocent bystanders. The realm also contains a significant number of innocent spirits — children who died young, peasants killed by war or famine before their time.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Maigo no Musha is not controlled by any single entity. The realm simply is — a collecting pool for souls that don’t fit neatly into other categories. Kitsu family shugenja of the Lion Clan are the Empire’s foremost experts in communicating with and assisting the spirits of Maigo no Musha; their ancestor communion rituals can sometimes allow a spirit to fulfill its final duty and pass on. Toritaka bushi (formerly of the Falcon Clan, now part of the Crab) specialize in the more martial aspect of dealing with these spirits when they become dangerous. The realm bleeds into Ningen-do most strongly at sites of great injustice, betrayal, or unfulfilled oaths.

**Game Effects:**

- Spirits from Maigo no Musha are the most common source of haunting events in the game. Unlike Gaki-do hauntings (which manifest as environmental corruption) these are typically personal — a ghost seeking a specific individual, item, or action.

- Quests generated by Maigo no Musha spirits are among the most narratively rich in the game: a ghost may task the player with completing their unfinished duty, delivering a message, avenging an injustice, or simply witnessing a final act.

- Fulfilling a spirit’s final request earns significant Honor and Glory bonuses. Destroying an onryou that cannot be reasoned with earns lesser but still meaningful rewards.

- The Kitsu family shugenja school has specific mechanical advantages when interacting with Maigo no Musha spirits — bonus dice on Commune rolls, ability to identify what a spirit needs without extended interaction.

- Broken oaths and betrayals in the game world accumulate as spiritual debt. A player or NPC who repeatedly betrays oaths without consequences may eventually attract Maigo no Musha attention — the spirits of those wronged by broken promises.

### 2.6.5 Meido — The Realm of Waiting

Also known as: The Realm of the Dead, Emma-O’s Domain

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Meido is not a destination but a transit point — the realm through which all mortal souls pass after death, awaiting judgment before being assigned to their proper place in the cosmic order. It appears to each soul differently, reflecting their life and personality back at them. The righteous see a peaceful landscape. The guilty see their worst acts replayed. The confused see nothing but fog. Emma-O, the Fortune of Death, rules Meido with absolute authority — he is severe, just, and utterly impartial. His court of judges reviews each soul’s life and assigns them to the appropriate realm: the heroic to Yomi, the condemned to Gaki-do or Toshigoku, the innocent to be reborn, the corrupt to Jigoku. Emma-O is not cruel; he is necessary. His court is the most comprehensive bureaucracy in the universe.

**Denizens:**

Emma-O’s bureaucracy is staffed by spirits who were once meticulous administrators in life — clerks, judges, and officials who now serve a cosmic function. The shinigami (death kami) serve as Emma-O’s enforcers, ensuring souls reach Meido and do not linger inappropriately in Ningen-do. Kitsu ancestors commune through Meido, and the Kitsu shugenja school’s most advanced techniques involve petitioning Emma-O directly. The realm also briefly holds the recently dead who have not yet been processed — a soul may linger at the edges of Meido for some time after death, accessible to sufficiently powerful shugenja or through specific ritual.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Emma-O controls Meido absolutely and does not welcome mortal visitors. Attempts to enter Meido while living are extraordinarily dangerous and require either divine permission, extreme magical power, or exploitation of a newly-dead soul’s brief lingering at the border. The Kitsu family has the closest legitimate access — their ancestor communion rituals allow them to speak with souls on the near side of Meido without actually entering. The Kuni family of the Crab have methods for communicating with recently-dead souls at the moment of death, though these are considered morbid by most standards. Resurrection — recalling a soul from Meido back to the living world — is theoretically possible but requires extraordinary spiritual power and Emma-O’s grudging cooperation.

**Game Effects:**

- When a named character dies, their soul enters Meido. A brief window (measured in game ticks) exists during which certain shugenja can attempt to communicate with the newly-dead soul — potentially gaining information about the circumstances of their death or delivering final messages.

- Resurrection mechanics exist but are extremely costly: requiring high-Rank shugenja, significant jade expenditure, and an Honor check. Failed resurrection attempts carry spiritual penalties.

- Emma-O’s judgment affects how NPCs are remembered — a character who died with significant Honor receives ancestor spirit benefits for their descendants. One who died in dishonor generates no such benefit.

- The Fortune of Death can be petitioned (never demanded) for information about the recently dead. This is legal but considered an unusual and significant favor, requiring substantial offerings.

- Ancestor veneration mechanics are tied to Meido: characters whose ancestors were judged favorably receive modest but meaningful passive bonuses. Characters who dishonor their family accumulate an ancestral debt that generates negative events.

### 2.6.6 Sakkaku — The Realm of Mischief

Also known as: The Realm of Tricksters

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Sakkaku is one of the most dangerous Spirit Realms not because its denizens are evil — they are not, by Jigoku’s definition — but because they are entirely amoral and treat the mortal world as a source of amusement. The realm itself is a chaotic, ever-shifting place that defies consistent description: rooms that rearrange themselves, paths that lead somewhere different each time, objects that are never quite where you left them. Its denizens find genuine delight in confusion, misfortune, and the comedic suffering of those who take themselves too seriously. Most critically for mortals: the trickster spirits of Sakkaku are extraordinarily gifted at deception, illusion, and manipulation — and they lie pathologically.

**Denizens:**

Kitsune fox spirits are the most famous denizens of Sakkaku, though they also have a significant presence in Chikushudo. In Sakkaku they are at their most deceptive — ancient kitsune can maintain human guises so perfect that only spiritual detection reveals them. Tanuki (raccoon dog spirits) are another common presence, shapeshifters even more prone to mischief than the kitsune. The realm also contains various bakemono (shapeshifting tricksters), will-o-wisps, and a variety of minor spirits whose primary purpose seems to be leading mortals in circles. The most powerful denizens are the Kuroiban-class kitsune — ancient fox spirits of immense power who have transcended mere mischief and pursue inscrutable agendas of their own.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Sakkaku is not controlled by any single ruler — the very idea of imposing order on this realm is comically impossible. It bleeds into Ningen-do most strongly along the borders of the Kitsune Mori and in places associated with illusion, deception, and confusion. A shugenja with Air Affinity can sometimes detect Sakkaku overlap — the kami in affected areas become unusually chatty and unreliable. The Scorpion Clan’s shugenja have historically had more experience with Sakkaku entities than most, given their shared interest in deception, though they find the trickster spirits frustratingly uncontrollable.

**Game Effects:**

- Sakkaku overlap zones generate random mischief events: travel delays, minor equipment failures, inexplicable rumors, items that mysteriously relocate.

- Kitsune NPC encounters can occur in or near the Kitsune Mori. These are always ambiguous — the fox spirit may be genuinely helpful, entirely deceptive, or both simultaneously.

- Characters who rely heavily on deception in their social interactions may attract Sakkaku attention — drawing the interest of trickster spirits who see kindred souls.

- Any divination attempted near a Sakkaku overlap zone has a chance of returning deliberately misleading information — the kami are being unhelpful on purpose.

- Tanuki spirits can be bargained with using sake and food — they are hedonists above all, and temporary cooperation can be purchased. The cooperation will not outlast their boredom.

- Sakkaku denizens cannot be permanently banished — only inconvenienced. Attempting to destroy them tends to amuse them. The most effective counter is to be genuinely uninteresting.

### 2.6.7 Tengoku — The Celestial Heavens

Also known as: The Heavens, the Divine Court

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Tengoku is the highest of the Spirit Realms, the seat of divine authority, and the source of the Celestial Order that governs all of reality. It is beautiful beyond mortal comprehension — a realm of perpetual harmony, where conflict exists only as a necessary force of creative tension rather than destructive struggle. The Kami reside here — the divine children of Amaterasu and Onnotangu, those who fell to Rokugan at the founding of the Empire and later ascended back to the Heavens after death. The Fortunes also reside here, as do the Celestial Dragons — immense beings of near-infinite power who embody the fundamental elements and forces of existence. Fu Leng’s absence from Tengoku — he was cast directly into Jigoku before the founding — is the original wound in the Celestial Order, the source of all corruption that followed.

**Denizens:**

The Kami (Hida, Doji, Togashi, Akodo, Shiba, Bayushi, Shinjo, and others) reside in Tengoku after death, watching over their mortal descendants. The Fortunes have their divine courts here, receiving prayers and dispensing blessings. The Celestial Dragons — the Dragon of Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Void, Thunder, and others — are not personalities in the mortal sense but rather forces of existence given form. Lady Sun (Amaterasu) and Lord Moon (Onnotangu) whose interactions and conflict established the current age’s celestial order also reside here in their transformed states. The most notable absence is Ryoshun, the Tenth Kami, who died within his father’s stomach before the Fall — his grave in the northern mountains is guarded by the Badger Clan.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Tengoku does not interact with the mortal world directly — to do so would violate the Celestial Order it embodies. Instead, it communicates through the Fortunes (who can manifest in the mortal world under specific circumstances), through Oracles (mortals chosen by the Celestial Dragons as conduits), through dreams and omens, and through the ambient spiritual pressure of divine will that makes the Empire function. The Emperor’s authority is explicitly derived from Tengoku’s mandate — the ‘divine right to rule’ is not metaphorical in Rokugan; it is a literal spiritual connection between the Emperor’s bloodline and the Celestial Court. When an Emperor is unworthy, Tengoku withdraws its mandate, and events conspire to replace him.

**Game Effects:**

- The Emperor’s divine mandate is tracked as a game variable. An Emperor whose actions consistently violate Tengoku’s values — gross dishonor, persecution of the innocent, neglect of ritual duties — accumulates Mandate Loss. Sufficient Mandate Loss triggers crisis events suggesting heavenly disapproval.

- Fortune shrines throughout the Empire generate passive bonuses when properly maintained. Neglected shrines generate negative events. Destroyed shrines generate significant Honor loss and spiritual penalties for the responsible party.

- The Oracles of the Celestial Dragons appear as occasional event triggers — cryptic messages that provide genuine (if obscure) guidance. Unlike Sakkaku tricksters, Oracles cannot lie, but they can be deeply unhelpful.

- Ancestor veneration connects to Tengoku through Yomi. Characters who maintain proper rituals and honor their ancestors receive minor but consistent spiritual bonuses.

- Divine intervention — a Fortune directly manifesting — is an extremely rare event reserved for moments of extraordinary crisis or extraordinary virtue. It cannot be reliably induced and should be treated as a game master event rather than a player resource.

- The Kami occasionally communicate with their clan Champions through dreams and omens. These events are scripted triggers tied to major clan decisions.

### 2.6.8 Toshigoku — The Realm of Slaughter

Also known as: The Realm of Endless Battle

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Toshigoku is the realm claimed by those who died consumed entirely by bloodlust, battle-rage, or the mindless desire for violence — warriors who found meaning only in killing and could not imagine existence without it. The realm reflects their nature perfectly: it is an endless battlefield where the dead fight eternally, dying and reviving to fight again, without purpose, without meaning, without end. Unlike the warriors of Yomi, who retain their nobility, the denizens of Toshigoku have been reduced to their most primitive essence — the killing instinct stripped of all context. The realm is not pleasant, but it is in a terrible way exactly what its inhabitants always wanted. Most of them, by now, have forgotten they ever wanted anything else.

**Denizens:**

The spirits of Toshigoku are called musha — warrior shades — though they should not be confused with the more dignified spirits of Maigo no Musha. These are hollow things, defined entirely by their desire to fight. Some retain enough personality to communicate, briefly, before the killing urge reasserts itself. The most dangerous denizens are the ancient generals of Toshigoku — warriors who died in the earliest ages of the Empire, consumed by millennia of battle, who have developed a tactical intelligence driven entirely by the desire to bring ever-more-interesting conflicts into existence. These beings are not entirely sane by mortal standards, but they are terrifyingly competent.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Toshigoku has no ruler, but its most powerful inhabitants have carved out territories in the endless battle. The realm bleeds into Ningen-do most strongly at the sites of great battles — places where thousands died in short order, saturating the spiritual landscape with violence. Bloodfields, the aftermath of major sieges, and sites of famous last stands can all develop Toshigoku overlap. Unlike most other Spirit Realm bleeds, which require some triggering condition, Toshigoku overlap tends to be self-reinforcing — violence attracts spirits of violence, which encourages more violence.

**Game Effects:**

- Battlefields where large numbers died generate Toshigoku bleed over time. Provinces with high Battle Score accumulation become spiritually ‘hot’ — experiencing increased bandit activity, samurai dueling challenges, and occasional spirit warrior sightings.

- Characters with very high Aggressiveness virtue scores (Shourido scale) are at risk of Toshigoku attraction — spirits drawn to those who share their fundamental nature. This is not Taint, but it is a spiritual vulnerability.

- The Matsu Berserker school has an unusual relationship with Toshigoku — their battle rage technique is known to draw spirit attention. High-rank Matsu Berserkers occasionally experience visitations from Toshigoku warrior spirits who wish to test them.

- A Toshigoku spirit successfully summoned to the mortal world fights with terrifying effectiveness but cannot be controlled — it attacks the nearest combatant regardless of allegiance.

- Extended sieges and high-casualty battles that occur in the game’s tactical layer can permanently mark a province as a Toshigoku bleed zone — a lasting consequence of particularly brutal conflicts.

### 2.6.9 Yomi — The Realm of Blessed Ancestors

Also known as: The Heavenly Fields, the Ancestral Realm

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Yomi is the reward for a life lived with honor — a realm of beauty, peace, and continued existence for those who fulfilled their duties, maintained their virtue, and died with their soul intact. It is not identical to Tengoku (which is reserved for the divine) but is the closest mortal souls can aspire to reach. In Yomi, the dead retain their personalities, their wisdom, and their connections to the living world, though they are no longer bound by mortal limitations. They can observe the mortal world, though they cannot directly intervene except through the specific channels of ancestor communion. The realm is genuinely beautiful — a perfected version of Rokugan’s finest landscapes, where the dead can pursue whatever brought them joy in life, free from the urgencies of political intrigue and military duty.

**Denizens:**

The blessed ancestors themselves, particularly the heroic dead of all clans and families. The Kitsu family of the Lion Clan acts as the primary bridge between Yomi and the mortal world — their shugenja school is built around ancestor communion, and the most powerful Kitsu can speak with Yomi’s inhabitants in detail. The Fortune Goemon (formerly Matsu Goemon) has his divine court in the border region between Yomi and Tengoku. The Ancestors available as Advantages in character creation derive their mechanical effects from the connection between their Yomi existence and their descendants in Ningen-do.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Yomi is accessed through ancestor communion rituals performed by shugenja — most notably the Kitsu. The Kitsu family maintains the most sophisticated understanding of Yomi’s geography and can navigate it with relative safety. Other shugenja can attempt ancestor communion but lack the Kitsu’s specialized training. The realm itself is not hostile to mortal visitors — it is welcoming — but reaching it requires passing through Meido, which is far less welcoming. The blessed ancestors of Yomi generally wish to help their descendants and will provide guidance when properly petitioned, though they cannot act directly in the mortal world.

**Game Effects:**

- Ancestor Advantages (purchased at character creation or earned through extraordinary deeds) represent active connections to Yomi — an ancestor who watches over their descendant and provides occasional assistance.

- Ancestor veneration rituals performed regularly generate small but consistent mechanical bonuses for the character’s family and household.

- The Kitsu shugenja school has explicit ancestor communion mechanics: they can ask questions of deceased NPCs through Yomi connection, potentially gaining information unavailable any other way.

- A character who dies with high Honor has their soul judged positively and sent to Yomi. Their descendants receive a one-time Honor bonus and a permanent minor ancestor spirit bonus.

- The ancestors in Yomi can be petitioned for guidance during crises — a formal ritual that costs Action Points and requires a shugenja, but can provide significant strategic information from a perspective unclouded by mortal political concerns.

- Ancestor Disadvantages (from the Heritage Tables) represent ancestors who ended up somewhere other than Yomi — in Gaki-do, Toshigoku, or Maigo no Musha — and whose unresolved spiritual state creates ongoing difficulties for living descendants.

### 2.6.10 Yume-do — The Realm of Dreams

Also known as: The Dream Realm

**Nature ****&**** Purpose:**

Yume-do is perhaps the most enigmatic of the Spirit Realms because every mortal enters it every night — and remembers almost nothing of the experience upon waking. It is the realm of the unconscious mind, of half-formed thoughts and symbolic visions, of the unprocessed fears and hopes that mortals carry but cannot fully articulate. Unlike the other Spirit Realms, Yume-do does not reflect a specific spiritual state or destination for souls; it is more of a medium through which the boundaries between minds and between realms become temporarily permeable. Prophetic dreams — visions of genuine future events, past events, or distant present events — occur when Yume-do’s barriers thin enough for real information to bleed through the symbolic dream-language.

**Denizens:**

Dream spirits and entities native to Yume-do are poorly understood because interaction with them is inherently indirect. The Baku — dream-eating spirits — are the most commonly identified, beings that feed on intense dreams (particularly nightmares) and can be petitioned to remove particularly damaging dreamscapes. Mu spirits are entities of pure potential, shapeless beings that take form from the dreamer’s own psyche. The most dangerous entities are those that have learned to cross from Yume-do into Ningen-do in a limited way — using sleeping mortals as a bridge. These beings can cause real physical harm through dream-attacks, though this requires extraordinary power or a particularly thin barrier between realms.

**Control ****&**** Access:**

Yume-do does not have a ruler in any conventional sense. The Dragon of Dreams (one of the Celestial Dragons) has nominal oversight, but the realm is too inherently chaotic for any form of control. Some shugenja schools — particularly those with Air Affinity or Void focus — have developed techniques for maintaining consciousness during the transition into Yume-do and navigating the dream-space with intention. The Tonbo Shugenja of the Dragonfly Clan have an unusually refined understanding of Yume-do, and their ancestor Tonbo Senkensha was famous for prophetic dreams. The Senkensha vassal family sometimes manifests her gift.

**Game Effects:**

- Prophetic dreams are a scripted event type — triggered by specific game conditions (approaching major crises, critical decision points, supernatural attention) and delivered as dream-vision sequences to the player character.

- Characters with the Divination skill at high rank can attempt to interpret dream imagery, potentially extracting specific information from otherwise symbolic content.

- The Tonbo Shugenja school has specific dream-interpretation mechanics not available to other schools.

- Persistent nightmares — triggered by Taint accumulation, witnessing traumatic events, or Yume-do bleed — impose minor but cumulative psychological penalties until addressed (typically through a shugenja’s ritual cleansing or Brotherhood meditation).

- Yume-do bleed in a region manifests as collective unusual dreaming among the population — a province event that may indicate an approaching crisis, a powerful spirit’s attention, or an unstable barrier between realms.

- Dream-walking — entering Yume-do with conscious intention — is an advanced and dangerous technique available only to high-Rank shugenja with appropriate training. Success can yield valuable information; failure can trap the shugenja in the dream-space.

### 2.6.11 Spirit Realms & Game Design Philosophy

The Spirit Realms are not merely lore — they are a systemic layer that affects almost every major game system. Each realm contributes to a category of recurring events, NPC behaviors, and crisis types. The key design principle is that the Spirit Realms are always present but rarely visible. They press against the mortal world continuously, and the game’s events reflect this pressure without necessarily explaining it. A village that burns for no apparent reason may be Gaki-do bleed or Toshigoku overlap — or it may be mundane arson. The player must investigate.

The Spirit Realms also provide a framework for understanding why the Empire’s religious infrastructure matters mechanically. Properly maintained shrines, regular ancestor rituals, Brotherhood monastic presence, and clan shugenja networks all function as a distributed system of spiritual maintenance — keeping the barriers between realms stable, ensuring the dead reach their proper destinations, and preventing the accumulation of spiritual debt that manifests as crisis events. A player who neglects the spiritual layer of the game in favor of purely military or economic optimization will eventually face spiritual consequences that cannot be addressed with armies or koku.
