## 11.5 Festival System — LOCKED

Festivals are confirmed as a game feature. Two tiers exist: Empire-wide canonical festivals on fixed calendar dates, and local village festivals procedurally generated per settlement with randomized dates, names, and minor effects.

**Tier 1 — Empire-Wide Canonical Festivals:**

- New Year's Festival (1st day of the Hare, Spring): Province-wide stability bonus. Temples cleaned. Toshidana altars built.

- Cherry Blossom Festival (early Spring): Prayers to Kuroshin for the harvest. Natural ritual trigger for Kami worship system — planting season blessing.

- Iris / Doll Festival (25th day of the Serpent, Spring): Social event. Gift-giving. Minor morale bonus.

- Chrysanthemum Festival (6th day of the Horse, Summer): All labor halted Empire-wide for 7 days. Mandatory productivity penalty — no farming, mining, or construction. Cannot be overridden by any lord.

- Kanto Festival (2nd day of the Rooster, Autumn): Minor production bonus for the Autumn harvest tick.

- Setsuban Festival (8th day of the Monkey, Autumn): Mandatory single-day ceasefire — all battles halted, no blood shed. Armies in the field are frozen for one day.

- Bon Festival (28th day of the Dog, Autumn): Ancestors honored. Province-wide ancestor worship event. All characters who observe it properly generate 1 WP toward the Great Fortunes split evenly, and gain +0.1 Honor. Connects to the Kami Worship System (Section 4.3.21).

- Festival of the River of Stars (9th day of the Boar, Winter): Political marriages and alliance negotiations gain a bonus modifier during this period.

- New Year's Eve (28th day of the Tiger, Winter): Temples purged. Minor Fortune worship WP totals reset heading into the new year — bonus tiers must be re-earned each year through active worship.

**Tier 2 — Local Settlement Festivals — LOCKED:**

Each settlement has locally generated festivals on fixed calendar dates, assigned at world generation and permanent thereafter. Local festivals are pure flavor — they carry no mechanical effects. Their purpose is atmospheric: they make each settlement feel distinct, give NPCs something to reference in Daily Conversation, and provide texture when a player passes through. No two villages celebrate the same combination of festivals on the same dates. Local festivals are not visible on the World Map and do not generate topics.

**Generation Rules: **Villages generate 1–2 local festivals. Towns and castle towns generate 2–3. Fortifications and temples generate 0–1 (military and religious settlements have less communal civilian life). Each festival is generated independently: select a theme from the weighted theme pool, select a format word, combine them into a name, and assign a random calendar date avoiding overlap with Tier 1 canonical festivals and other local festivals already assigned to the same settlement. Dates are spread across the four seasons — a settlement with two festivals places one in Spring/Summer and one in Autumn/Winter to distribute them across the year.

**Name Generation: **Each festival name combines a format word with a theme. Format words: Festival, Night, Day, Viewing, Prayer, Gathering, Blessing, Offering, Watching, Dance, Feast, Procession, Vigil, Contest, Opening, Closing, Remembrance, Celebration, Morning, Eve. Formula: “The [Format Word] of the [Theme]” or “[Theme] [Format Word]” — the generator selects between the two patterns randomly. Examples: “The Night of the Firefly,” “First Snow Viewing,” “The Blessing of the Anchor Stone,” “Mochi Pounding Festival,” “The Vigil of the Fallen Heroes.”

**Theme Selection Weights: **Each theme carries weight tags for terrain type (plains, coast, hills, mountains, forest, delta), settlement type (village, town, castle town), and clan ownership. The generator draws from the full pool of 300 themes, weighted by the settlement’s attributes. A coastal village draws heavily from Maritime & Fishing and lightly from Mountain & Forest. A castle town in Lion territory draws heavily from Martial & Remembrance. A Phoenix mountain village draws from Spiritual & Purification and Mountain & Forest. Universal categories (Nature & Seasonal, Cultural & Social, Food & Drink, Domestic & Household, Historical & Local) carry equal weight everywhere and ensure even landlocked mountain fortresses have culturally grounded celebrations. No theme is hard-blocked from any settlement — weights make inappropriate themes rare, not impossible. A mountain village that generates a Tide Watching festival is a curiosity, not an error — perhaps the villagers’ ancestors came from the coast.

**Player and NPC Interaction: **A player present in a settlement on a local festival day sees a text notification describing the celebration. NPCs reference upcoming and recent local festivals in Daily Conversation. A player who asks about local customs learns the settlement’s festival calendar. No AP cost, no roll, no mechanical consequence — the festival is something happening around the player, not something the player does.

**ASCII Map Overlay Sets: **On a festival’s IC day, decorative tiles are placed on the settlement’s ASCII map. Seven overlay sets cover all 12 theme categories. (1) Harvest (Agricultural & Harvest): sheaf bundles and drying racks near field-edge tiles, colored cloth strips on fence posts. Targets the field or village entrance zone. (2) Shore (Maritime & Fishing): garlands draped on boat tiles at the dock, small offering bowls at the waterline, painted oar propped at the harbor entrance. Targets the dock zone. (3) Trail (Mountain & Forest): cairn tile at the village entrance, shimenawa rope strung between trees at the settlement edge, hanging herb bundles. Targets the entrance or forest-edge zone. (4) Garrison (Martial & Remembrance): banner tiles at the garrison or training ground, weapon rack tile with polished arms, torch tiles flanking the entrance. Targets the garrison or dojo zone. (5) Shrine (Spiritual & Purification, Animal & Wildlife): extra lantern tiles at the shrine, offering tiles on the altar, colored thread tiles on the shrine fence, swept gravel pattern. Targets the shrine zone. (6) Market (Craft & Trade): temporary stall tiles at the market area, display goods on cloth, tool rack tile. Targets the market or commercial zone. (7) Town Square (Nature & Seasonal, Cultural & Social, Historical & Local, Food & Drink, Domestic & Household): bonfire or lantern string tile at the central gathering point, NPC cluster seated around it, seasonal decorations — cherry branches in spring, colored leaves in autumn, snow rabbit in winter. Targets the central square. If a settlement lacks the matching target zone, the overlay defaults to Town Square. Every settlement has a central gathering point. Overlays appear on the festival’s IC day and are removed the next day.

**Collision Rule: **Freestanding festival objects block movement — bonfire tile, temporary stall tile, weapon rack tile, cairn tile, display rack tile, offering table tile. These are physical objects occupying a tile. Attached decorations do not change collision — garlands on boat tiles, banners on walls, lantern strings hanging overhead, cloth strips on fences, shimenawa strung between trees. The underlying tile’s collision rules stay unchanged. Festival overlays may alter foot traffic through the settlement for the day — the main square has a bonfire narrowing the path, the market has stalls reducing walkable space. NPCs and players route around them. The next day the objects are gone and paths are clear.

**Discoverability: **Local festivals do not generate formal Topics (no tier, no momentum, no personal relevance score). Instead, NPCs carry knowledge of their home settlement’s festival calendar as part of their character data. When a Daily Conversation or Deliberate PC-Initiated Conversation fires between a player and an NPC from a different settlement, the NPC may mention an upcoming home festival if the festival date falls within the current IC season. The mention enters the player’s known information through the existing conversation topic exchange, but is not a formal Topic — it is a conversation detail, like hearing about someone’s family or hometown. Example: a player at court has a Daily Conversation with a visiting samurai from a nearby village and hears “The Firefly Night is next week in Quiet Brook.” The player now knows and can choose to travel there. No formal propagation, no court relevance. Just a thing someone said.

**Rokuyo — The Lucky/Unlucky Day System — LOCKED:**

Every IC day carries a Rokuyo designation — a six-day rotating cycle that determines the day’s auspicious or inauspicious character. This is not a mechanical modifier to rolls but a social reality that characters and NPCs observe. Actions taken on inappropriate Rokuyo days are noted by the social world. NPCs will comment, invitations issued on bad days carry less weight, and weddings or duels scheduled on inauspicious days reflect poorly on the arranger.

- Sensho: Lucky before noon, unlucky after noon. Best for beginning new ventures in the morning. Duel challenges issued in the morning are considered well-timed.

- Tomobiki: Unlucky for friends and funerals. Crematoriums closed. Social invitations issued on Tomobiki days are mildly inauspicious. A lord who schedules a court session on Tomobiki loses a small amount of social credibility with observant NPCs.

- Senbu: Unlucky before noon, lucky after. Afternoon court sessions are appropriate. Morning ceremonies are not.

- Butsumetsu: Unlucky all day except at shrines. Shrine offices closed to non-religious business. A character who issues a duel challenge, makes a public declaration, or calls in a favor on Butsumetsu is considered crass.

- Taian: Lucky. The best day for weddings, formal alliances, new court sessions, and calling in favors. No mechanical modifier — Rokuyo is a social reality, not a die roll modifier.

- Shakko: Unlucky except the Hour of the Horse (11am–1pm in IC time). Only one IC action window (the midday AP slot) is considered auspicious.

Implementation: The Rokuyo cycle is calculated from the IC day number. Day 1 = Sensho. Day 2 = Tomobiki. Day 3 = Senbu. Day 4 = Butsumetsu. Day 5 = Taian. Day 6 = Shakko. Day 7 = Sensho again. The cycle never resets — it runs continuously through the year.

**Championship Contests — LOCKED:**

The major championship tests determine who holds the Empire’s most prestigious positions. These connect directly to Section 22.8 (Positions of Power). They are annual events without fixed dates — they occur at intervals determined by the political situation and the Emperor’s schedule.

- Test of the Emerald Champion: Determines the Emperor’s personal champion and head of the Emerald Magistrates. Multi-stage competition involving iaijutsu, court performance, and political acumen.

- Test of the Jade Champion: Determines the head of the Jade Magistrates, responsible for Taint detection and Shadowlands threats. Shugenja-focused competition.

- Test of the Amethyst Champion / Amethyst Championship: Selected by ranking members of the Imperial families based on knowledge of the blade, the gift of speaking (oratory and poetry), and honorable nature. The Amethyst Champion represents the courtier’s duty to clan and Empire. Welcome in every court in the entire Empire regardless of location. Must make several trips each year to spread the Emperor’s attention to distant courts.

- Test of the Ruby Champion: Martial championship determining the master sensei of the Emerald Magistrates and Imperial Legions. The Ruby Champion personally selects the training regime and teachers for thousands of bushi serving the Empire. Maintains roughly a dozen well-appointed dojo throughout the Imperial holdings.

- Test of the Turquoise Champion: Extensive competition between artisans of all types, judged by a panel from the Imperial families. The winner embodies the most sophisticated and cultured aspects of the samurai caste and determines by default which discipline of artistry will be in vogue for the coming year.

- Topaz Championship: Annual gempukku competition held in Tsuma (Crane lands). The Great Clans send a handful of their finest young samurai to compete in the most prestigious coming-of-age ceremony in the Empire. The winner is declared Topaz Champion for one year and is typically offered prestigious positions — most frequently as an Emerald Magistrate. A very small elite subset of Emerald Magistrates known as Topaz Magistrates consists exclusively of former Topaz Champions.

- Tournament of the Emperor’s Favored: Prestigious Imperial invitation tournament. Winning generates significant Glory.

- Tournament of the Samurai: General martial competition open to all clans.

- Tournament of Thunder: Named for the Thunder who faced Fu Leng. The most prestigious martial tournament in the Empire. Winning generates a permanent reputation topic.

- Spring Archery: Traditional archery competition at the beginning of the season.

- Kumitae Tournament: Multi-day martial tournament in Autumn (see Dated Festivals above).

Championship outcomes generate Promotion or Appointment Tier 4 topics immediately upon conclusion. The winner’s Glory increases significantly. Losers who performed well still gain minor Glory. A character who wins a championship and holds the position is tracked as a Position of Power holder in Section 22.8.

**Championship Trigger Conditions — LOCKED:**

Championships fall into two categories. Annual championships fire on a fixed calendar schedule regardless of vacancy — only the Topaz Championship operates this way. The current holder simply loses the title when the next winner is declared. Vacancy-triggered championships (Emerald, Jade, Amethyst, Ruby, Turquoise) fire only when the current holder dies, retires, is removed, or the position otherwise becomes vacant. The Emperor calls the championship — it does not happen automatically. There may be a gap of one to three seasons between vacancy and tournament depending on the Emperor’s priorities and the political situation. Additionally, the Emperor can call an extraordinary championship at any time, forcing the current holder to defend their position or replacing them outright. This is rare and politically explosive, generating a Tier 2 topic immediately upon announcement.

**Candidate Selection — LOCKED:**

Each Great Clan nominates one candidate for vacancy-triggered championships. The clan’s Champion (or their designated proxy) selects the nominee using a weighted evaluation similar to the heir evaluation system (Section 22.5), with factors specific to each championship type. The Amethyst Championship is an exception — candidates are selected by the Imperial families, not by clan nomination, though clans may petition to have a candidate considered. Minor Clans may nominate if the Emperor permits it (varies by championship and by Emperor personality). Ronin cannot compete in any championship except the Topaz, where they may be permitted under unusual circumstances. For the Topaz Championship, only characters undergoing gempukku that year are eligible — young samurai at Insight Rank 1. Each clan sends 2–3 of their finest new graduates.

Selection criteria by championship type: Emerald — highest composite of Battle, Iaijutsu, and Courtier skills; Bushi school type strongly preferred. Jade — highest composite of Spellcraft, Lore: Theology, and relevant elemental Ring; Shugenja school type required. Amethyst — highest composite of Courtier, Etiquette, and Lore: History, plus Honor Rank; selected by Imperial families. Ruby — highest composite of Battle, Kenjutsu, and relevant teaching skills; Bushi school type preferred. Turquoise — highest composite of Artisan skills (any); Artisan school type preferred but not required. Topaz — broad gempukku test across Athletics, Kenjutsu or Iaijutsu, and Etiquette or Lore.

A player character who meets the minimum skill requirements for a championship can volunteer. Their clan Champion must approve the nomination — a Champion at Rival disposition (−11) or below toward the player will refuse. If the player’s skill composite is competitive, they are nominated.

**Championship Resolution — LOCKED:**

Each championship is resolved as a series of three contested skill checks. Each check represents a stage of the tournament. The candidate with the highest total across all three stages wins. Ties are broken by Honor Rank — the more honorable samurai wins a contested tournament.

The three stages per championship: Emerald — Iaijutsu/Reflexes, Battle/Perception, Courtier/Awareness. Jade — Spellcraft/Intelligence, Lore: Theology/Intelligence, contested elemental spell demonstration (highest Ring among candidates). Amethyst — Courtier/Awareness, Etiquette/Awareness, Lore: History or Poetry/Intelligence. Ruby — Kenjutsu/Agility, Battle/Perception, Lore: War/Intelligence. Turquoise — Artisan skill (candidate’s chosen art)/relevant Trait, Etiquette/Awareness, Performance/Awareness. Topaz — Athletics/Strength, Kenjutsu or Iaijutsu/Agility, Etiquette or Lore/Intelligence.

For NPC-only tournaments, the engine rolls each candidate’s three checks using their actual character sheet stats. Highest total wins. If a player character is competing, each stage is resolved as an actual roll on the MUD/ASCII layer — a real scene the player experiences. The player rolls against the field. Their total is compared to the NPC candidates’ totals (rolled by the engine simultaneously). The player knows their own performance but not the competitors’ scores until the results are announced.

A candidate’s Bushido and Shourido virtues do not modify the rolls directly, but they can affect behavior. A Yu (Courage) candidate might declare extra Raises for glory. A Seigyo (Control) candidate might attempt to sabotage a rival through the secret system (Section 12.8) before the tournament begins. These are emergent behaviors driven by existing NPC systems, not special tournament rules.

The winner receives the position immediately. The Promotion or Appointment Tier 4 topic fires. The character is tracked as the position holder in Section 22.8. If the previous holder was removed or forced to defend and lost, a secondary topic may fire depending on circumstances (Disgrace, Retirement, or Exile).

**Emperor’s Chosen Vacancies — LOCKED:**

The Emperor’s Chosen (Imperial Advisor, Imperial Chancellor, Imperial Treasurer, Voice of the Emperor) are NOT filled by tournament. They are direct Imperial appointments. When a Chosen position becomes vacant, the Emperor selects a replacement at the next Imperial Court session or Winter Court — whichever comes first. If neither is imminent, the Emperor can make the appointment by Imperial Edict delivered via the Imperial Herald through the letter system. Minimum delay: 14 daily ticks (3.5 real days). Maximum delay: one full season. The Emperor’s personality affects urgency — a Chugi (Duty) Emperor fills vacancies quickly; a Seigyo (Control) Emperor may deliberately leave a position vacant to consolidate power.

The Emperor NPC evaluates candidates using a simplified evaluation with the following weights. Disposition toward candidate: weight 20 — the Emperor picks someone he trusts. Clan balance: weight 15 — the Emperor avoids concentrating too many Chosen positions in one clan. Skill relevance: weight 15 — the candidate’s skills should match the position (Courtier/Etiquette for Advisor and Chancellor, Lore/Intelligence for Treasurer, etc.). Honor Rank: weight 10. Status: weight 5 — higher-Status candidates are easier appointments politically. Personality alignment: weight 10 — the Emperor’s own Bushido and Shourido virtues bias the selection.

The Imperial Herald is a special case — the position is inherited within the Miya family. When the Herald dies, the next Miya daimyo inherits the position automatically. The Emperor can theoretically appoint someone else but historically almost never does.

The Shogun is also a special case — it is not filled by replacing a vacancy. It is created as a new position when the Emperor decides the Empire needs one. This is a deliberate political act requiring an Imperial Edict at court, generating a Tier 1 topic. The Emperor selects the Shogun using the same evaluation logic above but with Battle and War Strategy skills heavily weighted (skill relevance weight increased to 25). The Shogun position is not filled at game start (Section 2.1.17).

A player character can be appointed to an Emperor’s Chosen position if their disposition with the Emperor is Friend (+31) or above and their skills match the position requirements. This would be one of the most significant achievements possible in the game.

**Emperor NPC Decision Profile:** The Emperor’s full decision-making system — including the five Emperor Personality Archetypes (Benevolent, Iron, Cunning, Warlike, Tyrant), standing and primary objectives, Winter Court host selection logic, and Shogun creation triggers — is defined in Section 55.10.1. The championship trigger conditions, candidate selection, and resolution mechanics above connect to the Emperor’s Strategic Review through the FILL_CHOSEN_VACANCY and CALL_CHAMPIONSHIP tasks defined in Section 55.10.

**Undated Celebrations — LOCKED:**

These festivals occur but have no fixed date — they are scheduled by local lords, the Emperor, or religious authorities based on circumstances. They are listed here for implementation reference.
