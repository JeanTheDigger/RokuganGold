## 4.3 Resource Systems

Rokugan's nobility publicly disdains commerce and trade — and yet the entire edifice of clan power rests on rice yields, population counts, and the flow of Koku. This hypocrisy is a core theme of the game, and the resource system is designed to make players feel it. Resources are not a management minigame; they are the silent foundation that makes war, politics, and ambition possible — and their absence is catastrophic.

The system uses four resources only. Complexity is intentionally kept low; the drama emerges from the consequences of scarcity, not from the act of tracking numbers.

### 4.3.1 The Four Resources & Currency

- Population (PU) — the fundamental atom. Represents people: labor, soldiers, tax base, and political legitimacy. 1 PU ≈ one village, approximately 500 people (see Section 4.3.13 for full scale reference). Everything else is derived from or consumed by PU.

- Rice — food. Produced seasonally by farming PU, consumed every season by all PU. The primary lever of famine and war logistics. Taxed upward through the hierarchy in Autumn.

- Koku — wealth. Generated by town PU every season. Flows upward through the hierarchy to pool at the Clan Champion level, then redistributed downward as stipends. 1 Koku = 1 Rice at baseline market price. At the lord level, Koku is an abstracted unit. At the individual character level, it is physical currency.

- Iron — raw military material. Produced every season by mining PU. Stockpiled locally, converted into Arms via Forges. Mining PU produce no Rice and depend entirely on imported food.

- Arms — finished military equipment. Produced from Iron at a Forge, or purchased with Koku. Required to field and maintain military units. Cost and upkeep scale with unit tier. Note: Arms is a derived resource — Iron converted through a Forge. It is not a raw resource in the same sense as Rice, Koku, or Iron. The ‘four resources’ refers to Population, Rice, Koku, and Iron as the primary tracked values. Arms is a conversion product that flows from Iron.

**Currency Denominations — LOCKED:**

Koku is the standard unit of wealth at the strategic (lord) level. Below the Local Daimyo, individual characters deal in physical currency broken into smaller denominations.

- 1 Koku = 5 Bu (ichibukin — silver coins, also commonly called bu).

- 1 Bu = 10 Zeni (copper coins).

- Therefore: 1 Koku = 50 Zeni.

Lords at Local Daimyo level and above think and transact in Koku units on the World Map. Individual samurai characters below that level carry and spend physical Koku, Bu, and Zeni in the MUD and ASCII layers.

### 4.3.2 The Rokugan Calendar — LOCKED

The year has 12 months divided into four seasons. Each month has both a common name and a noble name. Seasons are unequal in length — Winter is the longest at four months, Spring and Summer are three months each, and Autumn is two months. This asymmetry is mechanically significant: Winter is the longest lean period, the hardest season for armies in the field, and the season of Winter Court.

- Spring (3 months): Hare (Sun), Dragon (Moon), Serpent (Hantei). Planting season. No Rice production.

- Summer (3 months): Horse (Akodo), Goat (Doji), Monkey (Shiba). Growing season. No Rice production.

- Autumn (2 months): Rooster (Bayushi), Dog (Shinjo). Harvest and tax collection. Bulk Rice production occurs here.

- Winter (4 months): Boar (Hida), Rat (Togashi), Ox (Fu Leng), Tiger (The Tenth Kami). Lean season. No Rice production. Winter Court held.

Individual character stipends are paid monthly — 3 payments in Spring, 3 in Summer, 2 in Autumn, 4 in Winter. Two event cycles run in parallel: seasonal (Rice consumption, starvation check, population adjustment, Iron production, Koku generation) and monthly (Koku flows upward, stipends flow downward). See Section 4.3.5 for the full event sequence.

### 4.3.3 The Core Equation — LOCKED

The following values are confirmed and do not change. All other resource logic is derived from them.

- 1 civilian PU consumes 0.25 Rice per season (1.00 Rice per year). This is the base rate for all civilian PU — farming, mining, town commerce — and never changes regardless of circumstances. Military PU (levied, samurai, garrison) consume 0.35 Rice per season due to a 0.10 logistics surcharge (see Section 4.3.16).

- 1 farming PU produces 1.50 Rice per year at baseline on flat/fertile terrain. Delivered as a single Autumn harvest tick.

- Baseline surplus: 1.50 produced − 1.00 consumed = 0.50 Rice surplus per PU per year in peacetime on fertile land.

- 1 town PU produces 0.25 Koku per season (1.00 Koku per year) at baseline. Modified by terrain/location modifier.

- 1 Koku purchases 1 Rice unit at normal market prices. Therefore 1 Koku feeds 1 PU for one year under normal conditions.

- Tax is collected in Rice, not Koku. Koku is the denomination used to value and trade Rice, not the tax medium itself.

Rice is tracked at the village level. Koku is generated by town PU, pooled at each tier, flows upward to the Clan Champion, then redistributed downward as stipends. See Section 4.3.9 for full flow details. All values are tracked and displayed fractionally to two decimal places.

### 4.3.4 Terrain Modifiers — LOCKED IN PRINCIPLE, VALUES PENDING PLAYTEST

Each sub-tile has exactly one terrain type drawn from the master terrain vocabulary: Flat Plains, River Delta, Forest, Hills, or Mountains. This is a single shared tag read by every system in the game. The Rice production system reads the tag and applies a production multiplier. The army movement system (Section 11.7a) reads the same tag and applies a movement cost. The battle terrain system (Section 11.7) reads the same tag and applies combat modifiers. No sub-tile carries different terrain labels for different systems. Four overlay tags modify terrain without replacing it: Coastal (boolean, marks sub-tiles bordering the sea), Urban (derived from settlement presence, applies the +3 Defense battle modifier), River Crossing (approach condition adding +1 day to army movement), and Shadowlands (marks sub-tiles within the Shadowlands, triggers the Shadowlands combat and survival system on top of the base terrain). A Shadowlands-tagged Hills sub-tile is still Hills for movement and combat purposes, but also applies Taint exposure, no foraging, and the Shadowlands-specific rules from Section 11.7. The Shadowlands tag kills all Rice production entirely. Nothing grows in the Shadowlands. Crab provinces behind the Kaiu Wall are not Shadowlands-tagged and produce Rice at their normal terrain rate. The Rice production modifier below affects only Rice production. Consumption is always 0.25 per season regardless of terrain. Mountain provinces produce below subsistence, making them structurally dependent on imports or trade. This is intentional: it creates permanent strategic pressure (notably the Crab Clan’s reliance on the rest of Rokugan to hold the Kaiu Wall).

- River Delta: ×1.5 — produces 2.25 Rice per PU per year. The breadbasket regions; Crane and Unicorn lowlands, flood plains.

- Flat Plains: ×1.0 — produces 1.50 Rice per PU per year. Baseline. Most Lion and Scorpion territory.

- Forest: ×0.75 — produces 1.13 Rice per PU per year. Dragon foothills, some Phoenix lands. Slight deficit pressure.

- Hills: ×0.75 — produces 1.13 Rice per PU per year. Same production rate as Forest. Rough terrain limits agricultural capacity.

- Mountains: ×0.5 — produces 0.75 Rice per PU per year. Below the 1.00 subsistence threshold. Must import to survive.

### 4.3.5 Seasonal Harvest Cycle — LOCKED

Rice production follows a strict agricultural calendar. The year has four seasons; production occurs only once, in Autumn. Every village and every clan is always living off last Autumn's stockpile. This is not subject to change — it is the core mechanic that makes the timing of war, famine, and taxation meaningful.

- Spring — Planting: Rice is sown. No production. Each PU consumes 0.25 Rice. The farming PU present in a village during Spring sets the harvest ceiling for that Autumn. If part of a village's population is levied into an army during Spring, only the remaining civilian PU plant. There are no missing seasons — planting always occurs, but only for the PU still present.

- Summer — Growing: No production. Each PU consumes 0.25 Rice. The most dangerous season for armies in the field — stockpiles drain with no resupply.

- Autumn — Harvest and Tax: Farming PU produce their yield in a single tick. Formula: Autumn yield = farming PU present during Spring × 1.50 × terrain modifier. Tax is collected in Rice after the starvation check fires — not immediately after harvest. See the Full Seasonal Event Sequence below for exact order. Both values are tracked and displayed to two decimal places.

- Winter — Lean Season: No production. Each PU consumes 0.25 Rice. Winter Court is held. Armies in the field face the harshest supply conditions.

Example: A village of 2.00 PU on fertile plains (×1.0). At Spring, 0.50 PU are levied. Remaining farming PU = 1.50. Autumn yield = 1.50 × 1.50 × 1.0 = 2.25 Rice. Had no levy occurred, yield would have been 2.00 × 1.50 = 3.00 Rice. The levy cost the village 0.75 Rice at harvest.

**Full Seasonal Event Sequence — LOCKED:**

Two event cycles run in parallel. Seasonal events fire once at the start of each season. Monthly events fire once per month — 3 times in Spring, 3 times in Summer, 2 times in Autumn, 4 times in Winter.

**SEASONAL EVENTS (fire once at season start):**

- Rice consumption tick: all PU (village, city, castle) draw 0.25 Rice from their settlement’s stockpile.

- Starvation check: is any settlement’s stockpile now in deficit? If yes, determine starvation stage (Section 4.3.6).

- Population adjustment: growth or starvation PU change applies based on starvation stage.

- Iron production tick: mining PU generate Iron.

- Koku generation tick: town PU generate Koku. Rate: 0.25 Koku per town PU per season (1.00 Koku per year). Full seasonal Koku generated at season start, pooled at each tier, released as monthly stipends.

**MONTHLY EVENTS (fire once per month within each season):**

- Koku flows upward through hierarchy. Each tier passes their monthly share of Koku upward (seasonal pool ÷ months in season). Shares accumulate at each tier and continue up the chain to the Clan Champion pool.

- Stipends flow downward. Each lord releases one month’s worth of stipend from his Koku pool. Character stipends paid in physical currency (Koku, Bu, Zeni). If the lord’s pool is insufficient, reduced payment consequences apply (Section 4.3.9).

**SPRING ONLY (additionally fires at season start, before seasonal events):**

- Planting tick: counts farming PU currently present in each village. This number is locked as the harvest ceiling for next Autumn. PU levied after this point do not reduce the Autumn yield.

Continuous throughout Spring: normal IC days proceed. Court actions, movement, diplomacy, levy, military operations.

**AUTUMN ONLY — Step A (fires before seasonal events):**

- Harvest tick: Autumn yield calculated for each village. Formula: farming PU locked at Spring planting × 1.50 × terrain modifier. Yield added to village stockpile before consumption draws.

**AUTUMN ONLY — Step B (fires after starvation check, before population adjustment):**

- Tax cascade fires: taxable surplus (yield minus 1.00 Rice per PU subsistence floor) flows upward. Each tier takes their cut and deposits it into their settlement’s stockpile before passing the remainder up.

Full Autumn order: Harvest tick → Rice consumption → Starvation check → Tax cascade → Population adjustment → Iron production → Koku generation. Monthly Koku distribution and stipends fire at each month boundary within Autumn.

Continuous throughout Autumn: Winter Court preparation begins — invitations sent, agendas set. Harvest-related topics (Harvest Failure, Resource Shortage) generate if applicable.

**WINTER ONLY (additionally fires at season start):**

- Winter Court opens if Imperial or Clan Champion court is scheduled.

Continuous throughout Winter: the harshest season. Armies in the field face accelerated supply drain. Winter Court sessions run their full duration.

### 4.3.6 Population Dynamics — LOCKED

Population (PU) is not static. Every season, each village's PU changes based on food security, local stockpile, and recent history of conflict. Growth and decline are both percentage-based — larger villages gain and lose more PU in absolute terms at the same rate as smaller ones. There is no emigration in Rokugan; people stay. PU only leaves a village through death, military levy, or starvation. Natural pressure from war, raids, levies, and famine keeps population in check — there is no hard carrying capacity cap.

**Population Growth — LOCKED:**

Any village not in starvation grows each season. Growth scales with current PU — a larger village produces more people in absolute terms at the same rate. Three conditions contribute to the growth rate. Baseline and peace bonuses stack with whichever stockpile bonus applies. The two stockpile bonuses (modest and strong) are mutually exclusive — strong replaces modest rather than adding to it.

All growth rates below are annual rates. They are applied as seasonal increments (annual rate ÷ 4) at each seasonal tick — four applications per year. This means recovery from war and famine takes years, not seasons, preserving the weight of catastrophic events.

- Baseline growth: +5% of current PU per year (applied as +1.25% per season). Applies to any village not currently in a starvation stage.

- Modest stockpile bonus (+2%): village stockpile ≥ 1.00 Rice per PU (one full year of consumption in reserve). Total growth rate: 7% per year (applied as +1.75% per season).

- Strong stockpile bonus (+5%): village stockpile ≥ 2.00 Rice per PU (two full years of consumption in reserve). Replaces modest bonus, does not stack with it. Total growth rate: 10% per year (applied as +2.50% per season).

- Peace bonus (+3%): province has had no war, raid, or combat event for 4 or more consecutive seasons. Stacks with stockpile bonus. Maximum possible growth rate: 13% per year (applied as +3.25% per season).

Example: A village of 2.00 PU with a strong stockpile in a long-peaceful province grows at 3.25% per season: 2.00 × 0.0325 = +0.065 PU that season. Over four seasons the village grows from 2.00 to approximately 2.27 PU — a 13% annual increase. A famine-struck province that lost half its population would take roughly 5–6 years of sustained peace and good harvests to fully recover.

Combat Event Definition for Peace Bonus — LOCKED: A combat event is any of the following occurring within the province itself: a battle of any size fought within the province’s territory; a successful raid on the province; a siege beginning within the province; a hostile military unit moving through the province (enemy army, not allied passage). The following do NOT count as combat events: a battle in an adjacent province; a failed raid repelled before entering the province; a friendly army on allied passage; minor banditry below the garrison threshold (handled by the garrison system separately). Principle: the peace bonus reflects genuine stability felt by the civilian population. A distant war that does not touch them does not reset their peace counter.

**Population Decline — Starvation Cascade — LOCKED:**

Insufficient Rice is not a penalty slider — it is a threshold system that causes real, permanent harm. Starvation stages escalate the longer the deficit persists. PU loss is percentage-based, matching the growth model. People do not flee in Rokugan — they die or endure.

- Shortage (Rice < 0.25 per PU this season): −3% of current PU per season. No PU growth. Unrest rises. Loyalty to the ruling clan begins to erode.

- Hunger (Rice deficit persists into the 2nd consecutive season): −8% of current PU per season. Loyalty drops sharply. Neighboring clans may exploit the weakness diplomatically or militarily.

- Famine (Rice deficit persists 3+ consecutive seasons, or sudden total collapse — defined as: a settlement's entire Rice stockpile dropping to zero in a single tick due to raid, fire, confiscation, or catastrophic harvest failure): −20% of current PU per season. Province begins to depopulate. Desperate PU may pledge to a rival clan offering food. Shadowlands corruption risk increases as desperation opens doors to dark bargains.

Example: A village of 3.00 PU entering its fourth consecutive season of deficit is in Famine: 3.00 × 0.20 = −0.60 PU that season. Left unaddressed, the village will be devastated within a few seasons.

**Starvation Recovery — Gradual Step-Down — LOCKED:**

Starvation stages do not reset instantly. Relief reduces the severity one stage at a time — the institutional damage of prolonged hunger cannot be undone by a single good harvest.

- A season of adequate food (stockpile covers 0.25 Rice per PU consumption with no deficit) drops the settlement one starvation stage: Famine → Hunger, Hunger → Shortage, Shortage → Clear.

- Two consecutive seasons of adequate food are required to drop from Shortage to Clear. A single relief season while at Shortage reduces internal severity but the settlement remains at Shortage with PU loss suspended for that season. Only a second consecutive season of adequate food fully clears the deficit counter.

- While stepping down, the settlement does not suffer PU loss from the current stage during relief seasons — but it does not grow either. Normal population growth resumes only once the settlement is fully Clear.

- If the deficit returns during a step-down season, the counter immediately re-escalates to the next stage above the current one. A settlement stepping down from Hunger to Shortage that re-enters deficit jumps back to Hunger, not to Famine.

- Sudden total collapse (stockpile hitting zero in a single tick) always sets the stage to Famine immediately regardless of prior state.

Example: A village endures four seasons of Famine. Relief arrives. Season 1 of relief: Famine → Hunger (no PU loss, no growth). Season 2 of relief: Hunger → Shortage (no PU loss, no growth). Season 3 of relief: Shortage maintained, PU loss suspended. Season 4 of relief: Shortage → Clear. Normal growth resumes. Total recovery from Famine to Clear takes four seasons of uninterrupted adequate food — and the village still needs years of growth afterward to replenish lost population.

Recovery of lost PU follows the growth model (Section 4.3.6 Population Growth), meaning a famine-struck province may take many years of in-game time to rebuild.

### 4.3.7 The Tax Cascade — LOCKED (default rates and personality modifiers — see Section 19.5)

Tax is collected in Rice, not Koku. Each Autumn, after the starvation check fires, Rice flows upward through the feudal hierarchy in a cascading sequence of cuts. (Tax fires after harvest and starvation check — see Section 4.3.5 for the exact event order.) Each tier takes a percentage of what arrives from the tier below — not a percentage of the original harvest. The village retains subsistence first; everything above that is the taxable surplus that enters the cascade.

Taxable surplus = Autumn yield − village subsistence requirement (1.00 Rice per PU). This surplus, and only this surplus, is subject to taxation. A village that barely survives yields nothing upward.

The full chain is: Village → Local Daimyo → Provincial Daimyo → Family Daimyo → Clan Champion → Emperor. Each tier is a distinct political actor with their own interests, personality, and capacity for loyalty or corruption.

**Default cascade — 1.00 PU village, fertile plains (1.50 yield, 0.50 taxable surplus):**

- Village retains: 1.00 Rice (subsistence floor — untouchable under any circumstances).

- Local Daimyo collects: 40% of surplus = 0.20 Rice. Passes upward: 0.30 Rice.

- Provincial Daimyo collects: 30% of what arrives = 0.09 Rice. Passes upward: 0.21 Rice.

- Family Daimyo collects: 25% of what arrives = 0.05 Rice. Passes upward: 0.16 Rice.

- Clan Champion collects: 20% of what arrives = 0.03 Rice. Passes upward: 0.13 Rice.

- Emperor collects: 15% of what arrives = 0.02 Rice. Remainder stays with Clan Champion: 0.11 Rice.

Each tier takes a progressively smaller cut because each tier is more distant from the source and more dependent on the honesty of those beneath them. The Local Daimyo is the most powerful economic actor relative to his domain — he is closest to the village and takes the largest share. The Emperor receives the smallest amount per village, but aggregated across every village in Rokugan, Imperial income is enormous. The Clan Champion retains the remainder after the Imperial cut as their personal reserve.

Each lord also holds their own villages directly — these function identically to vassal villages except the lord is simultaneously the Local Daimyo and the collector, keeping that tier's full cut without passing it to anyone above the Local level. A Clan Champion with many direct holdings is wealthier per village but politically less connected to his vassals than one who governs through a deep hierarchy. This mirrors the Crusader Kings model of demesne vs. vassal holdings.

**Settlement Stockpiles and Rice Allocation — LOCKED:**

Every settlement — village, city, and castle — has its own Rice stockpile. When the tax cascade fires each Autumn, each lord deposits his retained cut into his own settlement’s stockpile before passing the remainder up the chain. This is the mechanism by which castles and cities are fed.

All civilian PU consume 0.25 Rice per season from their settlement’s stockpile regardless of their role — farming, mining, town commerce. Military PU (garrison, levied, samurai) consume 0.35 Rice per season due to a logistics surcharge (Section 4.3.16). Castle garrison PU and city town PU draw from their settlement’s stockpile exactly as village PU do.

The cascade allocation order:

- Village produces yield. Village retains subsistence floor (1.00 Rice per PU). Surplus flows to Local Daimyo.

- Local Daimyo takes his cut (40% of surplus). Deposits it into his settlement stockpile. Passes the remainder upward.

- Provincial Daimyo receives what arrives. Takes his cut (30%). Deposits into his settlement stockpile. Passes remainder upward.

- Family Daimyo, Clan Champion, and Emperor follow the same pattern at their respective tiers.

Each lord’s stockpile must sustain his settlement’s non-farming PU through all four seasons until the next Autumn harvest. If his cut is insufficient — because his villages had a poor harvest, the cascade was depleted above him, or his settlement has grown beyond what tax income can sustain — his stockpile runs dry and his PU enter the starvation cascade (Section 4.3.6). A lord’s castle can starve just as a village can.

**Personality Tax Modifier — LOCKED:**

Each lord has a Tax Modifier that adjusts their default collection rate. This modifier is derived from the lord’s Bushido and Shourido personality virtues (Section 19). Both virtues contribute independently and stack.

- Tax Modifier range: approximately −20% to +20% applied to the lord's default rate.

- Bushido Tax Modifiers: Jin (Compassion) −10%. Chuugi (Duty/Loyalty) +5%. Meyo (Honor/Glory) −5%. Gi, Rei, Yu, Makoto: 0%. Shourido Tax Modifiers: Kyōryōku (Strength) +10%. Seigyo (Control) +5%. Ishi (Will) +5%. Ketsui, Dōsatsu, Chishiki, Kanpeki: 0%. Both apply independently and stack. Effective range: −15% to +15%.

- Stability Effect per Season (0–100 scale): +15% tax: −3 Stability/season. +10% tax: −2 Stability/season. +5% tax: −1 Stability/season. 0% (standard): no change. −5% tax: +1 Stability/season. −10% tax: +2 Stability/season. A compassionate lord builds a loyal province but sends less Rice upward — which superiors notice and resent. A greedy lord bleeds their province toward Restless over time, eventually paying for the extra Rice in insurgency suppression costs.

- Corruption mechanism (future system): a lord may report a lower surplus than actually exists, pocketing the difference in Rice before the cascade begins. The discrepancy is invisible to superiors unless investigated. This is a deliberate design hook.

- The subsistence floor (1.00 Rice per PU) cannot be touched by any Tax Modifier under any circumstances. A lord who taxes into subsistence triggers the starvation cascade regardless of intent.

**Investigation and Audit System --- LOCKED**

Two forms of corruption exist in the resource cascade. Rice skimming: a lord underreports his taxable surplus before the cascade fires, pocketing Rice that should flow upward. Koku skimming: a lord underreports his town Koku generation before passing it up the chain, retaining a private reserve his superiors cannot see. Both are invisible to superiors by default — the superior receives the reported number and has no direct way to verify it without an investigation action.

**How Skimming Works --- LOCKED:**

- **Rice skimming:** The vassal reports a lower taxable surplus than actually exists. The difference stays in his own stockpile rather than entering the cascade. His superior receives less than they should and has no direct visibility into the village yield unless they physically inspect the province. The skimmed Rice is indistinguishable from a genuinely poor harvest unless the audit system flags it.

- **Koku skimming:** The vassal reports lower town Koku generation before passing it upward. The retained Koku enters a hidden private reserve tracked separately from his visible holdings. His superior sees a lower clan pool than actually exists. The skimmed Koku can fund private arms purchases, bribes, or personal enrichment without appearing in the flow the Clan Champion monitors.

**Detection --- LOCKED:**

A superior who suspects skimming may commit a character to an audit action. This costs 1 AP and requires the auditing character to physically travel to the suspect vassal’s province — accounts cannot be verified at a distance. The auditing character must have access to the vassal’s settlement records, which the vassal is obligated to provide to a superior’s representative but may delay, obstruct, or subtly falsify.

**Audit Roll --- LOCKED:**

Commerce (Mathematics) + Intelligence vs TN 20 (per the domain finance application already defined in Section 29). Each Raise beyond the TN reveals one additional specific irregularity — a discrepancy between reported yield and observable village conditions, a Koku figure that does not match the settlement’s town PU count, a stockpile level inconsistent with reported surpluses.

- **Failure (below TN 20):** No irregularities found. The auditing character either missed them or the vassal’s falsification was too clean. The vassal becomes aware the audit occurred and failed — he is now more careful. Future audit TNs against this vassal increase by +5 for the remainder of the year.

- **Success (meets TN 20):** One irregularity identified — the auditor knows something is wrong but has only partial evidence. The superior has grounds to confront the vassal but not enough for a formal accusation. Confrontation at this level gives the vassal the opportunity to explain away the discrepancy or make a partial restitution quietly.

- **Success with 1 Raise (TN 25):** Two irregularities identified. The pattern is clear enough for a formal accusation. The superior may present this as evidence of deliberate skimming. The vassal’s disposition toward the superior drops by −20 immediately — being caught is humiliating. The vassal must make restitution of estimated skimmed amount or face a formal Betrayal topic (Tier 3).

- **Success with 2+ Raises (TN 30+):** Full exposure. The auditor has reconstructed the skimming scheme completely — amounts, duration, method. This is admissible as a Tier 2 secret (Section 12.8) and can be presented to any court. The vassal faces disgrace, forced restitution, loss of position, and potentially seppuku depending on the superior’s judgment and personality. The evidence can also be used as blackmail leverage rather than exposed publicly, at the cost of −0.3 Honor to the blackmailer (Section 12.9).

**Passive Detection Signals --- LOCKED:**

A superior who never commissions an audit may still notice something is wrong through circumstantial signals. These are not proof — they are prompts. Each generates a Tier 4 topic (Financial Irregularity) that enters the topic system and may reach the superior’s awareness through normal information flow.

- The vassal’s reported harvest is consistently below what the province’s terrain and PU count should produce — three consecutive seasons of suspiciously low yields.

- The vassal is visibly spending beyond what his reported income can explain — expensive gifts at court, well-equipped troops, infrastructure improvements inconsistent with his declared Koku pool.

- A merchant, magistrate, or other character passing through the province reports market conditions inconsistent with the vassal’s reported shortfalls — a province supposedly in Shortage but with well-stocked merchants.

None of these signals trigger automatic consequences. They are information the superior may act on or ignore. A lord who notices but does nothing is choosing to tolerate it — perhaps because the vassal is otherwise loyal, perhaps because the lord lacks the political will to act, perhaps because the lord is skimming from their own superior and cannot afford the scrutiny an audit would invite. The corruption system works in both directions.

### 4.3.8 Koku Generation — LOCKED

Koku is generated by town PU every season. It is the steady income that funds infrastructure, Arms purchases, and political activity. Towns are settlements where PU engage in commerce, craftsmanship, and services rather than farming or mining.

- Base Koku generation: 0.25 Koku per town PU per season (1.00 Koku per town PU per year).

- Formula: Koku per season = town PU × 0.25 × location modifier.

- Town PU still consume 0.25 Rice per season — they must be fed like all other PU.

- A settlement may split its PU between farming, mining, and town activities in any proportion, tracked fractionally to two decimal places.

✓ DONE — Location modifiers for Koku generation fully defined in this section.

**Koku Generation Location Modifiers — LOCKED:**

Location modifiers multiply the base Koku generation rate (0.25 Koku per town PU per season). Modifiers stack multiplicatively when a settlement qualifies for multiple types.

**Bonuses:**

- Standard settlement: ×1.0 — baseline. No modifier.

- Castle town (settlement adjacent to or containing a major castle): ×1.2 — administrative center, samurai spending, local market activity.

- Crossroads (settlement where two or more major roads meet): ×1.3 — merchant traffic, toll income, market activity.

- Coastal (settlement on the sea coast): ×1.3 — fishing, coastal trade, access to sea routes.

- Port city (major coastal settlement with dedicated harbor infrastructure): ×1.5 — full maritime trade. The highest commercial multiplier available.

- River town (settlement on a major navigable river): ×1.2 — river trade, easier goods movement.

**Maluses:**

- Remote/isolated (no road connections, deep mountains, far from any trade route): ×0.7 — almost no merchant traffic, minimal commercial activity. Dragon mountain villages, deep Crab territory.

- Active conflict zone (province currently experiencing war, raid, or siege): ×0.5 — trade collapses, merchants flee, markets shut down. Temporary — clears when conflict ends.

- Bandit-infested (under-garrisoned province with active banditry): ×0.8 — merchants take alternate routes, trade income reduced. Clears when garrison is restored.

Stacking example: Ryoko Owari — port city (×1.5) and crossroads (×1.3) = ×1.95. This explains its status as the Empire’s largest commercial city despite Scorpion territory not being particularly fertile. A coastal town in an active conflict zone = ×1.3 × 0.5 = ×0.65 — the coast helps but war hurts more.

### 4.3.9 The Koku Flow — LOCKED (default rates and personality modifiers — see Section 19.5)

Koku moves through the feudal hierarchy in two directions, forming a complete cycle. Koku is generated seasonally at season start, pooled at each tier, then flows upward monthly — each lord passes their monthly share to their superior up to the Clan Champion. The Champion then redistributes it downward as monthly stipends. Rice flows up as tribute; Koku flows up as revenue, then back down as payment. A lord’s financial wellbeing depends entirely on the generosity and honesty of those above him. See Section 4.3.5 for exact timing.

**Upward Flow — Revenue:**

Each season, Koku is generated by town PU and pooled at each tier. Each month, each lord passes their monthly share of Koku upward to their superior. Monthly share = total seasonal Koku ÷ number of months in the season (Spring: ÷3, Summer: ÷3, Autumn: ÷2, Winter: ÷4). This continues up the chain until the Clan Champion holds the full clan pool each month. The Champion sees — or believes he sees — the total wealth of his clan each month. See the seasonal event sequence (Section 4.3.5) for exact timing: Koku generates at season start, flows upward monthly.

- Upward corruption: a lord may underreport his town Koku before passing it up, retaining a private reserve his superiors cannot see. This is a deliberate design hook — the investigation/audit system will determine how it is detected.

**Downward Flow — Stipends:**

The Clan Champion redistributes the pooled Koku downward as stipends. Each tier receives a share of what is passed down from above — not from their own town generation, which has already been sent up. Individual samurai characters below the Local Daimyo receive physical currency — Koku, Bu, and Zeni — paid monthly.

- Clan Champion retains: 40% of the clan-wide pool. Largest share — the Champion controls the war chest. Passes 60% downward.

- Family Daimyo receives: 25% of what flows down (25% of 60% = 15% of total pool). Passes remaining 75% of what arrived (75% of 60% = 45% of total pool) further down.

- Provincial Daimyo receives: 20% of what flows down from Family (20% of 45% = 9% of total pool). Passes remaining 80% of what arrived (80% of 45% = 36% of total pool) further down.

- Local Daimyo receives: 15% of what flows down from Provincial (15% of 36% = 5.4% of total pool). Passes remaining 85% of what arrived (30.6% of total pool) to individual characters as monthly stipends.

- Individual characters receive: monthly stipend in physical currency (Koku, Bu, Zeni) from their Local Daimyo’s pool. Amounts by rank are defined below.

- Downward corruption: a lord may pass less than the default rate downward, retaining the difference. Vassals receiving less than expected grow resentful over time.

- If a lord's received stipend is zero — because his superior's pool ran dry or was withheld — nothing flows further down. Characters at the bottom of a broke or corrupt chain receive nothing.

**Personality Stipend Modifier — LOCKED:**

- Each lord has a Stipend Modifier adjusting how generously they distribute Koku downward. The modifier is derived from the lord’s Bushido and Shourido personality virtues (Section 19), same structure as the Rice Tax Modifier. Bushido Stipend Modifiers (positive = pays more): Jin (Compassion) +10%. Meyo (Honor/Glory) +5%. Gi, Rei, Yu, Makoto, Chuugi: 0%. Shourido Stipend Modifiers (negative = hoards): Kyōryōku (Strength) −10%. Seigyo (Control) −5%. Ishi (Will) −5%. Ketsui, Dōsatsu, Chishiki, Kanpeki: 0%. Both apply independently and stack. Effective range: −15% to +15%. Disposition Effect per Season: +10% stipend: +2 disposition from direct retainers. +5% stipend: +1 disposition. 0% (standard): no change. −5% stipend: −1 disposition per season. −10% stipend: −2 disposition per season. A lord who hoards Koku gradually loses the loyalty of their samurai. A lord who pays generously builds devoted retainers but has less Koku for political spending, bribes, and military investment. Stipend personality modifiers: LOCKED. Values PROVISIONAL pending playtest.

- A generous lord passes more than the default downward, building loyalty but retaining less for himself.

- A miserly lord retains more, weakening loyalty below.

Individual character stipend amounts by position in the feudal hierarchy. Stipends reflect feudal relationship — who your lord is and what role you hold — not personal power or skill. A Rank 5 samurai serving a poor provincial lord earns less than a Rank 1 samurai serving a Clan Champion directly.

**Scale Note:** The Koku amounts in the cascade math above are in Koku units (World Map scale), where 1 Koku unit = 1 Rice unit = enough to feed 1 PU (~500 people) for one year. The stipend amounts below are in individual koku (character scale). 1 Koku unit = 500 individual koku. A Local Daimyo receiving 5.4 Koku units per year from the cascade holds 2,700 individual koku — more than sufficient to pay stipends to a large retinue. See Section 4.3.13 for the full unit scale reference.

- Direct retainer of the Clan Champion: 5 koku per month.

- Direct retainer of a Family Daimyo: 3 koku per month.

- Direct retainer of a Provincial Daimyo: 2 koku per month.

- Direct retainer of a Local Daimyo: 1 koku per month.

- Retainer of a retainer (indirect service, two steps removed): 3 bu per month.

- Ronin (no lord): No stipend. Must sustain themselves through mercenary work, odd jobs, or savings.

These are base amounts before the lord's Stipend Modifier is applied. A generous Clan Champion might pay 6-7 koku to their direct retainers. A miserly Local Daimyo might pay only 3-4 bu. The amount a character receives is a direct reflection of their lord's wealth, generosity, and regard for them.

Consequences for receiving reduced or no stipend:

- Reduced stipend (50-75% of expected): Disposition toward lord drops by -2 per missed payment. Generates T4-81 STIPEND_FAILURE topic — the lord, the affected samurai, and co-located vassals of the same lord hold the topic at generation.

- Severely reduced stipend (below 50%): Disposition toward lord drops by -5 per month. Loyalty begins to waver. Character may begin seeking alternative employment or accepting commissions that compromise their duties.

- No stipend at all: Disposition toward lord drops by -10 per month. After three consecutive months without payment the character's loyalty is in crisis — disposition collapse may lead to insubordination, appeals to the family daimyo, or skimming from assigned resources. Generates T4-81 STIPEND_FAILURE topic.

- No Honor penalty for the character receiving reduced pay — the fault lies with the lord, not the retainer. The lord, however, suffers Glory loss if their failure to pay stipends becomes widely known.

- A character who has gone without stipend for multiple months and has exhausted their savings may take the Debt disadvantage automatically.

Note on small lords and the Koku cascade: A Local Daimyo whose territory generates only a small Koku base receives very little from the downward stipend flow after the cascade takes its cuts at each tier. A province with 10 town PU generating 10 Koku/year yields approximately 0.54 Koku/year to the Local Daimyo after the full cascade (5.4% of total clan pool). This is intentional — small lords are structurally poor and depend heavily on their direct demesne holdings to fund their obligations. A Local Daimyo cannot sustain retainers from stipend income alone without significant direct holdings.

### 4.3.10 Iron, Arms & Military Units — LOCKED

The conversion chain from raw material to fielded army is: Mining PU (produce Iron) → Forge (converts Iron to Arms) → Military Unit (levied and equipped, requiring ongoing Iron upkeep for maintenance). Each step has a distinct cost and gate. No step can be skipped.

**Mining PU — Iron Production:**

Iron production works on the same PU logic as Rice production, but with two critical differences: it produces every season rather than only in Autumn, and mining PU produce zero Rice. A village can dedicate some or all of its PU to mining, trading food self-sufficiency for Iron output. A fully dedicated mining village is entirely dependent on imported Rice to survive.

- Base Iron production: 0.50 Iron per mining PU per season (2.00 Iron per PU per year).

- Formula: Iron per season = mining PU × 0.50 × mine quality modifier.

- Mining PU consume 0.25 Rice per season — identical to farming PU. They do not feed themselves. Food must be imported or supplied from farming PU within the same village or province.

- A village may split its PU between farming and mining in any proportion, tracked fractionally to two decimal places. Example: a 2.00 PU village with 1.00 farming PU and 1.00 mining PU on a standard vein produces 1.50 Rice/year and 2.00 Iron/year, but only feeds itself in peacetime if the Rice output covers all 2.00 PU consumption.

**Mine Quality Modifiers — LOCKED IN PRINCIPLE, VALUES PENDING PLAYTEST:**

Each mining province has a quality rating that multiplies Iron output for all mining PU within it. The structure mirrors Rice terrain modifiers — a single provincial multiplier applied to all village-level production within that province.

- Rich vein: ×1.5 — produces 0.75 Iron per mining PU per season. Deep mountain deposits, Kaiu territories, exceptional northern ranges.

- Standard vein: ×1.0 — produces 0.50 Iron per mining PU per season. Baseline. Most mountain and hill provinces with known deposits.

- Marginal deposit: ×0.5 — produces 0.25 Iron per mining PU per season. Shallow or partially exhausted deposits. Viable only with significant dedicated mining PU.

**PROVISIONAL:** *Mine quality modifier values subject to playtesting. The structural relationships (rich > standard > marginal) are fixed; exact multipliers may be tuned.*

**Mine Quality Degradation — NO:** Mine quality does not degrade over time. A rich vein remains rich indefinitely regardless of extraction intensity. Mines are permanent strategic resources. This decision prevents total Iron extinction across the map and keeps the economic layer stable over long game timescales.

**The Forge — Arms Production:**

- A Forge is a provincial infrastructure building costing 35 Koku to construct. Without a Forge, raw Iron stockpiles but cannot be converted into Arms.

- Conversion rate: 1.00 Iron → 1.00 Arms. No loss in conversion.

- Seasonal capacity: up to 3.00 Arms per Forge per season, limited by Iron available. If less than 3.00 Iron is supplied, the Forge converts only what is available. Additional Forges can be built to increase maximum throughput.

- Forges are valid military targets — destroying one cuts off Arms production without touching stockpiled Iron or Arms already in inventory.

**Arms Costs by Unit Tier (equip cost — one time):**

- Peasant Levy: 0.25 Arms per PU. Improvised weapons, minimal protection.

- Ashigaru: 1.00 Arms per PU. Standard infantry kit.

- Bushi Retainer: 2.00 Arms per PU. Quality weapons, partial armor.

- Elite Samurai (generic — deprecated for Great Clans): 4.00 Arms per PU. Full daisho, quality armor, associated equipment. Replaced by Clan Elite tiers below for Great Clans; retained only as a reference baseline.

- Clan Elite Tier 1 — Clan Samurai (Baseline): 2.50 Arms per unit. Clan-specific trained samurai with school equipment.

- Clan Elite Tier 2 — Specialized: 3.50 Arms per unit. Specialized tactical units with role-specific equipment.

- Clan Elite Tier 3 — Elite: 5.00 Arms per unit. The finest warriors in each clan’s arsenal with premium equipment.

**Iron Upkeep (ongoing cost per season — paid in Iron, not Arms):**

Fielded military units require a small ongoing Iron expenditure each season to remain at full combat effectiveness. Weapons dull, armor dents, equipment wears out. This keeps Iron permanently relevant in peacetime and means a supply blockade gradually degrades an enemy's military capacity — not just their ability to raise new troops, but their existing ones.

- Peasant Levy: 0.03 Iron per PU per season.

- Ashigaru: 0.10 Iron per PU per season.

- Bushi Retainer: 0.20 Iron per PU per season.

- Elite Samurai (generic — deprecated for Great Clans): 0.40 Iron per PU per season.

- Clan Elite Tier 1 — Clan Samurai: 0.25 Iron per unit per season.

- Clan Elite Tier 2 — Specialized: 0.35 Iron per unit per season.

- Clan Elite Tier 3 — Elite: 0.50 Iron per unit per season.

A unit that goes without upkeep suffers direct stat penalties (see Iron Upkeep Failure — Stat Penalties below). Two consecutive seasons without upkeep increases the penalty. A disbanded unit returns its PU to the civilian population; the Arms and upkeep obligation end.

Iron Upkeep Failure — Stat Penalties — LOCKED: Degradation directly modifies unit stats downward. There is no tier swap — the unit remains the same unit with worsening equipment condition. After 1 season without Iron upkeep: Attack −2, Defense −2, Morale −4, Morale Defense −2. After 2 consecutive seasons without upkeep: Attack −4, Defense −4, Morale −8, Morale Defense −4. These penalties apply directly from base — not the 1-season penalty plus an additional penalty stacked on top of it.

**Koku as an Alternative Path:**

- Koku can purchase raw Iron on the trade market — bought Iron is converted at a local Forge as normal.

- Koku can purchase finished Arms directly, bypassing the Forge — more expensive than local production but available to clans without Iron provinces or Forge capacity.

- Direct Arms purchase price in Koku is always higher than the equivalent Iron production cost, representing merchant markup.

This is the thematic engine of Rokugan's hypocrisy: a warrior clan that scorns commerce must quietly send Koku to merchants when their Iron runs dry. The samurai who purchased his sword from a market vendor is not supposed to exist. He does.

**Iron Market System — LOCKED:**

Iron is pooled at the clan level each season. After paying unit upkeep, the Clan Champion decides what to stockpile and what to release onto the market. Clans that mine more Iron than they need can convert surplus into Koku by selling it. Clans without Iron provinces must buy from those who have surplus.

**Clan Iron Cycle (fires each season after Iron production):**

- All Iron generated by clan mining PU pools at the Clan Champion level.

- Unit upkeep paid first: Iron is distributed to cover ongoing maintenance costs for all fielded units (see Iron Upkeep table above).

- Clan Champion allocates remaining Iron: stockpile reserve vs. market release. This is a strategic decision — stockpiling builds a war reserve, releasing generates Koku income.

- Market Iron is made available to other clans at the posted price (see Iron Market Price below).

**Iron Purchase Priority — LOCKED:**

When Iron is available on the market, purchase priority is determined by clan-to-clan disposition. This creates a direct economic consequence to diplomatic relationships — being on bad terms with an Iron-rich clan means you are last in line for their surplus.

- Priority 1: Clans with whom the selling clan has Friend disposition or above (+31 and above). First access, full quantity available.

- Priority 2: Clans at Acquaintance or Stranger disposition (−10 to +30). Access after Priority 1 is satisfied.

- Priority 3: Clans at Rival disposition or below (−11 and below). Access only after Priority 1 and 2 are satisfied. In practice, Enemy and Blood Enemy clans rarely get access — the selling clan can simply withhold.

- If multiple clans compete at the same priority tier, the clan with the highest numeric disposition toward the seller gets first access.

This means clan-to-clan disposition is not only a political and diplomatic metric but a direct economic one. An Iron-rich clan controls who can arm themselves. The Crane and Scorpion — Koku-rich but Iron-poor — must cultivate relationships with the Crab and Dragon to access their Iron surplus.

**Iron and Arms Market Prices — LOCKED:**

- Raw Iron market price: 0.8 Koku per Iron unit. Viable but costly compared to local production. A clan that sustains its military entirely through market Iron will drain Koku fast.

- Finished Arms — Peasant Levy: 0.4 Koku per PU equipped.

- Finished Arms — Ashigaru: 1.5 Koku per PU equipped.

- Finished Arms — Bushi Retainer: 3.0 Koku per PU equipped.

- Finished Arms — Elite Samurai (generic — deprecated for Great Clans): 7.0 Koku per PU equipped. Replaced by Clan Elite market prices below.

- Finished Arms — Clan Elite Tier 1 (Clan Samurai): 4.0 Koku per unit equipped.

- Finished Arms — Clan Elite Tier 2 (Specialized): 5.5 Koku per unit equipped.

- Finished Arms — Clan Elite Tier 3 (Elite): 8.0 Koku per unit equipped. Highest markup reflects extreme rarity and quality of equipment for the Empire’s finest warriors.

**Unit Upkeep Clarification — LOCKED:**

Units are equipped once with Arms (the one-time equip cost). Ongoing maintenance is paid in Iron — weapons need sharpening, armor needs repair, raw Iron feeds back into the Forge to maintain equipment condition. The upkeep rates in the Iron Upkeep table above are Iron costs per season, not Arms costs. This is intentional: Iron remains strategically relevant in peacetime, and a supply blockade gradually degrades an enemy’s existing military even without new battles.

### 4.3.13 Population Unit Scale — LOCKED

1 PU = approximately 500 people. This is the fundamental population atom. Settlements range from tiny hamlets of 0.1–0.5 PU to massive cities of hundreds of PU. Hamlets below 0.5 PU exist but are too small to track individually — they are absorbed into the nearest village for simulation purposes.

**PU and the Celestial Order (Section 2.1):** Civilian PU represent the bonge (heimin) class — farmers, craftsmen, merchants, and laborers who form the economic foundation of every settlement. When civilian PU are levied for military service, they become ashigaru — bonge temporarily serving as soldiers, consuming military Rice rates (0.35/season) and requiring Arms. Garrison PU are also bonge, serving as doshin (village officers) under samurai authority for local security. Samurai units (buke) are permanent military PU that exist outside the civilian population — they are not drawn from village PU and represent the professional warrior caste. This distinction is fundamental: levying troops depletes the bonge population and reduces economic output; samurai units cost Koku and Rice but never reduce farming capacity. The hinin (non-people) are not tracked as PU — they exist as background population with no economic or military representation in the simulation.

- Tiny hamlet: 0.1–0.5 PU (50–250 people). Below individual tracking threshold.

- Small village: 1.00 PU (~500 people). The base simulation unit.

- Large village: 2–4 PU (1,000–2,000 people).

- Small castle town: 10–20 PU (5,000–10,000 people).

- Major city: 100–200 PU (50,000–100,000 people).

- Otosan Uchi: ~100 PU at canon figures (~50,000 people). The Imperial Capital and seat of the Emperor.

- Ryoko Owari (Scorpion trade city): ~400 PU (~200,000 people). The largest non-Imperial city in Rokugan.

### 4.3.16 Military Consumption & Army Logistics — LOCKED

Raising an army creates a double burden on Rice supply: levied soldiers stop farming (reducing production) while continuing to eat (maintaining consumption). This is the core tension that prevents clans from simply raising infinite armies.

- A levied military PU consumes 0.35 Rice per season — slightly more than a civilian PU (0.25), representing logistics overhead, waste, and the cost of keeping men under arms rather than behind a plow.

- A levied military PU produces 0 Rice — they are no longer farming.

- Net swing of levying 1 PU before Spring planting (worst case): −1.50 Rice at Autumn harvest (production loss — the levied PU was not present at Spring planting) + +0.10 Rice/season extra consumption vs. civilian rate (0.35 military − 0.25 civilian) for each season the unit is fielded. The production loss is a single one-time hit at Autumn; the consumption delta is ongoing. If levied after Spring planting, the harvest ceiling is already locked and only the +0.10 Rice/season consumption difference applies for the remainder of the year.

- Arms are spent upfront to equip professional units (ashigaru, samurai retinues) — produced locally via Forge from Iron, or purchased on the open market with Koku. Levied peasant armies cost fewer Arms but fight less effectively and have worse morale.

**✓ RESOLVED:** *Arms equip costs fully defined in Section 4.3.10. Peasant Levy: 0.25 Arms/PU. Ashigaru: 1.00 Arms/PU. Bushi Retainer: 2.00 Arms/PU. Elite Samurai: 4.00 Arms/PU. Iron upkeep rates also defined there.*

✓ DONE — Permanent samurai retinues consume 0.35 Rice per season regardless of activity — the same rate as any military PU. A samurai at home in his lord’s castle still consumes at the military rate. This is intentional: the resource system uses a single military consumption rate to avoid tracking whether each unit is ‘active’ or ‘stationed.’ The 0.10 Rice/season difference from civilian rate (0.25) is the standing cost of maintaining a permanent professional force.

### 4.3.17 AI War Readiness Check — LOCKED (values pending playtest)

Before any clan AI decides to raise an army or declare war, it runs a logistics feasibility check based on the resource equations defined in Sections 4.3.5 through 4.3.16. This prevents the AI from making self-destructive military decisions — and when it overrides the check anyway (due to desperation, honor pressure, or scripted events), it creates emergent crisis situations.

The system has four phases: a pre-war Feasibility Ledger, an Alternative Ladder when the ledger fails, a mid-campaign Supply Status Monitor, and rules for player-initiated starvation strategies.

All AI decisions in this system follow the Core Design Philosophy (Section 20) — lords act on known information only. A lord does not know his enemy’s stockpile unless intelligence has been gathered. He estimates based on observable information: province size, visible army strength, recent harvest conditions, and what has been said at court.

#### Phase 1: The Feasibility Ledger

The Feasibility Ledger is a resource projection calculated at the moment a lord considers military action. It estimates whether the clan can sustain the proposed campaign without destroying itself. The ledger is not a single number — it produces a status verdict for each of the three strategic resources (Rice, Arms, Koku) and a composite result.

**Step 1 — Estimate Campaign Length**

The AI estimates how many seasons the proposed campaign will last, based on the military objective type:

- Provincial raid: 1 season.

- Border conflict: 2 seasons.

- Family war: 3 seasons.

- Clan war: 4 seasons.

Personality modifiers adjust the estimate. These reflect the lord’s temperament, not strategic analysis — an aggressive lord genuinely believes victory will come faster.

- Yu (Courage) and Strength: reduce estimate by 1 season (minimum 1). They expect to win quickly.

- Control and Knowledge: increase estimate by 1 season. They plan for attrition and worst cases.

- Determination and Will: no change. They commit to whatever length is required.

- All other personality types: use the default estimate.

**Step 2 — Calculate the Rice Budget**

This is the core equation. All values are calculated from the lord’s currently known resource state.

- **Current Rice stockpile** across all controlled settlements, summed.

- **Civilian consumption burn**: total civilian PU × 0.25 Rice × estimated campaign seasons.

- **Military consumption burn**: (existing military PU + proposed new levy PU) × 0.35 Rice × estimated campaign seasons.

- **Production loss from levy**: if levying before the Spring planting tick, each levied PU removes 1.50 Rice from the next Autumn harvest (they were not present at planting). If levying after the Spring planting tick, production loss is zero — the harvest ceiling is already locked per Section 4.3.5.

- **Projected harvest income**: farming PU remaining after levy × 1.50 × terrain modifier. This value is only counted if the estimated campaign spans at least one Autumn. If the campaign begins in Autumn or Winter and is projected to end before the next Autumn, no harvest income is projected.

- **Net Rice Position** = Current Stockpile + Projected Harvest Income − Civilian Burn − Military Burn − Production Loss.

**Step 3 — Calculate the Arms Budget**

- **Current Arms stockpile** at the clan level.

- **Projected Arms production**: number of Forges × 3.00 Arms/season (maximum throughput) × estimated campaign seasons, limited by available Iron supply.

- **Equip cost for proposed army**: levied PU × Arms cost per tier. Peasant Levy: 0.25 Arms/PU. Ashigaru: 1.00 Arms/PU. Bushi Retainer: 2.00 Arms/PU. Elite Samurai: 4.00 Arms/PU (per Section 4.3.10).

- **Ongoing Iron upkeep**: (existing military PU + proposed new military PU) × Iron upkeep rate per tier × estimated campaign seasons. Peasant Levy: 0.03 Iron/PU/season. Ashigaru: 0.10. Bushi Retainer: 0.20. Elite Samurai: 0.40 (per Section 4.3.10).

- **Net Arms Position** = Current Stockpile + Projected Production − Equip Cost − Iron Upkeep equivalent.

**Step 4 — Calculate the Koku Budget**

- **Current Koku treasury** at the lord’s tier and above (the lord knows their own pool; they estimate the Clan Champion’s pool based on historical patterns unless intelligence says otherwise).

- **Ongoing stipend obligations**: sum of all character stipends under this lord × estimated campaign seasons (per Section 4.3.9 stipend table).

- **Market purchase capacity**: if Rice or Arms are in deficit, can the deficit be covered by buying on the open market? Rice market price: 1 Koku per Rice unit. Iron market price: 0.8 Koku per Iron unit. Finished Arms prices per tier per Section 4.3.10 market table.

- **Net Koku Position** = Treasury − Stipend Obligations − Projected Market Purchase Costs.

**Step 5 — The Feasibility Verdict**

Each resource receives a status based on its net position:

**Rice Status:**

- Green: Net Rice Position ≥ 1.00 Rice per total PU after the campaign ends. The clan survives with a full year’s buffer.

- Yellow: Net Rice Position between 0 and 0.99 Rice per total PU. The clan survives the campaign but enters Shortage risk territory (per Section 4.3.6 starvation thresholds).

- Red: Net Rice Position is negative. The campaign will cause Hunger or Famine in the lord’s own settlements.

**Arms Status:**

- Green: Net Arms Position ≥ 0 after equipping all proposed units and covering upkeep. The army can be fully equipped and maintained.

- Yellow: Net Arms Position is negative, but the deficit can be covered by purchasing Arms or Iron on the open market with available Koku.

- Red: Cannot equip the proposed army at any cost — insufficient Arms, Iron, Forge capacity, and Koku combined.

**Koku Status:**

- Green: Net Koku Position ≥ 0 after stipends and any market purchases. No financial strain.

- Yellow: Net Koku Position is negative, but the lord can cover the deficit by reducing stipends (accepting the consequences defined in Section 4.3.9).

- Red: Cannot fund stipends, market purchases, or equip costs. Financial collapse.

**Composite Verdict:**

- **All Green** — War is feasible. The AI proceeds with the proposed military action.

- **Any Yellow, no Red** — War is risky. The AI proceeds only if the triggering objective has High priority (per Section 24 objective system) OR the lord’s personality favors aggression. Personalities that proceed on Yellow: Yu (Courage), Strength, Determination, Will. All other personality types enter Phase 2 to seek alternatives before committing.

- **Any Red** — War is not feasible by direct means. Enter Phase 2 (the Alternative Ladder).

- **All Red** — Desperation territory. Enter Phase 2 with the desperation flag set.

#### Phase 2: The Alternative Ladder

When the Feasibility Ledger returns Yellow or Red, the AI does not simply abandon its objective. It considers alternatives in a fixed priority order, shaped by personality and Honor. The AI walks down the ladder sequentially, recalculating the Feasibility Ledger after each rung, and stops as soon as the verdict improves to an acceptable level.

**Rung 1 — Scale Down the Army**

Reduce the proposed army size. Levy fewer PU. Substitute cheaper unit types (Peasant Levy instead of Ashigaru). Rely more heavily on existing permanent samurai retinues instead of raising new levies. Recalculate the Feasibility Ledger with the smaller, cheaper force.

If the revised verdict is All Green or acceptable Yellow, proceed with the reduced force.

**Rung 2 — Delay to Post-Harvest**

If the current season is Spring or Summer, delay the campaign until after the Autumn harvest. The incoming Rice eliminates or substantially reduces the Rice deficit. Recalculate with the post-harvest stockpile.

Personality gate: Yu (Courage) and Strength lords skip this rung entirely. Delay feels like cowardice to them — they would rather fight undermanned now than wait.

**Rung 3 — Purchase on the Market**

Spend Koku to buy Rice, Iron, or finished Arms on the open market. This option is available only if the Koku budget is Green or Yellow AND trade routes to a willing seller are open (per Section 4.3.18 — market access is available between adjacent provinces unless blocked by War Status, embargo, banditry, or Shadowlands incursion).

Iron Purchase Priority (Section 4.3.10) applies — the lord’s disposition with Iron-rich clans determines whether they can actually buy. A lord at Rival or Enemy disposition with the Crab and Dragon may find no Iron available at any price.

Recalculate with market purchases factored in.

**Rung 4 — Demand Extraordinary Tribute from Vassals**

Order vassal lords to contribute Rice or Arms beyond their normal tax obligation. This is legally permitted within Rokugan’s feudal hierarchy — a lord may demand additional tribute from those beneath him in the Celestial Order.

Costs and consequences:

- Disposition: −5 from each vassal who receives the demand. Applied immediately upon the demand being issued.

- Topic generation: Tier 4 Political topic generated — “Lord [X] levies extraordinary tribute.” Spreads through the normal topic system and may become ammunition at court.

- Vassal compliance: determined by the vassal’s disposition toward the demanding lord. A vassal at Friend disposition (+31 or higher) complies without question. A vassal at Acquaintance or Stranger (−10 to +30) complies but resents it. A vassal at Rival or below (−11 or lower) may refuse, requiring an Intimidate action or further disposition cost to enforce.

- Volume: a demanding lord may extract up to 25% of each vassal’s current stockpile (Rice or Arms) as extraordinary tribute. This is a hard cap — demanding more triggers automatic refusal and a Tier 3 Political crisis.

Personality gate: Jin (Compassion) lords will not demand tribute from any vassal whose settlements are currently at Shortage or worse. Gi (Honesty) lords will not demand tribute without first explaining the military need publicly, which costs them the element of surprise.

Recalculate with the tribute factored in.

**Rung 5 — Request Allied Aid**

Send a formal request to an allied clan or lord to contribute Rice, Arms, troops, or Koku. This creates a diplomatic obligation.

Requirements:

- Disposition of Friend (+31) or higher with the target lord or clan.

- A plausible justification — the requesting lord must be facing a genuine threat, not simply trying to build an offensive army at someone else’s expense.

Consequences:

- Creates a Favor obligation (per Section 12.10 Favor System). The aid constitutes a Moderate Favor (Tier 2) if the contribution is significant (more than 20% of the ally’s available surplus) or a Minor Favor (Tier 3) if smaller.

- The ally’s AI evaluates the request using their own Feasibility Ledger — they will not contribute if doing so puts their own settlements into Shortage.

Personality gate: Determination lords skip this rung entirely. They will never ask for help regardless of need — accepting aid creates an obligation, which is anathema to their virtue. Will lords also skip this rung once they have committed to a course of action.

Recalculate with allied contributions factored in.

**Rung 6 — Raid a Vulnerable Neighbor**

Launch a provincial raid (War Status Tier 3 per Section 53) against a weak neighboring province to seize Rice stockpiles directly. This is the dishonorable option. The AI considers it only after all honorable alternatives have been exhausted or rejected.

Target selection: The AI identifies the weakest adjacent province not currently protected by a garrison above 1.0 PU or an allied army. Provinces belonging to clans the lord is already at war with are preferred targets — raiding an enemy carries less additional diplomatic cost than opening a new front.

Raid mechanics:

- A successful raid transfers up to 50% of the target settlement’s current Rice stockpile to the raiding lord. “Successful” means the raiding army reaches the settlement and is not repelled by garrison or defending forces.

- The raided province suffers all normal consequences of a combat event (per Section 4.3.6 — peace bonus timer resets, potential PU loss from direct violence).

Costs:

- Honor: −1.0 (automatic, public). Raiding a neighbor’s food supply is a shameful act.

- Glory: −0.3.

- Disposition: −15 from the targeted clan (historical modifier — permanent). −5 from all other clans who learn of the raid (spreads through the topic system).

- War Status: if the two clans are not already at war, the raid triggers Provincial Raid status (Tier 3) per the escalation chain in Section 53.

- Tier 3 Military crisis generated: “Lord [X] raids [Province Y] for provisions.”

Personality gates:

- Jin (Compassion) and Gi (Honesty/Righteousness): will never raid for resources. These rungs are unavailable to them. Period.

- Meiyo (Honor): will only raid if a prior grievance or active objective targeting that clan exists — the raid must be justifiable within their personal code.

- Rei (Courtesy): will not raid without first issuing a formal demand for tribute to the target, giving them a chance to comply. If the demand is refused, the raid is then justified.

- All Shourido personality types: will consider raiding without restriction.

Recalculate with seized resources factored in.

**Rung 7 — Desperation Override**

If all six preceding rungs have been exhausted or skipped and the lord still cannot feasibly field the proposed army, the AI evaluates whether to override the Feasibility Ledger and raise the army anyway, knowingly accepting the consequences.

**The Desperation Threshold:** A lord enters desperation when ALL of the following conditions are met:

- Their Rice stockpile is below 0.50 Rice per total PU (approximately two seasons of civilian consumption remaining).

- They have a **critical objective** active. Critical objectives are defined as: defending against an active invasion of their own territory; resolving a Tier 1 or Tier 2 crisis that directly threatens their holdings; avenging a blood debt (the “Seek vengeance” standing objective from Section 18.4); or responding to a formal call to arms from their Clan Champion (Chugi-driven duty).

- At least one of the following personality conditions is true: the lord’s primary virtue is Yu (Courage), Chugi (Loyalty), Determination, Strength, or Will; OR the lord’s War Score is below 25 (Desperate per Section 53) and they are defending, not attacking.

If all conditions are met, the lord raises the army. The AI flags this decision internally as a Desperation Levy. The following consequences are automatic:

- All settlements under the lord’s control that enter Shortage, Hunger, or Famine as a result of the levy suffer the normal starvation cascade (Section 4.3.6) — the lord has chosen to sacrifice his people’s wellbeing for the campaign.

- Tier 3 topic generated: “Lord [X] raises a desperate levy — his people will starve.” This spreads through courts and can be used against the lord politically.

- If the lord’s primary Bushido virtue is Jin (Compassion), they suffer an additional −1.0 Honor for violating their own principle. The system does not prevent Jin lords from reaching desperation — but it makes them pay for the contradiction.

**Without a critical objective**, the lord does not override. A lord who simply wants to expand territory or pursue a standing objective will not starve his own people to do it. The objective system (Section 18) distinguishes between goals worth dying for and goals worth pursuing when convenient — the Desperation Override only fires for the former.

#### Phase 3: Mid-Campaign Supply Status Monitor

Once an army is fielded, the AI runs a Supply Status Check every season at the seasonal tick. This is not a repeat of the full Feasibility Ledger — it is a focused survival assessment of the army in the field and the settlements feeding it.

**The Supply Status Check**

Three checks fire in sequence:

**Check 1 — Home Front Status.** What is the starvation stage (per Section 4.3.6) of each settlement controlled by the lord? The worst-case settlement determines the Home Front status: Clear, Shortage, Hunger, or Famine.

**Check 2 — Army Supply Status.** Can the fielded army’s Rice consumption (0.35 Rice per military PU per season) be covered by the supply tether (per Section 11.7 Supply Tether System)? If the tether is intact and the supply source has sufficient Rice, the army is Supplied. If the tether is cut or the source is exhausted, the army is Unsupplied and enters the deprivation cascade per Section 11.7.

**Check 3 — Iron Upkeep Status.** Can ongoing Iron upkeep for all fielded units be covered from the clan’s Iron pool? If yes, equipment condition is maintained. If no, degradation begins per Section 4.3.10 Iron Upkeep Failure table: after 1 season without upkeep, Attack −2, Defense −2, Morale −4, Morale Defense −2. After 2 consecutive seasons, Attack −4, Defense −4, Morale −8, Morale Defense −4.

**The Response Matrix**

The AI’s response is determined by the combination of Home Front Status and Army Supply Status. Iron Upkeep Status is tracked separately — its consequences are automatic (stat penalties) and do not directly trigger strategic decisions.

**Home Front Clear, Army Supplied:** Continue the campaign. No action needed. The AI reassesses its War Score and objective progress normally.

**Home Front Shortage, Army Supplied:** The AI evaluates campaign progress. If the War Score is 65 or above (Winning or Dominant per Section 53), the AI pushes to finish the campaign quickly — victory is close, and the shortage will end when troops return to farming. If the War Score is below 65, the AI begins seeking peace or negotiation through the normal diplomatic channels (court actions, letters, proxy negotiation per Section 15).

Personality override: Yu (Courage), Will, and Strength lords ignore home Shortage and continue fighting regardless of War Score. Their people’s hardship does not override their commitment to the campaign.

**Home Front Hunger, Army Supplied:** Serious. The lord’s settlements are deteriorating. All personality types except Will begin actively seeking peace. The lord’s court representatives initiate peace feelers at the next available court session. If no court is scheduled within 1 IC season, the lord sends a formal letter to the enemy proposing negotiation (per the letter system mechanics).

Personality override: Will lords continue fighting through Hunger at home. Their course is set and will not change.

**Home Front Famine, Army Any:** Critical. The AI immediately seeks peace on any terms its personality will accept. “Acceptable terms” are determined by War Score and personality per the Peace Willingness system in Section 53.

The only exception: Will lords continue fighting through Famine at home. This is intentional — a Will lord who has committed to war will watch his provinces depopulate before changing course. This is one of the most destructive personality outcomes possible, and it is meant to be. Players who encounter a Will-driven enemy lord should understand that outlasting them is not a viable strategy — they must be defeated militarily or their lord must be killed, deposed, or overruled by a higher authority.

Even Yu (Courage) lords will negotiate at Famine stage — courage demands fighting the enemy, not starving one’s own people. Only Will overrides this.

**Any Home Front, Army Supply Cut:** The army’s supply tether (Section 11.7) has been severed. The army attempts to restore the tether by clearing the blockage (fighting through enemy forces cutting the supply line, or rerouting to an alternate supply source). If restoration fails within 1 IC season, the army retreats toward friendly territory.

Personality override: Determination lords hold position for 2 IC seasons before retreating instead of 1. They will endure greater deprivation before accepting the need to fall back.

**Retreat Logic**

When the AI decides to retreat, the army moves toward the nearest friendly province that meets EITHER of the following criteria: contains a Forge with available Iron (to address equipment degradation), or has a Rice stockpile above 1.00 Rice per local PU (able to absorb the army’s consumption without entering Shortage).

If no qualifying province exists within 2 provinces of the army’s current position, the army disbands:

- Levied PU (Peasant Levy, Ashigaru): return to their home village. If the home village was destroyed during the campaign, they return to the nearest surviving village in the same province. If no village in the province survives, the PU is lost — they scatter and die or become refugees (mechanically identical to PU death).

- Samurai units (Bushi Retainer, Elite Samurai): regroup at the lord’s castle. If the castle has fallen, they regroup at the Family Daimyo’s castle. Samurai do not scatter — they have a place in the social order and will find their way to the next authority figure in the chain.

- Garrison units called into field service: return to their assigned settlement.

A forced disband carries no Honor cost for the lord — it is understood that circumstances, not cowardice, drove the decision. However, it does generate a Tier 4 Military topic: “Lord [X]’s army disbands in the field.” This can be leveraged politically by enemies and rivals.

#### Phase 4: Player-Initiated Starvation Strategies

**Design Decision: Players can deliberately starve out a clan.** This is permitted and intended — it creates some of the most dramatic and morally complex situations in the game. However, it is extremely costly in Honor and has severe escalation consequences. Starvation warfare is the nuclear option of Rokugan.

**Permitted Actions**

**Blockade Trade Routes:** A military unit (minimum 1.0 PU) stationed on a trade route node prevents Rice, Iron, and Koku from flowing through it. This affects all clans that use the route, not only the intended target — collateral economic damage is inherent.

- This is a formal hostile action. It triggers War Status (Provincial Raid, Tier 3) with the clan whose trade is being blockaded, if War Status does not already exist.

- Honor cost: −0.5 per season the blockade is maintained. Blocking trade is dishonorable but less shameful than destroying food directly.

- The blockading unit must remain stationed at the node. If it leaves or is driven off, the route reopens immediately.

**Raid Harvests:** An army present in a province during the Autumn harvest tick can order the harvest destroyed. This sets the province’s Autumn yield to zero for that year. The farming PU are unharmed — only the crop is destroyed. The province enters immediate Famine from sudden total collapse (stockpile drops to zero, per Section 4.3.6 Famine trigger).

- Honor cost: −2.0 (automatic, public). Destroying food is one of the most dishonorable acts in Rokugan. An army that burns rice fields is remembered for generations.

- Glory cost: −0.5.

- Disposition cost: −20 from the targeted clan. This is a permanent historical modifier — it is never forgiven and never decays. −10 from all other clans who learn of the act (spreads through the topic system as a Tier 2 topic).

- Crisis generated: Tier 2 — Provincial Famine (immediate, in the targeted province).

- Topic generated: Tier 2 Military/Political — “Lord [X] destroys the harvest of [Province Y].” This becomes ammunition at every court session until it falls off the topic momentum scale.

- The Emperor may issue an Imperial Edict condemning the act. If issued, the lord who ordered the destruction suffers an additional −3.0 Honor (defying the Emperor’s moral authority) and all clans receive a +5 disposition bonus toward the targeted clan (sympathy effect).

Personality gates for AI lords considering harvest destruction:

- Jin (Compassion): will never order harvest destruction under any circumstances.

- Gi (Honesty/Righteousness): will never order harvest destruction — it is fundamentally unjust.

- Rei (Courtesy): will not order it — the act is barbaric and beneath a civilized lord.

- Yu (Courage): will consider it only if no other path to victory exists — destroying crops is not brave, merely ruthless.

- Meiyo (Honor): will not order it unless their personal code specifically demands the destruction of a hated enemy at any cost.

- Chugi (Loyalty): will order it if their lord commands it. Will not order it independently.

- Makoto (Sincerity): will order it only if they have publicly declared the intent to do so — no surprise attacks on harvests.

- All Shourido personality types: will consider harvest destruction without moral restriction. Control and Knowledge lords view it as a strategic tool. Strength and Will lords view it as an expression of dominance. Determination and Perfection lords view it as achieving the objective by any means.

**Siege Starvation:** Already fully defined in Section 11.7. A besieging army prevents supply from reaching a castle, forcing the garrison to live off stored provisions until they surrender, sortie, or starve. No additional rules are needed here — the siege system handles this case completely.

**What Players Cannot Do**

**Permanent terrain degradation is not possible.** A destroyed harvest recovers the following year if PU survive to replant in Spring. There is no mechanic for salting the earth, poisoning wells, or rendering a province permanently barren. This is a deliberate design boundary.

Thematic justification: Rokugan’s land is sacred. The kami inhabit the soil, the water, the air. Even the worst warlords in L5R canon — even the Shadowlands — did not permanently destroy arable land through conventional warfare. The Taint corrupts, but that is a supernatural effect governed by the Shadowlands system, not a military one. A lord who burns a harvest is a villain. A lord who destroys the land itself is an abomination beyond what the game’s social systems can accommodate.

Mechanical justification: permanent terrain degradation would create a death spiral with no recovery path. The starvation cascade (Section 4.3.6) already punishes provinces severely — years of recovery time, massive PU loss, cascading political consequences. Adding permanent damage on top of that would make military conflict in agricultural regions an extinction event rather than a dramatic crisis. The game needs provinces to be worth fighting over, not craters to be avoided.

#### Implementation Notes

**Tick Timing:** The Feasibility Ledger is calculated on demand — whenever an AI lord’s objective system (Section 18) generates a military primary objective, or when an external event (invasion, crisis, call to arms) triggers a military response evaluation. It is not a scheduled tick.

**The Supply Status Check fires at the seasonal tick** for every AI-controlled army currently in the field, immediately after the Rice consumption tick and starvation check (per the Full Seasonal Event Sequence in Section 4.3.5).

**Information Asymmetry:** The AI calculates the Feasibility Ledger using only information the lord actually possesses. The lord knows: his own Rice, Iron, Arms, and Koku stockpiles precisely; his own PU counts precisely; his vassals’ stockpiles (reported at tax time — may be inaccurate if the vassal is skimming, per Section 4.3.7); the approximate strength of neighboring provinces (observable, but not precise — estimating enemy stockpiles requires intelligence gathering). The lord does NOT know: the exact Rice stockpile of a non-vassal enemy province (must estimate or spy); whether an enemy’s Arms are degraded (requires battlefield intelligence or espionage); the enemy lord’s personality type (must infer from behavior over time, per Section 20).

**Logging:** Every Feasibility Ledger calculation and Alternative Ladder decision should be logged internally for debugging and balance tuning. The log should record: the triggering objective, each resource’s net position and status, which personality gates were hit, which rungs of the ladder were attempted, and the final decision. This data is invisible to the player but essential for playtesting.

#### Values Pending Playtest

The following values are locked in principle but may require tuning during playtesting:

- The Desperation Threshold (0.50 Rice per total PU) — may need adjustment if lords trigger desperation too readily or too rarely.

- Extraordinary tribute cap (25% of vassal stockpile) — may need adjustment based on how quickly it destabilizes vassal provinces.

- Raid seizure rate (50% of target stockpile) — may be too generous or too punitive depending on average province size.

- Honor costs for harvest destruction (−2.0) and trade blockade (−0.5 per season) — these are the primary deterrents and must feel appropriately heavy without making the strategies completely unusable.

- Disposition penalties for harvest destruction (−20 targeted clan, −10 others) — permanent modifiers are powerful; confirm they don’t make post-war diplomacy impossible.

- Campaign length estimates by objective type — the 1/2/3/4 season defaults may not match actual campaign durations once the full military system is in play.

### 4.3.18 Trade Routes & Koku Flow — LOCKED

Trade routes connect provinces and cities on the World Map. They are the mechanism by which Rice and Koku move between regions — and the channel through which the nobility's hypocritical relationship with commerce is expressed. A lord does not 'trade.' He 'permits merchants to operate' under his protection, and collects duty in return.

- Active trade routes generate bonus Koku for connected provinces each season.

- Trade routes can carry Rice — allowing a surplus province to feed a deficit province, either freely, by sale, or under political coercion.

- Routes can be disrupted by war, banditry (which increases during unrest), Shadowlands incursion, or deliberate political action (embargo).

- Rice price fluctuation is driven by supply and demand across connected trade routes — famine in one region raises prices across the network. A clan that controls key trade nodes wields enormous indirect power.

**✓ DONE --- Trade route connectivity: Provinces trade with adjacent provinces. Market access is available between neighbours unless blocked by War Status or active disruption (embargo, banditry, Shadowlands incursion). The Rice and Iron market systems (Sections 4.3.10 and 4.3.18) operate on this adjacency model. Goods locality resolved in Section 4.3.14 — no physical transport layer; existing abstractions (Supply Tether, adjacency markets, banditry drain) cover all use cases.**

**Rice Market System --- LOCKED**

The Rice market mirrors the Iron market system (Section 4.3.10) in structure but differs in two important ways: it is decentralised by design, and it includes an intra-clan sharing mechanic that generates Honor rather than Koku. Every lord at every tier of the feudal hierarchy is a potential independent seller of their own surplus Rice. There is no pooling upward for market purposes — each lord sells from their own stockpile at their own price.

**The Rice Surplus Decision --- LOCKED:**

Each season, after the tax cascade fires and each lord deposits their retained cut into their own stockpile, each lord evaluates their stockpile against their consumption needs. Rice above what is needed to sustain their settlement’s PU through all four seasons is genuine surplus — Rice they own outright with no obligation attached. A lord may then choose what to do with that surplus:

- **Stockpile it:** Retain the surplus as a buffer against future shortfalls, military campaigns, or emergency use. No immediate return but provides security.

- **Post it for sale:** Release some or all of the surplus onto the market at a price the lord sets. Generates Koku income. The lord chooses how much to release — they do not have to sell everything.

- **Share it within the clan:** Transfer surplus Rice to another lord within the same clan or family whose settlements are at Shortage or worse. Generates Honor rather than Koku. See Intra-Clan Sharing below.

**Rice Purchase Priority --- LOCKED:**

When a lord posts Rice for sale, access is determined by the same disposition-based priority system as Iron (Section 4.3.10). The selling lord’s clan-to-clan disposition toward the buyer determines their place in the queue.

- **Priority 1:** Friend disposition or above (+31 and above). First access, full quantity available at the posted price.

- **Priority 2:** Acquaintance or Stranger (−10 to +30). Access after Priority 1 is satisfied.

- **Priority 3:** Rival or below (−11 and below). Access only after Priority 1 and 2 are satisfied. A selling lord may withhold entirely from Enemy or Blood Enemy clans regardless of their ability to pay.

A desperate clan at war with an Iron-rich clan may also be at war with the only nearby Rice seller. Disposition is not merely diplomatic — it determines whether your people eat.

**Rice Price Mechanics --- LOCKED:**

Each lord sets their own price for their own surplus. The baseline price is 1 Koku per Rice unit — the established conversion rate throughout the resource system (Section 4.3.3). Individual lords deviate from this baseline based on demand signals.

- **Rising price:** Each season that buyers purchase the lord’s posted Rice at the current price, the lord raises their price by +0.25 Koku for the following season. Demand is demonstrably high — buyers will pay more. A lord who has been selling steadily for several seasons may be posting at 2.0 or 3.0 Koku per Rice unit. There is no fixed ceiling — a lord in a famine region who holds significant surplus may charge whatever the market will bear.

- **Falling price:** Each season that posted Rice goes unsold, the lord lowers their price by −0.25 Koku for the following season. Buyers have stopped — either they found cheaper elsewhere, they no longer need Rice, or the price has exceeded what they will pay. The lord drops the price trying to move the stock. If the price falls below the 1 Koku baseline and Rice still goes unsold, the lord continues dropping — surplus Rice that cannot be sold or eaten is a resource draining the stockpile for nothing.

- **Floor:** 0.25 Koku per Rice unit. A lord will not give Rice away for less than this — at this point it makes more sense to stockpile it than sell it. If demand remains zero at 0.25 Koku, the lord withdraws the Rice from the market entirely and returns it to stockpile.

This creates a genuinely decentralised market. Two lords selling Rice simultaneously may be at very different prices — one who has been selling into a famine region may be at 2.5 Koku while a lord further away with no recent buyers may be at 0.75 Koku. A buyer who needs Rice has to find who is selling, at what price, and whether the seller will deal with them. Disposition shapes access. Price shapes whether the buyer can afford it.

**Intra-Clan Rice Sharing --- LOCKED:**

A lord may transfer surplus Rice to any other lord within the same clan or family without requiring Koku payment. This is not commerce — it is a lord fulfilling their duty to those they are bound to. The transfer generates Honor for the giving lord rather than Koku income, scaled to the amount given and the severity of the recipient’s need.

**Eligibility --- LOCKED:**

- The receiving lord must have at least one settlement currently at Shortage or worse. A lord whose settlements are all Clear does not generate Honor by receiving shared Rice — they do not need it. The sharing must respond to genuine need.

- Sharing can flow in any direction within the clan — upward, downward, or laterally between lords of equal rank. A Provincial Lord sharing with a struggling Family Daimyo above him is as valid as a Clan Champion sharing downward. The feudal hierarchy does not restrict the direction of generosity.

- Only lords of the same clan or family are eligible. Sharing Rice with a lord of a different clan is commerce — it may still generate some disposition improvement but follows the sale mechanic, not the sharing mechanic.

**Honor Gain Scale --- LOCKED:**

- Recipient at Shortage, small transfer (less than 1.0 Rice): +0.1 Honor.

- Recipient at Shortage, significant transfer (1.0–3.0 Rice): +0.2 Honor.

- Recipient at Hunger, any transfer: +0.3 Honor. The need is serious.

- Recipient at Famine, any transfer: +0.5 Honor. Saving a province from starvation is a profound act of duty.

- Recipient at Famine, transfer large enough to resolve the crisis (bring the settlement to Clear): +1.0 Honor. Fully resolving a Famine within the clan is the highest act of Rice sharing.

Honor gains from sharing are public — the act is known within the clan and generates a Tier 4 topic (Generous Act) that spreads through the normal topic system. A lord who consistently shares Rice with struggling clan members builds a reputation for virtue that accumulates over time. A lord who hoards during a clan famine and sells to outsiders instead generates the inverse reputation — no Honor loss is automatically applied, but the political and social consequences within the clan can be severe.

### 4.3.19 Military Unit Types — LOCKED

Three distinct categories of military unit exist in the game, each with different origins, costs, and roles. They are not interchangeable — each fills a specific function in the military and civil ecosystem of a province or clan.

**Type 1 — Mustered Units (Levied):**

Raised from the civilian population (heimin/peasant class) when needed. Primarily Ashigaru and basic infantry. These represent the bulk of any wartime army. They are drawn from village PU using the levy system already defined — levying removes PU from farming, reduces Rice production, and adds military Rice consumption. Mustered units are temporary by nature: they are raised for a campaign and disbanded afterward, returning PU to civilian life.

- Source: village PU via levy.

- Cost: Arms (equip, per Section 4.3.10 table) + ongoing Rice consumption (0.35/season) + Iron upkeep (Peasant Levy: 0.03 Iron/season; Ashigaru: 0.10 Iron/season).

- Tier: Peasant Levy and Ashigaru primarily.

- Permanence: temporary. Disbanded after campaign or by lord's order.

**Maximum Levy Percentage --- LOCKED**

The levy system draws PU from a village for military service. The more PU levied, the less remain to farm, maintain the settlement, and sustain the social fabric of the community. The levy percentage is calculated as (levied PU ÷ total village PU). Three tiers govern the consequences of levying at different rates. The existing production loss formula (Section 4.3.16) applies in all tiers — these consequences are additional.

- **0–50% (Safe Zone):** No additional consequence beyond the production loss formula. The village loses workers but retains enough to function. This is the intended operating range for a lord who is managing his province responsibly.

- **51–65% (Warning Zone):** −5 Stability to the province immediately when the levy is called. The community feels the weight of losing more than half its people — a visible, public act of desperation. No ongoing seasonal drain. The immediate hit must be absorbed and recovered the slow way.

- **66–80% (Dangerous Zone):** −10 Stability to the province immediately, plus −3 Stability per season while the levied PU remain away. The ongoing drain reflects a community watching its people not return, growing more desperate each season. When the levied PU are disbanded and return, the ongoing −3/season stops immediately — but the initial −10 is permanent damage that must recover naturally. Lord Honor loss: −0.3 (public). Conscripting two-thirds of a village’s population is a visible act of ruthlessness.

- **Above 80% (Desperation):** −20 Stability to the province immediately, plus −7 Stability per season while levied. The village cannot maintain itself at this level — PU begins declining from abandonment even before starvation triggers. Lord Honor loss: −1.0 (public). This is not prohibited — a sufficiently desperate lord may do it — but it is the domain of the Desperation Override (Section 4.3.17) and the consequences compound with everything that system already imposes. A lord who strips a village above 80% is knowingly destroying it.

There is no hard mechanical ceiling above 80%. The system does not prevent a lord from levying every last PU from a village. It simply ensures the consequences are catastrophic and unrecoverable in any reasonable timeframe. The design boundary is social and economic, not a hard lock.

**Type 2 — Samurai Units (Permanent):**

The professional warrior class. Always present on the World Map as permanent units. These do not come from PU — they represent the samurai caste, which is socially and mechanically separate from the peasant population. Samurai units are always combat-ready, always visible on the map, and have continuous upkeep. They are the backbone of a clan's standing military power.

- Source: samurai caste — not drawn from village PU.

- Cost: higher Rice upkeep (0.35 Rice/season, same as all military PU), Iron upkeep scaled by tier (Clan Samurai: 0.25, Specialized: 0.35, Elite: 0.50 Iron/season), ongoing Koku stipend. More expensive per unit than levied troops. No PU cost — samurai caste units do not draw from village populations.

- Tier: Bushi Retainer and Elite Samurai primarily.

- Permanence: permanent. Cannot be disbanded without political/social consequences (dishonoring a samurai without cause has Honor and loyalty implications).

✓ DONE — Samurai unit upkeep rates: Rice upkeep 0.35/season (same as all military PU). Iron upkeep: Bushi Retainer 0.20 Iron/season, Elite Samurai 0.40 Iron/season (from Section 4.3.10 Iron Upkeep table). Koku stipend: ongoing, paid from lord’s monthly Koku pool.

**✓ DONE --- Named characters lead units at the Gunso threshold and above (Section 11.6). A named Chui commands a Company, a named Taisa commands a Legion, and so on up to Rikugunshokan. Player characters fight as individuals on the ASCII Map within the mass battle context, and can hold named command roles (Gunso, Chui, etc.) that apply their Battle skill as a bonus to their unit. See Section 11.6 Military Rank Structure and Section 11.7 Mass Battle Rules.**

**Type 3 — Garrison Units (Civil):**

Drawn from the heimin (bonge) class — peasants who serve as a standing local security force, referred to as doshin (village officers) in the Celestial Order (Section 2.1.9). Not primarily a combat unit, though they can participate in mass combat. Their core function is maintaining order in provinces and settlements: suppressing banditry, deterring crime, and keeping trade flowing safely. Some garrison troops receive individualized training from samurai and are classified as budoka — elevated commoners with martial capability but no samurai status. A province without adequate garrison suffers significant maluses to stability, trade income, and population loyalty.

- Source: heimin class. Drawn from town or village PU, similar to levy but assigned locally rather than campaigning.

- Cost: 0.75 Arms per PU (equip, one time) + 0.35 Rice per PU per season + 0.2 Koku per PU per season. Less expensive than samurai, more than raw levies. Full costs and size requirements defined in Section 4.3.11.

- Primary function: reduce criminality in assigned province or settlement. Maluses without garrison include: trade harassment reducing Koku output, increased banditry events, population unrest, and reduced tax collection efficiency.

- Secondary function: can be called into mass combat as auxiliary forces. Not elite, but contributes numbers.

- Permanence: semi-permanent. Assigned to a location and remain there unless redeployed.

✓ DONE — Garrison costs and size requirements fully defined in Section 4.3.11. See that section for all costs and size requirements.

### 4.3.20 Political & Courtier System — LOCKED

Politics in Rokugan is not a sideshow to military power — it is an alternative path to the same goals. A skilled courtier can halt a war, force a trade agreement, isolate an enemy clan diplomatically, or engineer a general's disgrace without a single sword being drawn. The political system exists to give courtier characters (both player and NPC) meaningful agency at the same level as military commanders.

**Core Concept:**

Political actions are resolved through a combination of the acting character's skills and the disposition between the parties involved. A Scorpion courtier attempting to convince a Lion general to stand down is not just rolling dice — the existing relationship between those characters, the political context, the setting (Winter Court vs. a battlefield tent), and the interests at stake all modify the outcome. Success can be partial, full, or backfire catastrophically.

- Political actions can: halt a military campaign, force a trade agreement, impose an embargo, demand tribute, forge or break an alliance, publicly humiliate a rival, or engineer a policy change within a clan.

- Resolution is skill-based (Courtier, Etiquette, Sincerity, Intimidation, Manipulation — per L5R4e) modified by disposition between characters.

- Disposition is a tracked value between any two characters — it reflects personal history, clan relations, past favors, insults, and debts. A courtier who has built up good will with a target has a significantly easier time than one operating cold.

- Context matters: the same action attempted at Winter Court, at sword point, or through a secret letter has different base TNs and consequences.

### 4.3.21 Kami Worship System — LOCKED

In Rokugan, the Fortunes are not abstract beliefs. They are real entities whose attention, pleasure, and displeasure manifest in harvests, battles, population growth, and the flow of wealth. The worship system gives temples and shrines mechanical weight and makes the spiritual life of the Empire felt across every production system, every army, and every province.

The Seven Great Fortunes require collective worship at every level of the feudal hierarchy. Failure to maintain their worship does not simply withhold a bonus — it draws their active displeasure, expressed as compounding maluses that stack from Empire down to Province. Minor Fortunes reward those who cultivate their favor with provincial bonuses, but impose no penalties for neglect. They are opportunity, not obligation.

**WORSHIP POINTS — LOCKED**

The unit of spiritual currency is the Worship Point (WP). WP are generated each season by worship locations and active prayer. The threshold each province must meet per season for each Great Fortune is 10 WP. The same threshold applies to every province regardless of clan size or military strength. Minor Clans with one province have one province worth of obligation. Great Clans with thirty provinces have thirty provinces worth of obligation — and thirty provinces worth of Shugenja and infrastructure to meet it. See cascade thresholds below for Family, Clan, and Empire-level obligations.

**PASSIVE WORSHIP — LOCKED**

Every worship location generates WP automatically each season from background population devotion. This represents heimin offering prayers, leaving offerings, and maintaining shrines. No Shugenja, no AP required. General locations distribute WP across all Seven Great Fortunes simultaneously. Dedicated locations focus all WP on one Fortune only at 3× the general rate.

General worship location passive generation per season:

- Roadside shrine — 0.5 WP split across all Seven Great Fortunes

- Village shrine — 1 WP split across all Seven

- Local shrine — 2 WP split across all Seven

- Temple — 4 WP split across all Seven

- Shinden — 8 WP split across all Seven. Note: Temples and Shinden are distinct settlements (Section 4.3.22), not shrine infrastructure. Shrines (roadside, village, local) are provincial improvements attached to existing settlements.

Dedicated worship location passive generation per season (single Fortune only):

- Dedicated roadside shrine — 1.5 WP to one Fortune Dedicated village shrine — 3 WP to one Fortune

- Dedicated local shrine — 6 WP to one Fortune

- Dedicated temple — 12 WP to one Fortune

- Dedicated Shinden — 24 WP to one Fortune

Passive worship alone is insufficient to meet the 10 WP threshold for most provinces. A dedicated temple (12 WP passive) meets it barely without active worship. A dedicated Shinden (24 WP passive) meets it comfortably. General temples and smaller shrines require regular Shugenja and monk visits to reach threshold. This is intentional — active worship is always required for most provinces.

**CASCADE THRESHOLDS — LOCKED (Values TEMPORARY — see note)**

Worship failure is evaluated at four levels. Each level checks aggregate WP across all provinces in that entity. Thresholds scale with entity size. A province that meets its own threshold but belongs to a Family that fails still carries the Family malus — collective failure affects everyone within it.

- Province threshold: 10 WP per Fortune per season. Applies to each individual province independently.

- Family threshold: 60 WP per Fortune per season. Aggregate across all provinces in the Family, with a collective efficiency discount — the threshold is slightly more forgiving than strict per-province sum.

- Clan threshold: 150 WP per Fortune per season. Aggregate across all Family territories in the Clan.

- Empire threshold: 800 WP per Fortune per season. Aggregate across all Clan territories in Rokugan.

⚠️ TEMPORARY VALUES — Province counts are not yet defined in the GDD. Family, Clan, and Empire thresholds must be recalculated once the full map and administrative hierarchy are designed. The structure — aggregate sum with collective efficiency discount at each tier — is LOCKED. The specific numbers are provisional and will be revised when the map is built.

**ACTIVE WORSHIP CONTRIBUTION RATES — LOCKED**

When a named character spends 1 AP on PERFORM_WORSHIP, they generate WP based on their type:

- Normal named character (lord, courtier, bushi, ronin): 1 WP flat

- Named monk (Brotherhood of Shinsei): 2 WP flat — reflects training and devotion, consistent but without the Shugenja’s variable upside

- Shugenja: 1 WP base (same as a normal character) + variable bonus from Lore: Theology + Ring roll vs TN 15 (up to +3 bonus WP from roll result, further amplified by location Free Raises). A Shugenja who fails the roll still generates the 1 WP base.

This creates a clear hierarchy: Shugenja (powerful, variable) > named monks (reliable, consistent) > everyone else (minimal but present) > passive background (always on, never sufficient alone). A province with regular monk visits and occasional Shugenja presence is spiritually healthy. A province relying on passive generation alone will fail its threshold.

**DIRECTED VS SPLIT WORSHIP — LOCKED**

Passive worship from background population always splits automatically across all Seven Great Fortunes. Active worship — any named character spending 1 AP on PERFORM_WORSHIP at a general location — gives the character a choice each time:

- Directed: All WP from that action go to one specific Fortune. The character chooses which Fortune to address. Used when a specific Fortune is approaching or past a failure threshold and needs immediate attention.

- Split: WP distributed equally across all Seven Great Fortunes. Used for general maintenance when no Fortune is in immediate danger, or for routine circuit worship without a specific concern.

Dedicated locations always direct their WP to their specific Fortune automatically — there is no split option at a dedicated shrine or temple. The dedication is the point.

For the NPC Decision Engine, PERFORM_WORSHIP decomposition checks known worship states from Divination results and selects directed or split accordingly. If a Fortune is at Restless or worse the engine directs all worship there. If all Fortunes are healthy it defaults to split. A monk with a specific PERFORM_RITUAL objective targeting one Fortune always directs. A CHISHIKI or CHUGI monk doing routine rounds defaults to split out of thoroughness.

**ACTIVE WORSHIP — LOCKED**

Any named character may spend 1 AP at a worship location to pray. This generates a flat contribution based on location size. Shugenja additionally roll Lore: Theology + the relevant Ring vs TN 15. The result determines bonus WP generated above the flat base. Beating TN by 5 = +1 bonus WP. By 10 = +2. By 15 = +3.

Location size gives Free Raises on the Shugenja roll:

- Roadside shrine / village shrine — no Free Raises

- Local shrine — +1 Free Raise

- Temple — +2 Free Raises

- Shinden — +3 Free Raises

Overflow WP does not carry forward to the next season. The check resets each season. Shugenja who perform Divination learn whether they are comfortably above threshold or cutting it close, but not the exact value.

**RING ASSOCIATIONS — LOCKED**

Each Great Fortune is associated with one Ring, used for the Shugenja worship roll:

- Benten (Romantic Love) — Air

- Bishamon (Strength) — Fire

- Daikoku (Wealth) — Water

- Hotei (Contentment) — Water

- Jurojin (Longevity) — Void

**INTEGRATED AND INDEPENDENT WORSHIP SITES — LOCKED**

Worship locations are not present in every settlement by default. They exist where they have been built historically or where a lord has invested Koku to construct them. At game start, significant settlements and sacred sites will have appropriate worship locations reflecting their canonical identity — great cities, clan capitals, and famous pilgrimage sites begin with temples or Shinden already present. Most villages and minor settlements begin with nothing. Shrines (roadside, village, local) are provincial infrastructure — attached to existing settlements, built with Koku, no founding population required. Temples, Shinden, and Monasteries are independent settlements on the World Map with their own resident populations. A shrine cannot become a temple. Full construction rules are in Section 4.3.22.

**DIVINATION — LOCKED**

Only Shugenja can read the current worship state of a Fortune. This is done through the Divination skill (Lore: Theology + Ring) once per season. Results are expressed as threshold tiers with flavor text, not numerical values. The check tells the Shugenja the current status — not the previous season or the projected future.

- TN 15 — Provincial worship status for one Fortune

- TN 20 (1 Raise) — Family status

- TN 25 (2 Raises) — Clan status

- TN 30 (3 Raises) — Empire-wide status

Threshold flavor text:

- “The Fortune is pleased” — threshold met comfortably, no malus

- “The Fortune grows restless” — approaching warning threshold

- “The Fortune’s gaze has turned away” — Tier 1 malus active (Restless)

- “The Fortune is displeased” — Tier 2 malus active (Displeased)

- “The Fortune is wrathful” — Tier 3 malus active (Wrathful), crisis-level

Shugenja who exceed the threshold also receive an indication of surplus — they know if they are comfortably over or barely over, but not the exact figure.

DIVINATION AP COST — RESOLVED (v574): Divination is embedded in PERFORM_WORSHIP, not a separate action. When a shugenja spends 1 AP on PERFORM_WORSHIP, they also learn the worship status of the Fortune(s) they prayed to. Directed worship reveals the status of that one Fortune. Split worship reveals the status of all seven Great Fortunes. No additional AP cost. The once-per-season frequency limit still applies — a shugenja who has already learned a Fortune’s status this season does not receive a second reading from subsequent worship. Consequence: shugenja who worship regularly always know the spiritual status of the Fortunes they serve. Shugenja who never worship are spiritually blind. The cost of Divination is the cost of worship itself — prayer and diagnosis are the same spiritual act.

**HONOR AND GLORY CONSEQUENCES — LOCKED**

When a lord’s Shugenja reports a failure threshold through Divination, the lord suffers immediate Honor loss. This is private knowledge — the lord knows their own spiritual negligence. If the failure becomes public knowledge through the topic system — other Shugenja in neighboring territories performing their own Divination and comparing results, or a Shugenja being bribed or coerced into revealing the information — the lord also suffers Glory loss.

This creates a genuine dilemma. Recovering from a serious worship failure requires help — more Shugenja, more resources, possibly requesting aid from allies. But asking for help means admitting the problem exists, which spreads the information and triggers the Glory loss. A lord must choose between suffering quietly and worsening, or asking for help and losing face.

**FAILURE CASCADE — LOCKED**

Worship failure generates crisis and applies maluses through a cascading hierarchy. Maluses stack downward — a province that fails at every level carries all four simultaneously.

- Province fails worship threshold — Tier 4 topic generated. Provincial malus only. Handled locally.

- Family fails worship threshold — Tier 3 crisis. Family malus stacks on top of any Empire or Clan malus already active.

- Clan fails worship threshold — Tier 2 crisis. Clan malus fires for all territories of that clan.

- Empire fails worship threshold — Tier 1 crisis. Global malus. Every province in Rokugan is affected simultaneously. Cannot be hidden.

A province within a Clan that met its threshold, within a Family that met its threshold, within an Empire that met its threshold carries zero malus — fully protected by collective worship at every level. A province that failed at every level carries Empire + Clan + Family + Provincial malus simultaneously. That province is in genuine spiritual crisis.

### 4.3.22 Settlement Creation & Fortifications — LOCKED

Settlements in Rokugan do not upgrade from one type to another. A village is always a village. A castle town is always a castle town. Cities and castle towns exist at game start as established historical centers and cannot be built during play. What lords can do is found new villages on suitable land, construct fortifications as distinct military settlements, and build worship locations (shrines as infrastructure, temples and Shinden as independent settlements). New villages can also form organically as surplus population seeks new land.

**SETTLEMENT TYPES — LOCKED**

- Village — agricultural settlement. Founded by Provincial Daimyo or formed organically. No inherent defenses. An army occupies it without siege.

- Castle Town — exists at game start only. Cannot be built. Has walls, garrison, and full siege mechanics (Section 11.7).

- City — exists at game start only. Cannot be built. Metropolis-scale settlement with full siege mechanics.

- Fortification — military structure only. No civilian population. Can be built by Provincial Daimyo on their own territory, or by a Family Daimyo or Clan Champion on any vassal territory. Requires a deployed garrison to provide any defensive value. Temple — distinct religious settlement. Independent location on the World Map with resident monks and Shugenja. Founded deliberately. Can be besieged. Not present by default in all locations — only where built or historically established. Shinden — great temple complex. The largest form of religious settlement. Independent World Map location. Significant founding population and Koku investment required. Monastery — Brotherhood of Shinsei settlement. Independent World Map location housing exclusively monks. Founded by Family Daimyo, Clan Champion, or the Brotherhood itself with lord’s approval.

**ORGANIC VILLAGE FORMATION — LOCKED**

New villages form organically when a province accumulates sufficient surplus PU above what existing settlements can comfortably support. The formation is driven by people, not by lord decisions — surplus population moves to available land and establishes a new hamlet. The lord whose province the new settlement forms in automatically becomes its lord.

Prerequisites for organic formation to fire:

- Surplus PU: existing settlements must have collective PU above their comfortable capacity

- Rice stockpile: source settlements must be healthy enough to seed the new hamlet — provinces in famine or shortage cannot generate organic settlements

- Stability: province must be above minimum stability threshold — unstable provinces generate refugees, not settlers

- Suitable terrain: available land within the province must be farmable (see terrain difficulty below)

Each migrating PU carries 1.0 Rice from the source settlement’s stockpile. The source loses both the PU and the Rice. The new hamlet starts with that Rice — enough for four seasons of consumption, covering the time until its first harvest.

**Terrain Difficulty for Organic Formation — LOCKED**

- Plains / Flatlands — easiest. Low surplus PU threshold, forms quickly

- River delta / Coastal lowlands — easy. Productive once established

- Forest / Light hills — moderate. Requires clearing. Higher PU threshold, slower to establish

- Heavy hills / Rough terrain — hard. Marginal farmland. Requires substantial surplus

- Mountains — very hard. Valley locations only. Extremely rare organically

- Shadowlands-adjacent / Tainted terrain — effectively impossible organically

**Cross-Province Migration — LOCKED**

Surplus PU may migrate across provincial and clan boundaries to found new settlements, but difficulty increases significantly at each boundary:

- Within the same Province — standard. Lord coordinates freely within their own territory

- Within the same Family — relatively easy. Family lords coordinate, terrain permitting. Higher surplus threshold than within a single province

- Between Families within the same Clan — rare. Requires high surplus, high stability on both sides, and political will from both Family Daimyo

- Between Clans — extremely rare and hard. Enormous surplus required, very high stability on both sides, explicit political agreement between the clans involved

PU in transit consumes Rice and is vulnerable. The physical movement of population between provinces follows the same logistics rules as army supply (Section 4.3.16).

**DELIBERATE VILLAGE FOUNDING — LOCKED**

A Provincial Daimyo may deliberately found a new village within their own province without waiting for organic growth. This represents clearing land, establishing infrastructure, and seeding the new settlement with population from existing villages.

Requirements:

- Authority: Provincial Daimyo only, within their own province

- PU cost: minimum 1.0 PU moved from existing settlements within the province to populate the new village

- Rice cost: 1.0 Rice per PU moved (the migrants’ survival seed, same as organic migration)

- Koku cost: 3 Koku units (World Map scale) — founding materials, tools, basic infrastructure. PROVISIONAL

- Terrain requirement: suitable land available within the province (same terrain difficulty scale as organic formation)

- AP cost: 1 AP from the founding lord or a designated agent present in the province

The new village starts with the moved PU and their carried Rice stockpile. It is immediately under the founding lord’s authority. It generates passive worship, taxes, and Rice production from its first season, though at reduced rates until it establishes fully.

**FORTIFICATIONS — LOCKED**

A fortification is a purely military structure — walls, towers, and defensive works with no civilian population. It has no economic output and generates no Rice or Koku. Its sole function is military defense.

Founding authority:

- Provincial Daimyo — may build on their own territory

- Family Daimyo — may build on any vassal territory within their family

- Clan Champion — may build on any territory within their clan

Cost: 5 Koku units (World Map scale). No initial PU required. PROVISIONAL.

**Fortification Garrison — LOCKED**

An empty fortification provides no defensive value. It must have a garrison deployed to it from a castle town or city before it functions militarily. The garrison consumes Rice from the fortification’s own stockpile at the standard military rate of 0.0039 Rice per PU per daily tick. The fortification must be supplied before and during any siege. If its Rice stockpile reaches zero the garrison starves.

Siege duration examples at maximum storage (0.5 Rice):

- 0.5 PU garrison: 0.5 ÷ (0.5 × 0.0039) = ~256 daily ticks (~64 real days, ~2.1 real months)

- 1.0 PU garrison: 0.5 ÷ (1.0 × 0.0039) = ~128 daily ticks (~32 real days, ~1.1 real months)

- 2.0 PU garrison: 0.5 ÷ (2.0 × 0.0039) = ~64 daily ticks (~16 real days, ~0.5 real months)

A larger garrison burns through food faster. The optimal garrison is small and disciplined — enough to defend the walls, not so large that it starves itself out before relief arrives. Resupply during a siege dramatically extends survival time.

**Fortification Defensive Values — LOCKED**

- Siege required: yes. An army cannot simply occupy a fortification — it must conduct a formal siege

- Defense bonus during storm assault: +2 Defense (lesser than town +3, significantly less than castle)

- Maximum food storage: 0.5 Rice

- Standard garrison: 0.5 PU minimum to activate defensive value

- No civilian population minimum — purely military

A fortification cannot be bypassed by an invading army for the same reason castles cannot — a garrisoned fortification on a supply route threatens the attacker’s logistics (Section 11.7 Why Castles Cannot Be Bypassed). An ungarrisoned fortification can be bypassed freely — it is just stonework with no one inside.

**WORSHIP INFRASTRUCTURE — LOCKED**

Worship locations in Rokugan fall into two distinct categories that are never interchangeable. Shrines are provincial infrastructure attached to existing settlements. Temples, Shinden, and Monasteries are independent settlements in their own right. A shrine cannot grow into a temple — if a lord wants a temple they found one as a new settlement. The two systems are entirely separate.

**Shrines — Provincial Infrastructure — LOCKED**

Roadside shrines, village shrines, and local shrines are infrastructure — small structures attached to existing settlements. They are built like the Forge: a provincial improvement requiring Koku, no PU, no population. They do not appear as distinct settlements on the World Map. They cannot be converted into temples. They are what they are.

Shrines are not present in every settlement by default. They exist where they have been built or where they historically existed at game start. A remote mountain village may have nothing.

Shrine construction costs and passive WP generation:

- Roadside shrine — 5 Koku, 1 season. 0.5 WP passive per season, split across all Seven Great Fortunes (general) or 1.5 WP to one Fortune (dedicated, 12 Koku).

- Village shrine — 15 Koku, 2 seasons. 1 WP passive per season split across all Seven (general) or 3 WP to one Fortune (dedicated, 30 Koku).

- Local shrine — 30 Koku, 3 seasons. 2 WP passive per season split across all Seven (general) or 6 WP to one Fortune (dedicated, 60 Koku).

The Fortune a dedicated shrine serves is chosen at construction and cannot be changed without demolishing and rebuilding. Demolition is instant but destroys the Koku investment entirely.

Construction authority: Provincial Daimyo on their own territory. Family Daimyo or Clan Champion on any vassal territory at their own Koku expense.

**Temples, Shinden, and Monasteries — Distinct Settlements — LOCKED**

Full temples, Shinden, and Brotherhood monasteries are independent settlements on the World Map. They have resident populations — monks, Shugenja, priests, pilgrims — and exist as named locations that can be visited, besieged, and destroyed. They may be located near other settlements but are always distinct from them.

Like villages and fortifications, temples and monasteries are founded deliberately. They are never created by upgrading a shrine. They require a location on the World Map, a founding population of monks or Shugenja staff, Koku for construction, and time to build.

Temple and Shinden construction costs and passive WP generation:

- Temple — 80 Koku, 4 seasons. Requires minimum 0.5 PU founding population (monks or Shugenja). 4 WP passive per season split across all Seven (general) or 12 WP to one Fortune (dedicated, 160 Koku).

- Shinden — 250 Koku, 8 seasons. Requires minimum 1.0 PU founding population. 8 WP passive per season split across all Seven (general) or 24 WP to one Fortune (dedicated, 400 Koku).

Monasteries follow the same founding system as temples but house exclusively Brotherhood of Shinsei monks. They generate WP primarily from their resident monk population performing active worship rather than from passive background devotion — their founding population IS their worship engine.

Founding authority: Family Daimyo or Clan Champion, on any suitable location within their territory. The Brotherhood of Shinsei may also found monasteries independently on unclaimed or permitted land — subject to the ruling lord’s approval.

Temples and Shinden under construction generate no WP until complete. If an existing worship settlement is being expanded — a temple becoming a Shinden — it maintains its current capacity throughout construction.
