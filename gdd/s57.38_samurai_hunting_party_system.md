## 57.38 Samurai Hunting Party System

### 57.38.1 Design Intent

The Samurai Hunting Party System models the ceremonial and social hunts that samurai organise to demonstrate prowess, build disposition with peers and patrons, and generate Glory through the killing of dangerous game. Historically, daimyo and samurai across Japan held formal hunts (takagari for falconry, yumi-tori for general hunting) as part recreation, part political theatre, part training. This system brings that behaviour into the simulation as an emergent social activity any samurai can initiate — not a lord-tier privilege, not an abstracted court event, but a first-class social mechanic that produces real ASCII map content when a player is involved and resolves through background engine rolls when only NPCs are involved.

The system is intentionally built on existing infrastructure rather than introducing new subsystems. Hunt invitations use the existing Daily Conversation System (Section 12.6) and the letter system (Section 12.7). Motivation to host a hunt is driven by existing NeedTypes (SEEK_GLORY, RAISE_DISPOSITION). The hunt itself, when a player is involved, becomes an ASCII mission generated through the Quest System (Section 56). Aftermath produces standard Tier 4 topics (Section 16.5) and Glory awards calibrated against the existing Glory event table (Section 4.6). The design surface is two new ActionIDs, one new Tier 4 topic type, one character sheet field, and supporting scoring entries in Annex A.

Per the Section 9 Design Intent (NPC actions resolve through engine dice rolls; player involvement triggers ASCII content), hunts involving only NPCs resolve entirely through background rolls against the target beast’s stats. Hunts involving a player character generate an ASCII mission spanning one to three map bubbles: travel, tracking, and engagement. The same world-state condition produces different resolution paths depending on who is involved.

### 57.38.2 ANNOUNCE_HUNT ActionID

ANNOUNCE_HUNT is the action a character fires to host a hunt. It is available to any samurai regardless of Status or lordship tier. Category 11 (Personal Development), alongside TRAIN, MEDITATE, CRAFT, and the artisan actions.

**AP cost:** 0. ANNOUNCE_HUNT does not consume AP. The action creates a Tier 4 Social topic in the host’s active topic pool and schedules a future hunt date. It is an act of intention, not of labour — comparable in cost terms to PUBLIC_DECLARATION, which also commits the speaker to a future act without consuming AP at declaration time. The AP cost lives in what the host does afterward: writing invitation letters (WRITE_LETTER, 1 free per IC day for non-lords), travelling to the hunt site (BEGIN_TRAVEL), and participating in the hunt itself (Quest System consumes the OOC day for player characters).

**Roll:** None. No skill roll is required to announce a hunt. The announcement itself is simply a statement of intent. Whether the hunt succeeds depends on the actual hunt resolution, not on the announcement.

**Contexts:** AT_OWN_HOLDINGS, VISITING, AT_COURT. Not valid in ON_CAMPAIGN, UNDER_SIEGE, or TRAVELING contexts — a samurai in those situations has more pressing concerns and the social infrastructure for organising a hunt is not present.

**Preconditions:** Character must have Hunting Skill rank 1 or higher — a samurai with no training in hunting has no credibility as a hunt host. The hunt location must be within travel range (target a province with appropriate wilderness terrain: forest, hills, or grassland; not Shadowlands, not pure coast, not urban provinces with no huntable terrain). The host must not have an active announced hunt already pending — one hunt at a time per character.

**ImmediateNeed fields:** target_province_id (the province where the hunt will take place); hunt_date_ic_day (the IC day the hunt is scheduled for; must be 7–21 IC days in the future to allow invitation and travel time); priority_invitee_id (int or null — when ANNOUNCE_HUNT fires under a RAISE_DISPOSITION NeedType, this field is populated with the target of that disposition; null otherwise).

**Effect:** Creates a Tier 4 Social topic “Hunt Announcement” in the host’s active topic pool. The topic carries the following payload: host_id, target_province_id, hunt_date_ic_day, host_status (snapshot of the host’s Status at announcement time). If priority_invitee_id is set, the host also immediately generates a high-priority WRITE_LETTER ImmediateNeed addressed to that character carrying the hunt topic — the priority invitation. The topic begins propagating through Daily Conversation and letters as any other Tier 4 Social topic would.

**Objective alignment:** SEEK_GLORY 65, RAISE_DISPOSITION 60. Scores sit below the pure-glory actions (PUBLIC_PERFORMANCE at 75, DELIVER_GIFT at 75) because a hunt is a larger-commitment event — appropriate when those smaller actions are unavailable or when the character’s lean favours outdoor activity over court performance. Personality filters (Annex C) modify these scores: Hiruma, Shinjo, Matsu, Usagi, Hida, and Toritaka school types receive a +15 lean on ANNOUNCE_HUNT; Doji, Otomo, Soshi, and Miya courtier schools receive a −15 lean (hunting is not their social language).

**Skill mapping:** Annex D entry: primary Hunting, secondary none, trait null (no roll). The primary skill is recorded for glory scaling and personality lean purposes — characters with higher Hunting gain greater topic relevance for their announcements (they are known as hunters).

### 57.38.3 REQUEST_HUNT_INVITATION ActionID

A character who hears about a hunt announcement and wants to attend fires REQUEST_HUNT_INVITATION. This is the mirror of the invitation flow — the guest requesting inclusion rather than the host extending an invite. Category 11 (Personal Development).

**AP cost:** 0 if verbal (the requester and host are in the same settlement — uses daily conversation mechanics). Otherwise uses the letter system — 1 free letter per IC day for non-lords, or 1 Civilian Order for lords. No separate AP cost for the request itself; the cost is the letter/conversation delivery.

**Roll:** None. The request itself carries no roll. The host evaluates it per the response logic in Section 57.38.4.

**Preconditions:** Requester must hold an active Hunt Announcement topic in their known_topics referring to the target host. The host must be within Status ±2.0 of the requester — requests beyond this band are not delivered (per Section 57.38.4 Status rules). The hunt must not have already occurred (hunt_date_ic_day must be in the future). The requester must not already be on the confirmed invitee list for this hunt (already invited, no need to request).

**Objective alignment:** SEEK_GLORY 50, RAISE_DISPOSITION 55, SEEK_PEACE 55. Scores reflect that requesting an invitation is less direct than announcing one’s own hunt but covers similar motivational ground. RAISE_DISPOSITION captures the ally-sponsored case — a character maintaining a relationship with the host fires this NeedType; attending an ally’s hunt is a natural form of disposition investment.

**Effect:** Generates a request record in the host’s pending_hunt_requests list (transient per-hunt field stored on the Hunt Announcement topic payload). The host evaluates pending requests at the next tick after the request arrives, per Section 57.38.4. Response is delivered back to the requester as a Tier 4 topic update (“invited” or “declined”).

**Skill mapping:** Annex D entry: primary Etiquette, secondary Courtier, trait null (no roll). Records skills for personality lean purposes.

### 57.38.4 Invitation and Response Logic

Invitations work in two directions: the host extends invitations to specific characters, and other characters request invitations they did not receive. Both flows use the same Status and disposition logic, applied symmetrically.

**Status band ±2.0:** Invitations and requests are bounded by Status. A host and a potential invitee must be within 2.0 Status of each other. Invitations or requests outside this band are not delivered by the system — a Status 1.0 ronin does not realistically host (or ask to attend) a hunt with a Status 4.5 Clan Champion. The social distance is too great; the letter does not reach the intended recipient’s attention, or if it does, it is treated as presumptuous and ignored without response.

**Host-initiated invitation — Peer (Status within ±0.5):** Standard. The invitee evaluates based on disposition toward the host and current objectives. Accepting is normal; declining requires a reason but is not a significant social slight. Default accept if disposition ≥ +11 (Acquaintance) and the invitee is not otherwise committed. Declining: small disposition loss (−1) from the host’s perspective, no Honor or Glory impact.

**Host-initiated invitation — Downward (invitee is 1.0+ Status lower than host):** The invitee is being honoured. Default accept regardless of disposition. Accepting generates +0.1 Glory for the invitee (per the Publicly Praise Someone anchor in Section 4.6 — the invitee is publicly elevated by the host’s attention) and +2 disposition toward the host. Declining a downward invitation is a significant social error: −0.2 Honor for the invitee and −5 disposition from the host — they have rejected a lord’s hospitality. Only compelling circumstances (active campaign, serious illness, competing lord’s order) make declining acceptable; the NPC Decision Engine applies a strong bias toward acceptance here.

**Host-initiated invitation — Upward (invitee is 1.0+ Status higher than host):** The host is requesting the higher-Status character’s company. Default decline unless disposition is Friend (+31) or higher, or unless the invitee has a specific reason to want to build the relationship (their own active SEEK_GLORY or RAISE_DISPOSITION NeedType targeting the host). Declining carries no cost — the higher-Status character owed the host nothing. Accepting is generous: +3 disposition toward the host from the invitee (the host is flattered and grateful), and the invitee gains +0.1 Glory at the hunt if they attend (being gracious with their station).

**Guest-initiated request — Peer (Status within ±0.5):** The host evaluates against disposition. Default accept if disposition ≥ +11 (Acquaintance). Declining a peer request is socially normal — the host controls the guest list — and carries a small disposition cost (−1) from the requester’s perspective.

**Guest-initiated request — Downward (requester is 1.0+ Status lower than host):** The host is being asked to include a lower-Status character. Default accept if disposition is neutral or positive — the request is flattering, and it is a minor act of generosity that costs nothing. Declining is socially acceptable but generates no disposition benefit. Accepting generates +1 disposition toward the host from the requester (they are grateful for the inclusion).

**Guest-initiated request — Upward (requester is 1.0+ Status higher than host):** A higher-Status character asking to attend is almost always an honour the host must accept. Default accept regardless of disposition — declining a higher-Status character’s request carries a serious social and Honor penalty (−0.2 Honor, −5 disposition from the requester). The host generally cannot afford to refuse. Accepting generates +3 disposition toward the host from the requester (gratitude for the hospitality).

**Rival exception:** If the requester has low disposition with the host (below −10), the host may decline even an upward request. This is socially understood — bringing a known rival into a hunt carries security and political risk. Declining a rival’s request carries no penalty at the host’s end regardless of Status. This is also the one case where the host is expected to accept a request they do not personally want: if the rival’s disposition is neutral or positive but their virtue profile marks them as politically aligned against the host’s faction, the host may still accept (taking the political cost of refusing exceeds the cost of the uneasy day).

**Who actually attends:** Only invited characters attend. Hearing about a hunt through the topic propagation system does not confer a right to attend — gatecrashing a Rokugani social event is a serious breach. A character who hears about a hunt and wants in must fire REQUEST_HUNT_INVITATION. The attendee list is built from: (1) the host, (2) the priority_invitee (if one was named), and (3) any character the host has invited or accepted a request from.

### 57.38.4a Travel to the Hunt

Accepting a hunt invitation is only meaningful if the invitee can physically reach the hunt location by the hunt date. Rokugan is large and travel is slow — a character in Crane lands cannot attend a hunt in Crab province 4 IC days from now. This subsection specifies how travel interacts with acceptance, using the existing travel system (Section 4.4) without introducing new travel mechanics.

**Feasibility preflight at acceptance time:** When an invitee’s engine evaluates whether to accept a hunt invitation, the feasibility check fires before the disposition/Status logic of Section 57.38.4 is applied. The engine computes travel_time_from(invitee_current_location, hunt_location_province) using the standard Section 4.4 travel rules — 1 sub-tile per real day at normal pace, faster for mounted characters on cavalry-eligible terrain per Section 11.7a. Add a 1-IC-day buffer for early arrival. If the invitee can depart within their next available AP window and arrive at the hunt province at least 1 IC day before hunt_date_ic_day, acceptance proceeds normally and the Section 57.38.4 evaluation applies. If the invitee cannot arrive in time, acceptance is blocked and the invitee returns a “too far to attend” decline response.

**Too-far decline:** A decline triggered by travel infeasibility is not a social slight. No disposition penalty applies at either end. The host receives a decline message flagged as “unable to attend due to travel distance” and understands the response as the distance issue it is. The existing disposition penalties from Section 57.38.4 (for example, −5 for refusing a downward invitation without cause) do not fire for a too-far decline. This protects the host-invitee relationship in cases where the host wanted the invitee present but scheduled the hunt too soon for distant characters to reach. If the host wanted a particular distant invitee to attend, they should have scheduled the hunt further out (closer to the 21 IC day maximum).

**Auto-scheduled travel on acceptance (NPC):** When an NPC invitee’s engine commits to acceptance after the feasibility check passes, BEGIN_TRAVEL is queued as a high-priority action on their next AP tick, targeting the hunt_location_province. The NPC begins travelling toward the hunt location and continues until arrival. Once arrived, the character’s context becomes VISITING the hunt province awaiting the hunt date; they remain there through the intervening IC days. Normal NPC Decision Engine activity resumes at the destination — the character may engage in court actions, conversation, or other activities at the hunt province while waiting, but BEGIN_TRAVEL is not re-fired to leave until the hunt concludes. This matches the existing travel-to-court and travel-to-meeting patterns established elsewhere in the NPC engine.

**Player character exception:** Player characters are not auto-scheduled. A player who accepts a hunt invitation is expected to manually fire BEGIN_TRAVEL when and if they choose. The feasibility check still applies at acceptance time — a player cannot accept an invitation they physically could not attend — but the actual travel execution is under player control. If a player accepts but does not travel in time and the hunt_date_ic_day arrives with the player still at their original location, the hunt proceeds without them. The player is treated as a no-show. The acceptance does not automatically convert to a decline mid-window — the player remains on the invitee list until the hunt date passes.

**Travel delay during transit:** An accepted invitee who begins travel on schedule but gets delayed en route (bandit attack, weather, illness, unexpected conflict, being caught by a higher-priority order from their lord) may fail to arrive by the hunt date despite sincere intent. The 1-IC-day buffer built into the feasibility check absorbs minor delays. Major delays that consume the buffer result in late arrival or no arrival. If the character arrives after the hunt has started but before it concludes, they are treated as a no-show for engagement purposes — the Kill Check has already fired in NPC-only resolution, or the ASCII mission has already spawned for player-involved resolution, and the late arrival cannot join mid-mission. They rejoin the social context at the hunt conclusion and receive no participant Glory. No disposition penalty applies — the host understands circumstances. A topic may fire noting the delay (“[Character] was delayed en route and missed the hunt”) at Tier 4.

**Same-settlement invitees:** An invitee already in the same settlement or province as the host at acceptance time has no travel requirement — they are already on site or can walk over trivially. The feasibility check auto-passes for same-province invitees. They continue normal activity until the hunt date and simply present themselves at the departure point when the hunt begins. This is the typical case for most hunt attendance — hosts most often invite local peers and their own vassals, all of whom share the host’s province.

### 57.38.4b Hunt Cancellation

A hunt announced between 7 and 21 IC days before its date passes through a vulnerable window. During that period the host may die, be imprisoned, be ordered elsewhere, be seriously wounded, or simply change their mind. The world does not wait politely. This subsection specifies how cancellations are detected, propagated, and socially accounted for. Cancellations come in two forms: involuntary (the host cannot attend for reasons outside their control) and voluntary (the host actively chooses to call the hunt off).

**Involuntary cancellation triggers:** The system auto-cancels a hunt when any of the following conditions become true for the host while their announced hunt is still pending: (1) the host dies — standard death event processing fires, and the cancellation is one consequence of many. (2) The host’s character sheet flags imprisoned or captive status (Section 11.3.14 legal_status entries of imprisoned, or narrative captivity via hostage exchange or kidnapping). (3) The host receives an order from their operational superior (Section 11.3.18) that physically conflicts with hunt attendance — specifically, a deployment or travel order to a destination where the travel time from that destination back to the hunt province exceeds the remaining IC days before hunt_date_ic_day. (4) The host is incapacitated — reaches Wound Rank Down or worse from combat, disease, poison, or other effect, and is not expected to recover by the hunt date based on their Stamina-driven healing rate. (5) The host is afflicted by a similar condition severe enough that the engine would reasonably treat them as unable to perform the hunt role.

**Involuntary cancellation processing:** When a trigger fires, the system retires the Hunt Announcement topic from all topic pools (the hunt will not happen, so anticipation is no longer relevant). A new Tier 4 Social topic is generated describing the cause: “[Host]’s hunt at [province] is cancelled — [Host] has [died / been imprisoned / been deployed to X / been gravely wounded].” In the death case, the hunt cancellation topic is subordinated to the Death topic from Section 16.5 — the hunt is a footnote, not the main event. No Glory or Honor penalty applies to the host. They did not choose this outcome, and Rokugani social norms recognise that circumstances can prevent any intended act. Accepted invitees who were travelling or about to travel receive no disposition change toward the host — the host is not responsible for the interruption. If the host died, standard grief disposition responses per Section 16.5 may apply through the death event regardless of hunt context.

**Voluntary cancellation — the CANCEL_HUNT ActionID:** A host who wishes to call off their own announced hunt fires CANCEL_HUNT. Category 11 (Personal Development). AP cost: 0 — the same zero-cost pattern as ANNOUNCE_HUNT; cancelling is simply communicating intent to unmake the prior intent. Roll: none. Contexts: AT_COURT, AT_OWN_HOLDINGS, VISITING, TRAVELING — wherever the host happens to be when they decide to cancel. Preconditions: the host must have an active announced hunt, the hunt_date_ic_day must still be in the future, and the host must not be dead, imprisoned, or incapacitated (those cases use the involuntary cancellation path). Effect: retires the Hunt Announcement topic from all topic pools; creates a new Tier 4 Personal topic “[Host] cancelled their announced hunt at [province]”; dispatches notification letters (per the hybrid notification rule below).

**Voluntary cancellation — social consequences:** The host takes −0.2 Glory. Calibrated below the Public Performance critical failure penalty of −0.3 (Section 4.6) because no disaster occurred — the host merely disappointed guests who were expecting an event. Each accepted invitee loses 1 disposition toward the host. The loss is small individually but accumulates: a host who repeatedly announces and cancels hunts will see meaningful disposition erosion with their social circle. The cancellation topic propagates normally through Tier 4 Social channels and may colour future social interactions the host has with characters who heard about it. No Honor penalty applies by default — cancelling is disappointing but not dishonourable in itself. If the circumstances of the cancellation indicate a specific breach (e.g., the host cancelled to avoid an obligation to a specific invitee, or the cancellation was a face-saving retreat from a political situation), additional Honor consequences may apply through the existing Section 4.6 event table at the simulation arbiter’s discretion.

**Hybrid notification to accepted invitees:** When a cancellation fires (voluntary or involuntary), accepted invitees learn about it through two channels operating in parallel. Primary channel: the cancellation topic enters the topic pool and propagates through normal Daily Conversation and letter mechanics (Sections 12.6 and 12.7). An invitee in the same settlement as the host or other informed characters will likely hear through conversation within 1–2 IC days. Secondary channel: each accepted invitee receives a priority notification letter at the moment cancellation fires. The letter is dispatched from the host’s character (voluntary cancellation) or from the host’s estate staff or immediate family (involuntary cancellation, including death). The notification letter is a special-case zero-cost message triggered by cancellation specifically — it does not consume the host’s letter allowance (per Section 12.7) or the estate’s resources. It travels at standard letter speed (1–3 IC days per province) and carries only the cancellation fact and reason. The letter does not carry a topic payload in the normal Section 12.7 sense — it is purely informational and is not mechanically equivalent to sending the cancellation topic. An invitee who receives the letter is guaranteed to have the cancellation fact before they arrive at the hunt site. The hybrid approach ensures notification reliability while respecting that communication in Rokugan normally flows through topics and letters.

**Invitees already en route:** An invitee who has already begun travelling toward the hunt location when cancellation fires has two options and the choice is theirs: (1) reverse course and return to their point of origin, or (2) continue to the hunt province for other reasons — to pay respects to a deceased host’s family, to express solidarity with an imprisoned host, to visit the province for its own sake. No mechanical penalty applies to either choice. An NPC invitee’s engine evaluates based on disposition to the host, current objectives, and other standing needs. A player invitee receives a notification in the UI when their character receives the notification letter or hears the topic through conversation and may redirect their travel at their next AP.

**CANCEL_HUNT restrictions:** CANCEL_HUNT can only be fired by the original host of an active announced hunt, before the hunt_date_ic_day arrives. A host who is dead, imprisoned, or incapacitated cannot fire CANCEL_HUNT — the involuntary cancellation path handles those cases. A hunt that has already been cancelled cannot be re-cancelled — the announcement topic is already retired. After cancellation, the host may announce a new hunt via ANNOUNCE_HUNT, subject to the standard precondition that no active announced hunt exists on that host (the previous cancellation cleared that flag).

**Annex D entry for CANCEL_HUNT:** action_id CANCEL_HUNT; primary_skill none; primary_emphasis none; primary_trait null (no roll); base_tn null; ap_cost 0; contexts AT_COURT, AT_OWN_HOLDINGS, VISITING, TRAVELING; requires_target false; requires_consumable none; validator_rules host has active announced hunt, hunt_date_ic_day in future, host not dead/imprisoned/incapacitated; hostile false; honor_cost 0 (default, may increase through Section 4.6 arbiter discretion); glory_cost −0.2 to host; output retires Hunt Announcement topic, creates cancellation topic, dispatches hybrid notification.

**Annex A entry for CANCEL_HUNT:** CANCEL_HUNT is not scored under standard NeedTypes for autonomous firing. The action exists primarily to support player decisions and a narrow set of engine cases (a new higher-priority order that the host accepts, a political calculus decision to withdraw from a compromised event). It is scored at 30 under RAISE_DISPOSITION for a specific case: when the host has a topic indicating that cancelling would repair a relationship with a specific character (e.g., the hunt was announced in a moment of pique and now the host wants to reconcile with the slighted party). Otherwise NPCs do not spontaneously cancel their own hunts — the action is usually either player-initiated or the consequence of a higher-priority lord order that the engine treats as a forced decline scenario.

### 57.38.4c Hunts During Winter Court

Winter Court is Rokugan’s central political season — a 120 IC day event at a single court location (Section 15.1) binding all attending characters to its rhythm. Yet historically, daimyo hosting Winter Court organised hunting parties as prestigious entertainment for their guests: a day in the local forests, a break from the perfume and intrigue of the court, a demonstration of the host’s hospitality beyond the formal sessions. This subsection specifies how the samurai hunting party system interacts with Winter Court so that court-adjacent hunts are supported without allowing attendees to effectively abandon the court under the guise of hunting.

**Proximity constraint:** When the ANNOUNCE_HUNT actor is AT_COURT at an active Winter Court (or any active court — the rule generalises to Clan Champion Courts and Provincial Courts as well), an additional precondition applies beyond the standard Section 57.38.2 checks. The target hunt province must be either (1) the same province the court is held in, or (2) an adjacent province reachable within 1 IC day of travel one-way from the court location per the Section 4.4 travel rules. Hunts announced with a target province further afield are blocked with a “too far from court” precondition failure — a Winter Court attendee cannot announce a hunt in their own home province three weeks of travel away and plausibly return in time to participate in court business. The constraint applies to hunts announced by court attendees; non-attendees are not affected and may announce hunts with the normal preconditions.

**Invitee travel check:** The travel feasibility check in Section 57.38.4a applies to Winter Court hunt invitations with a tightened window: an invitee who is also AT_COURT must be able to make the round trip (travel to hunt location, participate in the hunt, return to court) within 3 IC days total. For a same-province hunt this is trivial; for an adjacent-province hunt at 1 IC day each way plus 1 IC day of hunting, this fits exactly. Invitees who are not at the court but who would need to travel to attend a court-adjacent hunt follow the normal Section 57.38.4a rules; they are not subject to the tightened window because they are not returning to a court commitment.

**Court context preservation:** During a court-adjacent hunt, the host and attending court characters remain flagged AT_COURT for social accounting purposes. They have not abandoned court. Their physical location is VISITING the hunt province for the duration of the hunt, but their AT_COURT flag is retained as a secondary context. This matters for court-level topic participation: a character away on a court-adjacent hunt does not receive court-floor conversation content during their brief absence, but they are not treated as having left the court either — they resume normal court presence on return with no status penalty. If a character fails to return within the 3-IC-day window (travel delay, hunt disaster causing extended wound recovery, the character died on the hunt), their AT_COURT flag is then cleared and standard absence-from-court rules apply per Section 15.

**Court-hunt flavour on topic generation:** Hunts organised during Winter Court generate the normal announcement and aftermath topics per Sections 57.38.5 and 57.38.10, with an additional “at the court’s hunt” flavour tag. The tag affects topic propagation: because all court attendees are physically co-located at the court, the hunt topic spreads through the assembled characters much faster than a normal hunt topic would propagate across provinces. The Daily Conversation System (Section 12.6) reaches saturation on court-hunt topics within 1–2 IC days of the hunt resolving, compared to 5–10 IC days for a standard provincial hunt. Court attendees arriving fresh to the court (later in the session) may still hear about an earlier court-hunt as background social context. This reflects the real social dynamic: a hunt that happens “during the Crane’s Winter Court” is more memorable than one that happens in quiet isolation, because everyone important was there to talk about it.

**Host prestige bonus:** A host who runs a hunt that ends with the beast killed during an active Winter Court — whether a Success outcome (no casualties) or a Costly outcome (beast killed, a participant wounded) — receives an additional +0.1 Glory bonus. The bonus stacks on top of the full host Glory, whatever form that takes: if the host did not land the killing blow, they receive the +0.2 host bonus plus +0.1 Winter Court prestige (combined +0.3); if the host also fought as a combat participant alongside the other hunters, the additional +0.1 participant Glory per Section 57.38.8 also applies, producing +0.4 total host Glory for the hunt; if the host personally landed the killing blow, they receive +0.3 killer Glory plus +0.1 Winter Court prestige (combined +0.4). All three cases are valid. The bonus reflects the prestige of extending hospitality beyond the formal court sessions — the host has demonstrated that their largesse is not confined to the banquet hall. The bonus applies to any hunt held during the court regardless of whether the hunter is the actual court host (the daimyo or appointed figure hosting the court itself) or simply an attendee who ran a hunt during the session. Failed hunts (beast not killed, no casualties) and Disastrous hunts (a participant killed) during Winter Court do not receive the prestige bonus; a Disastrous hunt takes the standard −0.2 host Glory penalty per Section 57.38.8 (or the aggravated −0.4 if a non-combatant died) with no additional modifier — the high-profile setting does not amplify the loss, though the topic propagation speed means the embarrassment circulates quickly.

**One-hunt-per-court limit:** A character may announce no more than one hunt during any single Winter Court or Clan Champion Court session. The engine tracks this via a per-character flag set at ANNOUNCE_HUNT time during an active court and cleared at court close. This prevents hunt spam — a Crane host cannot monopolise court entertainment by announcing a weekly hunting party across 120 IC days of Winter Court, saturating attendees with accept/decline decisions. Multiple distinct characters may each announce their own single hunt during the same court, producing a realistic cadence where two or three hunts might occur during a long Winter Court across different hosts. Provincial Courts are short enough (Section 15.1) that the limit is rarely relevant, but the rule applies uniformly for consistency.

**Player considerations:** A player character attending Winter Court may announce or accept a hunt during the court, subject to the proximity and one-per-court constraints above. The OOC day cost of a player-involved hunt mission (1 OOC day = 4 IC days per Section 57.38.7) fits within the 3-IC-day court-hunt window acceptably — the additional IC day accounts for the mission’s narrative time rather than strict travel time and is absorbed by the court context preservation rule. Players who wish to use a Winter Court to generate ASCII hunt content can do so; players who prefer to focus entirely on court politics can ignore the hunt system during the winter session without penalty.

### 57.38.5 Topic Relevance Profile for Hunt Announcements

Hunt Announcement topics, like all Tier 4 topics, carry personal relevance scores shaped by the listener’s personality and relationship to the topic’s subject (Section 16.5 Topic Relevance). The Hunt Announcement profile determines whose conversations are most likely to carry the announcement and whose NPC Decision Engine is most likely to score a REQUEST_HUNT_INVITATION response highly.

**High relevance (topic is likely to surface in conversation, and the listener is likely to consider attending):**

- Listener is within Status ±2.0 of the host — they could plausibly attend.

- Listener has Hunting Skill 2 or higher — the activity itself is familiar and appealing.

- Listener’s school type has a hunting lean: Hiruma Scout, Shinjo Bushi, Usagi Bushi, Matsu Berserker, Hida Bushi, Toritaka Bushi, Kitsune Shugenja, Moto Bushi, Yoritomo Bushi.

- Listener has high Yu (Courage) virtue — hunts are a domain where courage and skill are tested together.

- Listener has positive disposition toward the host (+11 or higher) — they want to spend time with the host.

**Low relevance (topic rarely surfaces; the listener is unlikely to attend even if offered):**

- Listener is outside the Status ±2.0 band — even if the topic reaches them, it is not actionable.

- Listener has Hunting Skill 0 and a courtier or shugenja school lean (Doji, Otomo, Soshi, Asako, Isawa, Miya, Seppun).

- Listener is currently committed elsewhere: ON_CAMPAIGN, UNDER_SIEGE, engaged in a major court session, or scheduled for a duel within the hunt’s timeframe.

These relevance filters produce emergent social patterns: bushi-inclined characters’ hunt announcements circulate widely among their peers and lower-level attendees are drawn readily into a downward invite; courtier-inclined hosts find their announcements fading faster and their guest lists shorter unless they specifically invite people directly by letter. A Hiruma Scout can host a hunt and fill it effortlessly; a Doji courtier would struggle to do the same and most likely would not try.

### 57.38.6 Hunt Resolution — NPC-Only

When the hunt date arrives and no player character is attending, the hunt resolves through background engine rolls. No ASCII map is generated. The simulation is faithful but abstracted.

**Beast generation — terrain pools:** At the hunt date, the system generates a target beast drawn from the pool associated with the target province’s dominant terrain sub-tile. Terrain categories match the canonical list from Section 11.7a (Terrain Modifiers). Plains: wolf, boar, deer, fox, rare wild ox — open terrain that favours the chase and the bow. Forest: bear, boar, deer, wolf, with tiger as a rare result in southern forests (Scorpion and Crane provinces where the climate supports them). Hills: boar, wolf, deer, fox, with rare bears wandering down from mountain country — a transitional pool between Plains and Mountain. Mountain: mountain bear (larger and more dangerous than forest bears), wolf pack, wild goat (countable but not combatively dangerous), rare ozaru at the supernatural tier. Coastal: boar (cliff boar), wild goat, rare large cliff-dwelling predator — a thin pool; coastal provinces with no inland terrain may not support hunts at all. Urban and Shadowlands terrain types do not support hunts and are excluded by ANNOUNCE_HUNT preconditions (Section 57.38.2). A single beast is generated per hunt — the party encounters one target, not many.

**Beast generation — timing:** The beast is rolled at hunt date, not at announcement. The host and invitees do not know what they will face until they are in the field. This reflects the actual framing of a samurai hunting party: the host has not chased down a specific reported animal as a bounty — they have announced a hunt, gathered companions, and gone out looking for whatever game the province holds. Preparation is choosing proper weapons and bringing a capable party. If the party shows up with insufficient skill or wrong weapon mix for the beast that appears, that is a consequence of their choices, not of inadequate information from the system. The Hunt Announcement topic carries location and date but not beast species — invitees evaluate the opportunity based on the host, the province, and the season, not on a specific named quarry. The one exception is when a specific beast has already been generated as a world-state entity (a named creature tied to an active insurgency, threat, or supernatural incursion) in the target province; if the hunt date coincides with such a beast still being present, that entity is used as the target. This case is rare — most hunts generate a fresh beast from the terrain pool at hunt date.

**Beast generation — Crab Wall geography:** The Kaiu Wall is a real barrier. Tainted beasts from the Shadowlands do not wander freely northward into the Crab provinces behind the Wall. Crab provinces south of the Wall use the standard terrain pools above with no tainted-beast modifier — a hunt in Hida lands produces a normal mountain bear or boar exactly as a hunt in Dragon lands would. Tainted-beast risk is a world-state condition, not a terrain property, and applies only under specific circumstances (below).

**Beast generation — tainted-beast risk:** When a specific world-state condition is active on the target province, the generated beast may be a tainted specimen — an ordinary animal warped by Shadowlands influence, sick and aggressive but not a proper supernatural entity. The triggers are: (1) an active Kaiu Wall breach affecting this province; (2) a horde incursion passed through this province within the last 2 IC seasons; or (3) the target province is itself beyond the Wall (Hiruma ancestral lands that the Crab still claim but do not hold). If any trigger is active, a tainted variant of the normally-rolled beast replaces it with 15% probability. Tainted beasts use the normal stat block modified by standard Taint-creature rules (Fear 1, +5 Wounds, may inflict Taint exposure per Section 2.4). Hunting them is cleaner than hunting proper Shadowlands creatures but still carries Taint exposure risk for participants who draw blood. Outside these triggers, the tainted-beast chance is 0% — a normal Crane provincial hunt does not randomly produce a tainted boar.

**Beast generation — supernatural overlay:** Orthogonal to terrain and Taint, a second modifier fires when the target province has supernatural activity: an active spirit realm overlap event or elevated Province Taint Level (PTL) above the designated threshold. If eligible, there is a chance that the generated beast is a realm-touched variant: thematically aligned to the active overlap. Kitsune bakemono or spirit foxes near Chikushudo overlaps; toshigoku-warped bears or wolves near Yomi overlaps; gaki-do shade-beasts near Meido overlaps; spectral or sakkaku-tricked beasts near Sakkaku overlaps. The overlay modifies the selected beast rather than replacing it wholesale — the party tracks what appeared to be a normal bear and finds it wreathed in shadow, or tracks a normal wolf that turns out to cast no shadow at all. Probability: 5% baseline in a province with any qualifying supernatural activity, scaling to 15% at maximum intensity (active overlap plus elevated PTL plus a recent Tier 2 or Tier 3 supernatural incident topic in that province). In provinces with no qualifying supernatural activity, the chance is 0%. The supernatural overlay and the tainted-beast check are independent — both may fire, producing a tainted supernatural variant, though this combination is very rare.

**Beast generation — stat block source:** Beast stat blocks draw from Section 54 (bestiary). Normal animals use their bestiary entry directly. Tainted variants apply the standard Taint-creature modifiers to the base entry. Supernatural variants use their dedicated bestiary entry (ozaru, spirit fox, toshigoku bear). The probability values in this subsection (15% tainted, 5–15% supernatural) are PROVISIONAL pending playtesting — the design intent is “memorable but rare” and values may be adjusted to hit that target.

**Tracking resolution:** Hunt party rolls cooperative Hunting (Tracking) / Perception per Section 4.5.9. TN 15 (fresh trail, forest floor baseline) with the leader’s Hunting Skill Rank added to all participants’ rolls per the cooperative rule (Section 41). The leader is the party member with the highest Hunting Skill; ties are broken by highest Perception. On success: the party finds the beast, proceed to engagement. On failure: the beast is not located, the hunt ends with “beast not found” outcome (see Aftermath below).

**Engagement resolution — Kill Check:** NPC-only hunts do not grind through round-by-round combat. The engagement resolves as a single aggregate Kill Check. The party’s best hunter — the participant with the highest rank in Kyujutsu or Spears, whichever is higher (the proper hunting weapons per Section 57.38.7a) — rolls that weapon skill + Agility. If multiple participants share the top rank, the one designated as hunt leader adds their rank as a cooperative bonus per Section 41. Call this the party_kill_roll. The Beast TN is computed as the beast’s Armor TN plus half its Wound Rank threshold (a rough proxy for how hard it is to land a mortal blow through the creature’s defences and endurance). For a standard bear (Armor TN 20, threshold ~10): Beast TN 25. For an ozaru (Armor TN 30, threshold ~20): Beast TN 40. Outcome: if party_kill_roll ≥ Beast TN, the beast is killed. If it misses by less than 5, the beast is wounded and escapes — recorded as a Failed hunt. If it misses by 5 or more, the beast escapes unharmed — Failed hunt. If the entire party has Kyujutsu 0 AND Spears 0 (no proper hunting weapon skill between them), no party_kill_roll is possible — the hunt auto-resolves as Failed. This is the correct outcome: a party with no hunters cannot hunt, matching the weapon restriction in 57.38.7a.

**Engagement resolution — Casualty Check:** The beast attacks the party regardless of whether the Kill Check succeeded or failed — even a fleeing wounded beast can maul a hunter on the way out. The engine computes a beast_threat_roll from the beast’s Initiative + attack skill (rolled as a standard skill check per the beast’s stat block). The Party Defence TN starts as the arithmetic mean of all participating characters’ Armor TN values, plus a party size modifier of +3 per additional participant beyond the first (capped at +9 for a party of 4 or more). A larger hunting party divides the beast’s attention — the same beast is less likely to land a fatal blow on any one hunter when three others are drawing its focus. A solo hunter receives no party size modifier. Outcome: if beast_threat_roll ≤ Party Defence, no casualties — the hunt is clean. If beast_threat_roll exceeds Party Defence by 1–14, one participant is Hurt (Costly hunt). If it exceeds by 15–29, one participant is Down (Costly to Disastrous). If it exceeds by 30 or more, one participant is killed (Disastrous hunt). Victim selection: weighted random from participants, with weight inversely proportional to their best hunting weapon rank — lower-skilled hunters are more likely to be the one the beast catches. Non-combatants (no Kyujutsu and no Spears, taking the support role per Section 57.38.7a) are excluded from the victim pool; they stay at safe distance. If all party members are non-combatants, no Casualty Check fires — the hunt simply fails without danger.

**Engagement resolution — Kill Attribution:** When the Kill Check succeeds, kill attribution is deterministic: the killer is the participant who made the party_kill_roll (the party’s best hunter with a proper weapon). Most-damage-dealt is assigned to the participant with the second-highest rank in Kyujutsu or Spears (tiebreak: highest Agility). Other participants who had Kyujutsu 1+ or Spears 1+ are recorded as having engaged in combat and receive the participant Glory per 57.38.8. Non-combatant attendees (no hunting weapon skill) receive participant Glory as well per 57.38.7a — they attended and witnessed the kill, which is the social function of being there. All kills in NPC-only resolution are proper kills by construction: NPCs never voluntarily use their daisho (per 57.38.7a NPC Decision Engine behaviour), so the Honor-penalty and Glory-forfeiture branches of the improper-kill rule do not fire in background resolution. The self-defence exception likewise does not fire — the abstract resolution does not simulate individual weapon loss events.

**Outcomes:** One of four outcomes is assigned at hunt end: (1) Success — beast killed, no critical casualties. (2) Failed hunt — beast not found or beast escaped without killing any participants. (3) Costly hunt — beast killed but one or more participants were seriously wounded (Hurt or worse). (4) Disastrous hunt — a participant was killed by the beast, or the beast escaped after inflicting serious casualties. Glory and topic generation follow per Section 57.38.8 and 57.38.10.

### 57.38.6a Solo Hunts

A hunt in which the host is the only attendee — because no one accepted the invitation, or all invitees declined, or every accepted invitee cancelled before the hunt date — is a valid but socially different event. Samurai did historically hunt alone: a Hiruma Scout patrolling the border, a ronin with no companions, a monk in a mountain hermitage. A lone hunter with a bow is not a contradiction. But a hunting party by definition requires a party, and a solo hunt is not mechanically equivalent to a social one. This subsection specifies how solo hunts differ.

**Classification:** When the hunt date arrives and the confirmed attendee list contains only the host, the hunt is flagged hunt_type = “solo”. All standard hunts (any party of two or more) remain hunt_type = “party” and follow the normal rules of Sections 57.38.6, 57.38.7, and 57.38.8. The classification is determined at hunt date, not at announcement — a host who had accepted invitees at one point but lost them all to cancellation still runs a solo hunt on the day.

**Rules that still apply:** The core resolution mechanics operate normally with a single-participant party. The Tracking roll (Section 4.5.9) is made by the host alone with no cooperative bonus. The Kill Check (Section 57.38.6) uses the host as the party’s best hunter by default — their Kyujutsu or Spears skill is the party_kill_roll; no cooperative leader bonus applies because there is no one to lead. The Casualty Check targets the host by default; they are the only possible victim. If a Casualty Check produces a Hurt, Down, or Killed result, that result applies to the host alone. The weapon restriction of Section 57.38.7a still applies — if the host makes the killing blow with their daisho, the improper-kill Honor penalty and Glory forfeiture fire as they would in a party hunt. A proper kill still creates a hunt_trophies entry on the host’s character sheet per Section 57.38.9 — the host killed the beast and has the physical evidence of it, regardless of whether anyone witnessed the kill.

**Rules that change — Glory:** A successful solo hunt produces no Glory award. The host landed a kill with no witnesses, and in Rokugani social terms unwitnessed prowess generates no reputation gain. The +0.3 killer Glory, the +0.2 secondary-damage Glory, the +0.2 host Glory, and the +0.1 participant Glory of Section 57.38.8 all simply do not fire. A failed solo hunt likewise produces no Glory penalty — there was no audience to witness the failure. The one exception: if the killed beast is of the notable variant tier — tainted (per Section 57.38.6 tainted-beast risk), supernatural (per Section 57.38.6 supernatural overlay), or a world-state-named entity — the kill produces +0.3 Glory to the host regardless. The physical evidence of such a kill (the distinctive carcass, the unusual trophy, the host’s return from the wilderness bearing proof) is itself the news, and social reputation accrues from the accomplishment even without direct witnesses. This exception applies only to living hunters: if the solo hunter dies completing a notable kill, no Glory award fires. The character’s death is a standard Death topic and the hunt itself fades with them.

**Rules that change — topic generation:** A routine solo hunt — standard terrain-pool beast, whether successful or not — produces no aftermath topic. The hunt happens in private. The Hunt Announcement topic is retired silently at hunt date as usual. A minor Tier 4 observational topic may fire locally (“[Host] was seen hunting alone at [province]”) reflecting woodcutters and peasants noticing the host’s movement through the wilderness, but this is a low-momentum background topic rather than a proper hunt aftermath topic. A notable-variant kill fires the corresponding notable-beast topic per Section 57.38.10, since the kill is newsworthy regardless of audience. A host death on a solo hunt fires a standard Death topic per Section 16.5 — no Disastrous hunt escalation applies, because there was no hospitality breach. The samurai died pursuing their own business, and that is treated as the death event alone.

**The face-saving cancellation:** A host who announced a hunt and sees that no one has accepted by hunt date minus 1 IC day faces a social choice: go alone and accept the reduced social outcome, or fire CANCEL_HUNT (Section 57.38.4b) and accept the standard −0.2 Glory voluntary-cancellation penalty. Many hosts will choose the cancellation, especially those with high Meiyo (Honor) virtue or with high existing Glory to protect — showing up alone to a hunt one announced socially is a quiet form of loss of face that the host may prefer to avoid by cancelling preemptively. The NPC Decision Engine scores CANCEL_HUNT higher for hosts in this scenario proportionally to their Meiyo rank and current Glory, reflecting the real social calculation. A host with low Meiyo and low Glory has less to protect and is more likely to go anyway. This creates differentiated behaviour: Matsu Berserkers and Hiruma Scouts will more often hunt alone; Otomo courtiers and Doji Magistrates will more often cancel.

**Solo hunts and player involvement:** A player character hosting a hunt that nobody accepts may choose to go anyway. The ASCII mission generation (Section 57.38.7) still fires — the solo classification affects only Glory and topic outcomes, not whether the ASCII map is produced. A player on a solo hunt runs the full Tracking and Engagement bubbles alone. There is no priority_invitee NPC on the map, no support-role non-combatant, no other hunters. Just the player and the wilderness. This is a viable narrative mode — a player-character Hiruma Scout hunting alone on the Crab frontier is a perfectly authentic experience — but the player should understand they are accepting reduced Glory yield as part of the tradeoff.

### 57.38.7 Hunt Resolution — Player Involved

When one or more player characters are attending the hunt, the hunt generates an ASCII mission through the Quest System (Section 56). The mission spans one to three map bubbles depending on beast difficulty and outcomes.

**Bubble 1 — Tracking:** The hunting party enters the wilderness at a starting tile on an ASCII map representing the target province’s hunting ground (forest, hills, or mixed terrain). The beast’s trail begins at a known location within 5–10 tiles of the party’s entry point (the previous day’s kill site, a disturbed area, a sighting). Players make Hunting (Tracking) / Perception rolls per Section 4.5.9 — one roll per 5-tile segment. NPC party members roll in parallel; the player may or may not lead. The tracking bubble ends when the party reaches the beast’s current location or loses the trail entirely (per Section 4.5.9 failure tier 3).

**Bubble 2 — Engagement:** The beast is at close range. ASCII combat begins. The beast acts per its stat block — may charge, circle, flee, or set an ambush depending on beast type and intelligence. Player characters fight with their usual combat options. NPC party members fight at their engine-rolled combat performance, flagged as allies. The bubble ends when the beast is killed, escapes the map, or the party withdraws.

**Optional Bubble 3 — Chase:** If a cunning beast (wolf, tiger, ozaru, spirit beast) escapes the Engagement bubble at reduced health, a Chase bubble may generate: the beast flees through rougher terrain toward denser cover. Players track (Hunting rolls) while pursuing on foot or horseback. If caught, a second engagement fires. If the beast reaches the far edge of the chase map, it escapes and the hunt ends without a kill.

**OOC day cost:** A hunt mission consumes 1 OOC day (4 IC days) for all attending player characters, the same cost as a Mass Battle (Section 25351) or a major siege engagement. All AP for the day is consumed by the hunt mission. If multiple players attend the same hunt, they are placed on the same ASCII map and coordinate directly. This is the standard multiplayer hunt experience.

**Kill attribution in player-involved hunts:** The killing blow is the attack that reduces the beast to Out. The system tracks per-participant damage dealt. Killer gets +0.3 Glory. Most-damage-dealt (if different from the killer) gets +0.2 Glory. Other participants who engaged in combat get +0.1 Glory. NPC party members receive these awards the same way players do — if an NPC lands the killing blow, the NPC gets the +0.3 Glory.

**Non-combatant attendees on the ASCII map:** A character who attends a hunt without Kyujutsu or Spears (the non-combatant support role established in Section 57.38.7a) is still present as a tile entity on the ASCII map — they are socially part of the event and the player can interact with them. At the start of each bubble, non-combatants spawn alongside the party at the entry tile, flagged with the “hunt spectator” role. During the Tracking bubble, they follow the party at 1–2 tiles behind the front-line hunters, moving automatically with the group’s pace; they make no Tracking rolls themselves. During the Engagement bubble, automatic AI places and keeps them at a minimum of 4 tiles from the beast’s current position — if the beast approaches, they flee further. They have no combat actions available in their menu; they cannot attack, and they cannot be targeted by the beast unless they are the only remaining reachable target (all hunters dead or incapacitated). Players may approach them, engage them with Social Menu actions, share Void points with them at Friend-tier or higher disposition, and generally treat them as any other named NPC on the map. They count as present witnesses for Glory distribution per Section 57.38.8 — they receive the “other participants” Glory tier for attending a successful hunt, as 57.38.7a already specifies.

**Non-combatant death on the hunt:** Non-combatants are not invulnerable. They are TECHNICALLY in danger throughout the hunt — a wounded beast breaking through the hunter line and reaching a spectator is a rare but possible outcome, as is a second beast appearing in a supernatural-overlay scenario, or a player deliberately manoeuvring a non-combatant into harm (an action that carries its own consequences). If a non-combatant is killed during a player-involved hunt, the penalty is more severe than the standard Disastrous hunt treatment: the host suffers −0.4 Glory (double the normal −0.2 Disastrous penalty) because a non-combatant was in their safekeeping and they failed to protect them. The aftermath topic escalates to Tier 3 rather than Tier 4 — “[Non-combatant name] was killed by the beast at [Host]’s hunt in [province]” becomes regional news rather than purely local social gossip. The host may face additional Honor consequences through the existing Honor event table if circumstances warrant (e.g., gross negligence, improper invitation of unqualified guests). See Section 57.38.8 for the full consequence table.

### 57.38.7a Proper Hunting Weapons and the Daisho Restriction

Samurai do not hunt with their daisho. The Hunting skill description (Section 24.2, per L5R 4e RAW) states that hunting is never done with a samurai’s katana or wakizashi — it is performed with bow and arrow, or, for particularly large game, with spears. The daisho is a mark of samurai identity and ceremonial status; using it on an animal is improper, and using it during a formal hunt dishonours both the weapon and the hunt itself. This rule shapes how hunt engagements resolve and how Glory attaches to kills.

**Proper hunting weapons:** The two weapon skills whose rolls produce proper hunt kills are Kyujutsu (bow — Yumi, Dai-kyu, Han-kyu) and Spears (yari — Yari, Nage-yari). Any attack roll on the hunted beast using Kyujutsu or Spears is a proper hunt attack. Any attack on the beast using any other Bugei skill — Kenjutsu, Knives, War Fan, Jiujutsu, Heavy Weapons, Polearms, or any other weapon skill — is an improper hunt attack. Polearms (naginata, nagamaki) are excluded despite their spear-like form — the RAW text is explicit about spears, not long-bladed weapons.

**Soft block on improper weapons:** The ASCII mission does not prevent a character from drawing or attacking with an improper weapon. A player whose character draws their katana and swings at the beast is permitted to make the attack — but doing so carries real social and Honor consequences. The character has the autonomy to commit the breach; the system does not shield them from it. This matches how most Honor mechanics operate in the GDD — the choice is available, the cost is real.

**Honor penalty for the killing blow with an improper weapon:** −0.3 Honor to the character who lands the killing blow on the beast using a non-hunting weapon. Calibrated against Section 4.6: a genuine social breach, below the Reneging on a Public Declaration tier (−1.0) but real and visible. The penalty applies to the killer regardless of whether they are the host, a player, or an NPC. If the killing blow is made with Kyujutsu or Spears, no Honor penalty applies.

**Glory and trophy consequences for an improper kill:** An improper kill does not produce the normal hunt Glory distribution. No Glory is awarded to the killer, the most-damage-dealt participant, or the host. No hunt_trophies entry is created. The hunt is recorded as a failed hunt for topic generation purposes (per Section 57.38.10 Aftermath topic variant 2 or 4), not a successful one. In Rokugani social terms, the killer dispatched the animal but did not properly hunt it — the event is not a triumph. Participants who engaged with proper weapons still receive no Glory (the hunt overall is considered improper once the killing blow was dishonourable), but they incur no Honor penalty as long as they themselves did not strike with their daisho. A separate Tier 4 Personal topic fires: “[Killer] slew the beast improperly with their [weapon_type] at [Host]’s hunt in [province].” This is a shame topic attached specifically to the killer, distinct from the standard hunt aftermath topic.

**Self-defence exception:** If a character’s proper hunting weapon is lost, broken, dropped out of reach, or otherwise unavailable — and the beast is actively attacking them at close range — drawing and using the daisho in self-defence is not a breach. The character is fighting for their life, not completing a hunt. The Honor penalty and Glory forfeiture do not apply if all of the following are true at the moment of the killing blow: (1) the character had been using a proper hunting weapon earlier in the engagement; (2) that weapon is no longer usable (broken, out of ammunition, dropped more than 2 tiles away, or the character was disarmed); (3) the beast is in melee range of the character. These conditions are tracked automatically by the ASCII engine — no player declaration is required to claim the exception. If all three conditions hold, the kill counts as proper and normal Glory applies.

**Characters with no hunting skill:** A samurai may attend a hunt without having Kyujutsu or Spears. Courtiers, shugenja, and other non-bushi characters commonly attend hunts as social companions — they are invited for the social event, not for their combat role. Such characters participate in the travel and tracking phases normally but take a non-combatant role in the engagement phase. Specifically: if a character has both Kyujutsu rank 0 and Spears rank 0, the NPC Decision Engine (for NPCs) or the ASCII mission framing (for players) treats them as support rather than hunters. They remain at least 4 tiles from the beast during engagement, observe and cheer rather than attack, and contribute to the hunt’s social outcome without sharing in kill attribution. These characters gain the +0.1 participant Glory on a successful hunt for attending, just as NPC party members do. They do not gain trophy attribution regardless of outcome. A player character with no hunting weapon skill who chooses to close with the beast and attack with an improper weapon is making the same dishonourable choice any other character would — the soft block and Honor penalty apply normally.

**NPC Decision Engine behaviour:** NPCs attending a hunt select their weapon for the engagement phase according to the following priority: (1) highest-ranked Kyujutsu, if the NPC has the bow in their inventory; (2) highest-ranked Spears, if the NPC has a spear-family weapon in their inventory; (3) if neither, the NPC does not engage — they take the support role described above. An NPC never voluntarily draws their daisho against a hunted beast. The engine does not generate improper-kill outcomes for NPCs unless the beast itself closes into melee with an NPC who was using a bow, at which point the self-defence exception logic applies. This prevents the simulation from producing pointless Honor cascades — NPCs do not dishonour themselves on hunts through engine autopilot.

**Weapon availability:** Characters arriving at a hunt are assumed to be equipped for it. An invited character with Kyujutsu rank 1+ brings a yumi; with Spears rank 1+ brings a yari. This is handled automatically at hunt-date arrival — weapon inventory is adjusted to include the appropriate hunting weapon as a standard assumption (characters have access to clan armouries and travel equipment for the hunt). Characters with neither skill do not acquire a hunting weapon and attend in support role. Non-hunting weapons (katana, wakizashi, tanto) are not removed from inventory — a samurai wears their daisho at all times regardless of context. The weapon rule is about which weapon the character uses, not what they carry.

### 57.38.8 Glory and Honor Distribution

All Glory and Honor awards calibrate against the existing Section 4.6 event table. No new values are introduced.

**On a successful hunt (beast killed):**

- Killer (participant who landed the killing blow): +0.3 Glory. Calibrated against Public Performance success (Section 4.6).

- Second-highest damage dealt (if a different participant than the killer): +0.2 Glory. Calibrated against Perform Personally For masterful success.

- Other participating attendees who entered combat: +0.1 Glory each. Calibrated against Publicly Praise Someone (self-side).

- Host (if the host did not land the killing blow): +0.2 Glory. Calibrated against successful Public Performance hosting — the host produced a successful event and receives social credit for it.

- Participants gain +3 mutual disposition with every other participant they did not previously have a dispositon relationship with (they have now shared an adventure); +1 mutual disposition with each existing acquaintance at the hunt.

**On a failed hunt (beast not found or beast escaped without casualties):**

- No Glory change for any participant. Face is preserved — the hunt happened, participants tried, nothing dramatic occurred. The hunt topic fades normally.

- Host: no Glory penalty but no gain either. They hosted a dull event.

- Mutual disposition gains as in a successful hunt — participants have still shared a day’s adventure.

**On a costly hunt (beast killed but serious wounds inflicted):**

- Glory as successful hunt. The kill still counts.

- Wounded participants acquire the Injury topic per Section 16.5 Personal Topics (Injury or Illness) if their wound level is Hurt or worse. This may generate Tier 4 topic traffic at court and affect their availability for other activities until healed.

**On a disastrous hunt (a participant killed, or beast escaped after serious casualties):**

- **No success-side Glory fires:** On a Disastrous outcome, none of the successful-hunt Glory awards apply — not the +0.3 killer, not the +0.2 second-highest-damage, not the +0.2 host bonus, and not the +0.1 participant Glory (including for non-combatant attendees). Only the penalties in this section apply. This is true even in edge cases where the beast was technically killed but a participant also died — the death of the participant overrides the mechanical success of the kill for social accounting purposes. The hunt is remembered as the disaster that occurred on it, not the beast it took down.

- Host: −0.2 Glory. Calibrated against Public Performance critical failure (−0.3) with a slight adjustment downward because the host did not personally fail — circumstances did — but they did organise the event where disaster struck. The penalty is assigned regardless of whether the host was personally at fault; responsibility for the hunt’s outcome attaches to the host in Rokugani culture.

- If a participant died: a Death topic is generated per Section 16.5 Personal Topics. The manner of death (killed by the hunted beast) affects the topic’s framing — honourable death in a dangerous pursuit is respected, but the host’s Glory penalty remains.

- Participants gain no Glory. They share in the disaster.

- **Aggravated case — non-combatant killed:** If the character killed was a non-combatant attendee (a character with no Kyujutsu and no Spears, taking the hunt spectator role per Sections 57.38.7 and 57.38.7a), the host takes −0.4 Glory instead of the standard −0.2 — double the normal Disastrous penalty. The aftermath topic escalates to Tier 3 (“[Non-combatant name] was killed by the beast at [Host]’s hunt in [province]”), becoming regional news rather than local gossip. The rationale: a non-combatant was in the host’s safekeeping and the host failed that responsibility. Combat-participant deaths are tragic but expected risk; non-combatant deaths are a breach of hospitality. If circumstances indicate gross negligence (e.g., the host deliberately exposed the non-combatant, or invited a non-combatant to a hunt against a supernatural beast their party was manifestly too weak to face), additional Honor loss may apply through the Section 4.6 event table at the Game Master’s or simulation arbiter’s discretion.

**Honor:** Hunts do not directly produce Honor gains or losses for routine outcomes. Honor enters only in edge cases: declining a downward invitation (−0.2 Honor for the invitee, per Section 57.38.4) and declining an upward request (−0.2 Honor for the host). These are breaches of samurai hospitality norms and calibrated against similar Honor events in Section 4.6.

### 57.38.9 The hunt_trophies Character Sheet Field

A character who lands the killing blow on a hunted beast gains a new entry in their character sheet’s hunt_trophies array. This is not a Section 49 Artisan item — it is a narrative record with no crafting, no market value, no stats. It is a flag that marks the character as the slayer of a notable beast, available for reference by the Daily Conversation System and topic generators.

**Field definition:** hunt_trophies: Array[Dictionary]. Each entry: beast_type (string — e.g. “great bear”, “tiger”, “ozaru”, “spirit fox”); beast_name (string or null — a recognised name for notable beasts, null for generic specimens); hunt_date_ic_day (int); hunt_location_province_id (int); co_hunters (Array[int] — character_ids of other participants present at the kill, for later topic reference); hunt_id (int — the unique identifier of the originating hunt event).

**Usage:** The Daily Conversation System may reference hunt_trophies when generating topic content — a character with multiple notable trophies is marked as a renowned hunter, and this becomes social context that shapes how other characters talk about them. The field is read-only after the hunt resolves; it is not updated later. Default: empty array. No cap on entries — a lifetime of hunting can accumulate many trophies.

### 57.38.10 Topic Generation

**Announcement topic (created by ANNOUNCE_HUNT):** Tier 4 Social topic. Content: “[Host] is organising a hunt at [province] on [IC day].” Decays on the standard Tier 4 schedule — momentum holds or grows while discussed, fades when ignored. On the hunt date, the announcement topic is removed from all pools whether it was discussed or not (the event has occurred — anticipation is no longer relevant).

**Aftermath topic (generated at hunt end):** A new Tier 4 Social topic is generated based on the outcome. Four variants: (1) Successful hunt — “[Killer] slew a [beast_type] at [Host’s] hunt in [province].” (2) Failed hunt — “[Host] led a hunt at [province] but the beast was not found.” (3) Costly hunt — successful topic plus a linked Injury topic per wounded participant. (4) Disastrous hunt — “[Host]’s hunt at [province] ended in disaster; [participant_name] was [killed by / gravely wounded by] the [beast_type].”

**Escalation:** Disastrous hunt topics involving the death of a named character may escalate to Tier 3 if the victim was of significant Status (Status 3.0+) or politically prominent, or if the host is prominent enough that the event becomes empire-level gossip. A hunt death involving a Clan Champion, for instance, would be Tier 3 or Tier 2 news regardless of context. The standard Section 16.5 escalation rules apply.

**Trophy reference topics:** Notable kills (named beasts, particularly large specimens, supernatural beasts) are preserved in hunt_trophies and occasionally surface as flavour content in future conversations — “This is the character who killed the Bear of Shirei Mori” becomes background context that shapes social interactions years after the hunt. No mechanical effect; purely narrative.
