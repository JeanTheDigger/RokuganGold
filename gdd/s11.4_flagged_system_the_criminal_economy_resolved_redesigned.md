## 11.4 Flagged System — The Criminal Economy (RESOLVED — REDESIGNED)

Source: Emerald Empire 4th Edition, Chapter Seven (Criminals of the Empire), pp. 166–168. Full lore foundation in Section 2.8.10. Bandits, gangs, and pirates are economic actors, not just random events. They interact with the formal tax cascade (Section 4.3.7), trade route income, garrison effectiveness, Province Stability (Section 11.11), and the Commerce System (Section 51). This section documents the criminal economy as a gameplay system with lore-accurate foundations.

### 11.4.1 Bandits

Bandits have plagued the Empire since its foundation. Ronin who can find no other way to support themselves often turn to banditry, supplementing their numbers with embittered runaway peasants. In remote parts of the Empire where samurai are few on the ground, bandit gangs can prosper for years, traveling long distances to raid and extort lightly-defended peasant villages. In more settled regions, bandits favor raids against trade caravans, villages, rural temples, and other sources of lightly-defended wealth. Most bandit gangs are poorly organized and poorly led, controlled by whoever is strong enough to keep the rest in line. Internal dissension and violence are frequent, and battle tactics usually tend to be crude and direct. Most bandits will readily flee from a losing battle — they would not have become bandits if they believed in Bushido, after all. However, if they are unable to escape most bandits will fight bravely and to the death; they know only torture and execution await them if they are captured.

Somewhat more rarely, a bandit gang may acquire a capable and charismatic leader or series of leaders, allowing it to achieve long-term success and become a serious threat to the peace of the Empire. These sorts of bandit gangs usually wind up attracting large-scale military responses from the Empire. The most notorious example of this phenomenon is the long-lived gang called the Forest Killers, who terrorized many generations of Rokugani from their hidden lair in the Spine of the World Mountains. Magistrates do their best to take down these sorts of illegal organizations. Bandits are usually killed on sight, often in military campaigns — those who surrender are executed after a short trial. No punishment other than death is considered suitable for this type of transgression.

**Gameplay Integration:** Bandit activity scales with province conditions. Low Stability + weak garrison = higher bandit spawn chance. Bandits reduce trade route Koku income by raiding caravans. The Ronin Bandit Uprising insurgency type (Section 11.11) models large-scale bandit threats as a five-phase lifecycle with detection, investigation, and suppression mechanics. Bandit NPC archetypes and stat blocks are in Section 54.8. Key design point: bandits are a symptom of poor governance and famine, not a random event — a well-run province should generate no bandit insurgency because there is nothing for banditry to feed on.

### 11.4.2 Urban Gangs

Rokugan does not have yakuza-style organized Empire-wide criminal gangs; however, the beginnings of such organizations may be found in many places around the Empire, most notoriously in the various smuggling cartels and in the fireman gangs of Ryoko Owari. Smuggling cartels focus most of their attention on moving illicit goods (such as opium or gaijin items), but also do a brisk business in covertly transporting the more expensive legal goods (such as jewelry) to avoid tariffs. In contrast with bandits or pirates, smugglers usually try to avoid violence, maintaining a front of respectable activity and relying primarily on bribes and stealth to carry out their crimes. However, they are certainly not above paying a few ronin to kill a troublesome magistrate who is snooping into their affairs.

Many of the strongest cartels are based in Ryoko Owari and are to some degree controlled (or at least supervised) by the Scorpion Clan. During its heyday the Kolat conspiracy also covertly runs many such cartels, using them to monitor and manipulate the Empire’s trade. In the twelfth century, many of the smaller independent smuggling groups are unified into a major new cartel calling itself the Black Lotus, the first true Empire-wide smuggling operation in Rokugan’s history. Whether the Black Lotus is a preview of the future development of organized crime in Rokugan, or merely a shocking aberration, remains to be seen.

**The Fireman Gangs of Ryoko Owari:** In the city of Ryoko Owari Toshi, local gangs found a way of becoming “legitimate” by infiltrating the Fireman Gangs — the district teams assigned the duty of fighting fires. In a Rokugani city where most construction is of wood and paper, fires are a deadly threat, and all city districts organize Fireman Gangs from the local populace. In Ryoko Owari, however, the Fireman Gangs have evolved into independent criminal organizations, using their fire-fighting mission both as a perfect protection racket (“it would be a shame if we didn’t show up when a fire broke out, eh?”) and also as a cloak to protect themselves from legal interference. Many of the gangs are openly criminal, wearing grotesque tattoos to identify themselves and sponsoring various other forms of vice such as opium dens and extortion rackets. So far, such criminal methods have not spread beyond the City of Lies, but in the future, that might change.

**Gameplay Integration:** Criminal gangs do not generate a parallel Koku economy — they siphon from the existing one. Model gang activity as a negative modifier on the tax cascade (Section 4.3.7): a province with active criminal gangs loses a percentage of its legitimate tax and trade income to theft, protection rackets, and tariff evasion. This lost income simply vanishes from the lord’s perspective, manifesting as lower-than-expected revenue. The Scorpion relationship with Ryoko Owari’s gang economy is a clan-specific exception — the Scorpion may tolerate organized crime in exchange for information and political leverage, effectively choosing reduced Stability for increased intelligence. Gang presence is a Province Stability factor (Section 11.11) and gang operations are a Secret System category (Section 12.8).

### 11.4.3 Pirates (Wako)

Pirate gangs, or wako, are a perpetual threat to Rokugan’s rivers and seas. These gangs spring up frequently throughout the Empire’s history, especially during periods of war or political instability, recruiting their ranks from ronin and disaffected peasants. Most wako gangs are short-lived, eventually ending their careers at the hands of magistrates or clan navies if they don’t destroy themselves with in-fighting. The Mantis Clan at one time included many wako within its ranks, but after attaining Great Clan status it expelled its piratical elements — and in the process, created a number of new wako gangs, most notably the infamous Rolling Waves fleet.

Pirates sometimes acquire a romantic reputation among Rokugan’s common folk. Since they prey mostly on merchant shipping, few peasants feel the pain of piracy in the same way they do banditry, and popular legend often portrays pirates as “noble rogues” who steal from the wealthy and help the downtrodden. The most famous example of this view is the legend of Yasuki Fumoki, a Crab pirate from the seventh century who left his clan to wage a private war against the Crane aboard his mighty ship, the Deathless. Supposedly, Fumoki sank over a hundred Crane ships and hid the accumulated wealth on a remote island; it was lost forever when Fumoki died, hurling himself sword-forward into the throat of the King of the Orochi.

**Gameplay Integration:** Pirates reduce naval trade route income specifically. A province with coastal exposure and no naval garrison is vulnerable. Piracy scales with political instability and war — during wartime, wako gangs proliferate as displaced samurai and peasants take to the water. This connects to Section 11.9 (Ship Types and Naval Trade) and the Pirate (Wako) Fleet insurgency type in Section 11.11 and to the naval dimension of the War Status System (Section 53). Pirate NPC archetypes and stat blocks are in Section 54.8. The Mantis Clan’s historical piracy connection creates unique diplomatic tension — accusations of harboring wako are a potential Tier 3 court topic.

### 11.4.4 The Opium Trade

Trade in stolen and contraband goods has always been very profitable despite measures taken by the authorities to suppress it. Recreational opium is especially popular after the Unicorn’s return from the Burning Sands; such narcotics are created in Ryoko Owari Toshi and covertly transported to nearly every major city in the Empire. Similarly, some gaijin goods trickle into the Empire through the Unicorn, Phoenix, and Mantis lands, circulating through the black market to those who seek such exotic delights.

No one organization or person controls all of the Empire’s illegal traffic, and the balance of power constantly shifts as magistrates destroy those groups who catch their notice. Much of the Empire’s opium smuggling is controlled by Scorpion cartels, but there are also countless smaller groups who work through intermediaries (peasants or ronin) who can be easily discarded if they are discovered.

**Gameplay Integration:** Opium and gaijin contraband are specific trade goods in the Commerce System (Section 51). Like other criminal activity, the opium trade does not generate new Koku — it diverts existing economic output away from the formal tax cascade, reducing a lord’s visible revenue while enriching those who control the trade. A province that tolerates the opium trade accepts reduced tax income and Stability in exchange for avoiding the cost of suppression — and risks exposure. If discovered by Emerald Magistrates, it generates an immediate Tier 4 crisis topic (Section 16). The Scorpion Clan’s relationship with the opium economy is a defining clan-specific feature. Gaijin pepper (Section 2.8.9) is the most extreme contraband — possession constitutes treason.

### 11.4.5 Corruption as an Economic Force

Corruption is driven by economic pressure. A samurai’s stipend is usually too little to allow any sort of luxury, and sometimes not even enough to allow basic comforts. Greedier and less scrupulous samurai inevitably turn to ways to cheat and exploit the system around them, embracing vice and corruption in order to gain the wealth they crave. Magistrates and yoriki are especially prone to this, as they control trade permits, tariffs, and travel papers — all of which create opportunities for graft. Major bureaucracies like the Imperial Palace are positively riddled with such corruption.

A samurai stealing from his lord is considered an even more serious crime, and unfortunately is not an unknown one. Officials in charge of things like military logistics or tax collection may well oversee the transportation of thousands of tons of goods in a single year. For a samurai of loose morals, it can be very tempting to simply note that a portion of the delivery was damaged during shipping and then quietly move it to the black market, or even divert it for personal use. Corrupt samurai have been known to sell everything from rice to weapons and armor from their lords on the black market. These samurai may even link up with other miscreants to funnel material across the Empire, becoming part of a smuggling ring.

**Gameplay Integration:** NPC corruption emerges from the existing Personality System (Section 19) — a magistrate or official with low Sincerity and high Greed will naturally tend toward corrupt behavior through the NPC Decision Engine (Section 55), without needing a separate corruption score. A corrupt official reduces effective law enforcement in their jurisdiction and siphons Koku from the tax cascade as a percentage loss on collections in their area. Detection of corruption is a Secret System event (Section 12.8) that generates a Tier 4 court topic (Section 16) when exposed. From the player’s perspective as a lord, the key question is: do you appoint a competent but corruptible magistrate, or a less skilled but honest one? A corrupt magistrate in a vassal’s province is the vassal’s problem to detect and address — or to exploit.
