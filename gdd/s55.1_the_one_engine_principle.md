## 55.1 The One-Engine Principle

This section describes the NPC Decision Engine — the system that governs every decision made by every named character in the game world. Read this first. The technical detail that follows will only make sense once this conceptual foundation is clear.

**The Core Model — Four Layers:**

Every named character operates through the same four-layer decision model every time they have an Action Point to spend:

Objective → ImmediateNeed → Scored Actions → Winning Action

**Layer 1 — Objectives:** Every NPC has two objectives at all times. Their Standing Objective is who they are at a deep level — a lifelong drive that never completes, such as “expand my clan’s territorial influence” or “live by Bushido without compromise.” Their Primary Objective is what they are actively working on right now — a specific completable task, such as “secure an alliance with the Dragon Clan” or “defend Province X against Clan Y.” When the engine fires, it selects which objective is currently in focus: a reactive event takes priority, then an active crisis, then the Primary Objective, then the Standing Objective as fallback.

**Layer 2 — ImmediateNeed:** The winning objective is too abstract to act on directly. A decomposition function translates it into a concrete intermediate goal called an ImmediateNeed. “Secure an alliance with the Dragon Clan” becomes “I need to raise this specific person’s disposition right now.” “Defend Province X” becomes “I need to levy troops.” The ImmediateNeed specifies a NeedType (what kind of goal), a target (who or what), and a priority. It is the bridge between what the NPC wants and what they can actually do today. Without this layer the scoring system has nothing concrete to evaluate against.

**Multi-step chains:** Many objectives require several ImmediateNeeds in sequence before the final action becomes available. An NPC who wants to raid a harvest must first raise an army, then deploy it, then execute the raid. An NPC seeking to restore Honor may need to travel to a temple before they can worship there. The engine handles this naturally — the decomposition function fires fresh every AP, checks what prerequisites are currently met, and produces whichever ImmediateNeed is appropriate at this moment. The NPC does not plan the whole sequence in advance. Each AP is a fresh evaluation of “what is the best next step right now?” This makes the chain interruptible: a crisis that fires mid-sequence forces the engine to re-evaluate from the top of the priority cascade. The NPC adapts rather than blindly continuing a plan that circumstances have made irrelevant.

**Layer 3 — Scored Actions:** Every action available to the NPC in their current context is scored against the ImmediateNeed using seven weighted components: how well the action serves the immediate goal, the NPC’s disposition toward the target, their personality lean, their competence at the relevant skill, the urgency of the situation, their standing objective alignment, and their position on the relevant topic. Before scoring, hard filters remove any actions the NPC would never take regardless of score — a character who embodies Compassion will never raid a harvest no matter how high it might score. What remains is a ranked list of candidate actions, each with a total score.

**Layer 4 — Winning Action:** The highest-scoring action is executed. If two actions tie, a deterministic tiebreaker cascade resolves it. The same inputs always produce the same output. Every component score can be inspected to understand exactly why the NPC made the choice they made.

**One Engine, Every Context:** This four-layer model runs identically whether the NPC is at court, at their holdings, on campaign, under siege, or traveling. The context determines which actions are available in Layer 3 — a character at court can CHARM but cannot ORDER_LEVY. The model itself does not change. There is no separate court brain, no separate war brain, no special cases for any character type. A Scorpion courtier and a Crab general run the same loop. What makes them behave differently is their objectives, their personality filters, their skill ranks, and their topic positions — not different code.

**The One-Engine Principle:** Every named NPC — courtier, bushi, shugenja, monk, ninja, artisan, lord, ronin — runs the same loop every time they have an Action Point to spend. What differs is context (where they are), objective (what they want), personality (what they refuse and prefer), and competence (what they’re good at). There is no separate brain for any character type. Scoring data lives in JSON tables. Only decomposition functions — the if/else trees that convert abstract objectives into concrete ImmediateNeeds — require GDScript logic. The loop itself never changes.
