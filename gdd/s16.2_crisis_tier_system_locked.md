## 16.2 Crisis Tier System — LOCKED

Not all crises are equal in nature. Tier reflects the fundamental nature of the threat — independent of scope and duration. Tier and Scope multiply together to determine momentum gain rate.

**Tier 1 — Existential / Supernatural Threats:**

These transcend clan politics. Even enemies cooperate. Everyone's personal relevance score gets a massive multiplier regardless of geography. Examples: Shadowlands incursion, Maho cult spreading, Oni manifestation, Kolat conspiracy uncovered (Section 54.7i), Imperial succession crisis, Imperial civil war, Clan Champion succession crisis, Empire-wide worship failure (Section 4.3.21).

**Tier 2 Crisis Types — Major Political / Military:**

**War Escalation Ladder — LOCKED:**

Not every conflict begins as a Clan War. Conflicts escalate through stages, each with different momentum gain rates and different authorities who can speak at court.

- Provincial Raid: One lord raids another's province. Small scale, limited to those directly involved. Not yet a Clan War. Only the raiding and raided lords are relevant parties. Tier 3.

- Border Conflict: Raids escalate, more provinces drawn in. Still below Clan War level — the Clan Champions have not formally endorsed the conflict. Tier 3 trending toward Tier 2.

- Clan War: A Clan Champion formally declares war or officially endorses the conflict. The entire clan is now involved. Tier 2. Only the Clan Champion or their designated proxy can speak for the clan at court.

- Escalation is driven by the momentum system — a provincial raid that continues escalating eventually becomes a Clan War. Each stage increases the scope multiplier and momentum gain rate.

**Designated Proxy System — LOCKED:**

Only the relevant party or their designated representative can make binding commitments at court on their behalf. A courtier without explicit authority can advocate, persuade, and negotiate — but cannot commit their lord to anything.

- A provincial daimyo can only speak for himself and his provinces.

- A Clan Champion speaks for the entire clan — or formally designates a courtier to speak in their name with full authority.

- A designated proxy courtier can make binding commitments on their lord's behalf within the scope of their mandate.

- A courtier without formal designation attending a court can only advocate — their words carry no binding weight for their clan.

**Proxy Mandate — LOCKED:**

When a lord dispatches a proxy, they assign a specific mandate before departure. The mandate is not open-ended authority — it is a defined scope. A proxy operates as a fully functional political actor at court on all topics, spending action points, building disposition, influencing other characters. They may only make binding commitments within their mandate.

The mandate has three components:

- Mandate Topic: The specific crisis or decision the proxy has authority over. Example: 'The famine coalition in Region C.' The proxy can commit their lord only on this topic. On all other topics they advocate without authority.

- Decision Authority: Whether the proxy may commit yes or no, or only advocate. A proxy sent to observe and report has no commitment authority. A proxy sent to negotiate has full yes/no authority within the depth limit.

- Depth Limit: The upper bound of what the proxy may commit. Example: 'Up to 20 Rice units.' A commitment of 30 Rice would exceed the mandate.

The game system records the mandate at dispatch. Any commitment made by the proxy is checked against it automatically. A commitment within mandate is binding immediately. A commitment exceeding the depth limit or outside the mandate topic is flagged as out-of-mandate.

**Out-of-Mandate Commitments — LOCKED:**

A proxy may attempt an out-of-mandate commitment. This is difficult — the proxy is publicly committing their lord to something they were not authorized to agree to. The court sees this happen. The lord is then notified and must respond.

If the lord accepts the out-of-mandate commitment: the commitment stands and is binding. The lord absorbs significant Honor loss for lacking control over their own proxy. The proxy absorbs Honor loss for overstepping. Both pay. The political outcome is real.

If the lord refuses the out-of-mandate commitment: the commitment is retracted. The lord absorbs a smaller Honor loss for the public retraction — a word was given in their name and then withdrawn. The proxy absorbs severe Honor loss and a permanent historical disposition modifier — they committed their lord without authority and were publicly disavowed. Their value as a proxy is destroyed.

Either path has costs. The system creates genuine weight behind the act of dispatching a proxy — the lord is trusting someone with their political credibility.

**Proxy Mandate Data Structure (for implementation):**

- mandate_topic: the specific topic ID the proxy has authority over.

- decision_authority: boolean — can they commit yes/no or advocate only.

- depth_limit: maximum resource or commitment value authorized. Null if the decision is binary with no resource component.

- out_of_mandate_flag: set automatically if a commitment exceeds depth_limit or falls outside mandate_topic. Triggers lord notification and response window.

**Clan War:**

Two or more clans are in active military conflict with formal Champion-level endorsement. A court is called — typically by a neutral third party — with the sole objective of ending hostilities. The court has one purpose: getting both warring parties to agree to stop fighting.

- Tier: 2. Momentum gain: +2/tick base × scope multiplier. No personal relevance minimum — proximity and clan affiliation drive relevance entirely.

- End condition: Both warring parties formally declare a truce or peace terms AND military hostilities cease through the army system. Both conditions must be met. A ceasefire without formal terms is fragile — hostilities may resume.

Who can speak at court:

- Only the Clan Champions of the warring clans — or courtiers formally designated as their proxies — can make binding peace commitments on their clan's behalf.

- Third party lords present can use all court actions to pressure, persuade, and incentivize both sides toward peace. They cannot impose peace — only create the conditions where both parties choose it.

Possible outcomes:

- Full peace declared: Both sides agree to formal terms. War ends. Terms are publicly declared and binding under Honor. Momentum resets to 0.

- Truce declared: Temporary cessation of hostilities. Buys time but the underlying dispute remains unresolved. Momentum decreases significantly but does not reset to 0 — the crisis remains active at reduced intensity.

- No agreement: Court closes with no resolution. War continues. Momentum keeps building. If the winning side is accelerating their gains, they have little reason to agree to peace.

Consequences of inaction at maximum momentum (100):

- War spreads — adjacent provinces increasingly drawn into conflict, scope multiplier increases.

- Resource drain on warring clans accelerates through military systems — Rice and Arms deplete.

- Third party provinces suffer raiding and supply disruption even without formal involvement.

- The war does not resolve on its own — it continues until one side is destroyed or both agree to terms.

**Famine:**

A province or group of provinces is experiencing severe food shortage. One of the most mechanically grounded crises — the Rice system already defines exactly how food flows, how populations consume it, and what starvation does to PU. The court's job is to identify the cause of the famine and address it.

- Tier: 2. Momentum gain: +2/tick base × scope multiplier. Personal relevance driven strongly by clan and geographic proximity.

- End condition: The affected provinces return to positive Rice balance — stored Rice meets or exceeds consumption for 10 consecutive daily ticks. Resolved through the resource system.

Possible causes — each requires a different court response:

- Natural shortage (poor harvest, drought): Court response — lords with Rice surplus commit to transferring Rice to the affected provinces. Engages the resource transfer system directly. Crane clan historically dominates here — their agricultural surplus gives them enormous leverage.

- War causing the famine (armies consuming provincial Rice, farmlands destroyed): Court response — stop the war first. A famine caused by an active war cannot be resolved until the war crisis is addressed. Peace must be declared before Rice transfers are meaningful.

- Trade blockage (a clan or lord preventing Rice from reaching the affected provinces): Court response — the blockade must be lifted. The blocking party must commit to allowing free passage. If they refuse, an Imperial Edict at Winter Court can compel them.

Consequences of inaction at maximum momentum (100):

- PU starvation cascade activates as designed in Section 4.3 — Shortage → Hunger → Famine stages. Affected provinces begin losing PU permanently each tick.

- Military forces drawing supply from starving provinces enter deprivation cascade — armies weaken.

- Potential Peasant Revolt insurgency crisis spawns in severely affected provinces.

- The crisis does not resolve on its own without external Rice or removal of its cause.

**Tier 2 — Major Political / Military Crises:**

Serious but contained within the political framework. Personal relevance driven primarily by clan and proximity. No personal relevance minimum. Examples: Clan War, assassination of a major lord, succession dispute, famine affecting multiple provinces, trade route collapse.

**Tier 3 — Local / Regional Problems:**

Locally significant, limited broader relevance. Most lords outside the affected region have low personal relevance scores. Examples: Border raid, provincial famine, criminal organization, trade route disruption, minor clan dispute.
