## 56.12 Ruined Structure Template --- LOCKED

The Ruined Structure template generates a man-made building that has been abandoned and partially collapsed. Unlike every other template, this space was designed by people --- it has architecture. Walls, rooms, floors, doorways. But it is broken. Walls have gaps. Roofs have collapsed. Floors have holes. The structure provides cover and chokepoints like a building, but the decay creates unpredictable openings. High reuse --- applicable to Ronin Bandit Uprising, Maho Cult, Province Taint Manifestation, assassination missions, and spirit realm seeds. Appears in all terrain types.

### 56.12.1 Ruin Origin --- LOCKED

The ruin has a history. The simulation knows why it exists. The generator pulls the origin from the province’s history, which determines the architecture type and condition.

- **War Damage:** A previous clan conflict destroyed a castle, estate, or watchtower. The War Status System (Section 53) and Army Combat System (Section 11.7) produce battles that damage settlements and fortifications. A structure destroyed in a past war and never rebuilt becomes a ruin. Scorch marks, embedded arrows, dried bloodstains.

- **Famine/Depopulation:** A village or estate abandoned when the province’s PU collapsed. The Stability and population systems track this. A settlement that hit zero population is an empty structure slowly decaying. Personal belongings left behind, furniture in place, covered in dust.

- **Taint Corruption:** A building abandoned because the Province Taint Level made it uninhabitable. The PTL system in Section 11.11 forces people out of corrupted areas. The structure remains, spiritually scarred. Discolored walls, dead vegetation growing through the floor. Possible mechanical Taint exposure in certain rooms.

- **Peasant Revolt:** An estate sacked during an uprising. The Insurgency System models this at Strength 10 where the garrison is expelled. Broken furniture, looted rooms, fire damage.

- **Fire or Natural Decay:** A temple struck by lightning, an estate burned in an accident, or simply a structure abandoned long enough that the roof caved in. Generic decay without political or supernatural significance.

### 56.12.2 Size Categories --- LOCKED

Same Strength floor rule as other templates.

- **Small Ruin:** A single collapsed building. One or two intact rooms, rubble filling the rest. A watchtower base, a roadside shrine. Can house up to Strength 3.

- **Medium Ruin:** A compound or larger structure. Multiple rooms still usable, connecting corridors partially intact, some sections completely collapsed. An abandoned estate, a burned temple. Can house up to Strength 6.

- **Large Ruin:** A former fortification or estate complex. Many rooms across multiple connected buildings, courtyards, partially standing walls. Can house up to Strength 9.

### 56.12.3 Entry and Flow --- LOCKED

The ruin has architectural logic --- rooms connected by doorways, corridors, stairs. But decay has broken that logic. Walls have collapsed creating new openings. Doorways are blocked by rubble creating new dead ends. The original floor plan is disrupted.

**Entry Points:** The original entrance still exists --- a front door, a gate, a main archway. The bandits use it. But collapsed walls create gaps the player can enter through instead. The generator takes the original structure and punches holes in it --- each gap is an alternate entry. Small Ruins have one or two gaps. Medium have three or four. Large Ruins have many, making the perimeter porous.

**Interior Flow:** The unique hybrid. It plays like a building --- rooms, walls, doorways, sightline breaks. But it also plays like a partially open space --- collapsed sections create open areas where multiple rooms merged into rubble-strewn ground. The player moves between intact sections (tight, room-to-room, like indoor combat) and collapsed sections (open, exposed, rubble as cover).

**Verticality:** Ruins can have partial upper floors. A staircase to a half-collapsed second story. A watchtower with the top floor missing but the lower levels intact. Elevation within a structure --- something no other template offers. Enemies on a surviving upper floor have ranged advantage over the ground floor through gaps in the ceiling.

**Unpredictable Layout:** The generator starts with an architectural template --- estate, temple, watchtower, waystation --- then applies decay. Randomly collapse walls, block corridors, open ceilings. Every ruin is different because the decay pattern is different even when the base architecture is the same.

### 56.12.4 Population Placement --- LOCKED

The bandits have adapted to the ruin --- they use the intact sections and ignore the collapsed ones. Their placement reflects which parts of the building are still functional.

- **Sentry:** At or near the main entrance. But also possibly in an upper floor position or on a surviving wall section, using the building’s own height for observation. A sentry in a watchtower stump sees further than one on the ground.

- **Room Groups:** The ruin equivalent of Camp Groups. Clustered in the intact rooms --- the ones with walls still standing and some kind of roof. These are the livable spaces. Two to four enemies per occupied room. Rooms have windows, doorways, and wall gaps that create multiple sightlines into and out of each space. A Room Group can be observed through a hole in the wall before the player enters.

- **Rubble Lurkers:** Unique to this template. One or two enemies positioned in the collapsed sections, using rubble as cover. They are not in comfortable living spaces --- they are posted in the broken areas specifically to catch anyone sneaking through the gaps in the walls. Easy to miss because the player assumes collapsed sections are empty.

- **Upper Floor Holders:** If verticality exists. One or two on a surviving upper level. Ranged advantage through ceiling gaps and stairwell control. They see the ground floor layout below them. Taking the stairs up is a chokepoint. The player can also try climbing rubble to reach the upper level from a collapsed section --- slower but avoids the stairwell.

- **Leader Group:** In the most intact room, usually the largest surviving space. The room with the most walls still standing, fewest gaps, best defensive position. Might be an inner room with only one usable doorway --- the collapse has accidentally created a defensible position. Same 20-25% of roster.

**Density Pattern:** Clustered around intact sections. Collapsed areas between occupied rooms are empty or have a single Rubble Lurker. The player moves through dead space between pockets of resistance. Fights are room-to-room with breathing space between them --- unlike Stockade where everything is simultaneous or Ravine where it is sequential.

**Alert Propagation:** Mixed. Sound travels easily through a ruin --- collapsed walls do not muffle much. But the broken layout means enemies can hear a fight without being able to reach it quickly. Rubble blocks direct paths. An enemy two rooms away might hear combat but have to navigate around a collapsed corridor to respond. Alert is fast, response is slow.

### 56.12.5 Environmental Features --- LOCKED

The architecture itself --- broken --- is the primary feature.

- **Intact Walls:** Hard cover, sightline blockers. But they have gaps. A wall might be solid for six tiles and then have a two-tile hole where it collapsed. The player and enemies use walls as cover but neither side can trust them completely. A wall gap that the player did not notice means an enemy can see them when they thought they were hidden.

- **Collapsed Sections:** Open rubble fields where rooms used to be. Difficult terrain --- uneven footing, loose stone, broken timber. Movement is slower and noisier. But these sections are the shortcuts --- they connect parts of the building that the original corridors no longer reach because doorways are blocked.

- **Doorways and Corridors:** The surviving ones function as chokepoints, same as cave corridors. But some are partially blocked by rubble --- passable if the player squeezes through, but slow and loud. The bandits have cleared their own routes through the ruin. The player can follow those cleared paths or push through uncleared rubble.

- **Ceiling Gaps:** Where upper floors partially collapsed. These create vertical sightlines. An enemy on a surviving upper floor can shoot down through a ceiling gap. The player on the ground floor can see movement above through the same gap. Creates a layered combat space where threats come from above as well as the sides.

- **Stairwells:** If upper floors exist. Narrow, steep, one person at a time. The classic chokepoint. But a collapsed stairwell might have rubble piled at the base that can be climbed --- slower, louder, but avoids the defended approach.

- **Rubble Piles:** Scattered throughout. Partial cover, movement obstacles. Some are climbable --- reaching a wall top or an upper floor through a rubble slope. Some are unstable --- stepping on them shifts debris, generating noise, possibly causing a minor collapse that blocks a path or opens a new one.

- **Structural Instability:** Unique to this template. Some sections are on the verge of further collapse. A fight in an unstable room risks bringing down more ceiling or wall. Could trap enemies. Could trap the player. Could open new paths or close existing ones. The generator marks certain rooms or corridors as unstable --- visible through cracked walls, sagging beams, dust falling from above.

- **Darkness:** Varies by section. Rooms with intact roofs and no windows are dark like caves. Rooms with collapsed roofs are open to the sky --- full daylight or moonlight depending on time. The ruin alternates between dark interior spaces and bright open collapsed sections. This creates a rhythm of visibility that no other template has.

- **Origin Residue:** Tied to why the ruin exists. A war-damaged structure has scorch marks, embedded arrows, dried bloodstains. A Taint-corrupted estate has spiritual hazard --- possible Taint exposure in certain rooms, discolored walls, dead vegetation growing through the floor. A famine-abandoned building is empty and desolate --- personal belongings left behind, furniture still in place, covered in dust. This is cosmetic and atmospheric except for Taint-origin ruins, where the spiritual contamination has mechanical effect per the PTL system.

- **Fire Hazard (Enclosed Sections):** Rooms with intact roofs behave like cave chambers when fire is introduced --- smoke fills the enclosed space, reduces visibility to zero, and is lethal to anyone remaining inside. Collapsed sections with open roofs vent smoke upward and do not produce the suffocation hazard. This creates uneven fire behavior across the ruin: fire in an intact room is an evacuation weapon that clears the space but makes it impassable. Fire in a collapsed section burns structures and supplies but the smoke disperses. The player must read the ruin's condition --- which rooms have roofs and which do not --- before using fire tactically. Fire may also trigger structural collapse in unstable sections (see Structural Instability above), compounding the hazard unpredictably.

### 56.12.6 Objective Placement --- Ruined Structure Template --- LOCKED

The architectural layout gives objectives a spatial logic the wilderness templates lack. Things are in rooms, behind doors, inside the building’s own structure.

- **Kill the Leader:** Leader is in the most defensible intact room. The player might not know which room that is from outside. The ruin’s broken layout means the player discovers the leader’s position as they explore. Unless they scout through wall gaps and ceiling holes first --- the ruin’s decay actually helps reconnaissance if the player is patient. Escape depends on how porous the ruin is. A leader in a room with a wall gap behind them can flee through it. A leader in a truly intact inner room is trapped like a Stockade.

- **Recover Stolen Goods:** In a storage room or cellar if one exists. Ruins with basements or ground-level storerooms are natural for this. The goods are in the most structurally sound space because the bandits do not want their loot crushed by a ceiling collapse. This might mean the goods are in the hardest room to reach --- the one with the most intact walls and fewest gaps.

- **Burn the Camp:** Complicated here. The structure is partly stone, partly timber. Stone walls do not burn. Timber beams, roof sections, and furnishings do. Fire in a ruin is unpredictable --- it might trigger structural collapse in unstable sections. Could work for or against the player. A fire that collapses an unstable section might trap enemies or block the player’s exit. More chaotic than any other template. Not recommended if hostages are present or goods need recovering.

- **Rescue Hostages:** Held in an interior room. Possibly a cellar or a room with one blocked doorway serving as an improvised cell. The ruin’s architecture naturally creates confinement --- a room with a collapsed corridor on three sides and one guarded door is a prison without anyone building one. Wall gaps might let the player reach the hostage room without going through the guarded door --- if they find the gap.

- **Investigate:** New objective possibility unique to this template. Tied to ruin origin. A Taint-corrupted estate might contain evidence of what caused the corruption. A war-damaged castle might hold documents from the previous occupants. The investigation objective places specific items in specific rooms --- the player searches the ruin for information rather than fighting through it. Combat may be necessary to reach the room, but the objective is knowledge, not violence. This feeds back into the World Map as intelligence for the lord.

**Secondary Objectives:** The ruin itself is full of discoverable content. Old weapons in an armoury. A hidden room behind a collapsed wall that the bandits never found. Personal effects of the original occupants that have value or narrative significance. The ruin rewards thorough exploration more than any other template because the building had a previous life with its own contents.

**Placement Rule:** Primary objectives are in intact sections, never in collapsed areas. The player navigates through broken space to reach preserved space. Secondary objectives can be anywhere --- including in the rubble itself.

### 56.12.7 Resolution and Consequence --- Ruined Structure Template --- LOCKED

Same spectrum, shaped by the ruin’s mixed nature.

- **Full Success:** Primary objective completed. Strength reduction same as other templates by objective type. The ruin is cleared. Like Hilltop and Ravine, the structure cannot be destroyed --- it is already ruined. But a cleared ruin is a known position. The player knows the layout --- the wall gaps, the intact rooms, the rubble shortcuts, the unstable sections. Second visit is dramatically easier. If structural collapse happened during the fight, the ruin’s layout has physically changed --- some paths closed, others opened. The bandits would be reoccupying a different space than the one they lost.

- **Partial Success:** Player cleared some rooms but did not reach the primary objective. Strength reduced by kills. The room-to-room nature of ruin combat means partial success is common --- the player might clear the outer rooms and retreat before reaching the defended inner section. Each cleared room stays cleared. The bandits redistribute but they have fewer people to fill the same number of rooms. Gaps in their coverage appear.

- **Retreat:** Player pulled out. The ruin offers decent retreat options --- wall gaps the player entered through, rubble paths the bandits do not control. Easier to disengage than Stockade or Hilltop, harder than Ravine. Strength reduced only by kills. The bandits patch the entry points the player used --- block wall gaps with furniture, post Rubble Lurkers at the routes the player took.

- **Failure:** No Strength reduction. Insurgency remains at current Strength. See Section 56.10 for seed-specific failure consequences.

**Template-Specific Consequences:**

- **Layout Knowledge Persists:** The ruin does not change between visits unless structural collapse altered it. The player remembers room positions, wall gaps, rubble paths, unstable sections. Repeat missions are significantly easier. This makes the ruin the most intelligence-rewarding template --- even a failed first attempt teaches the player the entire floor plan.

- **Structural Collapse is Permanent:** If a fight caused a section to collapse, that change persists. A collapsed stairwell means the upper floor is no longer accessible by stairs --- the bandits and the player both lose that route. A wall that fell during combat creates a new gap that exists for all future visits. The ruin degrades further with each mission.

- **Origin Consequences:** If the ruin is Taint-origin, characters who fought inside may need Taint resistance checks per the PTL system. If it is war-origin, recovered documents or artifacts might have political value --- information about the previous conflict that current lords would find useful. The ruin’s history generates secondary value beyond the insurgency itself.

- **Reclamation Possibility:** Unique to this template. If the ruin is cleared and the province lord invests resources, the structure could be rebuilt. A restored watchtower becomes a garrison point. A rebuilt estate becomes a functioning settlement. This is a long-term province improvement that only exists because the quest seed led the player here. The ruin transitions from quest content to strategic asset. Rebuild mechanics deferred.

- **No Civilian Impact:** The ruin is abandoned. No PU loss, no infrastructure damage beyond what already happened.
