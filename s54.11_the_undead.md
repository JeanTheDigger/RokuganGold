## 54.11 The Undead

To the Rokugani death is not the end of existence but merely the next step on the Celestial Wheel. According to Rokugani religious belief, a soul in the kharmic cycle is born, lives, and dies, then is judged by the Fortune of Death and reincarnated into another life or another spirit realm. Those who live honorably purify their kharma and are reborn into a higher station, until they fulfill their ultimate destiny and ascend to Yomi. Those who live sinfully are punished with rebirth in a lower station, or even condemned to Toshigoku or Gaki-do. Worst of all, those who become Lost to the Shadowlands Taint are condemned eternally to Jigoku, where they become the malignant spirits known as Yokai.

For the Rokugani, to deny death is blasphemy. If a samurai cannot die, he cannot purify his kharma and fulfill his destiny. A soul that refuses death is also refusing the judgment of the Heavens on its life and kharma. Thus, any sort of misuse of the dead is a capital offense by Imperial law, and grave robbers are executed on capture. Prior to the rise of the Bloodspeaker Iuchiban in the sixth century, Rokugani funerals involved burial or entombment of the dead. After Iuchiban’s desecration of the Imperial tombs, however, Imperial law decreed that all bodies must be burned to prevent any further such desecration – bodies rendered to ash cannot be raised with unnatural spells.

The Rokugani view the soul as the most important aspect of every individual, for it is the soul which is the repository of kharma and which lives on when the body dies. A body without a soul is a thing of horror. Any attempt to reanimate or control the dead is doubly horrible to the Rokugani, a profound violation of the Celestial Order. Undead themselves are violations of everything right and proper in the world – bodies living without souls or souls without bodies – and the Rokugani regard them with both disgust and terror. Many samurai who are fearless in the face of an unwinnable battle are rendered pale and trembling by the sight of an undead abomination.

The greatest concentration of undead presence in Rokugan is of course in the Crab provinces, where the Taint constantly threatens to bring the dead back to life. The Shinomen Forest is also heavily associated with ghost stories and haunting. In the late twelfth century, the so-called Spider Clan – an organization of Lost masquerading as a Great Clan – took possession of the southern Shinomen. The Spider routinely murdered anyone who came close to their hidden lair, and the victims were frequently raised as undead by the clan’s maho-tsukai. Individual maho-tsukai have also raised undead to serve them throughout Imperial history, dating back to the infamous second-century shugenja Kuni Nakanu, whose forbidden researches into the nature of the undead would eventually form the basis for the dark magic which has plagued the Empire ever since.

### The Nature of the Undead

The most basic threat offered by the undead is a physical one. With the exception of certain kinds of ghosts, all undead are aggressive, seeking to attack and kill any living creatures they meet. Further, the undead do not face the physical limits of mortals – the power of an animating kansen, a Tainted spirit from Jigoku, provides a zombie all the strength and resilience it requires, and torn muscles or ruptured lungs are no more of an inconvenience than rotting flesh. Most undead are physically stronger and more resilient than their former living selves. The driving force behind most undead is hunger – this is most easily seen in the gaki, whose very nature is driven by eternal hunger for something from the living.

Beyond their direct physical threat most undead also behave like a plague or disease, spreading the Taint or transforming their victims into copies of themselves. Maho-tsukai are fond of using undead to attack remote villages with few defenders, allowing them to quickly overwhelm the defenders and swell their numbers with more undead, spreading like a cancer across the land. Jigoku’s twisted power sometimes leaves pieces of the mind intact within an animated corpse, so that the kansen controlling it can make the rotting body express fragments of its old personality and memories – there have even been reports of undead samurai demonstrating mastery of techniques they were renowned for in life.

OPTIONAL RULE – UNDEAD HUNGER: GMs who wish to represent the undead’s unnatural hunger mechanically can introduce the Hunger special ability (Rank 1–3, focused on a particular target: flesh, blood, certain emotions, etc.). When a Hunger is sated, the undead creature receives a bonus of +Xk0 to all Attack and Skill rolls (where X is the Rank of Hunger) for the next three Rounds. The duration can be re-set by further satisfaction of the Hunger.

TYPES OF UNDEAD: By far the most common undead are corpses reanimated by the Taint – this occurs spontaneously whenever a corpse becomes strongly Tainted. In game terms, anyone with at least 1 full Rank of Taint will have the possibility of returning from the dead if the GM so wills, and anyone with 3 or more Ranks will do so with certainty. In the Shadowlands, even a pure un-Tainted corpse will reanimate within 10 to 20 minutes. Re-animated corpses will most frequently become some form of zombie or revenant, but those brought back by the intrinsic Taint of the Shadowlands may also become hyakuhei, a more aggressive and semi-intelligent form of undead. Independent self-willed undead such as pennaggolans and harionago appear to originate in the Shadowlands – perhaps created by Jigoku from yokai, the souls of Taint-corrupted humans. Souls which return to the world as gaki or slaughter spirits are much more rare.

### Physical Undead

GHUL. In the lands known as the Burning Sands, beyond Rokugan’s western borders, the Senpet kingdom has been known to create undead through its religious rituals, and a band of cultists known as the Jackals have expanded these activities into darker and more fearsome methods. Their most plentiful creation are Ghuls – a fierce and aggressive form of undead, much like zombies but more cunning and sturdier. Distinguished from normal zombies by their emaciated but intact bodies, bestial teeth, and rapid movement, ghuls are capable of forming hunting packs on their own, employing primitive but effective tactics to hunt the human prey they crave. Ghuls are not Tainted, for they are created by gaijin magic that does not deal with the powers of Jigoku. In the latter half of the twelfth century, the Spider Clan begins taking control of these migratory ghuls and studying the techniques to create new ones. Although they possess an intrinsic cunning, ghuls have little real volition of their own and can easily be brought under the control of a powerful leader. They also occasionally produce a so-called ghul lord – a more powerful, at least partially self-aware ghul capable of human speech and sometimes maho.

HARIONAGO. Harionago are created from women whose violent death left a legacy of wrath and vengeance so intense that it attracts a yokai to possess and re-animate the corpse. They dwell on less-traveled roadways, far from civilized areas, where they can hunt lone travelers – usually male, chosen because they remind the animating spirit of the original murderer. When a Harionago is hunting it appears as a quite beautiful woman, well-kept and fetching, often playing the role of a simple frightened maiden. Her most notable feature is her hair, which is long, lustrous, and always adorned with small metal ornaments which on closer inspection seem to be hooks – these are actually part of the “hair” itself. An attentive traveler who notices the hooks may possibly be able to remember tales of the Harionago with a suitable Lore Skill roll (suggested TN 20). If a victim does respond and engage in conversation, the Harionago will be driven to try to elicit a specific emotional behavior or response generally tied to the events of her death – often laughter. If the desired emotional response occurs, the Harionago attacks. The barbed hair comes alive and tries to grapple and tear apart the victim. If she slays her prey, a Harionago will devour him to the bone. She does not like fighting multiple opponents and will usually try to flee engagements with groups.

HYAKUHEI. Hyakuhei are created from samurai who die in the Shadowlands, attracting unusually powerful kansen to bring their bodies back to unlife. Unlike most undead animated by the Shadowlands, Hyakuhei retain some of the intelligence they had in life along with a fierce animalistic cunning. Their hatred of the living is far more focused and deliberate than the mindless aggression of ordinary zombies. Their empty sockets glow with an eerie green light that hints at their true intelligence. Hyakuhei fight much like any living warrior in terms of tactics and ability – they are capable of forethought and planning, can wait for a more strategic moment to strike, and rarely attack alone, preferring to fight in packs employing group tactics. They will often travel in the company of normal zombies, impersonating these lesser creatures to keep their foes off guard. Fighting a hyakuhei is much like fighting a living samurai – but one who is immune to pain and doesn’t tire.

KEKKAI. If the blood from a death is not properly cleansed with purification rituals, it may attract kansen and on rare occasions result in the creation of a kekkai. Kekkai appear as tiny humans, a foot or less in height, blood red in color, with a sticky and slimy texture – clearly composed of blood, a clotted sticky mass in miniature human form, leaving trails of clotting blood behind them wherever they go. Kekkai do not appear to be capable of causing serious harm, but they inspire considerable terror in those who see them. They possess a childish malignance that delights in bringing fear to households or entire villages, and prolonged infestations will spread their Taint into the area. Shugenja can destroy them with purification rituals. If they are destroyed, they collapse back into a puddle of blood that quickly dries.

NUKEKUBI. These creatures appear to be weaker, more primitive forms of pennaggolan – found primarily in the Shadowlands and in lands known for hauntings and evil spirits, such as the southern Shinomen Forest. In form they appear to be human, but at night their heads detach from their bodies and float away in search of prey. When detached, the heads sprout sharp fangs and their eyes glow with malignant green-blue light. Unlike pennaggolan, they do not drink blood – rather, they feed on the pain and death-terror of their victims. They are normally quite difficult to kill, but their detached bodies are quite vulnerable while the heads are away hunting. Nukekubi are not particularly intelligent and their skills at deception are limited. They prefer to keep moving, visiting different communities as travelers and moving on before their depredations draw too much attention.

NUPPEPPO. Truly unspeakable creatures – animated mounds of flesh stripped from bone. They are not properly corpses like zombies, but are instead amorphous piles of rotting, stinking skin and tissue. They arise most frequently from the Taint-infused remains of those who have been killed in the most brutal manner, such as those torn apart by rampaging oni or hungry zombies. Nuppeppo are not particularly fast or durable, and their chief threat is the sheer horror they inspire. Many victims are so paralyzed by fear and revulsion that they are unable to resist effectively. If a nuppeppo is killed, its Tainted flesh will reanimate again within three days unless it is burned.

PENNAGGOLAN. Among the most clever and terrifying of undead, pennaggolan are vampiric creatures who appear outwardly human by light of day – they are both intelligent and skilled, able to maintain a long-term false identity and to masquerade as either a samurai or a commoner. Even worse, while they are in their human body their Taint cannot normally be detected. A pennaggolan’s true nature becomes apparent at night, when its head detaches from the body and rises into the air, dragging along with it the body’s various entrails (stomach, heart, intestines, lungs, and other viscera). This grotesque monstrosity flies through the air seeking lone victims to feed upon, seizing and strangling the victim with its entrails, then feeding on its blood. Pennaggolans have the ability to control and ration their hunger, and do not have to feed every night – this allows them to remain within the Empire and evade discovery for extended periods. Their one real flaw: when the pennaggolan is out hunting and feeding, its entrails swell and bloat, making it extremely difficult to squeeze back into its body afterward. Vinegar acts as a counteragent to this problem, and thus a pennaggolan is often accompanied by a lingering odor of vinegar. When pennaggolans choose to create more of their own kind, they drain the blood from a living victim rather than strangling it; three days later, the corpse rises as a new pennaggolan with the memories and skills of its former life, but now animated by a malignant spirit from Jigoku.

PLAGUE ZOMBIE. These entities are very similar to ordinary zombies in nature and appearance, but are active carriers of the disease which killed them. The disease is made even more virulent by the influence of the Taint, and those wounded or killed by plague zombies are extremely likely to become plague zombies themselves. Plague zombies usually travel in packs, often in the company of “normal” zombies and revenants.

### Undead Spirits

In contrast to the physical undead, these entities are spawned from souls condemned to certain of the spirit realms in punishment for their deeds in life. Creatures from these realms sometimes escape back into the mortal realm, plaguing the living with their malignant and destructive natures. The chief distinguishing feature of all of these beings is their nature as spirits, which makes them far more difficult to drive off or destroy than a physical undead creature.

All gaki share the following qualities: Immortality – Gaki cannot actually be killed, and if their physical form is destroyed, they reform within Gaki-do. Spirit. Superior Invulnerability – Gaki are immune to all illusion spells and to all spells, skills, and techniques which affect the mind or thoughts.

GAKI, GAKIMUSHI. The most malignant of gaki known to mortals. Gakimushi are heavily Tainted entities which seem to draw as much on the power of Jigoku as on their native Gaki-do. They feed on pain, and their preferred behavior is to capture mortals and torture them for days, weeks, or even months, feeding on their delicious agony. They resemble a giant humanoid insect with a long tail mounting a venomous stinger, and try to enter the mortal realm whenever possible, making lairs in remote places and dragging mortal captives there to feed upon.

GAKI, KWAKU-SHIN GAKI. These bizarre gaki are created from the souls of those who allow others to perish of cold rather than share the warmth they possess. They are quite rare, with typically only one or two created in a given generation. They take the form of pot-bellied men, often elderly, with no legs and a black iron cauldron for a stomach. The cauldron is searing hot, often glowing brightly, and causes exquisite pain to the gaki itself. It is driven relentlessly to find and feed upon mortals in order to quench the fire within its belly. Kwaku-shin gaki pursue their prey by hopping, never speak or otherwise communicate, and seem to exist only to pursue and consume their victims.

GAKI, SHIKKO-GAKI. Shikko-gaki are created from the souls of those who defiled graves and other resting places of the honorable dead. Their taste for vile food makes them easily Tainted. Physically, a Shikko-gaki is a foul creature resembling a hunched, ape-like beast with gray skin, long talons, and insect-like segmented eyes. Its lower torso fades off into vapor with no visible legs or feet, but nevertheless it moves in a hopping, arching motion that carries it rapidly toward its goal. Shikko-gaki actually retain their human intelligence, unlike many gaki, but are incapable of speech because their mouths are completely packed with irregular rows of jagged teeth. Since they crave dead flesh, they do not normally attack the living on their own, but if attacked they will fight back relentlessly.

GAKI, SKULL TIDE. Over centuries of sea-faring, countless sailors have betrayed their captains and crews in pursuit of riches. These murderers and thieves, obsessed with fattening their own coffers, are often condemned to be reborn into the Skull Tide – a giant corrupted gaki that dwells in the Tainted waters near the shore of the Shadowlands. The Skull Tide is a literal sea of skulls, a collective entity that attacks ships and devours sailors. Those who are killed become new skulls in the Tide. The Skull Tide is one of the most notorious hazards of the waters near the Shadowlands – the clacking sound of its hundreds of skeletal jaws snapping together is a source of terror to all Rokugani sailors. The Tide is restricted to the sea and seldom leaves the waters near the Shadowlands.

SLAUGHTER SPIRITS (Toshigokujin). When those whose souls are consumed with rage and violence die in the senseless frenzy of battle, they are condemned to Toshigoku, the realm of slaughter. Slaughter Spirits live in constant war, fighting bloody, senseless battles for no purpose. They repeatedly die at each others’ hands, only to rise again the next dawn to embrace the bloodshed anew. Rarely, slaughter spirits may find a way to pass out of Toshigoku and into another domain such as Ningen-do – they can pass through existing spirit gateways, or a violent battle may spontaneously open a temporary rift. Within the mortal world, Toshigokujin appear as they did in life, but with a face twisted by rage and a body which exudes a faint eerie glow. They attack everyone they see without mercy or quarter, until they are killed or the spirit gateway closes off. A slaughter spirit that is physically killed will return to Toshigoku.

### Notable Undead Villains

Kitsune Gohei, lord of the Minor Clan of the Fox, reigned during the late eleventh and early twelfth centuries. Never a happy man, he had a lifelong fear and horror at old age and death, and dreamed of a way to escape it. He also inherited his father’s outrage at the Empire’s treatment of the Minor Clans. When his father was killed for challenging the Crane sensei Kakita Toshimoko, and his sister threw her life away fighting the same implacable foe, his fear and anger became overwhelming. Corrupted by dreams and fears, Gohei began to seek forbidden knowledge. He learned of the lost Ancestral Sword of the Fox and of the Black Scroll hidden within it, and raised and trained his daughter Kitsune Ryosei to undertake the quest for the missing Ancestral Sword – never realizing she was meant to be the final sacrifice who would grant her father immortality. Although she was ultimately saved by a selfless Kitsune spirit, it did not change the fate of her father. Kitsune Gohei was transformed into a terrible self-willed undead monster, a Lost soul trapped in an undead body – a rare creature among the rarest. He exists only to corrupt, kill, and destroy, and remembers his daughter only with a perverse desire to bring her into the same foul existence.

When the ghuls of the Burning Sands began to appear in Rokugan in the late twelfth century, a handful of notorious Ghul Lords began to appear as well. Among the most formidable was a female creature appearing on the fringes of the largest and most aggressive hordes of ghuls, who seemed to have mastered the ways of magic, employing both Rokugani spells and maho. Some who have heard of this creature believe she is the undead form of a Jackal cultist named Fatina who worked in alliance with the Lost a few years before – her mastery of the Chuda Shugenja School made her a notable figure among the gaijin who came to Rokugan. Fatina is silent but powerful, with a commanding presence. She generally lets her ghuls do her fighting, staying far back to command their assault, but is not afraid to enter combat if need be. The ghuls under her command seem to be more intelligent than average, capable of more complex strategy than normal creatures of their type.

Yogo Junzo was the daimyo of the Scorpion Clan’s Yogo family at the start of the twelfth century. An elderly man of formidable magical skills, he was a close friend to Bayushi Shoju and assisted him in the planning and execution of the notorious Clan Coup. The defeat and death of Shoju shattered him – Junzo, like many Yogo, carried the family curse to betray what he loved, and he loved the Scorpion Clan. When Shoju died and the Scorpion were disbanded, Junzo sought vengeance by opening the Black Scrolls and unleashing their massive power on the Empire. The sheer power of the magic which Junzo unleashed killed his body, but such was the magical power he now commanded that his soul remained within his body. Like Kitsune Gohei, Junzo became a self-willed undead – a walking rotted corpse housing a Lost soul. Brilliant and mad, he gathered an army of undead and Shadowlands monsters to ravage the Empire. His depredations continued until the Day of Thunder, when the soldiers of the Unicorn Clan finally ran him down and destroyed his physical body.
