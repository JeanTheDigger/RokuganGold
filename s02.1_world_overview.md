## 2.1 World Overview

Rokugan is the Emerald Empire — a feudal civilization governed by divine mandate, rigid social hierarchy, and the code of Bushido. Founded over a thousand years ago when the children of the Sun and Moon fell from the Celestial Heavens, Rokugan’s society is built on the principle that every living soul has a place in the Celestial Order, and that place is not to be questioned.

### 2.1.1 The Celestial Order

The Celestial Order is the natural stratification of existence that governs all life in Rokugan. It is not a law imposed by mortal rulers — it is the fundamental structure of reality, inherited from the Celestial Heavens themselves. Every soul has a place: the Emperor at the apex, the samurai caste beneath him, the common people beneath them, and at the very bottom, those whose occupations are considered spiritually unclean. This hierarchy has remained stable for over a thousand years. Those who defy it — who forsake their station, who presume to rise above their place, or who reject the order entirely — are regarded as blasphemers and heretics.

Outside the Order entirely stand the gaijin — foreigners from distant lands who worship false gods and follow alien traditions. They are not truly considered people within the divine scheme. Beneath even the gaijin in Rokugani cosmology are the forces of the Shadowlands, whose very existence is an affront to the Celestial Heavens.

### 2.1.2 The Mandate of Heaven

The Kami — the divine children of Lady Sun and Lord Moon — fell to the mortal realm after Hantei’s epic battle with his father, during which eight of his nine siblings were freed from Lord Moon’s gullet. Falling cost the Kami their immortality but not their divine nature. They walked as gods among mortals, and it was only natural that they rose to control the region now known as Rokugan. The Kami constructed a society modeled on the order that existed in the Celestial Heavens — the Celestial Order.

### 2.1.3 The Emperor

The Emperor has been known as the Son of Heaven since the first Hantei took the throne. This is not merely a euphemism — the Heavens directly endorse Rokugan’s ruling dynasty. The Hantei Dynasty ruled for centuries until the Toturi Dynasty replaced it after the fall of the last Hantei, and the Iweko Dynasty followed after that. Each dynasty that has taken power has been seen as blessed and divinely selected. In recognition of this divine burden, each Emperor gives up his personal name upon ascending the throne, becoming simply the Hantei, the Toturi, or the Iweko.

The Emperor holds dominion over all land and all people. He grants portions of his land to those who swear fealty — most notably the Clan Champions, who divide it among their families and followers. In exchange, his subjects oversee, harvest, collect, and return taxes to the Imperial City. The Emperor’s bureaucracy serves as the clearinghouse for an enormous quantity of food, and in theory each link of the chain keeps sufficient supply to feed the local population. In practice, there have been eras when ruthless Emperors ensured this did not happen.

The Emperor is also the head of Rokugan’s state religion, which merged the two traditions of Shintao and Fortunism under the second Emperor, Hantei Genji. The Brotherhood of Shinsei, the principal religious organization, is theoretically headed by the Emperor, though he seldom exercises direct authority over it. The only other religious officials in Rokugan are shugenja, who are members of the samurai caste and therefore serve the Emperor as all samurai do.

The Emperor is sometimes treated as a semi-divine being, though few within the Empire are truly naïve enough to believe he is infallible. The assumption that the Emperor cannot make mistakes has been eroded over time, particularly by the actions of the infamous Hantei XVI (the Steel Chrysanthemum) and the troubled end of the Hantei Dynasty. Despite this, the allegiance of the samurai caste to the Emperor has remained largely unshaken, and the consequences for disloyalty have generally been dire.

### 2.1.4 The Kuge — Servants of the Emperor

The kuge are the true nobility among the samurai caste. Many trace direct descent from the Kami or serve the Emperor through an extremely limited degree of separation. The Emperor himself is technically a member of the kuge, though his position is elevated far above all others by divine blessing. Most kuge achieve their social status through heredity, though a handful enter through promotion to key appointed positions — the Emerald Champion, the Chancellor, the Jade Champion, the Voice of the Emperor, and the Imperial Advisor all automatically confer kuge membership, though it extends only to the office-holder and their immediate family while they hold the position.

The three Imperial Families — the Miya, the Otomo, and the Seppun — hold membership within the kuge, as do all who bear the Emperor’s dynasty’s family name. Below the Imperial families, the Clan Champions are perhaps the Emperor’s most significant servants. Their status extends to their immediate and extended families. The position of Clan Champion is generally hereditary, passed to the oldest eligible child upon death or retirement. Some clans consider these positions strictly patriarchal or matriarchal; for most, gender is not a major consideration. When direct heredity fails, some clans — notably the Lion — extend their search for a legitimate heir and appoint based on merit rather than bloodline.

The final ranks of the kuge are filled by the daimyo of the Great Clans’ non-ruling families and by the ruling daimyo of Minor Clans. These individuals are devoted servants of the Emperor, and their status is in part recognition for their service. Kuge status for Minor Clan daimyo extends only to their immediate family and is largely a gesture of respect rather than an acknowledgment of equal standing with Great Clan Champions.

### 2.1.5 Vassal Families

The system of fealty in Rokugan has created complex social organizations commonly referred to as families. The term “family” is something of a misnomer — few individuals bearing a family’s name are actually related to its founder. Many are not even connected through blood or marriage. Instead, prominent bloodlines are surrounded by numerous smaller but distinct lineages that together make up an entire family. Most samurai are buke — either their direct ancestors swore an oath to loyally serve a direct descendant of the family’s founder, and for their service they were honored with the right to bear the founder’s name and pass it to their children.

All these individuals are called vassals, though on occasion one distinguishes himself through heroic or noble service, and in recognition his lord will grant him a vassal name, setting him apart from other buke. This distinction rarely lasts longer than a few generations, after which the vassal families gradually fade into the ranks of the buke. But the vassal family bears its name proudly all the same. Vassal families are a peculiarity within the buke ranks — they have their own family name but still swear fealty to the kuge. Whether they have more status than other buke is debatable; in practical terms, the daimyo of a vassal family commands more respect than the average member of a greater family.

### 2.1.6 Local Daimyo

The most prominent and prestigious position to which most buke can aspire is a posting as a provincial or city governor. Such individuals are the only buke-class samurai permitted to use the term “daimyo” in reference to themselves, and they can choose and appoint their own ji-samurai assistants and vassals. In many ways they are quite powerful within their own domain, but their power is also extremely limited outside the territory they rule. The position of a local daimyo is difficult and precarious — they have attained the highest rank possible for one of the buke, but they can never rise higher. In order to retain their current position and pass it to their children, they must serve their clan and family without fail, driven to extremes of anxiety by even minor setbacks within their domain.

### 2.1.7 The Buke — Servants of the Kuge

The vast majority of Rokugan’s samurai belong to the buke class. Buke are not directly descended from the founders of whatever family they serve, but instead have sworn fealty to the family — or more likely are descended from those who swore fealty to a direct member of the ruling line. This is by no means an indication of short-term service; many family branches within the Great Clan families can trace their ancestry back to men and women who swore fealty to the Kami and their subordinates at the dawn of the Empire.

There are many striations of power and influence within the buke. First among equals are the daimyo of families that are not kuge — the various vassal families and the subordinate families of Minor Clans with more than one family name. Below them are the highest ranking members of the buke who have risen to some position of status among their peers, usually through merit or political maneuvering: provincial and city governors, military officers, hatamoto (personal vassals of major daimyo and Clan Champions), karo (advisors to a daimyo), and magistrates. The higher ranks also include younger children of kuge families who may be too poor to confer an inheritance on all of their children. All samurai of this rank are referred to as ji-samurai.

### 2.1.8 Ronin

Ronin are samurai by birth who lack a lord and a clan. They are a unique subset of the ji-samurai — samurai by their birth, a fact that cannot be ignored even by their greatest detractors. A samurai’s role in the Celestial Order is to serve, and without a lord a samurai has no place in society. For that reason, many consider ronin to be an anomalous element, the result of weakness and sin throughout history. The irony is that because the Celestial Order teaches samurai cannot stoop to menial labor, ronin are often forced into positions where their only options are banditry or mercenary service — both considered dishonorable. Some ronin who find a virtuous path and ignore the slander can even acquire a positive reputation; benevolent otokodate (ronin bands) can win the hearts of the common folk through persistent acts of honor and courage.

Ronin sometimes settle in their own communities, though it is more common for them to disperse throughout the Empire. The largest known ronin settlement is the village of Nanashi Mura (literally “no-name village”) on the outskirts of Dragon Clan territory, founded by a ronin named Fuyari — an exile from the Unicorn Clan. After much wandering, the Dragon agreed to let him build and maintain a village under their protection so long as he maintained law and order and paid taxes in a timely manner.

### 2.1.9 Budoka and Ashigaru

Budoka and ashigaru are members of the bonge (commoner) class who have been elevated above others of their rank because of their service to the samurai caste. Budoka are warriors taken from the ranks of the peasants and selected for individualized training by their samurai lords. Generally, a budoka is chosen and trained by one particular samurai. The practice is most common among the Crab and Mantis Clans — the Crab are willing to train almost any able-bodied warrior who can assist in fighting the Shadowlands, and the Mantis with their low samurai population are happy to add to their military ranks through budoka training. Magistrates in all clans also train budoka to assist in upholding law in remote villages; these peasants are sometimes referred to as doshin.

Ashigaru are the rank and file soldiers that fill the bulk of any army. A typical Great Clan field army is no more than half samurai, and for some clans the percentage may be much smaller. Ashigaru are peasant conscripts who have received only the most basic weapons training and formation drill. They are typically trained in the use of yari (spears), though some may be armed with bows. Ashigaru units are typically led by a budoka or by one of their own selected as having a talent for leadership — these men are called ashigarugashira. Despite their training and service, budoka and ashigaru are most assuredly not samurai. They are still considered property of their lord and can be discarded or killed without reservation.

### 2.1.10 The Clergy

The clergy is a vital component of Rokugani society, comprised primarily of monks belonging to the Brotherhood of Shinsei. The Brotherhood is a union of many different monastic orders, all devoted first and foremost to the study of the Tao of Shinsei. Some sects place the worship of the Fortunes first in their devotions, with the Tao serving as a supplement. Monks are generally not considered members of the samurai caste and are strangely absent from the Celestial Order in most representations. They are afforded tremendous respect by both peasants and samurai, making them a unique bridge between the upper and lower levels of Rokugani society. Monasteries dot the countryside, and it is rare to find a village of any size that does not have a monastery within a day’s travel.

The clergy is not limited solely to monks. Shugenja are priests of the kami, and as such they perform a wide variety of rituals and blessings the peasants consider essential to their ongoing survival — blessing crops, marriages, births, and deaths, as well as countless other events both mundane and significant. Because of their samurai status, most shugenja remain aloof from peasants and conduct their rituals purely as part of their duty to their lord and to the kami.

### 2.1.11 The Bonge — The Common People

The bonge class, also known as the heimin or “half-people,” comprises the vast majority of Rokugan’s non-samurai population — indeed the vast majority of its population of all kinds. The bonge include farmers, merchants, and craftsmen, as well as various other commoners such as servants, fishermen, and urban laborers. The life of a heimin is generally a difficult one, made up largely of labor in service to a samurai lord.

Foremost among the bonge are the farmers, who are considered the most important and worthy of heimin because their work feeds the entire Empire. After farmers, artisans and craftsmen are most valued — art is considered a noble endeavor, and heimin artisans of particular skill are often funded by a samurai patron. Below these heimin are the merchants, a necessary evil at best. Commerce is considered distasteful by samurai, and merchants are regarded with hostility even by their fellow heimin. In point of fact, successful merchants can easily become far wealthier than most samurai — rich enough to rival a provincial governor or even a family daimyo. They must be cautious about displaying their wealth lest they arouse the ire of the samurai class.

### 2.1.12 Social Mobility in Rokugan

Although Rokugan has a strict caste system, it is not completely unchanging. Samurai born into the buke can aspire to higher ranks if they prove themselves worthy or develop political contacts and networks of alliances. A tiny number may even vault from the bonge to the samurai class — during war, an ashigaru or budoka who achieves great deeds on the battlefield can, on rare occasions, win an opportunity to join the ranks of the samurai. Peasants can also scavenge battlefield weapons and armor, equip themselves with a daisho, and claim to be a ronin. This gambit can work so long as the peasant avoids anyone who might recognize him from his old life. On at least one occasion, this gave rise to an entire Minor Clan — the Clan War hero known as Toku began his life as a naïve peasant boy who picked up a pair of swords and was eventually permitted to found the Monkey Clan.

### 2.1.13 The Hinin — Non-People

The hinin, or “non-people,” are without question the lowest of all people in Rokugan. Unlike the heimin or the bonge, hinin are seen as little more than objects — not people at all. Most belong to the group known as eta, who work as morticians, leatherworkers, and garbage collectors. These tasks are considered so unclean they hopelessly contaminate the kharma of those who perform them. The work of the eta is not generally considered important by samurai, and thus most eta can be killed without hesitation for the slightest cause. Eta are not even permitted to speak to samurai unless expressly commanded to do so.

A unique subset of the hinin class that has come to work closely with samurai over the centuries are the torturers. Torture is considered a legitimate interrogation practice in Rokugan, but samurai magistrates would never stoop to touching the flesh of criminals themselves — instead, they have skilled hinin torturers on hand. Some magistrates also maintain eta assistants to help investigate crime scenes. This is particularly common among the Kitsuki family of the Dragon, who prize the knowledge that can be gleaned from close examination of a dead body.

### 2.1.14 The Gaijin

Gaijin have no place in the Celestial Order. They are foreigners and barbarians with no understanding of how all life sprang from the Lady Sun and Lord Moon at the dawn of time. Many Rokugani believe gaijin are not even created of divine blood, but instead sprang from some other source. The gaijin’s complete absence from and ignorance of the Celestial Order renders them less than human in Rokugani eyes. It is not impossible for gaijin to find their place in the Celestial Order by embracing its religion, but they can never be truly accepted without some manner of divine endorsement. The most notable exception are the Moto, who arrived in the company of Lady Shinjo herself — since not even an Emperor can overrule the word of a Kami, the Empire had no choice but to accommodate their presence within the Unicorn Clan.

### 2.1.15 The Shadowlands

While the hinin are beneath the Celestial Order and the gaijin outside it, the denizens and adherents of the Shadowlands are directly opposed to it. Jigoku is the antithesis of Tengoku, and the oni and those who ally with them are the nemeses of Rokugan. It is the duty and obligation of every Rokugani, regardless of position in the Celestial Order, to oppose the forces of the Shadowlands at every possible opportunity. Death at the hands of the Shadowlands is not considered a disgrace, for to die in such a manner means that one stood against the darkness. Collusion with the Shadowlands, however, is the most severe and unforgivable form of blasphemy imaginable. The souls of those who commit such crimes are usually severely punished, and if they have succumbed to the Taint their fate is inevitably condemnation to eternal torment within Jigoku.

### 2.1.16 Positions of Power

In the Empire of Rokugan, all political power theoretically rests exclusively with the Emperor. There are no exceptions to this rule — it is literally the mandate of the Celestial Heavens made manifest in the mortal world. However, it is the right (and frequently the desire) of the Emperor to parcel his authority out to chosen vassals in a manner that alleviates the burden of power without lessening his authority. This is how some of the most powerful and influential individuals in the Empire receive their mandates.

### 2.1.17 The Emperor’s Chosen

The Emperor’s Chosen are quite possibly the most powerful group of individuals in the Empire outside the Emperor himself and perhaps the Great Clan Champions. These are individuals personally selected by the Emperor to fulfill very specific roles in his immediate administration. These positions carry an incredible amount of social clout and political power, though for the most part they lack any significant military power save for the Shogun (when one exists). The Chosen are able to stand on equal ground with the Great Clan Champions, though most generally choose to continue observing their oaths of loyalty to their own Champion.

**The Imperial Advisor:** The Advisor holds influence that even other members of the Chosen can only envy. Typically the first member of the Chosen to be selected when a new Emperor takes the throne, the Advisor’s purpose is to offer personal political advice regarding the goings-on in the Imperial Court and the various other courts, especially those of the individual Clan Champions. The Advisor must remain fully informed on all major events transpiring throughout the Empire’s political environment, quickly establishing a network of allies throughout the various clan holdings who compile regular reports on everything of interest. Despite its higher profile, the office has less resources at its immediate disposal than others of the Chosen — there are no military forces or law enforcement agencies answerable to the Advisor. In truth, the Advisor makes his own resources from the assets of his allies, both inside the court and beyond.

**The Imperial Chancellor:** The Chancellor’s duty is to arrange and oversee the events of the Emperor’s Imperial Court. In theory a simple duty — the Chancellor merely establishes the agenda for the day when the Emperor is in attendance or oversees the court’s events in the Emperor’s absence. In practice, given the sheer number of attendees at the average Imperial Court session and their influence throughout the Empire, the Chancellor’s role is extremely taxing. The positions of Advisor and Chancellor are designed by the Imperial families to be just similar enough that the two frequently become rivals, preventing either from interfering with their “pure” service to the Emperor. When the Emperor is absent and the throne stands vacant, the Chancellor’s power within the Imperial Court is essentially without limit — and the only individuals who can pose a legitimate threat to that power are those who hold other high positions within the Imperial power structure, such as the Otomo family daimyo or the Amethyst Champion. The Chancellor is one of the few people in the whole Empire with essentially unrestricted access to the Emperor’s person.

**The Imperial Herald:** Unique among the Chosen, the Imperial Herald is an inherited position, typically held by the daimyo of the Miya family itself. The Herald serves as the personal herald of the Emperor and is responsible for overseeing the many lesser heralds who serve him — traditionally all drawn from the ranks of the Miya family. The Herald also oversees the adjudication of Miya’s Blessing, the charitable tradition whereby the Miya family sets aside a portion of their wealth (at least fifteen percent) each year to hire the finest craftsmen and procure vast quantities of food and supplies, then dispatch this aid to the three regions they judge to be most urgently in need of help. The Miya family never discusses this work outside of their annual planning sessions — they see it solely as a matter of duty and honor.

**The Imperial Treasurer:** The newest among the Emperor’s Chosen, the position of Imperial Treasurer was created at the beginning of the Iweko Dynasty. Previously, tax assessment and collection were the domain of the Emerald Champion, but the Iweko Dynasty chose to separate those duties. The Treasurer oversees the collection of taxes each year throughout the Empire — an incredibly complex task, since almost every province in Rokugan has a different means of calculating how much tax should be collected. Once the Imperial taxes are returned to the capital, the Treasurer is responsible for storing, inventorying, and distributing them to other Imperial agencies and to the clans as necessary. Due to the close association of the Treasurer with the concept of commerce, the position is held in slightly lower esteem than the other members of the Chosen. However, the Treasurer has complete control over the issuance of Imperial edicts concerning taxation, so no one dares to openly question his authority overmuch.

**The Voice of the Emperor:** Similar in some respects to the Imperial Herald, the Voice is a position that elevates a single individual to the privilege of personally speaking for the Emperor in matters of grave importance. The difference is that the Herald merely reports edicts written for the masses, while the Voice delivers personal, specific information. The Voice essentially transcends the need for wealth — found most frequently in the company of the Emperor’s person, the Voice has absolutely no need for money or material goods. The position has not been maintained consistently across the Empire’s long history; some Emperors have had no need of anyone to speak for them, while others have refused to speak directly to any of their subjects, embracing the notion that the voice of the divinely-mandated Emperor is too pure to be heard by mortals.

**The Shogun of the Empire:** The position of Shogun was first theorized in the legendary writings of the ronin tactician Sun Tao, who posited there would be times when the Emperor would be unable to defend his Empire and a separate military leader would be required. Many found this supposition blasphemous, as it implied the Emperor could not protect his own realm. It was not until many centuries later that the first Shogun was created by a canny Emperor of the Toturi Dynasty. The Shogun is unique among the Chosen in that the position has virtually no ties to the Imperial Court whatsoever — the Shogun rarely if ever makes an appearance at court, being most frequently deployed around the Empire or to its borders. The Shogun’s authority takes the form of an autonomous military organization called the Shogunate, with complete discretion in the composition and organization of this force, including the ability to hand-select officers from any clan. The Shogun can also draft forces from the Great Clans, though Champions may contest this. The Shogun has significant influence over the Imperial Legions — a check on the power of the Emerald Champion and vice versa, as both positions hold authority over the Legions and individual legion commanders have a great deal of leeway in determining which authority figure they more strongly identify as their commander.

**GAME START NOTE:** The position of Shogun is not filled at game start. It exists as a concept and a potential position that may be created during play if circumstances warrant it — most likely during a major military crisis or when the Emperor determines the Empire’s military needs exceed what the Emerald Champion can manage alongside his other duties. The creation of the Shogunate during play would be a significant political event generating a Tier 1 crisis topic.

### 2.1.18 The Jeweled Champions

The Jeweled Champions are the greatest example of social mobility and meritocracy in Rokugan’s otherwise rigid social structure. The Jeweled Championships are a series of tournaments that provide opportunities for all who earn the right to compete. Most contestants are from higher-ranking members of the kuge, but every clan holds internal competitions to determine who may participate, and it is the dream of every member of the buke to win a place in such a contest. Of the Jeweled Champions, only the Emerald and Jade are truly on the same level as the Emperor’s Chosen. The others, while commanding significant if specific influence, are still somewhat limited in the scope of their power.

**The Emerald Champion:** The most prestigious and powerful individual in the Empire outside of the Emperor himself. The Emerald Champion is the personal champion of the Emperor, responsible for defending his honor. The duties associated with the position have been formally codified as the enforcer of Imperial law. The Champion commands an extensive organization of Emerald Magistrates (sometimes called Imperial Magistrates) who are authorized to act in enforcement of Imperial law regardless of clan boundaries. For much of Rokugan’s history the Emerald Champion also handled tax assessment and collection. The Emerald Champion’s military power varies by era — prior to the Toturi Dynasty, the Imperial Legions generally answered only to the Emerald Champion, giving him considerable military clout. The subsequent emergence of the Shogun created a system where both positions hold authority over the Legions, with individual legion commanders choosing which authority they identify with more strongly.

**The Jade Champion:** In theory a magical counterpart to the martially-based Emerald Champion, the Jade Champion is in practice a position with a complex and conflicted history. The Championship is filled by one of the most powerful and knowledgeable shugenja in the Empire, selected by an extensive tournament. The Jade Champion is specifically tasked with enforcing all Imperial laws pertaining to the use of magic in any form. The Champion commands the Jade Magistrates and has a single military force, the Jade Legion, exclusively at his command. The most significant items of office are the Jade Seal (a badge that grants the bearer status as a Jade Magistrate or Jade Legionnaire) and the Mantle of the Jade Champion (an artifact commissioned by the Emperor and crafted by the Phoenix Clan’s best artificers, said to considerably amplify a shugenja’s natural ability to speak with the kami).

**⚙️ CROSS-REF (Law ****&**** Order):** The Jade Magistrates are documented in Section 11.3.4. They have the same authority as Emerald Magistrates but are limited to magical crimes, religious blasphemy, and Shadowlands influence. The three clan-level anti-maho organizations (Asako Inquisitors, Kuni Witch-Hunters, Scorpion Kuroiban) operate independently of the Jade Magistrates but with overlapping jurisdiction — see Section 11.3.5.

**The Amethyst Champion:** The Amethyst Championship has the most varied and unique history of any of the Jeweled Championships. The position has become associated with the duty of a courtier — the third major aspect of a samurai’s duty to clan and Empire (besides those of the bushi and shugenja). The position is selected by ranking members of the Imperial families based on qualities valued during the Championship’s previous incarnation, including knowledge of the blade and the gift of speaking, particularly oratory and poetry, as well as a generally honorable nature. The Amethyst Champion is considered to be welcome in every court in the entire Empire, regardless of location, and part of the duties require the Champion to make several trips each year to spread the Emperor’s attention to distant courts.

**The Turquoise Champion:** An individual intended to embody the most sophisticated and cultured aspects of the samurai caste — in short, the most elevated and gifted artisan among the samurai of Rokugan. The Champion is selected by means of an extensive competition between artisans of all types, judged by a panel from the Imperial families. The winner gains the position and, in most cases, determines by default the particular discipline of artistry that will be in vogue for the coming year.

**The Topaz Champion:** Far and away the least influential of the Jeweled Champions, and the one with the shortest duration — the Topaz Championship is an annual event held in Tsuma in the Crane lands, where the Great Clans send a handful of their finest young samurai to compete in the most prestigious gempukku ceremony in the Empire. The winner is declared the Topaz Champion for a period of one year and is typically offered a number of prestigious positions. The most frequent appointment is as an Emerald Magistrate, and a very small and very elite subset of Emerald Magistrates known as Topaz Magistrates consists exclusively of former Topaz Champions.

**The Ruby Champion:** An office that exalts the honorable and prestigious position of the sensei. The Ruby Champion is the master sensei of both the Emerald Magistrates and the Imperial Legions, organizations over which the Emerald Champion (and to a lesser extent the Shogun) holds dominion. The Champion is responsible for personally selecting the training regime and the teachers who will train thousands of bushi serving the Empire. The Ruby Champion has roughly a dozen extremely well-appointed dojo throughout the Imperial holdings, the largest of which adjoins the Emerald Champion’s personal estate near Otosan Uchi.

### 2.1.19 The Imperial Families

Unlike the Minor Clans, the Imperial families are not viewed as a mere adjunct to the Great Clans. While their numbers, lands, and holdings are considerably less than even the smallest of the Great Clans, in many respects they are much more important than any of those clans. As their name implies, the Imperial families have a direct connection to the ruler of the Empire. Three of the Imperial families that appear over the Empire’s history — the Hantei, the Toturi, and the Iweko — are truly “Imperial” in that the Emperors and Empresses who have reigned over Rokugan have come from their ranks. The other three families considered to be Imperial — the Seppun, the Otomo, and the Miya — stand close to the Throne by virtue of a combination of blood, duty, and history. All of them wield enormous political and cultural power, far out of proportion to their relatively small numbers and limited physical footprint on the landscape of Rokugan. The Great Clans all vie for the attention and favor of these small but powerful families.

**The Hantei Family:** For over one thousand years the Hantei were the ruling family of Rokugan. They trace direct lineage from the original Hantei, the Kami who fell from Heaven at the dawn of the Empire. After landing in the mortal realm, the Kami held a tournament to determine which of them would rule, and Hantei emerged victorious, becoming the first Emperor. The consolidation of the Empire continued through the First Hantei and his son, Hantei Genji (the Shining Prince), who established many of the basic institutions that would come to define the Empire — the Imperial Court, the office of the Emerald Champion, the unification of Shinseism and Fortunism into the single religion of Shintao, and the legal principle that all lands belonged to the Emperor. Subsequent Hantei rulers, known as tenno (Heavenly Sovereign), reigned for the next eleven centuries. A notable period of weakness occurred during the Gozoku Alliance (late fourth and early fifth centuries), when ambitious Great Clan nobles from the Phoenix, Crane, and Scorpion effectively seized control over the Throne. The Hantei line ended with Hantei XXXIX (Hantei Sotorii), who became the vessel for Fu Leng himself during the Clan Wars. Fu Leng’s defeat on the Second Day of Thunder also resulted in Hantei XXXIX’s death, ending the dynasty.

**Hantei Succession Conventions:** An important issue among the Hantei was what to do when there were too many potential heirs. Younger brothers and sisters of the heir created an inherent problem, since Imperial custom allowed only the current ruler (and spouse) to use the Hantei name. The solution was the Ritual of Abdication: younger siblings of the heir would formally renounce their claim to the Throne and to the Hantei name, then join the Otomo family (or occasionally the Seppun), undergoing the appropriate gempukku ceremony regardless of their age. An Imperial spouse whose child ascended to the Throne would also take the Otomo name to eliminate confusion. When an Emperor died without a clear heir, Imperial scholars would trace the family’s blood lineage to the nearest living candidate for ascension. These conventions are mechanically relevant to the succession system (Section 22.5) — the Emperor’s succession follows these rules rather than the standard lordship succession order.

**The Seppun Family:** The Seppun are the largest of the Imperial families and the closest of the three non-dynastic families to the Emperor. They trace their origins to a woman called Seppun, the very first mortal to greet the Kami after their fall from Heaven at Seppun Hill. Seppun offered herself to the Kami as their loyal servant, and Hantei accepted her service on behalf of his siblings. For above all other things, the Seppun are known throughout the Empire as the Emperor’s protectors. The Seppun miharu (guardsmen) are the personal bodyguards of the Emperor and his family. Every miharu is utterly devoted to the welfare of the Emperor and will not hesitate to place himself between his charge and danger. Only once in recorded history have Seppun Guardsmen betrayed this loyalty — when Hantei XVI (the Steel Chrysanthemum) ordered the death of his own mother, some miharu chose to participate in the act of regicide, after which all involved committed seppuku. The Seppun also maintain the Hidden Guard, an institution that protects the Emperor against supernatural threats. The shugenja of the Hidden Guard are devoted priests of the kami who are prepared as the miharu to place themselves between the Emperor and harm, particularly adept with wards and other defensive forms of magic.

**The Otomo Family:** The Otomo are among the oldest families in the Empire, second only to the Seppun in age. Otomo was the younger son of a great tribal warlord, fostered as a boy to Seppun’s tribe. He became a skilled diplomat and later used his talents to save his tribe after it was nearly destroyed by his older brother’s warmongering. Otomo lobbied Hantei Genji to allow Miya to found his own family, and was forever indebted to Otomo for this. The Otomo’s primary role since the fall of the Gozoku conspiracy has been to serve as the serpents in the garden of the courts — ensuring the Great Clans can never unite against the Emperor. They developed and refined a courtier approach called seiyaku (“covenant”), an ironic title given that its techniques are specifically designed to sow discord. The Otomo keep the Great Clans perpetually off-balance, focused entirely on one another as rivals and enemies. They do not hesitate to use underhanded tactics such as deception, bribery, blackmail, and conventional courtly methods like flattery and trading favors. Their family motto, attributed to Otomo himself, is: “In difficulty lies opportunity.” The Otomo also serve as the repository for the Hantei bloodline through the Ritual of Abdication, and they fill many key positions in the Imperial bureaucracy.

**The Miya Family:** The Miya family is the youngest of the three non-dynastic Imperial families. Miya was a loyal follower of Otomo, a young man barely past his gempukku, quick-witted and charming, who was chosen by the Emperor to spread the news of victory over Fu Leng to all those living in the still largely wild Empire. Miya’s journey revealed to him the desperate state of the common people, and he soon began not just delivering news but helping rebuild — restoring buildings, digging new wells, tilling soil, and helping in every possible way. This practice became known as the Emperor’s Blessing (later Miya’s Blessing). The Miya have always been the smallest Imperial family with the least direct influence on the Throne, but they are the most popular and beloved throughout the Empire. They carry the Emperor’s edicts and pronouncements to his citizens, and since they are readily welcomed by all the clans, the Miya serve as guileless, dispassionate eyes and ears of the Imperial families, noting and recording events affecting the Empire without bias and reporting them back to the Emperor. The Miya are also known as peacemakers, using their authority and prestige as representatives of the Imperial Throne to negotiate peace in inter-clan conflicts. The position of Imperial Herald is traditionally held by the Miya daimyo (Section 2.1.17).

### 2.1.20 The Imperial Bureaucracy

The Imperial Bureaucracy is outwardly simple in its organization but vastly complex in detail. As rigid as the hardest steel, it nonetheless changes constantly, adapting to the whims of its masters. Commoners fear it, samurai curse it, and even the powerful find it troublesome. Without it, though, Rokugan would cease to function as an organized state. At its core, the Bureaucracy is an enabler — it does not issue edicts, collect taxes, deploy troops, or police the Empire directly, but it makes all those things possible. The Bureaucracy is organized into a series of Ministries, each designed around a particular function of governance.

**The Ministry of Exchange:** Responsible for planning and overseeing the collection of taxes and their subsequent disbursement for mandated uses. The Ministry records annual Imperial taxes and revenues (though actual collection through most of history has been a function of the Emerald Magistrates) and their subsequent distribution. Originally taxes consisted mainly of Rice, with a form of equalization to balance poor harvests in some regions with better ones elsewhere. With the appointment of the Imperial Treasurer as one of the Emperor’s Chosen, the Ministry became known as the Imperial Treasury and began using its own operatives to collect taxes instead of relying on the Emerald Magistrates.

**The Ministry of Service:** Small in scope, focused narrowly on the Empire’s human resources. Conducts the periodic Imperial census and maintains the records collected from it. Occasionally, after a major war, famine, disease, or other catastrophe has depopulated parts of the Empire, the Ministry oversees the relocation of farmers, laborers, and artisans to the affected areas.

**The Ministry of Territories:** Responsible for overseeing the lands and holdings of the Empire — conducting and maintaining records of surveys and cartographic works, enumerating real estate holdings, and seeing to the design, development, and maintenance of infrastructure such as roads and bridges. Particularly enmeshed in the affairs of the clans due to its complex responsibilities and frequent border adjustments resulting from conflicts or political agreements. This Ministry, as well as the Ministry of Justice, falls under the political oversight of the Emerald Champion.

**The Ministry of Justice:** An enabler providing administrative and bureaucratic support to the Imperial and clan magistrates who maintain law and justice in the Empire. Ministry bureaucrats maintain legal records, act as the functionaries of the courts, and oversee the details of punishments. The Ministry of Justice falls under the official jurisdiction of the Imperial Chancellor, but the Emerald Champion also holds considerable sway in its policies and operations.

**The Ministry of Martial Levies:** Largely an enabler responsible for providing administrative support to the Imperial Legions (and more indirectly to the clans). Records of troop levies and armaments are maintained by this Ministry, which also arranges matters such as mustering and discharging levies, maintaining supplies, and movement and housing troops throughout the Empire. For most of history this Ministry fell under the Emerald Champion’s oversight; after the creation of the Shogun, it became answerable to that individual.

**The Ministry of Proclamations and Protocols:** Not a “ministry” in the same sense as the others, but rather a small cadre of senior officials responsible for overseeing the entire Imperial Bureaucracy. Contains the Department of Heraldry (authorizing and maintaining records of all heraldic symbols), the Department of Edicts (ensuring effective promulgation of Imperial decrees), and the Department of Oversight (whose magistrate-like powers are focused on policing the rest of the Imperial Bureaucracy itself).

**The Imperial Archives:** The repository for the endless multitude of legal documents, proclamations, edicts, missives, and records produced by the entire Imperial Bureaucracy. The Archives span many locations throughout the Empire and are filled to the rafters with carefully stored documents. The Archives fall under the jurisdiction of the Imperial Chancellor.

**DESIGN NOTE:** The Imperial Bureaucracy and its Ministries are lore context for the existing mechanical systems. The Ministry of Exchange maps to the tax cascade (Section 4.3.7) and the Imperial Treasurer’s role. The Ministry of Service maps to the World Population System (Section 52). The Ministry of Territories maps to the province and settlement systems (Sections 4.3.13, 11.11). The Ministry of Justice maps to the magistrate hierarchy (Section 11.3). The Ministry of Martial Levies maps to the military unit system (Section 4.3.19) and the levy mechanics. The Bureaucracy itself is not a separate mechanical system — it is the institutional backdrop that justifies why the resource cascade, law enforcement, and military administration systems function the way they do.

### 2.1.21 What Is Discussed at Court

There is little of real significance discussed at the provincial court level. However, in those courts set in major cities, or in provinces that have significant trade routes passing through, matters of commerce and trading can take up considerable time and effort. Regional courts in trade cities such as Nikesake, Ryoko Owari, or Samui Kaze Toshi often become the focus of attention for merchant patrons from numerous clans and families. At the Imperial Court and Clan Champion courts, the full weight of Rokugan’s political machinery is brought to bear — alliance negotiation, war declarations, marriage arrangements, resource disputes, and matters of Imperial law are all addressed through the formal court process defined in Section 15.

### 2.1.22 Social Structure — Mechanical Cross-References

The social structure described above is mechanically represented across multiple GDD systems. The Status Ranks table (Section 4.6) maps every position in the Celestial Order to a numeric Status value, with kuge at 7+, buke at 0.5–6 (ji-samurai through provincial governors), elevated bonge at 0.1–0.4 (ashigaru, doshin, budoka, craftsmen, merchants), and hinin at negative values. The tax cascade (Section 4.3.7) and Koku flow (Section 4.3.9) model the Emperor’s claim on all land and production through the feudal chain. The Population Unit system (Section 4.3.13) represents bonge as civilian PU, with ashigaru levied from them and samurai (buke) existing as separate permanent military units (Section 4.3.19). The succession system (Section 22.5) models how lordship positions pass through the kuge hierarchy, with confirmation authority matching the Celestial Order’s chain of command. Ronin mechanics appear in Section 4.3.11 (mercenary recruitment) and Section 11.11 (Ronin Bandit Uprising insurgency type). The clergy is represented through the Brotherhood of Shinsei monk schools (Section 29.13) and the Fortune system (Section 2.5). The Shadowlands threat is mechanically represented through the Taint system (Section 42), Maho (Section 43), and the Province Taint Level system (Section 11.11).
