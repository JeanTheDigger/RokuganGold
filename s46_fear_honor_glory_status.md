# 46. Fear, Honor, Glory & Status

**FEAR:**

Any time a character is confronted with something that might make his courage falter, the GM can make him roll to resist a Fear effect. Fear effects are given a Rank from 1 to 10. To resist a Fear effect, a character rolls Raw Willpower (TN = 5 + (5 × Fear Rank)). All characters may add their Honor Rank to the total of this roll.

- A character who fails to resist a Fear effect suffers a penalty to all die rolls equal to -Xk0, where X is the Fear Rank.

- This penalty lasts until the end of the encounter, unless the source of the Fear is removed.

- Catastrophic failure: If a character fails a roll to resist Fear by 15 or more, he is overwhelmed and either flees or cowers helplessly (GM’s determination).

**HONOR:**

Honor reflects how an individual samurai rates his personal ability to adhere to the tenets of Bushido and fulfill his duties. Honor is primarily internal, but also has an external component — a samurai’s Honor greatly impacts how he carries himself and how others perceive him. Honor and Glory are composed of 10 Points per Rank; accumulating 10 Points advances the Rank by 1.

**Honor Ranks**

**⚙️ CROSS-REF (Law ****&**** Order):** Crimes have defined Honor, Glory, and Status consequences by severity. Minor crimes (dishonorable conduct): small Glory loss, reprimand. Serious crimes (corruption, violence): major Honor and Glory loss, seppuku or execution. Capital crimes (treason): execution without seppuku, punishment extends to family, Status zeroed. Crime severity tiers and their consequences are documented in Section 57.47 (Crime Severity Classification and Consequence Mapping). At-act Honor loss uses Table 2.3 (this section), scaled by Honor Rank. At-conviction Glory, Infamy, Status, Topic, and Sentence are flat values in Section 57.47. The ritsuryo principle (Section 11.3) makes Honor Rank the primary factor in legal proceedings — a character’s testimony weight equals their Honor Rank multiplied by 5.

- Ranks 0–1 — Honorless Dog: Does not adhere to or recognize Bushido. Cannot be trusted. Values only self-interest.

- Ranks 2–3 — Untrustworthy: Not particularly honorable, but makes a marginal effort to uphold principles under certain circumstances.

- Ranks 4–5 — What Is Expected: The standard against which most samurai are measured. Makes an effort every day to adhere to Bushido and bring honor to family and clan. Human and flawed, but not spiraling into shame.

- Ranks 6–7 — Exceptional: Understands the inherent strength of the code. Capable of resisting temptations through conviction and strength of will. May occasionally abandon virtue for practicality, but only rarely.

- Ranks 8–9 — A Soul Above Question: Has put aside personal feelings and concerns, replacing them with zeal for duty and Bushido. Paragons of virtue to some; rigid and inflexible to others.

- Rank 10 — Strength of a Thousand Ancestors: Perhaps one soul in ten thousand. Represents the greatest and most virtuous heroes of an entire Empire. Virtually beyond reproach. Completely selfless and devoted to lord, clan, and Emperor.

**Gaining ****&**** Losing Honor**

A character gains and loses Honor based on actions. The amount gained or lost depends on how honorable the character committing the act is — a character of Honor Rank 10 gains less Honor for showing courtesy to enemies because that is expected, whereas a character of Honor Rank 2 gains proportionately more for the same act. The GM decides whether Honor changes should be automatic or narrative.

**The Strength of Honor**

Characters add their Honor Rank to the total of any roll made to resist Intimidation or Temptation Skills, and to all rolls to resist Fear effects of any kind.

**Discerning Honor**

Any character may attempt to discern another samurai’s Honor Rank by making a Lore: Bushido/Awareness roll (TN 30). If successful, the person making the roll can discern the Honor Rank of the samurai being evaluated.

**Optional Rule: The Honor Roll**

Once per game session, after failing a Skill, Trait, Ring, or Spell Casting roll, a character may opt to re-roll the attempt at the same TN, but using their Honor Rank in rolled and kept dice instead. Example: a character of Honor Rank 6 who fails an attack roll re-rolls with 6k6. If the second roll succeeds, the action succeeds. If it also fails, not only does the original action fail but the character also loses a full Rank (10 Points) of Honor, as his faith in his own capabilities is shaken. GMs should carefully consider whether to use this rule.

**GLORY:**

Glory is a measure of an individual’s fame throughout the Empire as a result of personal accomplishments. Unlike Honor and Status, Glory is very fluid and can vary wildly from the results of a single duel, skirmish, or battle. Gaining Glory requires official recognition by a lord or superior — a soldier cannot increase his Glory Rank until his lord or commanding officer commends him.

**Effects of Glory**

The higher a samurai’s Glory Rank, the greater the probability he will be recognized when he encounters people he has not met before. When this happens, the GM can make a Lore: Heraldry/Intelligence roll for the strangers (TN 50 − (current Glory Rank × 5)). If the roll is successful, the strangers recognize the samurai and respond appropriately.

**Gaining ****&**** Losing Glory (Table 2.4)**

- Acknowledgment: Publicly acknowledged by someone of Status 7+: +1 Rank

- Blood Feud: Avenging a feud gains Glory equal to opponent’s Status or Glory (lower of the two)

- Caught in a Lie: Lose double the Glory gained by the act about which the samurai lied

- Caught using a Low Skill: −3 Glory points (−0.3). Fires when another character succeeds on the detection roll against the Low Skill use (e.g., Investigation/Perception vs Forgery TN, Investigation/Perception vs Stealth TN). The Low Skill user’s shame becomes public knowledge. If the Low Skill use also constitutes a crime (forgery of official documents, theft, assassination), the crime system’s own Infamy and Glory consequences (Section 11.3.9g) apply on top — the Low Skill discovery penalty and the crime penalty stack

- Completing a Quest: Gain Glory points equal to half the Glory Rank of the individual issuing the quest

- Craftsmanship: Gain one Glory point for each Raise made when creating an item

- Defeat: Officer who loses a battle or duelist who loses a duel loses opponent’s Glory

- Duels: Winning a fair duel gains Glory equal to 1/3 of opponent’s Status or Glory

- Family Dishonor: Lose one Glory Rank if a close relative commits an act worthy of seppuku

- Gifts: Gain Glory equal to Status/Glory if given a gift by a higher-ranking samurai

- Idleness: Lose one Glory point for every week without any Glory gain

- Indifference: Being ignored for prior bad behavior: reduce all Glory gains by 1, increase losses by 2

- Immortality: Inspiring a work of art gains Glory equal to the Glory Rank of the most prominent admirer

- Learning: Gain one Glory Rank when gaining an Insight Rank

- Marriage: Increase the Glory Rank of the lower-Glory spouse to one less than the higher-Glory spouse

- Public Bragging: After gaining Glory, may gain 1 additional point through public bragging

- Romance: One point of Glory for public declaration of love (without identifying lover)

- Skirmishes: Gain one Glory point for defeating bandits, ronin, etc.

- Status: Gain Glory equal to the amount of Status gained

- Stealing Credit: Gain the amount of Glory normally gained for the act being usurped

- Warfare: Gain 3 Glory points for surviving a battle; 6 if on the winning side

**Infamy**

Characters who gain Glory for committing criminal or dishonorable acts may instead be given Infamy, which is similar to Glory in all respects save that it inspires fear and revulsion rather than awe or respect. Individuals with Infamy can be recognized in the same manner as those with Glory, but the response of those who recognize them is vastly different. If a character has both Glory and Infamy, the total Ranks are combined to determine the likelihood of recognition. Infamy is most easily attained via the Infamous Disadvantage or through actions during play at GM’s discretion.

**STATUS:**

Status is the measure of an individual’s role and influence within Rokugani society. Those with higher Status command more resources, authority, and wealth; those with low Status have little authority over anything, perhaps even their own lives. Status is a largely static attribute that changes infrequently. Generally speaking, the only way to increase Status is to gain appointments or promotions from someone higher in the hierarchy of their family, Clan, or the Imperial bureaucracy.

**Effects of Status**

Status grants a samurai authority over other samurai of lower Status, but only if those samurai exist within the same hierarchy. A Lion samurai with Status 3 cannot issue commands to a Mantis samurai with Status 2 unless the two are members of the same military organization or a comparable social order; otherwise the Lion simply has no authority over the Mantis. Countermanding a superior’s orders results in lost Honor and possibly a loss of Status.

**Gaining ****&**** Losing Status**

Status may only be gained by formal promotion by a lord or another higher-ranking individual. Losing Status is less common and occurs as punishment for some failure of duty. In the instance of an unfair demotion, a samurai can conceivably protest to a figure of greater authority. If the demotion is found to be unjust, the wronged party’s Status is restored and the unjust individual loses Honor. However, an inappropriate protest typically results in even more severe punishment.

**Status Ranks (Table 2.7)**

The Status scale maps directly onto the Celestial Order (Section 2.1). Status 7+ corresponds to the kuge — the true nobility who serve the Emperor directly or through minimal separation. Status 0.5–6 corresponds to the buke — the vast majority of the samurai caste, from ji-samurai of vassal families (0.5) up through average clan samurai (1), military officers, magistrates, and provincial governors (6). Ji-samurai are the lowest-ranking samurai but are still samurai — they are buke, not bonge. Status 0.1–0.4 encompasses elevated bonge (ashigaru at 0.4, doshin at 0.3, budoka at 0.2, craftsmen and merchants at 0.1) — commoners whose service to the samurai caste grants them marginal standing but no samurai status. Status 0 marks ronin and peasant levies — ronin are samurai without a lord, technically within the caste but without a place in the social order; levies are bonge temporarily under arms. Negative Status values represent hinin — non-people whose occupations are considered spiritually unclean (entertainers, leatherworkers, torturers, gravediggers). Monks stand outside this hierarchy entirely at a fixed Status of 1.

**Monks**

Monks occupy a unique position in the social order. While respected for their wisdom and devotion to the Tao of Shinsei, they have no true place in samurai society. Monks are considered to have a Status of 1 and a Glory of 0. Monks may gain both Glory and Status, but only gain half the amount of Glory that would normally be awarded to a member of the samurai caste.
