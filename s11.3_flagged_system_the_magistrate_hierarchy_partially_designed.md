## 11.3 Flagged System — The Magistrate Hierarchy (FULLY DESIGNED)

Source: Emerald Empire 4th Edition, Chapter Seven (Law and Order), pp. 149–168. Full lore foundation documented in Section 2.8. A full law enforcement hierarchy exists distinct from the military garrison system. Important clarification: Clan Magistrates are NOT subordinate to the Emerald Magistrates in normal circumstances — they answer to their own clan’s daimyo and the Emerald Magistrates only have jurisdiction over Imperial law violations and crimes that cross clan boundaries. The two hierarchies operate in parallel, with the Emerald Magistrates able to override clan magistrates in specific circumstances, not as a general rule. This distinction matters for gameplay — a clan magistrate is a tool of the clan, while an Emerald Magistrate is a tool of the Emperor.

### 11.3.1 Peasant Enforcers (Doshin)

Law enforcement is generally placed in the hands of the ruling class, the samurai. However, it is simple truth that there are not enough samurai to uphold law and order in every single village in Rokugan. For every samurai in a town, there are a hundred commoners who perform the smaller yet essential tasks of everyday life. Peasants and eta must be regulated but are far too numerous to be closely monitored by samurai. This necessity has given rise to the lowest ranking form of law enforcement officer, called a doshin, a peasant elevated to watch over a town or city district, permitted by law to carry certain types of weapons (primarily non-lethal ones) for that purpose.

These peasant enforcers are the descendants of simple law enforcers from the beginning of the Empire, even before the Kami fell and the Celestial Order was established. In modern times, doshin are appointed by samurai and are most often found either in large cities (where the sheer population overwhelms samurai law enforcement) or in small villages far from the main traffic routes (where samurai often visit only once or twice a year). They are often simple farmers or laborers, distinguished from their fellow heimin only by the modest authority and duty the samurai have bestowed upon them. Most of the time their duties are simple, and during periods of inactivity village doshin usually work at a second profession. In larger cities being a doshin is a full-time job, and the local clan magistrate will often choose an entire force of doshin to assist him in upholding law and order.

Doshin have jurisdiction only over their local area — a village and the immediately surrounding area, for example, or a district within a city. Doshin always report to their local clan magistrate, and a conscientious magistrate often uses them not only for direct law enforcement but also as a network of informal enforcers and intelligence agents. Doshin only have the authority to punish crimes committed by peasants and eta, not samurai. Their foremost duty is to ensure peace within the commoner community so that work and public order will not be disrupted. They handle minor crimes such as theft and assault, and resolve them quickly and efficiently. For more serious crimes, including murder, kidnapping, treason, and blasphemy, the doshin must turn over authority to their supervising magistrate. Doshin are expected to have some martial proficiency to aid in their line of work. Though doshin positions are not officially filled by heredity, some villages have family lines that have proven their worth to their samurai lords and thus regularly serve in this martial capacity.

**⚙️ MECHANICAL HOOK:** Doshin are modeled through the magistrate system, not as a separate Population Unit — they reduce minor crime and unrest in their province. Connect to Section 52 (World Population) as a specialist PU type. A province without doshin generates more unrest from petty crime. In larger cities, the doshin count should scale with population. Their effectiveness is limited to commoner-class crimes; samurai crime requires magistrate intervention.

### 11.3.2 Yoriki (Deputies)

There are many crimes that are serious enough to require the attention of a samurai, but magistrates (whether clan or Imperial) are few in number and have numerous duties demanding their attention. Consequently, most of them make use of samurai assistants called yoriki, with each magistrate deploying anywhere from one or two yoriki all the way up to a dozen or more. Yoriki function as direct personal deputies to the magistrate, and are considered to share his authority for investigating crimes and punishing criminals. They are required to report daily to their superior, keeping him well informed of their investigations and the results. A yoriki who fails to do so is likely to lose his position quickly, a great loss of face and honor.

Since yoriki are trusted to act in the name of their superior, magistrates try to choose them carefully, although they may also find themselves saddled with inept or obstructive yoriki appointed by their superiors. Emerald Magistrates, due to their need to maintain freedom from clan influence, will often hire ronin as yoriki — wave-men are usually loyal to whoever is paying them, work for relatively meager wages, and tend to have no distracting commitment to other factions.

**⚙️ MECHANICAL HOOK:** Yoriki as a Named Character assignment (Section 22). A magistrate NPC’s effectiveness scales with the number and quality of yoriki assigned. Emerald Magistrate yoriki should have no clan affiliation bias (ronin preference). A yoriki’s daily reporting creates an information flow mechanic — a magistrate with competent yoriki has better intelligence about crime in their jurisdiction.

### 11.3.3 Clan Magistrates

Most law enforcement within the Empire is handled by clan magistrates, usually appointed by the local daimyo or governor, more occasionally by a higher authority such as a family daimyo or Clan Champion. Clan magistrates are empowered to investigate any crime which takes place within their area of jurisdiction — traditionally the clan boundaries, although often magistrates are appointed with a more limited area of authority, such as a single city. If a clan magistrate visits an area outside of his immediate jurisdiction, it is considered proper etiquette to notify the local authorities. A clan magistrate who repeatedly strays outside of his authority, or refuses to notify others of his presence, can be reprimanded for his actions. Repeated infractions can lead to the magistrate being dishonored and stripped of his position.

Clan magistrates supervise the yoriki and doshin who serve under their command and are expected to personally patrol their jurisdiction with at least some degree of regularity. They check travelers to make sure they have legitimate travel papers. All crimes that occur within a magistrate’s lands fall under his purview, from thievery and murder to trivial matters like property disputes. Although doshin can handle many of the lesser crimes that occur in the land, when more serious incidents happen, especially those involving samurai criminals or victims, a clan magistrate must be available to step in and deal with the investigation. Clan magistrates also oversee the collection of clan taxes and the administration of tariffs on goods being transported through clan lands for the purpose of sale or trade.

Clan magistrates must defer to Emerald Magistrates in situations where their authority overlaps. A few crimes are so heinous that a clan magistrate will nearly always call for additional aid from higher authorities. Crimes that violate Imperial Law, such as the practice of maho or an organization plotting against the throne, require the aid of Emerald Magistrates. Crimes that are Empire-wide in scope are also the purview of the Emerald Magistrates, being well beyond a clan magistrate’s jurisdiction. It is worth noting that a position as a magistrate is usually not something which a samurai earns through training or expertise in the law. Rather, most magistrates are appointed to their positions — perhaps because their superiors consider them trustworthy or capable, or because they have earned a reward for their loyal service. Some magistrates are appointed because they have pulled strings and accumulated political influence in order to win a higher position among the buke, and it is these sorts who are most likely to succumb to corruption or abuse of power.

**⚙️ MECHANICAL HOOK:** Clan Magistrate is a Named Character position (Section 22.8). Each province should have at least one. Their effectiveness (skill, Honor, corruption level) directly affects province crime rate and stability. Appointment is political, not merit-based — a daimyo NPC may appoint a loyal but incompetent magistrate, or a skilled but politically inconvenient one. Connect to Province Stability (Section 11.11), the Tax Cascade (Section 4.3.7), and the Travel Paper system (Section 11.2). A corrupt magistrate is a hidden stat that reduces effective law enforcement and increases crime, but may increase local Koku income through bribes (see Section 11.4).

### 11.3.4 Emerald Magistrates

The Emerald Magistrates are the highest legal authority in the Empire, the personal representatives of the Emerald Champion, who is in turn the Emperor’s direct champion and lieutenant. Emerald Magistrates serve Imperial law everywhere, regardless of clan borders. They can move across and through clan territory freely if their mission requires it. Emerald Magistrates have jurisdiction over a wide variety of crimes that are considered threats to the Empire or the Emperor. They also are usually responsible for collecting Imperial taxes (except during those eras when the office of Imperial Treasurer is active).

Most major cities in the Empire have at least one Emerald Magistrate, and sometimes a team of them, permanently assigned to maintain law and order and guard against abuses of power by the local authorities. Other Emerald Magistrates are assigned more generally to clan territories or geographic regions. The Emerald Champion usually keeps a significant number of his magistrates in the Imperial Capital, ready to be sent out to investigate whatever problems might catch his attention. The Jade Magistrates are a specialized variant of the Emerald Magistrates, and exist only in those eras when the Jade Champion’s office is active. They have the same authority and powers as the Emerald Magistrates, but are limited to investigating magical crimes, religious blasphemy, and Shadowlands influence.

### 11.3.5 Specialized Anti-Maho Organizations

In addition to their general magistrates, several clans maintain more specialized law enforcement groups with specific duties. The most notable of these are three clan organizations dedicated to hunting down those who use maho, forbidden blood magic. The Scorpion Kuroiban (Black Watch), the Crab Witch-Hunters, and the Phoenix Inquisitors all are considered to have the same authority as clan magistrates, but with jurisdiction only over crimes relating to maho and the Shadowlands.

**The Asako Inquisitors** are dominated by the Asako family but also include samurai from the Phoenix Clan’s other families. They are the most prominent and socially acceptable of the three groups, especially during eras when the Jade Champion’s office is left vacant. Most Rokugani daimyo are willing to allow Inquisitors into their court and show them deference and respect.

**The Kuni Witch-Hunters** are regarded by most Rokugani as uncouth superstitious louts. The organization includes shugenja but is most notorious for its small order of mystical warriors who battle the Shadowlands with both the strength of their arms and the purity of their spirits. Witch-hunters, or tsukai-sagasu, often wander freely across the Empire, showing little respect for clan boundaries or authorities, rooting out the influence of Jigoku wherever they can find it.

**The Scorpion Clan’s Kuroiban (Black Watch)** is a secretive group maintained by the clan’s two shugenja families, the Soshi and the Yogo. Like the other two, it is tasked with rooting out maho and Shadowlands infiltration, but it operates in the shadows and most samurai are not even aware of its existence. The Kuroiban does share information and resources with the Kuni, however, marking a rare instance of Crab-Scorpion cooperation in defense of the Empire.

**⚙️ MECHANICAL HOOK:** These three organizations function as roaming anti-maho/anti-Taint agents on the World Map. Model them as special Named Characters with cross-boundary movement rights. They are natural counter-agents for Maho Cult insurgencies (Section 11.11). The Kuni Witch-Hunters ignore clan boundaries (potential diplomatic incident generator). The Kuroiban operates as a covert agent (Section 12.8 Secret System). The Asako Inquisitors are the “polite” option that daimyo prefer.

### 11.3.6 The Charter of the Emerald Magistrates

The powers and duties of the Emerald Magistrates are extensive and have changed relatively little since the days when Soshi Saibankan and Doji Hatsuo first established them as the principal enforcers of the Empire’s law. The Charter lays out their areas of authority and the powers and methods they can use to enforce that authority.

**⚙️ CROSS-REF (Law ****&**** Order):** The following secret categories derive from the Law and Order system: Official Corruption (magistrates, governors, or tax officials engaged in graft — Section 11.4.5), Smuggling Operations (cartel activities, opium trade, gaijin contraband — Section 11.4.2 and 11.4.4), Ninja Activities (Shosuro Infiltrator operations, Goju incursions, Kolat assassinations — Section 2.8.8), Unauthorized Gaijin Pepper Use (Section 2.8.9), and Maho Practice (tracked separately via the Taint system but detection falls to the specialized organizations in Section 11.3.5). The Scorpion Kuroiban itself operates as a secret organization — most samurai are not even aware of its existence.

**Jurisdiction:** Crimes Against the Emperor (extends to the Imperial family, Otomo, Seppun, and Miya lines). Shadowlands Incursions (after military containment; the Magistrates are responsible for tracking down and eliminating isolated infiltration). Fugitives from the Law (any criminal who crosses from one clan’s provinces to another). Organized Blasphemy (defiance of the Emperor’s religious conventions). Civil Disorder (widespread civil disorder or general disregard for the law). Crimes of an Imperial Nature or Scope (theft, murder, treason, robbery, armed uprising, smuggling, and tax fraud when planned on an Empire-wide scale).

**Duties:** Tax Collection (overseeing the yearly tax harvest — most of these duties transfer to the Imperial Treasurer when that office is active). Authorizing Travel Papers (Emerald Magistrates are the lowest-ranking Imperial officials authorized to sign travel papers granting permission to travel beyond a single clan’s provinces; Clan Champions and family daimyo have similar authority, while clan magistrates and governors can only sign papers for individuals to leave clan lands to travel to a specific location in another clan’s territory). Protection of Visiting Dignitaries. Briefing the Emerald Champion (traditionally monthly; in practice, reports sent by messenger, frequently in cipher). Notification of Non-Jurisdictional Crimes to local authorities. Briefing the Governor on request (any daimyo can request a full accounting of activities).

**Restrictions:** Incidental Law Enforcement (not the duty of Emerald Magistrates to perform simple patrols or detect petty crime). Problems of Limited Scope (purely local crimes outside their charter). Authorization of Blood Feuds (Emerald Magistrates have no authority whatsoever in the matter of blood feuds — violation is adhered to with almost religious consistency and leads to serious complaints against the Emerald Champion). Levying of Troops (no authority to assume command of troops, except under specific Rights). Acceptance of Gifts and Monies (not permitted to accept gifts, favors, or special considerations for any portion of their duties).

**Rights:** Right of Questioning (detain non-samurai at any point; samurai of equal or lesser social standing only if caught in the act, otherwise an Order of Appearance is required; “questioning” in Rokugani legal context can include torture unless the person is a child or elderly). Right of Sentencing (pass sentence once a signed and notarized confession is obtained). Right to Prior Information (one full day advance notice from local authorities before large-scale military or police action). Right to Commandeer Troops (with daimyo notification and demonstrated need; must guarantee compensation for troop usage). Right of Imperial Levy (petition Emerald Champion for Imperial Legion forces — a matter most severe that risks angering the Emperor). Right of Privy Investigation (override a lesser magistrate’s investigation with a formal Order that must be signed, specify the crime, and explain why the two investigations cannot proceed in tandem).

### 11.3.7 Orders of Appearance

In instances where Magistrates are faced with criminal behavior by samurai of greater status than they, or wish to question a samurai-caste suspect of equal or lesser status who was not caught in the act of a crime, an Order of Appearance must be obtained before questioning or arrest can take place. An Order of Appearance contains a detailed accounting of the crime in question, and must be signed by either the Emerald Champion, the provincial daimyo or city governor, or the chief local clan magistrate. This can lead to tensions and problems if the samurai being sought for questioning is closely associated with the local authorities.

**⚙️ MECHANICAL HOOK:** An Order of Appearance is a court action (Section 15) that requires political capital. A high-Status suspect is harder to question than a low-Status one. The signatories (Emerald Champion, daimyo, governor, chief magistrate) each have their own political motivations and may refuse to sign if the suspect is their ally. From the player’s perspective as a lord, the Charter defines what Emerald Magistrates operating in your territory can and cannot do. A magistrate’s Rights (Commandeering Troops, Imperial Levy, Privy Investigation) can disrupt a lord’s plans; their Restrictions (no blood feud authority, no incidental enforcement) create gaps the lord must fill with clan magistrates and garrison forces. An Emerald Magistrate investigating corruption in your province is a political crisis. An Emerald Magistrate who catches your rival smuggling is an opportunity. The Charter is the rulebook governing a powerful NPC type that the player cannot directly control.

Investigation/trial gameplay loop: Section 11.3.13 (investigation loop), Section 11.3.14 (legal_status state machine), Section 11.3.15 (sentencing). Integration with Province Stability: Section 11.3.19 (crime suppression bridged to insurgency system). The Clan Magistrate vs Emerald Magistrate tension is preserved --- clan magistrates answer to daimyo, Emerald Magistrates answer to the Emperor, jurisdictional conflicts resolved through escalation (Section 11.3.9d, 11.3.16e).*

**The Ritsuryo Principle — DESIGN CONSTRAINT (LOCKED):**

Source: *Secrets of the Empire*, pp. 117–118 (Otomo Kuchiru and the ritsuryo legal system).

The Rokugani legal system is built on the principle of ritsuryo: testimony and honor weigh more heavily than physical evidence. This was codified by the courtier Otomo Kuchiru, who drew on the teachings of the magistrate Soshi Saibankan to establish a legal framework in which a person’s sworn word carries weight proportional to their Honor Rank, and in which lying under testimony is considered functionally impossible for an honorable samurai.

When the magistrate system is fully designed, the following principle is a hard design constraint — it must be mechanically reflected in any legal proceeding mechanic:

**In any legal proceeding, a character’s testimony is weighted by their Honor Rank.** A character with Honor 7.0 testifying against a character with Honor 3.0 has an enormous structural advantage — not because the system is corrupt, but because the legal philosophy holds that a more honorable person is more trustworthy by definition. Physical evidence can support or undermine testimony, but it cannot replace it. A magistrate cannot convict on evidence alone without at least one sworn statement from a person of standing.

Gameplay tensions this creates:

A high-Honor NPC who is lying is extremely difficult to challenge in court. The player needs to find someone of equal or higher Honor to counter-testify, or needs to produce evidence so overwhelming that the magistrate cannot ignore it — and even then, the magistrate faces a social crisis in publicly doubting the word of an honorable samurai. A low-Honor player character telling the truth faces structural disbelief — their word counts for less regardless of its accuracy. They need allies, evidence, and political leverage to compensate. The Scorpion Clan operates at a permanent legal disadvantage due to their generally lower Honor values — historically the Scorpion have compensated through information networks, blackmail, and ensuring cases never reach court in the first place. Magistrate characters must weigh Honor-weighted testimony against their own judgment — a magistrate who rules against a high-Honor witness takes a significant social risk.

Mechanical sketch (to be expanded when Section 11.3 is fully designed): Testimony Weight = Honor Rank x 5 (base weight in any contested legal proceeding). Physical Evidence Weight = fixed value by evidence type (murder weapon found = 20, eyewitness testimony from a bonge = 5, financial records = 10, etc.). The side with the higher total weight wins the legal proceeding. A magistrate may issue a ruling against the higher-weighted side, but doing so costs them Glory and generates a Tier 4 topic. Status also affects courtroom standing — a Provincial Daimyo’s testimony carries more institutional weight than a ji-samurai’s, regardless of Honor. Design the interaction carefully so that both Honor and Status matter, but Honor is the primary axis per the ritsuryo principle.

**✅ DESIGNED:** *The magistrate system is now fully designed in Sections 11.3.8 through 11.3.19. The ritsuryo principle is mechanically implemented through the Testimony Weight formula (Section 11.3.10): effective weight = (Honor Rank x 5) - (Infamy x 3). All legal proceedings use this formula. The investigation loop (Section 11.3.13), evidence accumulation (Section 11.3.13f), and sentencing (Section 11.3.15) all integrate the ritsuryo constraint.*

(2) Zone Event Log (Section 29.15.24): used by the investigation loop (Section 11.3.13) for crime scene reconstruction, witness identification, alibi verification, and Kitsuki's Eye presence data recovery. (4) CrimeRecord (Section 11.3.14e): world-level crime record with concealment_tn, perpetrator ground truth, and discovery tracking.

### 11.3.8 Crime: Treason Against Lord --- LOCKED

A vassal secretly working to undermine, depose, or betray their lord. This includes political treason (building a coalition to replace the lord), military treason (planning a coup), and external treason (feeding intelligence to an enemy clan, Kolat membership). Unlike financial crimes (audit trail) or maho (spiritual symptoms), treason produces almost no automatic signals. A treasonous vassal performs the same actions as a loyal one --- attending court, writing letters, building relationships --- with hidden intent.

**Severity: **Capital. Always. Treason is treason --- the lord offered a position of trust and the vassal betrayed it. Punishment is seppuku (offered first) or execution. If the convicted vassal refuses seppuku: exile to ronin status, massive Honor loss, Infamy gain. The vassal's family is not mechanically affected --- the individual made the choice, the individual pays.

### 11.3.8a Detection --- No Automatic Signals

There is no loyalty score visible to the lord. Detection comes entirely through intelligence actions and behavioral signals. The lord evaluates loyalty through the same systems available to any NPC: objective progress reports, disposition pattern analysis, intelligence reports, and personality-driven instinct.

**Signal 1 --- Objective stall: **A loyal vassal's actions correlate with their lord's assigned objectives. A treasonous vassal splits AP between their cover objective and their real objective. The assigned objective stalls. REASSESS_OBJECTIVE fires (Section 55.29). The lord notices slow progress. This is not evidence of treason --- incompetence, bad luck, and difficult targets produce the same signal --- but it puts the vassal on the lord's radar.

**Signal 2 --- Disposition anomaly: **The lord's intelligence network (PROBE, READ_CHARACTER, informants) reveals the vassal has high disposition with unexpected characters --- the lord's rivals, members of other factions, characters the vassal has no legitimate reason to befriend. Courtiers build wide networks, so this is ambiguous. But a pattern of relationship-building that serves the lord's enemies rather than the lord's interests is suspicious.

**Signal 3 --- Zone event log review: **A magistrate or intelligence-focused NPC reads the zone_event_log (Section 29.15.24) and finds meetings that don't match the vassal's assignment. 'Why was my military advisor meeting with a Scorpion courtier three times this season?' This produces a Tier 3 or 4 topic. Purge timer: 1 IC season --- evidence must be gathered before it expires.

**Signal 4 --- Intercepted correspondence: **INTERCEPT_LETTER captures a letter from the vassal to an enemy. Content reveals hostile intent. This is the strongest single piece of evidence but requires someone to specifically target the vassal's correspondence, which only happens if earlier signals raised suspicion.

**Signal 5 --- Co-conspirator failure: **The vassal's allies get caught independently. A Kolat operative is exposed and their contact list includes the vassal. A co-conspirator is PROBEd and their SECRET includes knowledge of the plot. The vassal is exposed through someone else's failure.

**Signal 6 --- Self-incrimination: **The vassal takes an action that only makes sense if they are disloyal. Voting against their lord's topic position without legitimate reason. Blocking their lord's ally's action. Failing to respond to a direct order in a way that benefits the lord's enemy. Observable by NPCs with high Dosatsu or Investigation skill.

**Smart traitor countermeasures: **A competent traitor manages signal 1 by spending enough AP on the assigned objective to show progress --- just slowly. They explain delays as legitimate difficulty. Their success at avoiding detection depends on their lord's personality: a Yu lord demands fast results and catches stalls early, a Dosatsu lord is observant but patient, a trusting Jin lord gets blindsided, a paranoid Seigyo lord investigates everyone and catches traitors early but alienates loyal vassals with constant surveillance.

### 11.3.8b Evidence Accumulation

Evidence accumulates as weighted points against the suspect. The lord can only formally accuse the vassal once the total crosses the accusation threshold. Both a single piece of hard evidence and a slow pattern of circumstantial signals can reach the threshold independently.

**Evidence weights:**

HARD EVIDENCE (any one may reach threshold alone):

  Intercepted letter with explicit hostile intent: 50

  Co-conspirator testimony (convicted traitor names them): 45

  Confession (extracted through PROBE or social pressure): 50

  Caught in act of hostile action (zone_event_log + witnesses): 40

CIRCUMSTANTIAL EVIDENCE (accumulates over time):

  Objective stall (per season of zero progress): 5

  Disposition anomaly (high disposition with lord's enemy): 8

  Suspicious meeting (zone_event_log, per incident): 5

  Unexplained absence from duty: 3

  Voting against lord's topic position without stated reason: 5

  Failed to execute direct order (outcome benefited enemy): 10

ACCUSATION THRESHOLD: 40

  Hard evidence: one item at 40-50 crosses threshold alone.

  Circumstantial: 5-8 signals over multiple seasons required.

### 11.3.8c Defense Hearing

When a lord formally accuses a vassal of treason, the vassal receives a hearing. The accused makes a Sincerity roll contested against the accumulated evidence weight. The ritsuryo principle (Section 11.3) applies --- the vassal's Honor Rank weights their testimony. A high-Honor vassal protesting innocence is structurally harder to convict.

**Successful defense: **Evidence total is halved (not zeroed). The lord remembers the pattern. However, the vassal gains a temporary political shield: re-accusing the same vassal without substantial new evidence (at least 20 new points) costs the lord Honor (false persecution) and damages disposition with all other vassals. This creates breathing room for the vassal but the clock is still ticking.

**Failed defense: **Lord proceeds to judgment. The accusation stands.

### 11.3.8d Authority Chain

A lord can only convict vassals of lower Status than themselves. A provincial daimyo convicts their own vassals. If a provincial daimyo suspects their family daimyo of treason, they must escalate --- present evidence to the clan champion or petition the Emperor. A higher lord (clan champion, family daimyo above the convicting lord) can intervene in any treason case: pardon, overturn, or commute the sentence. Pardoning treason is politically costly --- it undermines the authority of all lords.

AUTHORITY TABLE --- TREASON:

  under_investigation: Lord assigns investigation. Magistrate or trusted vassal PROBEs.

  accused: Lord presents evidence. Vassal receives defense hearing.

  decreed_guilty: Lord decides punishment. Seppuku offered first.

  refused_seppuku: Exile. Ronin status. Massive Honor loss. Infamy gain.

  acquitted: Evidence halved. Political shield active.

  pardoned: Higher lord overrides. Politically costly.

### 11.3.8e Public Consequences

A treason conviction generates a Tier 2 public topic: 'Lord X convicted Vassal Y of treason.' This ripples through the political system --- other lords tighten surveillance, the convicted vassal's former contacts become nervous, and anyone working with the traitor must decide whether to distance themselves or risk exposure.

**Co-conspirator naming: **The convicting lord chooses whether to name co-conspirators in the public topic. This is a strategic decision driven by personality. A Gi lord names everyone --- honesty demands it. A Seigyo lord keeps names private --- they surveil the remaining conspirators while the co-conspirators think they are safe. Naming: all co-conspirators immediately flagged, network blown open. Not naming: names stay in lord's IntelDB, quiet investigation continues.

**False accusation consequence: **A lord who accuses a vassal of treason and fails to convict (vassal successfully defends) loses Honor and damages disposition with all vassals. The chilling effect of a false treason accusation is severe --- other vassals fear they could be next.

### 11.3.8f Kolat Membership as Treason

Kolat membership constitutes treason. However, the Kolat conspiracy is unknown to the general population at world start (kolat_exposure_level = 0, Section 54.7c). A lord cannot charge 'Kolat membership' if they do not know the Kolat exists.

**Below kolat_exposure_level 60: **Kolat operatives caught in the act are charged with conventional crimes --- treason, espionage, conspiracy against the lord. The Kolat is never named in legal proceedings. The charges describe actions: 'conspiring with enemies of the clan,' 'conducting unauthorized intelligence operations,' 'feeding information to a foreign power.' The underlying organization remains invisible.

**At or above kolat_exposure_level 60: **'Kolat membership' becomes a recognized, standalone criminal charge. At this threshold, institutional knowledge is sufficient to support a formal legal category. Below 60, a magistrate who says 'this man is a member of the Kolat' sounds like a conspiracy theorist. At 60+, the accusation carries legal weight.

**Jurisdiction above 60: **Kolat crimes fall under Imperial authority --- Jade Magistrate, Emerald Magistrate, or Seppun Hidden Guard can investigate and convict. The local daimyo cannot overrule. Same jurisdictional weight as maho. The Kolat threatens the Celestial Order politically as maho threatens it spiritually --- both warrant Imperial jurisdiction. The Emperor can pardon. No one else.

**Kolat compartmentalization: **Catching one Kolat operative does NOT automatically expose the network. The Kolat's sect structure (Section 54.7c) means an operative may not know who else is involved. Co-conspirator testimony is limited to what the operative actually knows --- typically their handler and possibly one or two lateral contacts. The network survives individual exposure by design.

### 11.3.8g Lord Response to Suspicion

A lord who suspects but cannot yet prove treason has several options. These are not legal actions --- they are strategic responses available through existing engine systems.

**Increase surveillance: **Assign an intelligence vassal to PROBE/EAVESDROP on the suspect. Costs AP and risks the suspect noticing (zone_event_log records the surveillance actions too).

**Restrict access: **Reassign the vassal away from sensitive objectives. Give them a frontier posting. Remove them from court. This limits damage but tips off the vassal that they are suspected.

**Test loyalty: **Assign an objective that would conflict with the suspected betrayal. 'Go negotiate peace with the faction I think you're secretly working for.' Observe whether the vassal executes faithfully or sabotages.

**Confront directly: **PROBE the vassal in person. Demand explanation for the anomalies. The most Rokugani approach --- honor demands directness. But it gives the vassal a chance to lie (Sincerity vs. Investigation).

**Wait for proof: **Do nothing visible. Increase passive intelligence gathering. Wait for the traitor to make a mistake. Patient lords (Seigyo, Kanpeki, Dosatsu) prefer this. Yu and Ketsui lords lack the patience and tend to confront or restrict early.

### 11.3.9 Crime: Unsanctioned Killing of a Samurai --- LOCKED

Killing a samurai outside the sanctioned duel framework. This crime applies only to samurai killing samurai --- killing of heimin and hinin is covered separately. The core distinction: Rokugan provides a legal, honorable mechanism for lethal conflict between samurai --- the duel, sanctioned by both daimyo. Killing outside that framework is a crime because the sanctioned path existed and was not used.

### 11.3.9a Three Tiers of Severity

**Tier 1 --- Unsanctioned Duel Death (Minor): **Both samurai drew willingly. A challenge was issued, the form of the duel was followed, but neither daimyo had granted formal sanction. This is technically illegal but culturally tolerated. Source: Emerald Empire 4e, Chapter Seven. Punishment: house arrest (days to weeks), recompense to the victim's family (at minimum, the victim's swords returned), Honor loss (-0.3). If the winner follows protocols --- returning swords, paying recompense, showing respect --- the matter is usually settled quickly. If the winner fails to follow protocols, the magistrate reclassifies the case as Tier 2 murder. Political connections modulate consequences: a well-connected duelist with court allies may face no punishment at all. A duelist without protectors faces harsher penalties. A daimyo may reassign a troublesome duelist to a remote posting to prevent further incidents. Special case: samurai from clans at war with each other are assumed to have standing permission. No legal consequences unless their superiors explicitly withdraw permission after the fact.

**Tier 2 --- Open Killing Without Duel (Serious): **Cutting someone down in anger, on the road, during an argument, in a confrontation where no challenge was issued. The killer used their blade outside any ritual framework. Even if the victim also drew, the absence of a formal challenge makes this murder, not a duel. Punishment: seppuku offered. If the lord shows leniency (personality-dependent --- Jin and Gi lords are more likely to offer mercy), exile to ronin status is possible. Honor loss: -1.0. Glory loss: -0.5. Infamy gain if the killing was public.

**Tier 3 --- Covert Killing (Capital): **Poison. Ambush. Assassination. Hired killers. Any method that denies the victim the chance to draw their blade. This is the act of a coward and a traitor to the samurai code. Punishment: always capital. Seppuku offered as the only mercy --- if refused, execution. No exile option. No leniency. Honor loss: -2.0. Glory loss: -1.0. Infamy gain regardless of whether the act is public, because investigation will eventually reveal the method.

### 11.3.9b Status Modifier

The victim's Status relative to the killer affects severity. Killing upward --- a samurai killing someone of higher Status --- escalates the severity by one tier. Tier 1 (unsanctioned duel) becomes Tier 2 (serious). Tier 2 (open killing) becomes Tier 3 (capital --- no leniency). Tier 3 (covert killing) remains capital but generates a higher-tier topic (Tier 2 instead of Tier 3) and a larger inter-clan disposition hit.

Killing downward --- a samurai killing someone of lower Status --- allows the lord greater leniency. A provincial daimyo who kills a ji-samurai in a rage may face a reprimand, Honor loss, and a short period of house arrest rather than seppuku. The lower the victim's Status relative to the killer, the more latitude the lord has. This is thematically accurate to Rokugan's hierarchical society --- the Celestial Order places higher-Status samurai above lower, and the legal system reflects that.

No consequences apply to the killer's family. The individual committed the act, the individual pays.

### 11.3.9c Duel Sanction Requirement

A duel is only sanctioned if both daimyo of each participant agree. This makes duel permission a political tool. A daimyo can protect a weak vassal by refusing to sanction --- the challenger cannot legally proceed. A daimyo can weaponize permission by granting it when they know their champion will win. A daimyo who consistently refuses duel sanctions appears cowardly; one who consistently grants them appears aggressive. Both choices generate disposition modifiers and court topics.

If a samurai kills outside the duel framework when a duel could have been pursued, the absence of even an attempt to seek sanction is noted by the magistrate. 'You did not challenge. You did not seek your lord's permission. You simply killed.' This shifts the magistrate's disposition toward harsher judgment.

### 11.3.9d Jurisdiction

**Same-clan killing: **Handled by the shared lord. The lord investigates through their magistrate and decides punishment. Straightforward.

**Cross-clan killing: **Jurisdiction starts with the victim's clan. The victim's lord assigns a clan magistrate to investigate. The killer's clan can request to send their own magistrate to observe or participate --- this is a diplomatic gesture showing good faith. If the killer's clan refuses to cooperate, the victim's clan's outrage increases (disposition hit amplified). If the two clans disagree on the verdict or punishment --- and the characters involved have sufficient Status --- either side may petition for an Emerald Magistrate to take over the case as a cross-clan Imperial matter.

**Clan response modulates disposition: **When Samurai A of Clan 1 kills Samurai B of Clan 2, Clan 2 loses disposition toward Clan 1. The size of the hit depends on Clan 1's response. If Clan 1 immediately cooperates, investigates, and punishes Samurai A, the disposition hit is smaller --- Clan 1 demonstrated that they take the matter seriously. If Clan 1 protects Samurai A, stonewalls the investigation, or refuses to acknowledge the crime, the disposition hit is larger and generates a Tier 3 diplomatic topic: 'Clan 1 refuses to address the killing of [Samurai B].'

### 11.3.9e Evidence and the Ritsuryo Principle

Murder leaves a body --- more physical evidence than treason. Both physical evidence and Honor-weighted testimony (Section 11.3, Ritsuryo Principle) are valid grounds for conviction. A magistrate can convict on strong physical evidence: the body, the zone_event_log (Section 29.15.24) placing the killer at the scene, the murder weapon identified, witness testimony from any caste. However, the ritsuryo principle still applies --- a high-Honor samurai's denial carries significant testimonial weight. A magistrate who convicts a samurai of Honor 7.0 against their sworn denial takes a social risk (Glory loss, Tier 4 topic).

**Covert killing evidence degradation: **Covert killers who clean up evidence are harder to catch. No witnesses, body disposed of or staged as accident, zone_event_log entries purge after one IC season (Section 29.15.24). If nobody discovers the crime within one season, physical evidence degrades significantly. The killer may escape justice entirely unless a co-conspirator talks, a Kitsuki investigator reads the zone_event_log before it purges, or subsequent intelligence reveals the act.

### 11.3.9f Trial by Combat

The accused may demand a duel to prove their innocence --- trial by combat, divine judgment through the blade. The accuser or the victim's family is obligated to accept. If they refuse the duel, they implicitly agree that the accused did nothing wrong and the case is dismissed.

**If the accused wins: **Fully cleared. The Fortunes have spoken. Evidence is wiped, the case is closed, legal_status returns to clear. No lingering evidence total, no re-accusation on the same charge. However, winning trial by combat carries heavy disposition hits. The victim's clan and allies do not forgive easily --- divine judgment settles the legal question but not the political one. The disposition hit scales with the victim's Status: a ji-samurai's death cleared by combat is a minor grudge, a family daimyo's death cleared by combat poisons relations for generations.

**If the accused loses: **They are dead. The Fortunes have rendered judgment. The case is resolved by the accused's death whether or not they were actually guilty.

**Champion substitution: **Either side may appoint a champion to fight on their behalf, per standard duel rules. This means a courtier accused of murder can have their clan's best duelist fight for them. A clan that employs this tactic is spending political capital --- it works, but it looks like they are hiding behind a champion rather than facing judgment personally.

### 11.3.9g Honor and Infamy Costs

HONOR LOSS BY METHOD:

  Unsanctioned duel death: -0.3

  Open killing (blade, no duel): -1.0

  Covert killing (poison, ambush): -2.0

GLORY LOSS:

  Unsanctioned duel death: 0 (may gain Glory if cause was noble)

  Open killing: -0.5

  Covert killing: -1.0

INFAMY:

  Unsanctioned duel death: none

  Open killing (public): Infamy gain

  Covert killing: Infamy gain when discovered

TOPIC GENERATED:

  Unsanctioned duel death: Tier 4 (minor gossip)

  Open killing: Tier 3 (serious, named characters)

  Covert killing: Tier 3 (Tier 2 if victim was high Status)

### 11.3.9h Wartime Exception

Killing enemy samurai during wartime is not a crime --- it is war. Battlefield killing, siege combat, and military engagements produce no murder charges. However, treatment of the defeated is a matter of etiquette, not law. Killing prisoners, killing surrendered enemies, or killing enemy samurai in occupied territory outside of active combat is not a criminal offense but carries Honor loss, Infamy gain if made public, and disposition modifiers with observers. 'You may kill your enemy in battle. How you treat him after the battle is over tells the world who you are.'

Samurai from clans at war who engage in unsanctioned duels are assumed to have standing permission from their lords. No legal consequences unless superiors explicitly withdraw permission after the fact.

### 11.3.9i Authority Chain

AUTHORITY TABLE --- UNSANCTIONED KILLING:

  under_investigation: Victim's clan magistrate (cross-clan) or lord's magistrate (same-clan).

  accused: Magistrate presents evidence. Accused may demand trial by combat.

  decreed_guilty: Lord decides punishment per severity tier.

    Tier 1: House arrest, recompense, Honor loss.

    Tier 2: Seppuku offered. Exile possible with leniency.

    Tier 3: Capital. Seppuku offered. No leniency.

  acquitted: Cleared (if by trial by combat, disposition hits still apply).

  pardoned: Higher lord overrides. Politically costly.

  Escalation: Emerald Magistrate if cross-clan disagreement and sufficient Status.

### 11.3.9j Self-Defense

If a samurai kills another samurai in self-defense and this is proved, no crime has been committed. The defender did not choose to fight --- they responded to a lethal threat. Self-defense is proved through two channels: the zone_event_log (Section 29.15.24) records who acted first, establishing that the attacker initiated the hostile action before the defender responded. Honor-weighted witness testimony (Section 11.3, Ritsuryo Principle) from samurai who observed the attacker draw first. Both forms of evidence are valid. If neither exists --- no witnesses, zone_event_log purged --- self-defense is difficult to prove and the case defaults to standard Tier 2 investigation.

### 11.3.9k Killing a Ronin

Ronin are technically samurai, so the crime exists in principle. However, a ronin has no lord, no clan, and no family to demand justice. Without a complainant, the crime goes uninvestigated and unprosecuted. A ronin who dies with no connections simply dies. The exception: a ronin employed by a lord, under the protection of a patron, or who had cultivated powerful friends. In these cases the patron or ally may act as complainant and demand investigation. This is driven by disposition and political interest, not the legal system --- nobody is obligated to pursue justice for a ronin, but anyone with sufficient standing may choose to.

### 11.3.9l Conspiracy to Murder

Ordering another person to kill is the same crime as killing, at the same severity tier as the method used. A lord who orders a vassal to assassinate an enemy by poison is guilty of Tier 3 covert killing alongside the assassin. A lord who orders a vassal to cut down a rival openly is guilty of Tier 2. Proof requires establishing the order was given --- intercepted letters, the assassin's testimony, witness to the command. The ritsuryo principle applies: a high-Honor lord's denial of having given the order carries substantial testimonial weight, making conspiracy harder to prove than the act itself.

### 11.3.9m Manslaughter

Accidental killing --- training accidents, duels to first blood where a wound proves fatal, sparring sessions gone wrong --- is not a crime. No legal_status progression fires, no investigation, no trial. However, manslaughter produces disposition changes: the victim's family, lord, and allies lose disposition toward the killer. The size of the disposition hit depends on the circumstances and the victim's Status. A training accident between equals where both participants knowingly accepted risk generates a small hit. A reckless act that kills someone generates a larger one. The killer's lord may impose non-legal consequences: reassignment, public apology, recompense to the victim's family.

### 11.3.9n Attempted Murder

A samurai who attempts to kill another samurai and fails --- the victim survives --- is guilty of attempted murder. The method classification still applies: an attempted covert killing (failed poisoning, botched ambush) is treated more harshly than an attempted open killing (drew a blade and was disarmed or stopped). Punishment: exile to ronin status. Seppuku is not typically offered for attempted murder because the victim survived --- the offense is serious but not irreversible. Honor loss matches the method: -1.0 for attempted open killing, -2.0 for attempted covert killing. The victim's clan may still demand seppuku through diplomatic channels, and the killer's lord may grant it if the political pressure is sufficient, but exile is the standard sentence. The Status modifier (Section 11.3.9b) applies: attempting to kill a higher-Status samurai may escalate to capital.

### 11.3.10 Honor and Infamy in Legal Proceedings --- Cross-Cutting Rule --- LOCKED

This rule applies to ALL crimes defined in Section 11.3, not just a specific crime type. It extends the Ritsuryo Principle (Section 11.3, Design Constraint) with explicit mechanical consequences for Honor and Infamy in legal proceedings.

**Low-Honor accused: **A samurai with low Honor (Rank 3 or below) who denies charges carries less testimonial weight. Their denial is worth Honor Rank x 5 = 15 or less against the prosecution's evidence total. A samurai with Honor 1.0 contributing only 5 testimonial weight is nearly indefensible by word alone --- they need overwhelming physical evidence or a high-Honor witness willing to vouch for them. This makes it structurally easier to convict low-Honor samurai, which is thematically correct: Rokugan's legal system assumes honorable people tell the truth.

**High-Infamy accused: **A samurai with Infamy (any Infamy score above 0) faces a further penalty. Infamy represents a public reputation for dishonorable behavior. In legal proceedings, Infamy acts as a negative modifier on testimonial weight: effective Testimony Weight = (Honor Rank x 5) - (Infamy x 3). A samurai with Honor 5.0 and Infamy 3 has effective testimony weight 25 - 9 = 16 --- barely more than an Honor 3 samurai with no Infamy. A samurai with Honor 2.0 and Infamy 5 has effective testimony weight 10 - 15 = 0 (floored at 0). Their word is legally worthless.

**Low-Honor or high-Infamy accuser: **The same principle applies in reverse. A samurai bringing charges against another must testify under oath. If the accuser has low Honor or high Infamy, their accusation carries less weight. A dishonorable samurai accusing an honorable one faces structural disbelief --- the magistrate and court are predisposed to believe the higher-Honor party. This means a Scorpion with Honor 3.0 accusing a Crane with Honor 7.0 needs substantial physical evidence to overcome the testimonial gap. The Scorpion Clan's generally lower Honor values place them at a permanent legal disadvantage, historically compensated through information networks, blackmail, and ensuring cases never reach court in the first place.

**Interaction with trial by combat: **Trial by combat (Section 11.3.9f) bypasses the ritsuryo testimonial system entirely. A low-Honor accused who cannot win on testimony can demand trial by combat instead, shifting the contest from words to blades. This is one reason trial by combat exists --- it provides an alternative path to justice for those who cannot win in a system weighted by Honor. However, the accuser is obligated to accept, and if the accused wins, the case is fully closed regardless of the Honor disparity.

### 11.3.11 Crime: Magistrate Corruption and Bribery --- LOCKED

A magistrate who accepts bribes to bury investigations, drop charges, or issue favorable rulings. This is a specific and critical form of corruption because the magistrate IS the legal system --- a corrupt magistrate doesn't just commit a crime, they destroy the mechanism that catches criminals. Severity: treason-equivalent. A magistrate holds an appointed trust. Betraying that trust is betrayal of duty, which is betrayal of lord and Emperor.

### 11.3.11a Detection

The magistrate controls what gets reported, making self-detection impossible. Detection comes from external sources:

**Accuser complaint: **If someone filed an accusation and the case disappears without resolution, the accuser notices. 'I brought charges and nothing happened.' The accuser can escalate to a higher authority --- another magistrate, or the daimyo directly. 

**Case review: **A higher-ranking magistrate or an Emerald Magistrate reviews active and recently closed cases. An investigation that was dropped without a verdict, an acquittal with thin evidence, or a case where the accused's disposition toward the magistrate spiked suspiciously --- all are signals. This is an active investigation action, not automatic.

**Zone event log: **The zone_event_log (Section 29.15.24) records the magistrate meeting with the suspect's associates. A meeting between a magistrate and the family of an accused person, especially at an unusual location or time, is circumstantial evidence. Kitsuki investigators are particularly effective here.

**Co-conspirator exposure: **The briber gets caught for something else and names the magistrate to reduce their own sentence. The most common exposure path --- the corrupt relationship only needs one side to crack.

### 11.3.11b Attempting to Bribe a Magistrate

A character attempting to bribe a magistrate uses the existing BRIBE_FOR_INFO ActionID mechanics: Temptation/Awareness vs the magistrate's Etiquette/Willpower, weighted by the magistrate's Honor. A high-Honor magistrate is structurally resistant to bribery --- their Honor acts as a modifier to the resistance roll.

**Failed bribe attempt: **The magistrate refuses. The attempt itself becomes evidence against the briber --- 'Why would you try to buy me off if you had nothing to hide?' The failed bribe adds evidence weight to the briber's existing case (evidence_total += 15). It is also a separate offense: attempting to corrupt an Imperial officer. This offense generates its own investigation entry independent of the original case.

**Successful bribe: **The magistrate accepts. The current investigation is buried --- the case is dropped, evidence is suppressed, the accused is cleared or charges reduced. However, the bribe creates a co-conspirator. The magistrate and the briber now share a secret. If either is exposed through any subsequent investigation, the other is implicated. The magistrate's corruption is a Tier 1 secret (Section 12.8) on both characters' sheets.

### 11.3.11c Jurisdiction

A corrupt magistrate cannot be investigated by their own court. Investigation requires either another magistrate of equal or higher rank, or an Emerald Magistrate taking over the case. An Emerald Magistrate can invoke the Right of Privy Investigation (Section 11.3.6) to supersede the corrupt magistrate's authority and access all case records. If the corrupt magistrate is themselves an Emerald Magistrate, only the Emerald Champion or the Emperor can order the investigation.

### 11.3.11d Scope of Exposure

When a corrupt magistrate is caught, only current active cases are affected. Past closed cases are not automatically reopened. The reasoning: past verdicts were issued and accepted. Reopening every case a magistrate ever handled would destabilize the entire legal system. If a specific past case warrants review, it must be independently investigated by another magistrate on its own merits --- the corrupt magistrate's exposure does not constitute automatic grounds for reopening.

### 11.3.11e Punishment

Treason-equivalent. Seppuku offered. If refused: execution, exile, stripping of office and Status. A corrupt Emerald Magistrate faces Imperial judgment --- the Emerald Champion decides. A corrupt clan magistrate faces their daimyo. The conviction generates a Tier 2 topic: 'Magistrate X convicted of corruption.' This damages the appointing lord's reputation --- they chose this magistrate, and the appointment reflects on their judgment. Disposition hit to the appointing lord from all characters who learn of the conviction.

### 11.3.11f Bribery Pipeline --- Full Sequence --- LOCKED

The complete sequence from crime to cover-up. Each step is a discrete game state transition with its own mechanics and failure points.

**Step 1 --- Crime committed. **The perpetrator's legal_status enters under_investigation. An investigation is active.

**Step 2 --- Magistrate assigned. **The daimyo or Emerald Champion assigns a magistrate to the case. This generates a Tier 4 topic: 'Magistrate [X] is investigating [crime description].' The topic enters the normal topic propagation system --- characters at court, characters receiving letters, characters who PROBE for information can all acquire this topic.

**Step 3 --- Briber discovers magistrate identity. **The perpetrator (or an ally acting on their behalf) must have the Step 2 topic in their known_topics before they can act. Without knowing which magistrate is handling the case, there is no target for the bribe. Discovery happens through standard intelligence channels: hearing the topic at court, receiving it in a letter, PROBE, OBSERVE_COURT_ATTENDEES, or the magistrate directly approaching the suspect for questioning (which reveals the magistrate's identity automatically).

**Step 4 --- Approach. **Two paths. Direct approach: the briber meets the magistrate in person. The zone_event_log records the meeting. No deniability --- if the bribe fails, the magistrate knows exactly who offered it. Intermediary approach: the briber sends a third party to carry the offer. The intermediary becomes a co-conspirator who knows both the briber and the magistrate. Safer for the briber (deniability if the intermediary is caught alone) but creates a third person who can expose the entire arrangement. The intermediary uses the same Temptation/Awareness roll to deliver the offer.

**Step 5 --- Bribe offered. **Two currencies. Koku: a direct payment. Leaves a financial trail --- if the magistrate's wealth is later audited (Section 4.3.7 audit system), unexplained Koku is evidence of corruption. Favor: a political promise --- a posting, a marriage arrangement, making a problem disappear, future support at court. Leaves no financial trail. Only the co-conspirator relationship connects the briber to the magistrate. Favor bribes are structurally harder to detect but create a longer-term obligation that can be leveraged.

**Step 6 --- Magistrate resistance roll. **Temptation/Awareness (briber) vs Etiquette/Willpower (magistrate), modified by the magistrate's Honor Rank. A magistrate with Honor 7.0 adds +35 to their resistance (Honor Rank x 5 per the ritsuryo principle applied to personal integrity). A magistrate with Honor 3.0 adds only +15. The bribe amount or favor value modifies the briber's roll: a larger bribe or a more valuable favor increases the Temptation roll's effective total.

**Step 7a --- Refused. **The magistrate rejects the bribe. The failed attempt adds +15 to the evidence_total against the briber in the existing investigation. 'Why would you try to buy me off if you had nothing to hide?' Additionally, the attempted bribery is a separate offense: attempting to corrupt an Imperial officer. This generates its own investigation entry. If the approach was direct, the magistrate knows exactly who offered. If via intermediary, the magistrate knows the intermediary but may not know the briber's identity --- the intermediary becomes the target of a secondary investigation.

**Step 7b --- Accepted. **The magistrate takes the bribe. Three consequences fire:

First: the magistrate buries the current investigation. Physical evidence (intercepted letters, murder weapons, financial records) in the magistrate's possession is destroyed permanently --- if another magistrate later takes over the case, this evidence no longer exists. Testimony and witness accounts are suppressed but not destroyed --- the witnesses still exist and can be re-interviewed by another investigator. The case is dropped or the accused is acquitted with a thin rationale.

Second: the magistrate and the briber become co-conspirators. A Tier 1 secret (Section 12.8) is written to both characters' sheets: 'Magistrate [X] accepted a bribe from [Y] to bury the investigation of [crime].' If either character is later exposed through any investigation, the other is implicated. The secret persists indefinitely.

Third: the magistrate takes a hidden Honor loss of -0.5. This is not public --- no one sees the magistrate's Honor drop. But the system tracks it. Lower Honor makes the magistrate easier to bribe next time (lower resistance roll) and reduces their testimonial weight in future cases (Section 11.3.10). A magistrate who accepts multiple bribes spirals: each acceptance lowers their resistance to the next one. This is the corruption spiral --- the first bribe is the hardest.

**Step 8 --- Active cover-up. **If another party independently possesses the same evidence that was suppressed (a witness who also told someone else, a letter that was copied before interception), the corrupt magistrate must actively argue against it. They use their own Honor-weighted testimony (Section 11.3.10) to discredit the evidence: 'I investigated this thoroughly and found no substance to these claims.' The magistrate's word carries weight proportional to their Honor --- but each accepted bribe has lowered that Honor, making subsequent cover-ups progressively less convincing.

### 11.3.11g NPC Decision Engine --- Bribery Evaluation

The NPC Decision Engine evaluates bribery as an option when a character's evidence_total in an active investigation approaches the accusation threshold. This evaluation is personality-gated and risk-assessed.

**Trigger: **evidence_total reaches 25 or above (accusation threshold is 40). The NPC becomes aware that the investigation is progressing dangerously. The engine evaluates whether to attempt bribery alongside other defensive options (fleeing, destroying evidence personally, seeking political protection, preparing a legal defense).

**Personality gates:**

ALWAYS BLOCKED (will never attempt bribery):

  GI: Honesty cannot conceive of corrupting another person's duty.

  MAKOTO: Sincerity finds the deception fundamentally repulsive.

  MEIYO: Honor will face judgment rather than compound dishonor.

CONDITIONAL (will consider based on circumstances):

  JIN: Blocked unless conviction would harm innocents under the NPC's protection.

  YU: Blocked. Courage faces the accusation directly. Exception: if the NPC

      is protecting someone else (lord, family), Yu may bribe to shield them.

  REI: Blocked unless the NPC can arrange it through an intermediary with

       complete deniability. Courtesy will not sully itself with a direct approach.

  CHUGI: Permitted if lord-assigned. A vassal protecting their lord's interests

         through bribery is fulfilling duty. Blocked if self-serving.

NO RESTRICTION (will evaluate purely on risk/benefit):

  SEIGYO: Natural briber. Evaluates magistrate's Honor (can they be turned?),

          cost (Koku or Favor), and exposure risk. Strongly prefers intermediary.

  KETSUI: Will bribe if it serves a committed objective. No moral hesitation.

  DOSATSU: Evaluates the magistrate's personality first (READ_CHARACTER) before

           deciding whether a bribe would even work. Will not waste resources on

           a magistrate who will refuse.

  CHISHIKI: Views bribery as a tool. Prefers Favor over Koku (no financial trail).

  KANPEKI: Will bribe only if success probability is high. Will not attempt a

           low-probability bribe --- the risk of failure outweighs the benefit.

  KYORYOKU: Prefers direct approach. Intimidation lean --- may INTIMIDATE rather

            than bribe, which is a hostile action and escalates differently.

  ISHI: Once committed to bribery as a strategy, will not deviate. Will escalate

        the offer rather than accept refusal.

**Risk assessment: **Before attempting, the engine evaluates: the magistrate's estimated Honor (from known_topics, PROBE results, or reputation --- higher Honor = lower success chance), the cost (Koku reserve or Favor obligation), the exposure risk (direct vs intermediary, zone_event_log exposure window), and the consequence of failure (+15 evidence, separate offense). A Seigyo NPC with a high Dosatsu secondary will only attempt the bribe if all factors align favorably. A Ketsui NPC attempts it as soon as the trigger fires.

### 11.3.11h Evidence Destruction Mechanics

When a corrupt magistrate destroys physical evidence, the evidence_total for the original crime drops by the exact weight of the destroyed item. An intercepted letter worth 50 evidence points removes 50 from the total. A murder weapon worth 40 removes 40. If the evidence_total drops below the accusation threshold (40), the case can no longer proceed to formal accusation --- the investigation stalls.

**Physical evidence (destroyable): **Intercepted letters, financial records, murder weapons, stolen goods, forensic findings. Once destroyed by the magistrate, the physical object is gone permanently. If another magistrate takes over the case, they will never find it.

**Testimony (suppressible, not destroyable): **Witness accounts, confessions, informant reports. The magistrate can suppress these by declining to record them or dismissing them in the case file, but the witnesses still exist. Another magistrate re-interviewing the same witnesses recovers the testimony at its original evidence weight. Suppressed testimony remains in the evidence_total while the corrupt magistrate controls the case --- it is only functionally removed if no one else ever re-interviews the witness.

**Knowledge persistence: **Destroying physical evidence does not destroy knowledge of that evidence. If other characters knew the letter existed --- because they saw it, were told about it, or hold a topic referencing it --- that knowledge persists in their known_topics. If another party raises the existence of the destroyed evidence, the situation becomes a contested testimony under the ritsuryo principle (Section 11.3.10): the accuser argues 'this evidence existed' while the corrupt magistrate argues 'I found no such evidence in my investigation.' Each side's claim is weighted by their Honor Rank. The corrupt magistrate is betting their credibility --- but each accepted bribe has reduced their Honor (Section 11.3.11f Step 7b), making successive cover-ups progressively less convincing.

### 11.3.11i Third-Party Bribery

The briber is not always the accused. A lord may bribe a magistrate to protect a vassal. A Kolat operative may bribe to protect a cell member. An ally may act out of high disposition toward the accused. The pipeline (Section 11.3.11f) operates identically regardless of who initiates --- the third party needs the magistrate identity topic in their known_topics and a motive to act.

**Chain of intermediaries: **The criminal can instruct an intermediary to carry out the bribe on their behalf: criminal tells intermediary, intermediary approaches magistrate. This creates a three-person co-conspirator chain. Each link adds deniability for the criminal but adds a point of failure --- three people now share the Tier 1 secret. If any one of them is exposed, the chain can unravel. The intermediary may not know the criminal's identity if the instruction was passed through a dead drop or anonymous channel (Kolat infrastructure excels at this), but they know the magistrate's identity and the nature of the arrangement.

**Lord protecting vassal: **A lord who bribes a magistrate to protect a guilty vassal is committing magistrate corruption (Section 11.3.11) on top of whatever the vassal did. If exposed, the lord faces treason-equivalent charges and the vassal's original case reopens. This is a high-stakes gamble: the lord is trading their own legal exposure for their vassal's freedom.

### 11.3.11j Magistrate-Initiated Extortion

A corrupt magistrate may approach the suspect directly: 'I have evidence against you. Perhaps we can reach an understanding.' This reverses the standard bribery pipeline --- the magistrate solicits rather than being approached.

**Mechanics: **The magistrate rolls Temptation/Awareness against the suspect's Etiquette/Willpower. However, a failed roll does not mean the suspect refuses. A guilty suspect knows they are guilty and may want the deal regardless --- a failed Temptation roll means the suspect has negotiating leverage and bargains the price down. A 20 Koku demand becomes 10 Koku. A major Favor becomes a minor one. The suspect accepts even against a failed roll because the alternative is conviction.

**Detection profile: **Magistrate-initiated extortion has a different detection profile than standard bribery. The magistrate controls the timing and location of the approach, reducing zone_event_log exposure. However, the magistrate is the one taking the initiative, which means their behavior pattern changes --- a magistrate who begins meeting privately with suspects they are investigating is visible to anyone watching the magistrate. Yoriki (Section 11.3.2) who work closely with the magistrate may notice unusual behavior.

**Personality of the magistrate: **Not all corrupt magistrates extort. Extortion requires proactive dishonesty --- the magistrate must initiate. Seigyo magistrates are natural extortionists (calculated exploitation). Kyoryoku magistrates may intimidate rather than negotiate ('cooperate or I will make this very public'). Dosatsu magistrates identify which suspects are vulnerable and approach selectively. Gi and Makoto magistrates never extort --- they are incapable of initiating corruption even if they might be vulnerable to a well-crafted bribe under extreme pressure.

### 11.3.11k Failed Bribe as Evidence

When a bribe attempt fails (the magistrate refuses), the attempt becomes evidence in the active investigation (+15 evidence_total, per Section 11.3.11f Step 7a). Whether the magistrate also reports the attempt publicly is a separate personality-driven decision.

**Gi magistrate: **Reports immediately to the daimyo. Generates a Tier 3 topic: '[Suspect] attempted to bribe Magistrate [X] during investigation of [crime].' Public knowledge. Maximum damage to the briber.

**Chugi magistrate: **Reports to their lord privately. The lord decides whether to make it public. No automatic topic generation --- the information stays within the lord's intelligence.

**Meiyo magistrate: **Reports formally through proper channels. Generates an internal report but not a public topic. The information is available to any subsequent investigator who reviews the case file.

**Seigyo magistrate: **Does not report. Holds the failed bribe attempt as private leverage over the briber. 'You tried to bribe me. I said no. But I remember.' This creates an asymmetric power relationship --- the magistrate can use the threat of exposing the bribe attempt to extract concessions from the briber at a later date, independent of the current case.

**Yu magistrate: **Reports loudly and publicly. May confront the briber directly in court. Generates a Tier 3 topic. Yu finds the attempt personally insulting --- someone thought they could be bought.

**Dosatsu magistrate: **Holds the information, observes the briber's subsequent behavior. Uses the failed attempt as intelligence to understand the briber's network and methods. Reports only when it serves the investigation.

### 11.3.12 Crime: Violence Against Persons --- LOCKED

Non-lethal violence between samurai outside any duel framework. A punch at a sake house, a beating after an argument, shoving someone at court. This is samurai-on-samurai only --- samurai violence against heimin is ignored by the legal system unless it disrupts economic productivity at a scale that draws the lord's attention. If blades are drawn and someone dies, the crime escalates to Unsanctioned Killing (Section 11.3.9). If both parties drew willingly in the form of a duel, the incident falls under the unsanctioned duel rules (Section 11.3.9 Tier 1). Violence Against Persons covers everything below lethal force and outside the duel framework.

### 11.3.12a Severity

Minor crime. This is the lowest tier of samurai crime that the legal system addresses. Punishment is proportional: house arrest (days to weeks), Honor loss, disposition hits, possible banishment from the area where the incident occurred. The lord may reassign or demote the offender to a remote posting. No seppuku, no exile, no capital punishment. Property damage caused during the incident must be paid for by the offender --- a samurai who wrecks a sake house pays for repairs.

HONOR LOSS: -0.2 (public loss of composure and breach of conduct).

GLORY LOSS: -0.1 (minor public embarrassment).

INFAMY: None unless the incident was particularly brutal or repeated.

TOPIC: Tier 4 (minor gossip). 'Samurai [X] and Samurai [Y] brawled at [location].'

### 11.3.12b Detection

Trivial. Brawls are public by nature. The zone_event_log records the incident automatically. Witnesses are plentiful. Doshin (Section 11.3.1) in cities handle the immediate response --- separating the combatants, reporting to the local clan magistrate. In smaller settings without doshin, any samurai present may intervene or report the incident. Detection is not the challenge with this crime --- the question is whether anyone with authority cares enough to prosecute.

### 11.3.12c Status Modifier

As with murder (Section 11.3.9b), violence upward (attacking a higher-Status samurai) is treated more harshly than violence downward. A ji-samurai who strikes a provincial daimyo faces serious consequences --- house arrest at minimum, possibly escalating to exile if the daimyo demands it. A provincial daimyo who strikes a ji-samurai gets a private reprimand from their lord at most. Cross-clan violence is handled by the victim's clan magistrate, with the same escalation path as murder (Section 11.3.9d): victim's clan first, killer's clan can request to participate, Emerald Magistrate on escalation.

### 11.3.12d Duel Pretext

Non-lethal violence creates duel pretext. If Samurai A beats Samurai B in a sake house, Samurai B now has legitimate grounds to issue a formal duel challenge (ISSUE_DUEL_CHALLENGE, Section 29.15.24). The zone_event_log records the violence, establishing the provocation. This is one of the most important mechanical consequences of this crime --- a fistfight that should have been minor can escalate into a lethal duel if the victim chooses to pursue satisfaction through the duel system. The provocation flag (Section 29.15.24) is written on the victim's record, referencing the violent action.

### 11.3.12e Repeated Offenses

A samurai who commits violence repeatedly develops a reputation. Each additional offense adds Infamy. After multiple incidents, the lord is pressured to act more decisively --- reassignment to a remote posting, stripping of court privileges, or formal censure. A samurai with a pattern of violence is a political liability for their lord: the topic 'Lord [X]'s vassal [Y] is a known brawler' generates court pressure on the lord to control their retainer. Mechanically: each repeated offense within a rolling window of four IC seasons increases the punishment tier and generates a higher-tier topic (Tier 4 on first offense, Tier 3 on third offense within the window).

### 11.3.13 The Investigation Loop --- LOCKED

This section defines how crimes are investigated from commission to evidence accumulation. Every crime in Section 11.3 feeds through this loop. The investigation loop is the bridge between 'a crime happened' and 'evidence_total reaches the accusation threshold.'

### 11.3.13a Presence and Stealth in Lesser Zones

**Open presence (default): **A character entering a Lesser Zone is automatically noticed by everyone present. No roll. Both parties add each other to their met_characters set (Section 29.15.24). This is the normal state --- samurai move through the world openly.

**Stealthy presence: **A character may attempt to enter or remain in a Lesser Zone without being noticed. The character rolls Stealth (Stealth/Agility). Every character already present in the zone makes a passive Perception roll (Investigation/Perception) --- this roll is automatic and hidden. The observers do not know they are making it. Any observer whose roll meets or exceeds the Stealth roll knows the stealthy character is present. Any observer who fails does not --- as far as they are aware, the stealthy character was never there.

**One-sided awareness: **The stealthy character does not know which observers spotted them and which did not. They know who is in the zone (they can see), but they do not know who saw them. This creates uncertainty after a crime --- the criminal knows the monk was there but does not know if the monk noticed them.

**Zone event log: **The zone_event_log (Section 29.15.24) always records the stealthy character's presence regardless of Stealth roll result. The log is system-level ground truth. Human witnesses can be fooled; the log cannot. This is the Kitsuki's Eye's unique advantage --- it reads the log and can find characters that no conscious witness noticed.

### 11.3.13b The Witness Pool

When a crime occurs in a Lesser Zone, the witness pool is the set of characters present who successfully perceived everyone else present. This pool is determined at the moment of the crime by the passive Perception rolls made against any stealthy characters.

**Witness knowledge: **A witness who passed the Perception check knows the criminal was present. They do not automatically know the criminal committed the crime --- they know who was there, not what happened. A magistrate interviewing the witness learns the presence data. The magistrate then cross-references presence data with the crime to identify suspects.

**Witness recall: **Any character can attempt to recall who was present in a Lesser Zone at a given time. This is a flat Intelligence roll. The TN scales with elapsed time: same IC day = TN 10, same IC month = TN 15, previous IC month = TN 20, two months ago = TN 25, approaching one season = TN 30. Beyond one season, memories are too degraded to be reliable. The Kitsuki's Eye technique provides a bonus to this recall roll. Testimony generated from recall is valid evidence under the ritsuryo principle (Section 11.3.10), weighted by the recalling character's Honor.

### 11.3.13c The Criminal's Post-Crime Phase

Between committing the crime and being investigated, the criminal has a window to act. This phase occurs before a magistrate is assigned and before anyone knows a crime has happened (or in the case of murder, before the body is discovered). The criminal's actions during this window shape the investigation that follows.

**Step 1 --- Recall who was present. **Immediately after the crime, the criminal makes an Intelligence roll to recall who else was in the Lesser Zone. TN 10 (same day --- easy). Success gives the criminal a list of characters they remember seeing. Failure means they cannot recall clearly and must act blind. This is the criminal's only source of information about their exposure.

**Step 2 --- Evaluate risk. **Based on the recall, the criminal assesses: how many potential witnesses exist, what their Status and Honor are (higher-Honor witnesses are more dangerous because their testimony carries more weight), and whether any of them are likely to have noticed the criminal (were they looking in the right direction, were they paying attention, were they drunk). This is a decision point for the NPC Decision Engine --- personality determines the response.

**Step 3 --- Witness tampering (optional). **The criminal may attempt to neutralize potential witnesses before the investigation begins. Four options, each with its own risk:

BRIBE WITNESS: Temptation roll vs witness's Etiquette/Willpower.

  Success: witness agrees to say nothing. Co-conspirator created (Tier 2 secret).

  Failure: witness now knows the criminal has something to hide. +10 evidence

  if the witness reports the bribe attempt to the magistrate.

INTIMIDATE WITNESS: Intimidation/Willpower vs witness's Etiquette/Willpower.

  Success: witness is frightened into silence. No co-conspirator --- fear, not

  complicity. But the intimidation is a hostile action, writes provocation flag,

  and is recorded in the zone_event_log.

  Failure: witness is now hostile and motivated to report. +10 evidence.

KILL WITNESS: Eliminates testimony permanently. But creates a second murder

  investigation. The criminal must now conceal two crimes. Each additional

  killing adds exposure risk exponentially.

DO NOTHING: Hope the witness did not notice, or that their testimony will not

  matter. Zero risk, zero mitigation.

**Step 4 --- Establish false alibi (optional). **The criminal proactively tells others 'I was at the temple all evening' before anyone asks. This is a Sincerity (Deceit) roll made against no opposition --- the lie is simply planted. It becomes contested only when a magistrate checks the alibi by interviewing the people the criminal claims to have been with. A false alibi that is confirmed by a co-conspirator (someone willing to lie for the criminal) is strong. A false alibi that falls apart under investigation (the temple monk says 'I never saw them') generates +10 evidence.

### 11.3.13d Crime Scene Examination --- New ActionID: EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE

**ActionID: EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE. **Category 5 (Intelligence). Cost: 1 AP. Skill: Investigation/Perception vs the criminal's original concealment roll (stored as the crime's concealment_tn when the crime was committed). Location: the Lesser Zone where the crime occurred. The magistrate must be physically present at the scene.

**Concealment roll at time of crime: **When a crime is committed, the criminal makes one concealment roll. The skill depends on the method of the crime. Poison: Medicine/Intelligence. Assassination or ambush: Stealth/Agility. Open killing: no concealment roll --- concealment_tn is 0, meaning any Investigation roll succeeds. The concealment roll result is stored on the crime record as concealment_tn. This number does not change --- it is set once at the time of the crime.

**Magistrate examination: **The magistrate rolls Investigation/Perception against concealment_tn. Success: the magistrate finds physical evidence at the scene. The evidence weight scales with the margin of success. Meeting the TN exactly: minor traces (evidence weight 10 --- a partial footprint, a faint smell, a disturbed object). Exceeding by 5+: significant evidence (weight 20 --- blood traces pointing to a method, a discarded tool, a witness account of strange sounds). Exceeding by 10+: major evidence (weight 30 --- the murder weapon, a clear trail, identifiable personal effects left at the scene). Each called Raise reveals one additional item of evidence at +10 weight. Failure: the magistrate finds nothing usable at the scene.

**Evidence decay: **Physical evidence at the scene degrades over time. The magistrate's effective roll is penalized based on elapsed time since the crime. Same IC day: no penalty. Same IC week: -2. Same IC month: -5. Previous IC month: -10. Approaching one season: -15. Beyond one season: the scene is effectively clean --- normal degradation (rain, foot traffic, cleaning) has destroyed usable traces. This creates natural time pressure for the investigation.

**Kitsuki advantage: **The Kitsuki Method (Section 2.8.5) interacts with this system. A Kitsuki investigator using the Eye reads the zone_event_log for presence data that witnesses may have missed. Additionally, the Kitsuki school's emphasis on physical evidence over testimony means their EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE rolls may benefit from school techniques that grant bonuses to Investigation rolls at crime scenes. The specific Kitsuki school technique bonuses are defined in the Bushi/Magistrate school reference (Section 29).

### 11.3.13e Magistrate Investigation Actions

Once assigned to a case, the magistrate spends AP over multiple days to accumulate evidence. The following actions are available during an investigation. Each costs 1 AP unless noted otherwise.

**EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (Section 11.3.13d): **Physical evidence at the crime location. Investigation/Perception vs concealment_tn. Can only be performed once per scene per magistrate --- you either find what is there or you do not.

**PROBE (existing ActionID): **Interview a specific character. The magistrate targets a witness, suspect, or associate. Contested: Courtier or Investigation/Awareness vs target's Etiquette/Awareness. Success: the target reveals information they know --- presence data, suspicious behavior, relationships. If the target is the suspect, the magistrate may learn disposition anomalies, nervousness, or contradictions. Evidence weight: 10-20 per successful interview depending on what is revealed.

**READ_CHARACTER (existing ActionID): **Assess a suspect's demeanor. Investigation/Perception vs target's Sincerity/Awareness. Success: the magistrate detects deception, nervousness, or guilt. This does not produce admissible evidence on its own but directs the investigation --- the magistrate now knows who is lying and can focus PROBE actions accordingly.

**Kitsuki's Eye (existing technique): **Reads the zone_event_log directly. Reveals presence data regardless of Stealth. TN scales with age (Section 29.15.24). The only investigation action that bypasses human witness limitations entirely.

**CHECK_ALIBI (new investigation sub-action, no separate ActionID --- resolved as part of PROBE): **When a suspect has established an alibi, the magistrate interviews the people the suspect claims to have been with. If the alibi is genuine, it clears the suspect (evidence_total -= alibi weight). If the alibi is false, the contradiction generates +10 evidence. A co-conspirator supporting a false alibi must pass their own Sincerity (Deceit) vs the magistrate's Investigation/Perception --- if they fail, both the alibi and the co-conspirator are exposed.

### 11.3.13f Evidence Accumulation and Thresholds

Evidence accumulates as the magistrate completes investigation actions. Each successful action adds its evidence weight to the case's evidence_total. The accusation threshold is 40 --- when evidence_total reaches 40, the magistrate has sufficient grounds to formally accuse the suspect.

EVIDENCE WEIGHTS:

  EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (minor traces): 10

  EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (significant): 20

  EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (major): 30

  EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE (per Raise): +10

  PROBE (witness testimony): 10-20

  READ_CHARACTER (detected deception): 0 (directs investigation, not evidence)

  Kitsuki's Eye (presence data): 15

  Failed bribe attempt: +15

  False alibi exposed: +10

  Co-conspirator testimony: 20-30

  Intercepted correspondence: 20-50 (depends on content)

  Confession: 50

HARD EVIDENCE (can reach threshold alone):

  Intercepted letter detailing the crime: 50

  Confession under interrogation: 50

  Murder weapon with identifiable marks: 40

ACCUSATION THRESHOLD: 40

BRIBERY EVALUATION TRIGGER: 25 (Section 11.3.11g)

### 11.3.13g Time Pressure and Evidence Decay

The investigation is a race against time. Multiple decay mechanisms pressure the magistrate to work quickly while giving the criminal incentive to stall.

**Zone event log purge: **The zone_event_log purges entries older than one IC season (Section 29.15.24). Once purged, the Kitsuki's Eye cannot recover presence data. This is the hard deadline for the most powerful investigation tool in the system.

**Witness recall decay: **The Intelligence roll to recall who was present in a Lesser Zone increases in TN over time (Section 11.3.13b). Witnesses interviewed promptly provide clear testimony. Witnesses interviewed months later provide vague or unreliable testimony.

**Physical evidence decay: **The magistrate's EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE roll is penalized by elapsed time (Section 11.3.13d). A scene examined the day of the crime yields maximum evidence. A scene examined two months later may yield nothing.

**Recorded evidence is permanent: **Once a magistrate records testimony (through PROBE) or documents physical evidence (through EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE), that evidence is locked into the case file. It does not decay further. The decay affects only unrecorded evidence --- the magistrate is racing to capture evidence before it fades, not to prevent captured evidence from disappearing.

**Criminal stalling tactics: **A criminal benefits from delay. Avoiding the province, refusing to meet the magistrate, using intermediaries to slow correspondence, exploiting court procedures to defer hearings --- all of these run down the clock. The NPC Decision Engine evaluates stalling against the criminal's personality: Seigyo stalls systematically, Yu refuses to hide and faces the investigation directly, Gi cooperates with the investigation even if guilty.

### 11.3.13h Investigation Entry Points by Crime Type

**Immediate discovery (murder, violence): **A body is found or a public incident is reported. The daimyo is informed within 1 IC day. A magistrate is assigned within 1-3 IC days depending on magistrate availability and the daimyo's attentiveness. The investigation begins quickly --- the crime scene is fresh and witnesses have clear memories.

**Gradual signal accumulation (treason, skimming): **No single triggering event. Instead, signals accumulate: the audit system flags a revenue discrepancy (Section 4.3.7), a vassal's objectives stall suspiciously (Section 55), correspondence contains anomalies, disposition patterns shift. The lord or a vigilant advisor notices the pattern and assigns an investigation. Entry is slower --- the crime may have been occurring for seasons before anyone looks. Physical evidence may have already decayed, but the systemic evidence (financial records, behavioral patterns) persists longer than scene-level evidence.

**Specialized detection (maho): **Jade Magistrate patrols, Kuni Witch-Hunter investigations, Asako Inquisitor networks, or Provincial Taint Level spikes trigger detection. The investigation is handled by the specialized magistrate hierarchy (Section 11.3.4-5), not the standard clan magistrate system. Detection tools are different --- Sense Evil, Jade petal tests, Lore: Maho knowledge --- but the evidence accumulation loop is the same.

### 11.3.14 The legal_status State Machine --- LOCKED

Every named character has a legal_status field on their character sheet. This field tracks their position in the legal system across all crime types defined in Section 11.3. All crimes share the same state machine --- the crime type determines the consequences at each state, but the states and transitions are universal.

### 11.3.14a States

LEGAL_STATUS STATES:

  clear

    Default state. No investigation, no suspicion. The character

    is not involved in any active legal proceeding.

  suspected

    Signals are accumulating but no magistrate has been assigned.

    This state applies to gradual-detection crimes (treason,

    skimming) where the lord or an advisor notices anomalies

    but has not yet committed to a formal investigation.

    The suspect does not know they are in this state.

  under_investigation

    A magistrate has been assigned. The investigation loop

    (Section 11.3.13) is active. Evidence is being accumulated.

    The suspect may or may not know they are under investigation

    depending on whether the magistrate has approached them.

  accused

    evidence_total has reached the accusation threshold (40).

    The magistrate has formally presented charges. The accused

    knows they are charged. Defense options activate: Sincerity

    defense (Section 11.3.8c), trial by combat (Section 11.3.9f),

    political intervention.

  decreed_guilty

    The accused has been found guilty. Punishment is determined

    by the crime type and severity tier. The lord decides the

    specific punishment within the range defined by the crime.

  acquitted

    The accused has been cleared. Defense succeeded, evidence

    was insufficient, or trial by combat was won. legal_status

    returns to clear. The character cannot be re-accused on the

    same charge with the same evidence. New evidence can open

    a new investigation.

  pardoned

    A higher lord has overridden the guilty verdict. The

    character is legally cleared but the pardon is publicly

    known. Generates a Tier 3 topic. Disposition hits to the

    pardoning lord from characters who believe the guilty

    verdict was correct.

  fugitive

    The suspect or accused fled before the investigation

    concluded or before sentencing. The character is wanted.

    Magistrates in any province may detain them. Generates

    a persistent Tier 3 topic: '[Character] is a fugitive

    from justice.' The character cannot hold office, attend

    court, or operate openly without risk of arrest.

### 11.3.14b Transitions

STATE TRANSITIONS:

  clear -> suspected

    Trigger: signals accumulate (audit flags, disposition anomalies,

    behavioral patterns). Applies to gradual-detection crimes only.

  clear -> under_investigation

    Trigger: immediate discovery (body found, public violence,

    maho detected). Skips suspected state entirely. Magistrate

    assigned within 1-3 IC days.

  suspected -> under_investigation

    Trigger: lord decides accumulated signals warrant a formal

    investigation. Magistrate assigned.

  suspected -> clear

    Trigger: signals prove false or dissipate. The lord decides

    no investigation is warranted. No record remains.

  under_investigation -> accused

    Trigger: evidence_total reaches accusation threshold (40).

    Magistrate formally presents charges.

  under_investigation -> clear

    Trigger: investigation concludes without sufficient evidence.

    Magistrate closes the case. Also triggered by successful

    bribery (Section 11.3.11f) burying the investigation.

  accused -> decreed_guilty

    Trigger: defense fails. Sincerity defense roll unsuccessful,

    political intervention insufficient, or the accused offers

    no defense. Lord renders judgment.

  accused -> acquitted

    Trigger: defense succeeds. Sincerity defense halves evidence

    below threshold, trial by combat won, or political intervention

    convinces the lord to dismiss charges. Returns to clear.

  decreed_guilty -> pardoned

    Trigger: a higher lord overrides the verdict. Politically

    costly for the pardoning lord. Generates Tier 3 topic.

  under_investigation -> fugitive

    Trigger: the suspect flees the province during investigation.

    The investigation continues in absentia. If evidence reaches

    threshold, the character is accused and decreed guilty in

    absentia (no defense possible while fugitive).

  accused -> fugitive

    Trigger: the accused flees before sentencing. Automatic

    guilty verdict rendered in absentia. Sentence applies if

    the fugitive is ever captured.

  fugitive -> decreed_guilty

    Trigger: fugitive is captured. The in-absentia verdict is

    enforced. The character may request a new hearing at the

    capturing lord's discretion but has no right to one.

### 11.3.14c Multiple Concurrent Investigations

A character can be the subject of multiple legal proceedings simultaneously. The legal_status field is not a single value but an array of active case entries: legal_status: [{case_id: int, crime_type: string, state: string, evidence_total: int, magistrate_id: int or null, concealment_tn: int}]. Each case progresses independently through the state machine. A character may be clear on one charge, under_investigation on another, and accused on a third. A conviction on any charge fires its punishment independently --- a character convicted of murder does not have their skimming investigation dropped.

### 11.3.14d Character Sheet Integration

The legal_status field on a character's sheet tracks their relationship to specific crimes. It references a world-level CrimeRecord (Section 11.3.14e) rather than storing crime data directly. Evidence accumulates against a specific suspect, not against the crime in general --- two suspects for the same crime may have different evidence totals.

CHARACTER SHEET FIELDS (Legal):

  legal_status: [{

    crime_record_id: int (references the world-level CrimeRecord),

    state: string (suspected / under_investigation /

      accused / decreed_guilty / acquitted / pardoned / fugitive),

    evidence_total: int (evidence accumulated against THIS suspect),

    evidence_items: [{type: string, weight: int, source: string,

      timestamp: int (IC day)}],

    accusation_timestamp: int or null,

    verdict_timestamp: int or null

  }]

Default: legal_status: [] (empty array --- no active cases).

### 11.3.14e World-Level Crime Record

A CrimeRecord exists independently of any suspect. When a crime occurs, the system creates a CrimeRecord in the world state. This record persists whether or not a suspect is ever identified. A murder creates a CrimeRecord the moment the victim dies. A skimming crime creates a CrimeRecord when the audit system flags a discrepancy. The CrimeRecord is the crime itself --- the legal_status entries on character sheets are individual characters' relationships to that crime.

CRIME RECORD (world state):

  CrimeRecord: {

    crime_id: int (unique identifier),

    crime_type: string (treason / murder / skimming / maho /

      magistrate_corruption / violence / other),

    location: lesser_zone_id (where the crime occurred),

    victim_id: int or null (null for victimless crimes like skimming),

    perpetrator_id: int (system-level ground truth --- the character

      who actually committed the crime. Hidden from all characters.

      Only the system knows this. Investigation discovers it.),

    concealment_tn: int (set at crime commission from

      perpetrator's concealment roll. Section 11.3.13d.),

    crime_timestamp: int (IC day),

    discovered: bool (has anyone found evidence the crime occurred?),

    discovery_timestamp: int or null (IC day when discovered),

    magistrate_id: int or null (assigned investigating magistrate),

    suspects: [int] (character IDs identified during investigation),

    scene_examined: bool (has EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE been performed?),

    status: string (open / closed_insufficient_evidence / solved / buried)

  }

**Lifecycle: **A CrimeRecord is created the moment a crime occurs (system-level, not character-level). It starts with discovered: false if the crime was covert (no witnesses, body not yet found). Discovery happens when: a body is found, a witness reports, the audit system flags a discrepancy, or a patrol encounters evidence. Once discovered, the entry point logic (Section 11.3.13h) fires and a magistrate may be assigned. The magistrate's INVESTIGATE_CRIME objective (Section 57.16) references the crime_id. As the investigation progresses, suspects are added to the suspects array and corresponding legal_status entries are created on those characters' sheets. The CrimeRecord's status transitions from open to solved (suspect convicted), closed_insufficient_evidence (investigation exhausted), or buried (magistrate bribed, Section 11.3.11f).

**Undiscovered crimes: **A crime with discovered: false has no investigation, no magistrate, no suspects. It exists only as system-level ground truth. The crime may be discovered later through zone_event_log review (Kitsuki's Eye), accidental discovery (someone stumbles on the body), or the perpetrator's subsequent actions drawing attention. If the zone_event_log purges before discovery (one IC season), and no witnesses exist, and no physical evidence was left (concealment_tn was high and nobody examined the scene), the crime may never be discovered. The CrimeRecord persists indefinitely but remains inert. The perpetrator got away with it.

### 11.3.15 Sentencing --- Personality-Driven Punishment Selection --- LOCKED

When a character is decreed_guilty (Section 11.3.14b), the daimyo decides the punishment. The daimyo always makes this decision personally --- it is not delegated to the magistrate. Each crime defines a punishment range from lightest to harshest (e.g., murder Tier 2: exile to seppuku). The daimyo selects a point on that range based on a leniency score.

### 11.3.15a Leniency Score

The leniency score is a single value that determines where on the punishment range the daimyo lands. Higher leniency = lighter punishment. Lower leniency = harsher punishment. The score is calculated from three components.

LENIENCY SCORE = personality_base + disposition_modifier + pressure_modifier

PERSONALITY BASE (determined by daimyo's primary virtue):

  Bushido virtues:

    JIN: +30 (Compassion strongly favors mercy)

    GI: 0 (Honesty applies the law as written --- no lean either way)

    REI: +15 (Courtesy prefers to handle matters with grace and restraint)

    YU: -10 (Courage believes in facing consequences directly)

    CHUGI: 0 (Duty follows what the law prescribes --- no personal lean)

    MEIYO: -10 (Honor demands that dishonor be answered firmly)

    MAKOTO: +10 (Sincerity considers the full human context)

  Shourido virtues:

    SEIGYO: varies (Control evaluates political advantage --- see below)

    KETSUI: -15 (Determination sees punishment through without hesitation)

    DOSATSU: 0 (Insight evaluates each case individually --- no fixed lean)

    CHISHIKI: 0 (Knowledge weighs precedent and consequence --- no fixed lean)

    KANPEKI: -20 (Perfection demands the ideal standard be upheld)

    KYORYOKU: -15 (Strength punishes weakness and failure)

    ISHI: -20 (Will does not relent once judgment is made)

**Seigyo special case: **A Seigyo daimyo does not have a fixed personality base. Instead, Seigyo evaluates which punishment best serves their interests. If the convicted is useful (high competence, valuable role), Seigyo leans toward leniency (+20). If the convicted is a political liability or an obstacle, Seigyo leans toward harshness (-20). If the convicted's punishment can be leveraged for political gain (demonstrating strength to rivals, earning favor with the victim's clan), Seigyo picks whichever option produces the best political outcome. The engine evaluates this through the existing objective alignment system --- the punishment that best serves the daimyo's active objectives scores highest.

### 11.3.15b Disposition Modifier

DISPOSITION MODIFIER:

  Daimyo's disposition toward convicted character, scaled:

    Disposition +50 to +100 (Friend/Devoted): +20 leniency

    Disposition +20 to +49 (Acquaintance/Warm): +10 leniency

    Disposition -10 to +19 (Stranger/Neutral): 0

    Disposition -30 to -11 (Rival/Cold): -10 leniency

    Disposition -100 to -31 (Enemy/Blood Enemy): -20 leniency

A daimyo who likes the convicted will lean toward mercy. A daimyo who despises them will choose the harshest available punishment. This is thematically accurate --- Rokugani justice is personal, not abstract. A lord who punishes a friend harshly is making a statement about duty. A lord who punishes an enemy lightly is making a statement about magnanimity.

### 11.3.15c Political Pressure Modifier

The topic generated by the crime (Section 11.3.13h) measures public attention. The daimyo responds to this pressure --- harsher punishment protects the lord's reputation when the court is watching.

PRESSURE MODIFIER:

  Crime topic tier and momentum:

    No active topic (crime was private/contained): 0

    Tier 4 topic (minor gossip, low momentum): -5

    Tier 3 topic (serious, moderate momentum): -10

    Tier 2 topic (major, high momentum): -20

    Tier 1 topic (crisis-level, widespread): -30

  Cross-clan modifier (stacks with topic tier):

    Victim is from another clan: -10 additional

    Victim's clan actively pushing for harsh punishment: -15 additional

Negative pressure reduces leniency --- pushing the daimyo toward harsher punishment. A private crime with no topic pressure allows the daimyo maximum discretion. A Tier 2 cross-clan murder with the victim's clan demanding justice leaves the daimyo little room for mercy without looking weak.

### 11.3.15d Mapping Leniency to Punishment

Each crime defines a punishment range. The leniency score maps to a point on that range. Exact thresholds vary by crime but follow a consistent pattern:

LENIENCY SCORE RANGES:

  +30 or above: lightest available punishment for the crime tier.

  +10 to +29: light punishment with some additional consequence

    (longer house arrest, public apology, recompense).

  -10 to +9: standard punishment as defined by the crime tier.

  -30 to -11: harsh punishment. Upper end of the crime tier's range.

  Below -30: harshest available punishment. For crimes where the

    range allows it, this may escalate one tier (e.g., a murder

    Tier 2 with leniency below -30 becomes effectively capital ---

    seppuku demanded, exile refused).

**Floor and ceiling: **The leniency score cannot reduce a punishment below the crime's minimum for that tier. A Tier 3 covert killing is always capital regardless of leniency --- there is no lighter option. Similarly, leniency cannot make a crime disappear --- the minimum punishment for any decreed_guilty verdict is public acknowledgment of guilt.

**Gi override: **A Gi daimyo ignores the leniency score entirely and applies the standard punishment for the crime tier. Gi does not consider disposition, political pressure, or the convicted's usefulness --- the law is the law. This makes Gi daimyos predictable, which their vassals may appreciate (fair treatment) or resent (no consideration for context).

### 11.3.16 Fugitive Extradition --- LOCKED

When a character's legal_status enters fugitive (Section 11.3.14a) and they flee to another clan's territory, the investigating clan must locate the fugitive and request their return through diplomatic channels. The investigating clan's magistrate has no jurisdiction beyond their clan's borders. This section defines the intelligence, diplomacy, and escalation pipeline for cross-clan fugitive recovery.

### 11.3.16a Locating the Fugitive

The investigating clan does not automatically know where the fugitive went. A fugitive who uses BEGIN_TRAVEL leaves zone_event_log entries along their route, but these purge after one IC season (Section 29.15.24) and the investigating magistrate cannot access logs in foreign provinces without cooperation. Locating a fugitive in foreign territory requires intelligence actions.

**LOCATE_CHARACTER NeedType: **The existing LOCATE_CHARACTER NeedType (Section 55.A) decomposes into BEGIN_TRAVEL, PROBE, OBSERVE_VIA_EYE, LISTEN_REFLECT, RUN_COURIER_ROUTE, WRITE_LETTER, and ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE. This handles the magistrate or an assigned agent tracking the fugitive through informants, letters, and movement.

**Topic propagation: **A fugitive of notable Status moving through foreign territory may generate a Tier 4 topic: 'A stranger matching [description] was seen in [province].' The fugitive's Status determines visibility --- a ji-samurai can vanish easily, a family daimyo is recognized everywhere. The investigating clan can acquire these topics through normal channels (court, letters, PROBE) and narrow the search.

**Fugitive concealment: **The fugitive may actively conceal their identity. A Sincerity (Deceit) roll to maintain a false identity, a Stealth roll to avoid populated areas, or simply traveling to a remote province where nobody knows their face. The higher the fugitive's Status and Glory, the harder concealment becomes --- famous faces are hard to hide.

### 11.3.16b Extradition Request

Once the fugitive is located, the investigating clan sends a formal extradition request to the harboring clan's lord. This is a diplomatic action: a formal letter (Section 12.7) or a court action (NEGOTIATE, Section 15.4) requesting the return of the fugitive. The request identifies the fugitive, states the crime, and demands cooperation.

The request generates a Tier 4 diplomatic topic: '[Clan A] requests extradition of [fugitive] from [Clan B].' This puts the harboring lord on notice publicly --- how they respond is visible to the court.

### 11.3.16c Harboring Lord's Decision

The harboring lord evaluates the extradition request through the NPC Decision Engine. The decision is not binary (accept/refuse) but a scored evaluation of multiple factors.

EXTRADITION EVALUATION FACTORS:

  Disposition toward requesting clan:

    Friend or above (+31 and above): +20 toward cooperation

    Neutral (-10 to +30): 0

    Rival or below (-11 and below): -20 toward cooperation

  Fugitive's usefulness to the harboring lord:

    No useful skills or role: 0

    Competent in a needed area (military, court, shugenja): -15

      (negative = resist cooperation --- the fugitive is worth keeping)

    Possesses valuable intelligence about the requesting clan: -25

  Political leverage opportunity:

    Harboring lord sees a bargaining chip: -20

      (resist cooperation to extract concessions first)

    No leverage value: 0

  Crime severity:

    Tier 4 (minor --- violence, dishonorable conduct): 0

    Tier 3 (serious --- open killing, skimming): -5

      (harboring a serious criminal is embarrassing)

    Tier 2 (major --- treason, covert murder): -15

    Tier 1 (maho, imperial treason): -30

      (no sane lord harbors a maho user --- the Jade Magistrates will come)

  Fugitive's Status:

    Status 1-2 (ji-samurai): 0 (nobody notices)

    Status 3-4 (mid-rank): -5 (harder to hide)

    Status 5+ (daimyo or higher): -15 (impossible to hide, everyone knows)

  Personality base (harboring lord's primary virtue):

    GI: +30 (returns the fugitive on principle)

    MEIYO: +20 (honor demands proper conduct)

    CHUGI: +10 (duty to maintain inter-clan order)

    JIN: +10 (compassion may cut either way --- mercy for the fugitive

         or justice for the victim)

    REI: +15 (courtesy demands cooperation with formal requests)

    MAKOTO: +10 (sincerity values honest dealings)

    YU: 0 (courage has no strong lean)

    SEIGYO: -20 (Control sees the fugitive as a resource to exploit)

    KETSUI: 0 (no lean)

    DOSATSU: -10 (Insight evaluates what intelligence the fugitive offers)

    CHISHIKI: -10 (Knowledge values what the fugitive knows)

    KANPEKI: +10 (Perfection demands proper order)

    KYORYOKU: -15 (Strength keeps what it has)

    ISHI: -15 (Will does not yield to external demands)

  COOPERATION THRESHOLD: 0

    Score >= 0: Lord cooperates. Fugitive returned.

    Score < 0: Lord refuses or delays.

### 11.3.16d Harboring Lord's Response Options

**Cooperate: **The fugitive is arrested and returned to the requesting clan. Disposition gain from the requesting clan (+5 to +10 depending on the crime severity). The matter is resolved. Quick and clean.

**Negotiate: **The lord agrees to return the fugitive but demands something in return. A Favor, a trade concession, political support on a court topic, or withdrawal of support from a rival. The fugitive becomes a bargaining chip. The extradition request topic remains active until the negotiation concludes, applying ongoing political pressure. Seigyo lords strongly favor this option.

**Refuse: **The lord explicitly refuses to return the fugitive. Consequences: disposition hit from the requesting clan (-10 to -20 depending on crime severity). Topic escalates: 'Clan [B] refuses to return fugitive [X] to Clan [A]' (Tier 3). Other clans form opinions --- clans allied with the requesting clan lose disposition toward the harboring clan. The harboring lord is making a public statement that they value the fugitive (or dislike the requesting clan) more than inter-clan legal cooperation.

**Deny knowledge: **The lord claims they do not know the fugitive is in their territory. This is only viable if the fugitive has low Status and is genuinely hard to find. If the requesting clan has strong intelligence placing the fugitive in the harboring lord's territory, the denial is transparently dishonest --- treated as a refusal with an additional -5 disposition for the insult of a bald lie.

### 11.3.16e Escalation

**Emerald Champion intervention: **If the harboring lord refuses and the crime is serious enough (Tier 2 or above), the requesting clan can petition the Emerald Champion for Imperial intervention. The Emerald Champion can issue an Imperial warrant compelling the harboring lord to surrender the fugitive. Defying an Imperial warrant is itself an offense --- the harboring lord must weigh whether the fugitive is worth the Imperial displeasure. Gi and Chugi lords comply with Imperial warrants immediately. Seigyo and Ishi lords may still resist if they calculate the benefit outweighs the risk.

**Covert extraction: **The requesting clan sends agents into the harboring clan's territory to capture the fugitive without permission. This is a sovereignty violation --- if the agents are caught, it generates a Tier 3 diplomatic incident: 'Clan [A] agents operating illegally in Clan [B] territory.' The harboring lord gains a legitimate grievance. The covert extraction uses standard Stealth and combat mechanics on the ASCII map --- it is a mission generated by the world state. Success returns the fugitive but damages inter-clan relations. Failure exposes the operation and makes the diplomatic situation worse.

**Accept the refusal: **The requesting clan drops the matter. The fugitive remains free in foreign territory. The in-absentia conviction stands but cannot be enforced. The fugitive topic persists at low momentum as a lingering grievance. If the fugitive ever returns to the requesting clan's territory or is encountered by their magistrates, they can be arrested on the standing warrant.

### 11.3.17 Magistrate Allocation and Conviction Cascade --- LOCKED

This section defines how many clan magistrates exist per administrative unit and what happens administratively when an office-holder is convicted.

### 11.3.17a Magistrate Count

CLAN MAGISTRATE ALLOCATION:

  Province: 1 clan magistrate per province.

  City: 1 additional clan magistrate per city.

  Otosan Uchi: 1 clan magistrate per district (special case).

EXAMPLES:

  Rural province, no city: 1 magistrate.

  Province with a city: 2 magistrates (1 provincial + 1 city).

  Province with two cities: 3 magistrates.

  Otosan Uchi: 1 per district (17 districts confirmed per Section 2.3).

Each clan magistrate has jurisdiction over their assigned area --- the provincial magistrate covers the province, the city magistrate covers the city. When a crime occurs, the magistrate for that jurisdiction handles it. If that magistrate is already occupied with another investigation, no other clan magistrate is available --- the crime waits until the magistrate finishes or the daimyo intervenes.

### 11.3.17b Yoriki

Each clan magistrate has a complement of yoriki (Section 11.3.2) --- samurai deputies who assist with investigations. Yoriki are named NPCs who run through the NPC Decision Engine like any other character. They are samurai whose lord is the daimyo but whose operational superior is the magistrate. The magistrate directs yoriki through ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE within the legal hierarchy: 'Interview this witness,' 'Examine this scene,' 'Travel to [province] and check this alibi.' Yoriki can perform investigation actions independently --- PROBE, READ_CHARACTER, EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE --- under the magistrate's direction. This means a magistrate with two yoriki can run three investigation threads simultaneously: the magistrate handles the political and court-facing aspects of the case while the yoriki handle legwork across multiple locations. Yoriki count per magistrate fluctuates from one to a dozen depending on the jurisdiction's needs and the daimyo's willingness to assign samurai to law enforcement. A rural province magistrate may have one or two yoriki. A city magistrate may have four or five. A magistrate in a major jurisdiction like an Otosan Uchi district may have up to a dozen. The daimyo decides the allocation --- assigning more yoriki improves legal coverage but pulls samurai from other duties. A magistrate who loses yoriki (transferred, killed, reassigned by the daimyo) has their investigation capacity reduced proportionally. Yoriki have their own personality, skills, Honor, and standing objectives. A magistrate with a Gi yoriki and a Seigyo yoriki will get very different investigation results from each --- the Gi yoriki pursues truth relentlessly while the Seigyo yoriki may cut corners or leverage information for personal advantage. The magistrate's UPHOLD_LAW standing objective (Section 57.16.9) applies only to the magistrate, not the yoriki. Yoriki act on the magistrate's orders, not on their own initiative.

### 11.3.17c Emerald Magistrate Scarcity

Emerald Magistrates are extremely rare --- a handful serve the entire Empire. They are not allocated per province but assigned to specific cases by the Emerald Champion. An Emerald Magistrate arriving in a province is an event --- it means something has escalated beyond the clan's ability or willingness to handle it. Emerald Magistrates have Empire-wide jurisdiction and can override any clan magistrate (Section 11.3.6). Their scarcity means most crimes are handled entirely within the clan system. Imperial intervention is reserved for cross-clan crimes, treason, maho, and cases where local justice has failed.

### 11.3.17d Scarcity as Gameplay

The one-magistrate-per-province allocation is deliberately scarce. A single magistrate can handle one investigation at a time. If two crimes occur simultaneously, one waits. If the magistrate is corrupt, the entire province's legal system is compromised with no backup. If the magistrate is killed, there is no replacement until the daimyo appoints one --- which requires finding a qualified samurai, which may take a full IC season. This creates real governance pressure: a daimyo who neglects their magistrate appointment, appoints an incompetent, or loses their magistrate to assassination has a province where crimes go uninvestigated.

### 11.3.17e Conviction Cascade for Office-Holders

**Magistrate convicted: **All active cases assigned to the convicted magistrate are suspended. The daimyo must appoint a replacement magistrate. The replacement receives all suspended case files and continues from where the previous magistrate left off --- evidence already accumulated is preserved, but the replacement must re-examine any evidence they want to rely on (they did not personally gather it). If the convicted magistrate was corrupt (Section 11.3.11), current cases they handled are reviewed for irregularities but past closed cases are not automatically reopened (Section 11.3.11d).

**Governor or provincial daimyo convicted: **The lordship succession system (Section 22.5) fires. The next eligible character in the succession order assumes the position. Province administration continues through the transition period with reduced efficiency --- Stability drops by 5 during the transition. If no eligible successor exists, the family daimyo appoints one directly.

**Family daimyo or clan champion convicted: **Major political event. The clan's succession system fires (Section 22.5). This generates a Tier 2 political topic affecting the entire clan. Allied and rival clans react through the disposition and court systems. The transition period is longer and more disruptive --- Stability drops across all provinces in the family or clan during the transition.

**Appointment gap: **Between conviction and replacement, the position is vacant. A vacant magistrate position means no investigations can begin. A vacant governor position means no provincial decisions are made (tax rates freeze, no new construction, no levy orders). The daimyo above the vacant position must fill it --- the longer the vacancy, the worse the administrative consequences.

### 11.3.18 Operational Hierarchy --- LOCKED

Rokugan's authority structures operate on two parallel tracks: the feudal hierarchy (lord_id) and the operational hierarchy (operational_superior_id). The feudal hierarchy is permanent --- your lord is your lord until succession, exile, or death changes it. The operational hierarchy is situational --- it assigns day-to-day command authority based on role, posting, or mission. The two tracks coexist. A samurai always has a feudal lord. They may or may not have an operational superior depending on their current assignment.

### 11.3.18a Character Sheet Field

operational_superior_id: int or null

  Points to the character's immediate operational superior.

  null = no operational superior (character receives objectives

    only from lord_id through the feudal hierarchy).

  Set when the character is assigned to an operational role.

  Cleared when the assignment ends.

operational_hierarchy_type: string or null

  The type of operational relationship. Determines which rules

  govern objective assignment and the scope of authority.

  Values: 'military', 'legal', 'delegation', null.

**military_rank: enum or null
The character's current military rank within the Akodo rank system.
Values: null (no military rank — civilian, monk, courtier not serving in army), HOHEI (private — rank and file samurai assigned to a Company), NIKUTAI (corporal — assumes command of a squadron if the Gunso falls), GUNSO (sergeant — commands a guntai of ~20 soldiers within a Company), CHUI (lieutenant — commands a Company), TAISA (captain — commands a Legion), SHIREIKAN (commander — commands a Go-hatamoto section), RIKUGUNSHOKAN (general — commands a Go-hatamoto). HOHEI, NIKUTAI, and GUNSO are serving ranks, not command ranks. These characters are assigned to a Company but do not command it. Their commanded_unit_id is null. Instead they carry an assigned_company_id pointing to the Company they serve in. Their operational_superior_id points to their Company’s Chui. They fight in the Battlefield Bubble during mass combat, roll the Battle Table, and trigger Heroic Opportunities — individual excellence is not limited by rank. A Gunso with Battle 5 and Insight Rank 4 contributes more to a battle through Heroic Opportunities than many Chui, even though they don’t command the Company.
assigned_company_id: int or null
Points to the Company the character is assigned to as a serving soldier (HOHEI, NIKUTAI, or GUNSO). null for characters who are not serving in a military unit, or for commanders (CHUI+) who use commanded_unit_id instead. When the Company moves, the character moves with it automatically — same as commanders. A character’s current_settlement_id always matches their assigned Company’s current_location_id.
Set when the character is appointed to a military command. Cleared when they are relieved of command, retire, or the unit disbands.
commanded_unit_id: int or null
Points to the specific unit the character commands — a company_id, legion_id, section_id, or army_id depending on their rank.
null = not currently commanding a unit.
Bidirectional link: the unit's commander_id points to this character, and this field points back to the unit. Both must update together.
commanded_unit_type: enum or null
The type of unit commanded. Determines which data table to look up.
Values: null, COMPANY, LEGION, SECTION, ARMY.
Always matches military_rank: CHUI commands COMPANY, TAISA commands LEGION, SHIREIKAN commands SECTION, RIKUGUNSHOKAN commands ARMY.
Kolat exception: **The Kolat conspiracy (Section 54.7) operates a parallel covert hierarchy that cannot use the standard operational_superior_id field because that field is visible on the character sheet and would expose the conspiracy. Instead, Kolat agents use a hidden kolat_superior_id field (defined in Section 54.7c, Master character sheet additions) that is invisible to all non-Kolat systems. Orders flow through SEND_KOLAT_DIRECTIVE rather than ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE. A Kolat agent may simultaneously hold a public operational_superior_id (serving a magistrate or military commander) and a hidden kolat_superior_id (serving their Sect Master). The two hierarchies coexist without the public one being aware of the hidden one.

### 11.3.18b Engine Integration

ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE (Section 55.10) currently uses HierarchySystem.get_direct_vassals(lord_id) to determine who can receive objectives. This is extended: the engine also checks for characters whose operational_superior_id matches the assigning character. Both lord_id and operational_superior_id can assign objectives. When both a lord and an operational superior assign conflicting objectives, the operational superior's objective takes priority for day-to-day actions --- the character follows operational orders. However, the lord can override by issuing a direct order that explicitly countermands the operational chain. This is rare and signals a breakdown in the delegation of authority.

**Traversal: **The operational hierarchy is traversable. Any character can walk up the chain: query operational_superior_id of their superior, then that superior's operational_superior_id, and so on until null is reached. This gives the character awareness of the full command structure above them. Objectives only flow from the immediate superior --- a Shireikan does not skip the Taisa to assign tasks to a Chui. However, a higher operational superior can override a lower one's orders in exceptional circumstances (see 11.3.18f).

### 11.3.18c Legal Hierarchy --- Magistrate and Yoriki

When a daimyo assigns yoriki to a magistrate, each yoriki's operational_superior_id is set to the magistrate's character ID. operational_hierarchy_type = 'legal'. The magistrate assigns investigation objectives to yoriki through ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE. The yoriki's lord_id remains the daimyo --- the daimyo can recall them, reassign them, or overrule the magistrate. But day-to-day, the magistrate directs their work.

EXAMPLE:

  Daimyo Takeshi (lord_id for all three)

    -> Magistrate Isamu (operational_superior_id: null, reports to lord)

      -> Yoriki Kenji (operational_superior_id: Isamu)

      -> Yoriki Mei (operational_superior_id: Isamu)

  Isamu assigns: 'Kenji, interview the witnesses at the docks.'

  Isamu assigns: 'Mei, examine the crime scene at the warehouse.'

  Isamu handles: the political side --- reporting to Takeshi, court appearances.

### 11.3.18d Military Hierarchy

The traditional Akodo military rank system (Emerald Empire 4e, Chapter Five) establishes a multi-level operational chain. When a samurai is assigned to a military unit, their operational_superior_id is set to their immediate commanding officer. operational_hierarchy_type = 'military'. The full chain:

MILITARY CHAIN:

  Hohei (Private) -> Gunso (Sergeant)

  Nikutai (Corporal) -> Gunso (assumes command if Gunso falls)

  Gunso -> Chui (Lieutenant) [commands a squadron of ~20 soldiers]

  Chui -> Taisa (Captain) [commands a kaisha (company) of 7 squadrons, ~153 officers and soldiers]

  Taisa -> Shireikan (Commander) [commands a daibutai (legion) of 6 companies + 1 reserve company, ~750 troops]

  Shireikan -> Rikugunshokan (General) [commands a section or wing of a go-hatamoto, overseeing 4-12 legions]

  Rikugunshokan (General) [commands a go-hatamoto (full clan army) of up to 48 legions, 1-3 per clan] -> Family Daimyo or Clan Champion (feudal lord)

  Each level sets operational_superior_id to their immediate commander.

  At the top, the Rikugunshokan's operational_superior_id is null ---

  they receive objectives from their lord_id (the daimyo or Champion).

When a samurai is mustered into an army, their operational_superior_id is set. When they are discharged or the army disbands, it is cleared. A samurai in garrison follows the same pattern: operational_superior_id points to the garrison commander. The Kaiu Wall command structure (Section 2.4.9) is a permanent military operational hierarchy: Taisa → Shireikan → Clan Champion. These operational_superior_id assignments are standing, not campaign-dependent.

**Named NPC threshold (amended): **The smallest unit in the mass combat system is the Company, commanded by a Chui. The bulk of a Company’s soldiers (unnamed hohei, nikutai, and gunso) are abstracted into Population Units (PU) and do not have character sheets. However, named NPCs and player characters CAN hold the ranks of Hohei, Nikutai, or Gunso within a Company. These named individuals serve alongside the abstracted PU soldiers. They carry the rank as flavor and for Heroic Opportunity eligibility, but they do not replace the PU abstraction — the Company’s combat stats (Health, Attack, Defense, Morale) still represent the full unit including its unnamed soldiers. A named Gunso leading a squadron is one of seven squadron leaders in the Company, not a separate tactical entity. The operational_superior_id chain therefore begins at Chui level: Chui → Taisa → Shireikan → Rikugunshokan. The Hohei-through-Gunso ranks listed above exist as lore and flavor but are not tracked as individual characters in the simulation. A Company's combat effectiveness is represented by its PU stats, not by the individual soldiers within it.

### 11.3.18e Courtier Delegations

When a clan sends a delegation to a foreign court, the delegation leader directs the other members' court actions. The delegation members' lord_id remains their daimyo back home, but their operational_superior_id is set to the delegation leader's character ID. operational_hierarchy_type = 'delegation'. The delegation leader assigns court objectives: 'Approach Lord Kakita about the trade agreement,' 'Gather intelligence on the Scorpion delegation,' 'Deliver this gift to the host.' Delegation members execute these assignments using their own skills and personality --- the leader directs what, the member decides how.

EXAMPLE:

  Crane Clan Champion (lord_id for all)

    -> Delegation Leader Doji Haruki (operational_superior_id: null)

      -> Courtier Doji Akemi (operational_superior_id: Haruki)

      -> Yojimbo Kakita Ryo (operational_superior_id: Haruki)

      -> Shugenja Asahina Yuki (operational_superior_id: Haruki)

  Assignment ends when the delegation returns home.

  operational_superior_id is cleared.

### 11.3.18f Override Rules

**Higher operational superior override: **A higher operational superior can override a lower one's orders. A Shireikan can countermand a Taisa's orders to a Chui. This is rare and socially costly --- it signals that the Shireikan does not trust the Taisa. A Shireikan who frequently overrides their Taisa damages disposition between them and generates a Tier 4 topic: 'Shireikan [X] bypasses Taisa [Y].' The override is mechanically simple: the higher superior issues a new ASSIGN_VASSAL_OBJECTIVE targeting the subordinate directly, which replaces the lower superior's objective.

**Feudal lord override: **The feudal lord (lord_id) can always override the operational superior. The daimyo can recall a yoriki from the magistrate's direction, pull a soldier from their commander's authority, or reassign a delegation member. This clears or changes the character's operational_superior_id. The operational superior has no recourse --- the feudal lord outranks all operational authority. However, doing this without good reason damages the lord's relationship with the operational superior whose chain they disrupted.

**Conflicting objectives: **If a character has active objectives from both lord_id and operational_superior_id, the operational objective takes priority for AP spending. The lord's objective remains in the objective stack but is deprioritized. The character is 'on assignment' --- their lord's standing objectives (advance family name, seek glory) are paused while they execute operational tasks. When the operational assignment ends (operational_superior_id cleared), the lord's objectives resume normal priority.

### 11.3.18g Assignment and Clearing

OPERATIONAL_SUPERIOR_ID IS SET WHEN:

  Legal: daimyo assigns yoriki to a magistrate.

  Military: samurai is mustered into a unit or assigned to garrison.

  Delegation: samurai is assigned to a delegation departing for court.

  Kaiu Wall: samurai is posted to a tower (standing assignment).

OPERATIONAL_SUPERIOR_ID IS CLEARED WHEN:

  Legal: magistrate is removed, investigation concludes, or daimyo recalls.

  Military: army disbands, campaign ends, or samurai is discharged.

  Delegation: delegation returns home.

  Kaiu Wall: samurai is reassigned from the Wall (rare).

  Any: operational superior dies. Subordinate falls back to lord_id

    objectives until a new operational superior is assigned.

  Any: feudal lord explicitly recalls the character.

**Single operational superior: **A character can only have one operational_superior_id at a time. They cannot serve two operational masters simultaneously. If the feudal lord (lord_id) assigns a new operational role while an existing one is active --- for example, calling a yoriki to military service during an active investigation --- the new assignment overwrites the old one. The previous operational superior loses the subordinate immediately. The daimyo's authority as feudal lord supersedes any operational assignment. The overwritten operational superior is not consulted --- the daimyo decides. This may damage disposition between the daimyo and the previous operational superior if the reassignment disrupts their work.

### 11.3.18h Refusing an Operational Order

A subordinate's personality filter (Annex C, Section 55.7) may block an operational order. A Gi yoriki ordered to suppress evidence, a Jin soldier ordered to execute prisoners, a Makoto delegation member ordered to deliver a deceptive message --- the personality filter prevents the action from firing. This is not a bug. It is correct characterization. The question is what happens next.

**Immediate consequence: **The operational superior loses disposition toward the subordinate (-10 to -20 depending on how critical the refused order was). The subordinate's action for that AP resolves as DO_NOTHING --- they stand their ground but take no alternative action. The refusal is visible to anyone present (zone_event_log records the interaction).

**Escalation to the daimyo: **The subordinate may attempt to inform the daimyo (lord_id) that the operational superior gave an improper order. This is extremely risky. The subordinate sends a letter or requests an audience with the daimyo. The daimyo evaluates the claim. Three outcomes are possible:

**Daimyo believes the subordinate: **The operational superior is investigated or removed. The subordinate is vindicated but has made a permanent enemy of the operational superior (disposition floor: -50, no decay). If the operational superior is a corrupt magistrate, this triggers the magistrate corruption pipeline (Section 11.3.11). The daimyo's disposition toward the subordinate increases (+5 to +10 for loyalty in reporting wrongdoing).

**Daimyo sides with the operational superior: **The subordinate is guilty of insubordination. Consequences: reassignment to a lesser posting, Honor loss (-0.3), disposition loss from the daimyo (-10). The operational superior's authority is reinforced. The subordinate was right to refuse on principle but wrong to escalate --- the daimyo has spoken.

**Daimyo does not investigate: **The daimyo dismisses the complaint without looking into it. The subordinate's relationship with both the daimyo and the operational superior is damaged. The subordinate learns that the chain of command will not protect them. This is the worst outcome for the subordinate --- they have exposed themselves as a troublemaker to both superiors without achieving anything. Personality-driven: a Seigyo daimyo dismisses complaints that don't serve their interests. A Gi daimyo always investigates.

**NPC Decision Engine: **Whether the subordinate escalates is personality-driven. Gi always escalates --- honesty demands it. Chugi does not escalate --- duty means following orders, even uncomfortable ones. Yu confronts the operational superior directly rather than going over their head. Seigyo calculates whether escalation serves their interests. Makoto escalates only if they believe the daimyo will listen. The escalation decision is evaluated as a standard NPC Decision Engine action: the subordinate weighs the risk (insubordination charge, disposition loss) against the principle (the order was wrong) through their personality filter.

### 11.3.18i Disposition in Operational Relationships

The existing disposition system (Section 12.2) handles operational relationship quality through standard action-based changes. Every interaction between operational superior and subordinate --- assigning tasks, providing feedback, sharing credit or blame, resolving disagreements --- generates disposition modifiers through the normal system. No separate periodic tick is needed. A magistrate who listens to their yoriki (LISTEN_REFLECT), acknowledges good work (CHARM), and assigns tasks fairly builds positive disposition naturally. One who takes credit, assigns dangerous tasks without support, or treats subordinates with contempt erodes it through the same mechanics.

**Starting baseline: **When operational_superior_id is set, a starting disposition modifier is applied if the two characters have not previously met. This is a Category 1 permanent modifier (Section 12.2) representing professional respect for the assigned role. Military: +5 (soldiers respect the chain of command by default). Legal: +5 (yoriki respect the magistrate's appointed authority). Delegation: +10 (delegation members were chosen for compatibility --- the delegation leader and members have usually met before). These baselines are small --- they set a starting floor above Stranger but are quickly overridden by actual interactions. Personality compatibility modifiers (Section 12.2, Virtue Compatibility Table) still apply on top of these baselines as with any first meeting.

### 11.3.19 Crime Suppression --- LOCKED

Magistrates and yoriki actively suppress organized criminal activity. This section connects the magistrate legal system (Section 11.3) to the existing insurgency suppression system (Section 11.11, Phase 5). It does not define a separate suppression mechanic --- organized crime is already modeled through the insurgency system. Ronin Bandit Uprisings, Urban Criminal Networks, smuggling operations, and pirate activity are all insurgency types with full five-phase lifecycles in Section 11.11. The magistrate's role is to detect these insurgencies (Phases 1-4) and commit to suppression (Phase 5) through the existing mechanics.

### 11.3.19a The Magistrate's Role in Insurgency Suppression

The insurgency system (Section 11.11, Phase 5) requires a character or military unit to commit to a province for suppression. The magistrate or their yoriki IS that committed character. When a magistrate with UPHOLD_LAW (Section 57.16.9) detects an insurgency in their jurisdiction, their INVESTIGATE_CRIME objective decomposes into the suppression action defined in Section 11.11. The suppression roll, the TN (insurgency Strength x 5), and all success/failure outcomes are those of Section 11.11 --- not a separate system.

**Magistrate detection advantage: **A magistrate actively investigating their jurisdiction detects insurgencies faster than passive systems. ORDER_PATROL rolls against insurgency Concealment. A magistrate with yoriki running multiple patrols simultaneously has more chances to detect per season. The UPHOLD_LAW standing objective drives the magistrate to patrol proactively, meaning provinces with zealous magistrates catch insurgencies earlier --- often in Phase 2 or 3 rather than Phase 4 when they've already caused significant damage.

**Bushi vs courtier magistrate: **A bushi-school magistrate commits personally to the suppression roll, using their Battle skill. A courtier-school magistrate commits yoriki to the suppression while handling the legal and political aftermath. If the courtier magistrate has no bushi-trained yoriki, they request military support from the daimyo --- the garrison or a military unit provides the suppression force. The magistrate's contribution in this case is intelligence: their investigation identified the target, which the military force then acts on.

### 11.3.19b Player Involvement --- ASCII Map Missions

NPCs never experience the ASCII map layer (Section 9 --- Design Intent). NPC suppression resolves through the background dice roll defined in Section 11.11 Phase 5. When a player character is personally involved --- as the magistrate, as a yoriki, accompanying the suppression force, or ordered to participate by their lord --- the suppression generates an ASCII map mission instead of resolving through a background roll.

ASCII MAP MISSION TYPES (player involvement only):

  RAID_BANDIT_CAMP: Combat. Ronin Bandit Uprising insurgency.

  RAID_SMUGGLING_OPERATION: Stealth/combat hybrid.

    Capturing intact yields evidence for further investigation.

  RAID_GANG_HIDEOUT: Urban combat. Urban Criminal Network insurgency.

  INTERCEPT_PIRATE_VESSEL: Naval/coastal. Deferred pending Section 11.9.

  ARREST_SUSPECT: Apprehension. Track and capture a specific individual.

The ASCII map mission outcome replaces the background dice roll. Success produces the same effects as a successful Phase 5 suppression (Strength reduction, Stability gain). Failure produces the same consequences (no reduction, casualties). The player's tactical decisions determine the outcome rather than a single roll.

### 11.3.19c Suppression Drive --- NPC Decision Engine

Magistrates with UPHOLD_LAW evaluate crime suppression through the INVESTIGATE_THREAT NeedType when an insurgency is detected in their jurisdiction. The decomposition routes to the existing insurgency Phase 5 suppression action. Suppression priority is modified by crime severity (bandit/pirate +15, smuggling/gang +10), Province Stability (below 50: +20 urgency, below 25: +30 urgency), and personality (Gi +15, Yu +10, Meiyo +10, Chugi +10, Kyoryoku +15, Seigyo evaluates political benefit).

### 11.3.19d Consequences of Successful Suppression

All consequences are those defined in Section 11.11 Phase 5: insurgency Strength reduced, Stability gain, crisis momentum reset. Additionally, the magistrate system adds: captured criminals are processed through the legal system (heimin criminals face swift justice per Section 11.4.1, samurai criminals enter the investigation loop). Recovered evidence may reveal connections to larger networks, corrupt officials, or the Kolat --- feeding new INVESTIGATE_CRIME objectives. The magistrate and participating yoriki gain Glory. The daimyo gains disposition toward an effective magistrate.

### 11.3.19e Doshin Recruitment

Magistrates and yoriki can recruit local doshin (Section 11.3.1) to assist with both investigations (Section 11.3.13) and suppression actions. Doshin are peasant enforcers who know the local area, know the local people, and provide manpower that samurai law enforcement lacks. A yoriki arriving in an unfamiliar province to investigate a crime or suppress an insurgency is operating blind without local intelligence --- doshin bridge that gap. Player characters serving as yoriki can recruit doshin in the same way.

### 11.3.19e.i Recruitment

**Action: **Recruiting doshin is not a separate ActionID. When a magistrate or yoriki arrives in a province and begins an investigation or suppression action, they automatically have access to whatever doshin are present in the province. The doshin serve the local magistrate by default (Section 11.3.1) --- a visiting yoriki with the magistrate's authority can direct them. If the local magistrate refuses to cooperate (rare, but possible with a corrupt or hostile magistrate), doshin recruitment requires the daimyo's explicit authorization.

**Doshin availability: **Doshin count scales with settlement size (Section 11.3.1 mechanical hook). A small village may have one doshin. A large city may have dozens organized into district forces. A province without doshin (no appointment, previous doshin killed or dismissed) has no local support available. The number of available doshin directly affects the bonus they provide.

### 11.3.19e.ii Mechanical Bonus

DOSHIN BONUS TO INVESTIGATION:

  Doshin provide a flat bonus to PROBE and EXAMINE_CRIME_SCENE

  rolls when the crime involves heimin witnesses or heimin-level

  activity. Doshin know who lives where, who was seen doing what,

  and which peasants are lying.

  Small doshin force (1-2 doshin): +3 to investigation rolls

  Medium doshin force (3-5 doshin): +5 to investigation rolls

  Large doshin force (6+ doshin): +8 to investigation rolls

DOSHIN BONUS TO SUPPRESSION:

  Doshin serve as guides, scouts, and auxiliary force during

  suppression actions. They know the local terrain, can identify

  bandit hideout locations, track smuggling routes, and point

  out gang-controlled buildings.

  Small doshin force (1-2 doshin): +3 to suppression rolls

  Medium doshin force (3-5 doshin): +5 to suppression rolls

  Large doshin force (6+ doshin): +8 to suppression rolls

DOSHIN BONUS TO SAMURAI-LEVEL INVESTIGATIONS:

  Doshin cannot arrest or confront samurai, but they observe.

  They can report a samurai suspect's movements, visitors,

  and habits. This provides intelligence, not evidence ---

  doshin testimony carries minimal weight under ritsuryo

  (Section 11.3.10) but directs the yoriki's investigation.

  Bonus: +3 to PROBE rolls targeting samurai suspects

    (doshin intelligence provides leads, not proof).

  Bonus does not scale with doshin count for samurai

    investigations --- one observant doshin is as useful

    as ten for tracking a samurai's movements.

### 11.3.19e.iii Doshin Risk

Doshin committed to a suppression action face the same critical failure risk as named characters, but they are heimin --- their deaths carry no Honor or Glory consequences. A critical failure on the suppression roll may kill doshin. Lost doshin reduce the province's law enforcement capacity until replacements are appointed (one IC season for the local magistrate to train new ones). A province that loses its doshin to a botched bandit raid is worse off than before --- the criminals are emboldened and the province has no ground-level enforcers.

### 11.3.19e.iv Player Yoriki and Doshin

A player character serving as a yoriki can recruit doshin as described above. On the ASCII map, recruited doshin appear as allied NPC units accompanying the player during investigation or suppression missions. They are not powerful fighters --- they carry non-lethal weapons (Section 11.3.1), have low combat stats, and will flee from serious threats. Their value is local knowledge: a doshin NPC on the ASCII map can point out hidden paths, identify NPCs by name, warn about dangerous areas, and provide information about the local criminal landscape that the player character would not otherwise know. They are guides and informants, not soldiers. Full ASCII map companion behavior, command interface, and AI priority stack: Section 57.46.

### 11.3.19e.v Doshin Stat Blocks

Doshin are heimin with limited martial training. They are not samurai. They have no school, no techniques, no Void Points. Their value is local knowledge and numbers, not combat prowess. Two tiers exist: village doshin (part-time, minimal training) and city doshin (full-time, experienced). Both appear as allied NPCs on the ASCII map when recruited by a player character.

### 11.3.19e.vii Doshin Availability and Recruitment Limits

Doshin exist automatically in every settlement above the remote/isolated threshold. They are a natural product of any community --- the local magistrate formalizes their authority but does not need to actively appoint them. Availability is determined by four factors.

DOSHIN AVAILABILITY FORMULA:

  var base = get_doshin_baseline(settlement.pu)

  var available = base - settlement.doshin_losses

  if settlement.type == 'village' and (season == SPRING or season == AUTUMN):

      available = ceil(available / 2)

  if province.stability < 25:

      available -= 2

  available = max(available, 0)

**Baseline: **Lookup from the settlement PU allocation table (Section 11.3.19e.vi). Already tracked on every settlement.

**Losses: **An integer counter on the settlement: doshin_losses. Increments when doshin are killed during suppression (Section 11.3.19e.iii), murdered by criminals, or otherwise removed. Decrements by 1 per IC season as the community trains replacements. A settlement that lost 3 doshin in a failed raid recovers one per season --- three seasons to return to full strength.

**Seasonal absence (village doshin only): **Village doshin are part-time farmers (Section 11.3.1). During Spring (planting) and Autumn (harvest), half the village doshin force is unavailable --- they are in the fields. City doshin are full-time and unaffected by season.

**Stability reduction: **A province in the Broken stability range (0-25) loses 2 doshin from availability. These doshin have deserted, been intimidated into inaction, or been co-opted by the criminals they were supposed to fight. This does not increase doshin_losses --- the doshin return to availability if Stability recovers above 25.

### 11.3.19e.viii Recruitment Limits

A magistrate or yoriki recruiting doshin for a mission cannot take the entire available force. The settlement needs ongoing law enforcement during the operation. Recruitment limits:

RECRUITMENT LIMITS:

  Maximum recruitable: ceil(available / 2)

    Half the available doshin, rounded up. The other half

    remains on regular duty in the settlement.

  Doshin headman: never recruited for field operations.

    The headman stays at the settlement as the local

    intelligence hub and coordinates the remaining doshin.

  Daimyo override: if the daimyo explicitly authorizes,

    the full available force can be committed. The

    settlement takes under-garrisoned consequences

    (Section 4.3.11) for law enforcement during the

    absence. Stability drops -2 per season the settlement

    has no doshin on regular duty.

Recruited doshin are committed for the duration of the suppression action (one season per Section 11.11 Phase 5) or the duration of the ASCII map mission for player involvement. They return to regular duty when the operation concludes. If doshin are killed during the operation, doshin_losses on the settlement increments accordingly.
