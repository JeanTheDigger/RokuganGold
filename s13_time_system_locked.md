# 13. Time System — LOCKED

The game runs on a persistent roleplay server model, similar to Neverwinter Nights persistent worlds. The server clock advances continuously regardless of who is online. Time has two layers — mechanical time and narrative time — which are deliberately decoupled.

**Mechanical Time — LOCKED:**

All resource production, army movement, seasonal progression, court action budgets, and world events run on the server clock. This advances automatically.

- 1 tick = 6 real-world hours = 1 in-game day.

- 4 ticks = 1 real-world day = 4 in-game days.

- 1 in-game month (30 IC days) = 7.5 real-world days (~1 real week).

- Spring (3 months, 90 IC days) = 22.5 real-world days (~3 real weeks).

- Summer (3 months, 90 IC days) = 22.5 real-world days (~3 real weeks).

- Autumn (2 months, 60 IC days) = 15 real-world days (~2 real weeks).

- Winter (4 months, 120 IC days) = 30 real-world days (~1 real month).

- 1 full in-game year (360 IC days) = 90 real-world days (~3 real months).

- Winter Court (120 IC days, the full Winter season) = 30 real-world days (~1 real month).

- Players who log in once per real-world day experience four full in-game days per session.

**Narrative Time — LOCKED:**

Roleplay scenes between players operate in a narrative bubble where time is fluid. A scene can last 3 real hours and represent 30 in-game minutes, or compress hours of in-game time into a brief exchange. This is accepted and expected on a roleplay server. Narrative time does not need to map precisely to mechanical ticks.

- Players declare their mechanical actions once per tick — what their character did during that in-game day.

- Players roleplay those actions as richly and slowly as the story demands, independent of tick timing.

- This decoupling allows deliberate, character-driven storytelling to coexist with a continuously advancing mechanical world.

- When a player is offline, their character exists in a default state — resting in their holdings, maintained by standing orders issued to their household.

**Bubble Time — Scene-Based RP — LOCKED:**

When players engage in an active RP scene (a conversation, a duel, a negotiation, a ceremony), that scene operates in Bubble Time — a frozen IC moment that persists until the scene concludes, regardless of how long it takes in real time. The world clock continues to advance around them.

- A scene is anchored to the IC timestamp when it began. All outcomes from the scene are retroactively applied to that timestamp when the scene ends.

- Characters in an active Bubble Time scene are considered occupied. They cannot spend Action Points, take world map actions, or participate in other scenes simultaneously. Their AP for that IC day are held in reserve until the scene concludes.

- Other characters in the same IC location may join an active scene if it makes narrative sense. Joining characters also become occupied for the scene’s duration.

- If a scene spans multiple real-world hours (and therefore multiple IC ticks), the character’s mechanical state is frozen at the scene’s start timestamp. Stipends, resource ticks, and world events that fire during the scene are queued and applied when the scene ends.

- A scene that produces a mechanically significant outcome (a character death, a public declaration, a duel result) registers at the scene’s anchor timestamp. This prevents a character from dying in a bubble while the world map has already moved past that moment.

- Players may end a scene at any time by mutual agreement or by physically leaving the IC location. Unresolved scenes that are abandoned default to no outcome.

**Why This Works:**

- The 6-hour tick means a full in-game year takes ~3 real months — slow and deliberate, fitting for a roleplay server, but fast enough that stories progress and seasons change meaningfully. A player experiences roughly four seasons turning per real-world quarter.

- The day/night rhythm of real life creates natural variety — players logging in at different times encounter different in-game moments without requiring a strict day/night split system.

- The decoupling of narrative and mechanical time is the standard solution used by NWN persistent worlds and similar games — it respects both the mechanical systems and the roleplay culture simultaneously.
